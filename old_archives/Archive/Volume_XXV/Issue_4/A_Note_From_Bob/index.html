A Note From Bob
"The most basic question is not what is best, but who shall decide what is best."

The question is posed by Thomas Sowell, senior fellow at the Hoover Institution and one of the most insightful conservatives of our day. Ultimately, the answer must come down to who has the knowledge to decide well. Information is scarce and expensive Ð rarely after childhood do we find ourselves in a situation where the solution is clear and the problem one of moral strength. More often, the "solution" is merely a trade-off of one valuable thing against another, of justice against equality, fairness against freedom. The value of each choice is murky and difficult to decipher, the more so for those far away from the situation at hand. Indeed, it's childish in itself to imagine that others' choices are clear-cut and their measured indecision merely indicates a lack of moral will.

Should Stanford Hospital microwave its waste or incinerate it? Phrased differently, should the hospital's budget be spent on treating its patients or ensuring further that its waste poses no danger to the outside world? Either way, some people will be hurt and some will be helped. Who should make that decision Ð those at hand in the hospital, accountable to the bottom line for the health of their patients and to the EPA for the healthiness of their waste? Or students

accountable to nothing but their own conscience? Answering 'students' would ensure a solution that flatters the students' consciences Ð whether or not it measures help against harm in way that benefits the most people. Indeed, as we investigate on the front page, the students pressured the hospital to make a decision, seeing their choices as a matter of "environmental racism" on the part of the hospital administrators rather than a weighing of their duty to their patients against their duty to the surrounding community. Is the community better off as a result of the students' actions? Given that Stanford Hospital says it planned to switch anyway, the answer is at best unclear.

Who shall decide whether you eat grapes or not - yourself or a committee of your peers? You know more about your own opinions and your own tastes than they do. Neither of you likely knows much about the impacts of your decisions on the lives of grape workers. From Stanford, it's hard to know whether grape workers even support the boycott. As we report on the front page, the answers are neither clear-cut nor obvious. But, as Matt Barrett discusses on page five, there are compelling reasons to vote against the boycott and compelling reasons for the University to end the vote altogether.

Often, the question boils down to whether the University or any organization should take a stand on a political issue. Embroiling an institution is politics is a dangerous matter Ð very slight differences in feeling can lead to vast changes in policy. Whether the margin of victory is 327 votes or two hundred thousand, only one man will be inaugurated President. As our page three coverage of Hoover Fellows Bruce Bueno de Mesquita and David Brady discussed, a whole nation can have doubts the morning after - but it can't unmake its choice.

The Review asked Stanford's new Provost, John Etchemendy, those same questions. His responses are on page seven. On page six, Scott Rasmussen examines "Who shall decide?" from a different angle: the ideas of intellectuals that have shaped Ð or misshaped - our society. The conclusion? Let's not leave the decisions up to them.

In the end, who decides must be the one with the most knowledge Ð the one closest to the scene. That's why schemes which rely on placing control in the hands of third-parties Ð normally the government - fail on such a regular basis. In this case, each student can choose whether to help or hinder the free choice of his peers to eat grapes. May we all choose wisely.
