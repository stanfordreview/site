Opinion
It's Time To End The Boycott

Grape boycott supporters at the Carn-uva-l in White Plaza.
Photo by Connie Zhou.
We are on the eve of the annual grape boycott. Since 1988 and more so since1994, when four students staged a hunger strike to show their support for the United Farm Workers grape boycott, Stanford students have voted every year on whether their dining hall should serve grapes. It is time for the administration to reform its policy and cancel the vote. Students who wish to participate in the boycott should be able to do so, but students who wish to separate themselves from the issue should also be able to do so.

The boycott is not valuable because it harms the very grape workers that it is ostensibly designed to protect. If consumers do not buy grapes, then the grape industry will not make a profit, and the grape workers will lose their jobs. This is precisely why the Grape Workers and Famer's Coalition, a group consisting of the individuals whom the boycott affects, continues to oppose the boycott. Furthermore, the boycott reflects a dispute between grape growers and the UFW, and Stanford students need not necessarily be involved.

Stanford students have clearly recognized the decreasing value of the boycott over the last several years. In 1996, 100% of dining units on campus supported the boycott. In 1997, the percentage supporting the boycott dropped to 72%. In 1998, it dropped further to 59%. In 1999, just 32% of dining units supported the boycott. Harvard and Yale have apparently recognized this trend already, for both of those schools have abandoned the vote on the grape boycott.

But the most important reasons the University should cancel the boycott are more fundamental. Even though the reasons to support the boycott are not convincing and student support of the boycott has declined significantly, some students still choose to support it, and they should be able to do so. Stanford should cancel the vote, however, because the process is biased, resources could be used more efficiently, and Stanford should not engage in partisan politics.

First, we must consider how the process is biased. Professor Luis Fraga once said, concerning the boycott, that students can "choose not to participate". However, this is not true. Students who support the boycott will of course want to participate. But suppose a student does not support the boycott. The student has two options. He or she could vote against the boycott, thereby participating in the process by definition. Or, he or she could abstain from voting, thereby tacitly accepting the outcome of the vote. The process is biased towards students who already want to participate. Furthermore, the vote carries over from one year to the next. This means that students in any dorm must accept the previous year's vote for the first few months of school. Many students, therefore, must accept the boycott without ever getting the opportunity to vote at all.

Going a few months without grapes may seem like a small sacrifice. It would be, if there were no other solution. But the solution is obvious. Cancel the vote and let students who support the boycott support it and leave those who oppose it alone. In the past, students have complained that this is not enough because students who support the boycott still have to pay a board bill, and thus indirectly support grape growers if their dining hall buys grapes. However, if there is sufficient support for the boycott in any given dining hall, students will not eat grapes, and the dining hall will naturally stop buying grapes. If there is enough support for the vote to pass, then there is enough support for the boycott to be effective anyway. Indeed, that same argument cuts the other way: students who oppose the boycott are being forced to support the boycott.

Additionally, it is a misuse of resources to continue to educate students about just the grape boycott. Every year, many students wonder why they are asked to vote about grapes to the exclusion of every other agricultural product. Assistant Dean Tommy Lee Woon, who is in charge of grape education on campus, has a response to this curiosity. He explains that Stanford holds the grape vote because the grape boycott is the only one that the United Farm Workers endorse. This response, of course, begs the question, "Why is the United Farm Workers Union determining Stanford University administrative policy?" There is no good answer to that question. If, every fall, Stanford spent an equal amount of time educating students about labor union and economic issues in a broader sense, students could then choose for themselves which particular issues are most important. Yet, the University continues to focus itself narrowly, on grapes alone.

Even beyond this, it is still inappropriate for the University to sponsor the grape vote. This is because, by supporting the vote, Stanford inappropriately involves itself in a political issue. Each year, Stanford can claim that a certain number of dining units support the boycott. Of course, this number can never be truly accurate since not all of the students in any dining unit actually support the boycott. Nonetheless, Stanford continues to be able to make this claim, and it continues to endorse the vote. By endorsing the vote, Stanford endorses the boycott. Phil Maxwell, a member of the Grape Workers and Farmer's Coalition, observes, "The endgame of this effort is to use the name of Stanford." Stanford should educate its students about politics but is should not engage in politics. When Ralph Nader spoke at Stanford, many University officials were concerned that it would appear that Stanford was endorsing Nader. They knew this was inappropriate because Stanford cannot involve itself in partisan politics. It is a paradox that the University knows this, yet continues to endorse the grape boycott.

Stanford's efforts are not completely misguided: labor issues will always be important. But students can boycott grapes on their own, and it is academically and politically irresponsible for Stanford to continue to require the vote.

Matthew Barrett is an undeclared sophomore. He is a strong supporter of a student's right to choose grapes.
