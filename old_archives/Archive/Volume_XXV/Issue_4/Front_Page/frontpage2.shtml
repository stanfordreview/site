Front Page
Students Rally To Protest Dioxin
by Grey Baker
Staff Writer

Nearly 150 Stanford students gathered at White Plaza on November 1st to protest the "environmental racism" of the Stanford Hospital and Medical Center. Organized by Students for Environmental Action at Stanford (SEAS), the hour-long student rally expressed outrage through the clanging of pots and pans, a twelve-foot puppet, and the Radical Cheerleaders.

"Stanford sends its waste to predominantly African American and Latino Communities. Is this racist?" yelled Tim Ly, co-coordinator of SEAS. "Yeah, f*** yeah." Other students answered colorfully.

Those communities, other student activists added, were "targeted by corporations" and were not enjoying democracy. "Can you image a medical waste incinerator in Beverly Hills, or in Palo Alto?" one young woman asked. "No, because they have the money and the political clout to stop it!"

As speakers' shouting was amplified by PA systems, a gangly twelve-foot puppet representing the "racist" Stanford darted in and out of the crowd, waving its arms and menacing passers-by.

Throughout the rally, the Radical Cheerleaders, a squad of students complete with pomfrons, riled up the crowd with their signature chants. "Toxic waste, burned in a haste, doesn't make a very nice place, Stanford Medics, trying to make a buck, dumping toxins on communities down on their luck, we don't want it, we don't like it, we want to make it stop."

This is not the first campaign put together by SEAS: several years ago, the same organization pressured the University to adopt a "socially responsible investment fund." Through such campaigns, SEAS has sought "to make the university a more moral and ethical organization." In their mission statement, SEAS vividly defines their world view. "We believe that racism and elitism are ever present in our lives and that we must confront them at every level, including within our own organization."

Students offered three demands for the Hospital at the protest - to stop sending Stanford's medical waste to the IES incinerator, which they say releases poisonous dioxins into the surrounding communities; to use other methods of disposal like microwaving while the hospital's contract still is in effect; and to phase out PVC plastics and mercury use at the hospital.


Racist SEAS puppet
After having met with hospital administrators several times to seemingly no avail, one orator expressed her frustration. "Ten months ago, we asked them to microwave the waste. We're getting tired of meetings and empty promises." Mr. Ly went even further to say that the hospital was not just breaking promises, but actively dismissing the SEAS campaign. "Stanford refuses to make the switch."

Directly following their Tuesday protest, SEAS leadership walked to the Stanford Medical Center to meet once more with Lou Sasken, Vice President of General Services at the hospital. Following the meeting, SEAS claimed "significant" progress in its efforts to stop Stanford medical waste from being sent to an incinerator in Oakland. Leaders of SEAS concluded that "This is a victory for the East Oakland community, for Stanford students, and for the Stanford Hospital!

Was it the SEAS pressure on the Hospital that forced them to make the switch? Lou Sasken denies that the SEAS protests have had any impact, stating that any changes in medical waste disposal at the hospital were inevitable. "The IES contract we started two years ago, and it was better technology than the one we had before that, and the one that I think we're going to is better yet. There's always been a progression."

He also makes it clear that the hospital has always been concerned with the environmental impact of its waste. "Every year, we look at what the technology is doing, where it's going. There are some things that are environmentally unacceptable about medical waste...we try to reduce the impact of what those things are." To those ends, Mr. Sasken has plans for short and long-term changes to medical waste disposal.

"What we're going to be able to do hopefully by the end of this month," says Mr. Saksen, "is to work with IES to take ... all the plastic and almost all the dioxin producing trash, microwave them, grind them up, and put them in a land fill. We had been working on that since last March."

For the long term, the hospital is in talks with IES and other waste disposal companies to set up a steam sterilization plant on campus.

To Mr. Sasken, the hospital is acting deliberately to improve its waste disposal technologies and is implementing those technologies as soon as it is realistically feasible. Mr. Sasken does not view SEAS's outrage as helpful.

"They [SEAS] don't have a lot of patience. Let's say we get our numbers hack from on-sight handling [of waste disposal] and find out it's not feasible, I'm sure [SEAS] will say that's wrong."

And on that issue, both SEAS and the Hospital would agree. SEAS will continue to pressure the hospital until it's demands have been met, because, in the words of SEAS members, "Stanford, Stanford, it's no joke, your waste is making Oakland choke!"
