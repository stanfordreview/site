The Last Page
Investigating The Union Force Field
Has Stanford neglected its scientific obligations to the endless pursuit of knowledge and the benefit of the community as a whole? Surely if a committee of professors discovered a method to keep pesticides out of fields they would investigate it to find the full implications.

Sadly, such a discovery was made more than five years ago, but the only reference to it is in an obscure policy document - the "Report of the Grape Policy Committee." That document notes the UFW's position that organic grapes should be included in the grape ban because they "will have pesticide residues...from windblown or off-target spraying on other fields." However, the document determined that grapes grown on unionized farms do not suffer from this problem. Shockingly, the report stops here, without any investigation as to the nature of this miraculous phenomenon, which we at the Review call the "union forcefield."

At first view, the union forcefield seemingly annihilates pesticides as they are blown toward the enclosed farm. Some researchers have postulated the existence of a fifth force, the "union force", in order to explain this phenomenon. In fact, such a fifth force offers the possibility of a "Grand Union Field Theory" that might unite together all four previously known forces into one.

The Review's crack team of grape researchers has begun an investigation into this fifth force of nature, and the results, while preliminary, are startling. We challenge the University as a whole, as well as grape-interested organizations such as MEChA and SEAS, to join with us in investigating this potentially world-shaking discovery. Below we offer our experiments to the watching eyes of the world.

Issue 1:

Question: Can the union forcefield be used as a purification method? Specifically, if grapes contaminated with pesticides are brought onto a unionized farm, will the field remove the pesticides? Will that effect persist once the grapes are removed from the farm?

Experiment: Grapes from organic farms were carried into a unionized farm where they were tested for major pesticides. They were then removed from the farm and tested again to see if the effect persisted. Note: To avoid the cost of third set of tests, it was presumed that, as noted by the Grape Policy Committee, the organic grapes were already contaminated.

Result: Neither test found significant quantities of pesticides on the grapes.

Conclusion: The union forcefield can indeed be used as a purification method for grapes.

Issue 2:

Question: Is the strength of the union forcefield dependent on the nature of the union contract? Are there particular policies necessary to enable the union forcefield (a certain wage? working hours? must pesticide restrictions be stated in the contract?)

Result: The Review was unable to investigate this matter directly due to the expense involved in opening dozens of unionized grape farms. The Review therefore defers to the work of the Grape Policy Committee, which did not feel any need to examine the union contract before deciding that it satisfactorily barred pesticide use. We conclude that the terms of the contract are irrelevant to its ability to block pesticides.

Issue 3:

Question: Is the union forcefield related to the phenomenon which prevents non-union workers from working on a unionized farm without paying the union to recruit workers in unrelated industries?

Result: There are a large number of differences in the properties of these effects. For example, non-union workers do not spontaneously cease to exist on entering a unionized farm.

Issue 4:

Question: Does the union forcefield violate the law of the conservation of mass-energy?

Result: The union forcefield does appear to challenge traditional notions of conservation of energy, and for a time Review researchers believed that the pesticides were indeed annihilated, requiring a revision of modern physics. Further investigation, however, has lead us to postulate that the pesticides are actually converted directly into energy, the "union-motive force."

Issue 5:

Question: If the union forcefield is capable of directly converting energy to matter, is it similar to a fusion reactor? Can it be used to create a fusion reactor? Result: Sadly, it appears not. The Review was unable to find any way to start a fusion reactor using union labor. We were not successful in finding funding for our final experiment, which was to have built an entire farm inside a giant, transparent nuclear reactor and then sprayed the reactor with large quantities of DDT.

Henry Towsner is a sophomore majoring in Math and Computational Science. The cartoon is by Ryan Parks, a senior majoring in Computer Science.
