Book Review
What Liberals Think (And What You Should Think about what Liberals Think)
The Burden of Bad Ideas: How Modern Intellectuals Misshape Our Society By Heather MacDonald

A prominent woman conducted a "listening tour" in New York this year. She talked to citizens of all walks of life in schools, in the press, and in the inner city. She saw society's problems, and identified ways to make things better. But her name was Heather MacDonald, not Hillary Clinton, and she was writing a book exposing the errors of the Left, rather than running for Senate to perpetuate them.

Heather MacDonald gives an honest look at the philosophical and practical shortcomings of the modern welfare state in The Burden of Bad Ideas: How Modern Intellectuals Misshape Our Society. Released in 2000, the book attempts to blame the left-wing intelligentsia for a litany of alleged problems with America: welfare entrapment, public school underperformance, Smithsonian Institution political-correctness, and even popular reaction to the February 4 New York Police Department shooting of Amadou Diallo.

If these examples seem unrelated to you, don't worry. Ms. MacDonald manages to link them together in a sweeping condemnation of the philosophy of the Left: "What are these destructive and blinding ideas? First among them is the notion that the poor cannot be held responsible for their own behavior, because they are victims of an unjust and racist American society. Buttressing this idea are a series of corollaries: that government can assume the role of parents; that America's ineradicable racism and sexism require double standards for minorities; that reason is a tool of male oppression; and that education is not about knowledge but ethnic empowerment."

To illustrate the development of modern attitudes regarding poverty, Ms. MacDonald relates the century-old history of the Hundred Neediest Cases, an annual Christmas feature of the New York Times describing the situations of the city's most needy citizens and soliciting donations on their behalf. Ms. MacDonald attempts to correlate shifts over time in the types of individuals receiving aid with shifts in perceptions of the poor: "The prototypical needy case in the first decades of the appeal was a struggling widow or plucky orphan; today's is more likely to be a single mother of five who finds her welfare check inadequate. This change reflects one of the century's most momentous cultural developments: the transformation of elite opinion regarding poverty and need."

Does it? Or does it reflect another of the century's most momentous cultural developments: the transformation of the poor itself? In a time when Horatio-Alger-type orphans are the poorest, then it seems reasonable that a program targeting the poor will assist them. Likewise, when single mothers are the poorest, it is reasonable that they will receive assistance. The conservative's rebuttal is that the intellectual transformation causes the social transformation, but Ms. MacDonald fails to make this case effectively. Without it, the Hundred Neediest Cases example falls short: it criticizes the obvious problems in modern urban society but does not justify choosing to blame the Left for causing the problem.

The book does a better job of explaining how modern liberal philosophy originally gained popular acceptance. Ms. MacDonald believes that the old foundations - Carnegie, Rockefeller, and Ford, for example - bankrolled the adoption of progressive ideas into modern political currency. "The results, from the 1960's onward, have been devastating," Ms. MacDonald claims. "Foundation-supported poverty advocates fought to make welfare a right-and generations have grown up fatherless and dependent. Foundation-funded minority advocates fought for racial separatism and a vast system of quotas-and American society remains perpetually riven by the issue of race. On most campuses today, a foundation-endowed multicultural circus has driven out the very idea of a common culture, deriding it as a relic of American imperialism." Ms. MacDonald establishes a solid connection between the money of the Left and the application of its ideas in activist programs, but is silent on the role liberal ideology played in the two revolutions in government entitlements that to this day shape the face of the inner city: Roosevelt's New Deal and Johnson's Great Society.

Nonetheless, Ms. MacDonald speaks up clearly about the results of left-wing activism in the present. She chastises the public education system's implicit dogma of what Ms. MacDonald calls, "Anything But Knowledge," where discussions of discrimination, identity, and feelings are prioritized over the presentation of objective facts and real-world skills. The result, states Ms. MacDonald, is that "other countries wallop us routinely in international tests." The phenomenon is the same in law school, where, Ms. MacDonald explains, "Exotic courses in victomology come with an opportunity cost . . . A variety of interdisciplinary studies like 'Law and Literature' or 'Law and Psychology' are crowding out traditional courses in commercial law, bankruptcy, and jurisdiction, so that students come out of school with less legal knowledge than did students three decades ago."

Even the emblem of American knowledge and science, the Smithsonian Museum, has fallen victim to educational revisionism. Ms. MacDonald reports that "recent visitors to the Smithsonian's Natural History Museum were greeted with some unpleasant news: the museum was contaminated. Not by asbestos or toxic chemicals, mind you, but by far more noxious substances: racism, sexism, and anthropocentrism." Ms. MacDonald describes how the museum's politically correct leadership substituted dialogues and narratives for exhibits and facts and concludes, "ironically, the only place one can consistently find traditional curating in the Smithsonian these days is in the non-Western art museums-the Sackler and Freer galleries of Asian art and the National Museum of African art. All three are elegant and understated, displaying art and even ritual artifacts as aesthetic objects, rather than as social texts."

The danger of twisting objective truth for political purposes in formal establishments of knowledge is that it becomes altogether too easy and convenient, with real societal consequences when it happens in other settings (the current emotional appeals over voter irregularities in Florida being a case in point!). As a case in point, Ms. MacDonald describes how in the aftermath of the Diallo shooting, liberal protest groups and the press distorted facts about the shooting itself and about the record of the New York Police Department. While the New York Times reported that "a culture of police abuse has struck universal fear into blacks and Hispanics," Ms. MacDonald responds, "Almost nothing in this tale was true. Residents of the city's most crime-ridden neighborhoods are far more positive about the police than the press ever hinted. Empirical data show a police department more cautious with the use of force than at any time in recent history." The result: New York public opinion reflected this manufactured fear, and Mayor Rudy Giuliani's approval ratings dropped by twenty points.

Ms. MacDonald is at her best in explaining the many absurdities of modern society, from educational revisionism to popular reaction to the Diallo shooting. However, in identifying each molehill of liberal folly, she glances over the mountain of citizens trapped in poverty. Ms. MacDonald makes a reasonable case that our society is badly misshapen, and that modern intellectuals have bad ideas, but in the most important example, she does not uphold her burden of connecting the two.

Scott Rasmussen is a sophomore. His parents taught him that there are no stupid people, only Democrats with stupid ideas.
