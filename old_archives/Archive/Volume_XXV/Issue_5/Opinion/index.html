Opinion
Don't Censor Gaieties: Fantasy Is Not Reality
Censorship has once again reared its ugly head at Stanford. In the name of political correctness, Big Game Gaieties will now submit its scripts to the ethnic centers for approval. Instead of being the humorous, irreverent jab at all aspects of Stanford life, it will be censored by a self-selected, self-righteous group of students who lack the ability to distinguish fantasy from reality and believe everyone else does too.

If the staff of next year's Gaieties has any doubts about listening to the "suggestions" for how to whitewash the script (no offense intended), they need only look to this year's producer, Patrick Crosetto, who was fired for transgressing once against the Native American community. If that is what happens when he failed to anticipate the offense, the penalty for any producer who fails to remove when ordered by an ethnic center will doubtless be worse, if possible.

It is horrifying that a group of well-educated, intelligent Stanford students was able to lose their sense of perspective so completely that they were offended by Gaieties. Perhaps most telling was the sophomore who expressed concerns to the Daily that seeing a white woman dressed in stereotypical Native American garb would somehow make other students believe that all Native Americans dress like that. This year's Gaieties included such things as the replacement of Tresidder Express (TresEx) with "TresXXX," President Hennessy summoning Dean of Students Mark Wais as a Pokemon-style warrior against a Berkeley student, and the same controversial mock-Native American joining representatives from other groups to form a spontaneous Village People formation on stage. Apparently the concern of the protestors was that, set among plausible scenes like these, students could be confused into believing that the student dressed as a Native American was engaged in a realistic representation of all members of that race.

The other complaint offered was that the scene mocked Native American beliefs and customs. Of course, Gaieties mocked everyone's beliefs and customs; most groups were able to take it in good humor and make the distinction between that and malicious disparagement.

It is more horrifying that Ram's Head capitulated so completely. Ram's Head could and should have stood up for itself. It could have defended itself on free speech grounds. It could have supported its producer, who defended the scene but agreed to tone it down. Instead its board of directors fired the producer for a single offense and agreed that its script, which it used to go well out of its way to keep secret, will be sent to the ethnic centers for approval.

It is most horrifying that the Stanford administrators sided with the protestors and put pressure on Ram's Head. Indeed, Dean Wais warned that all four administrators who appeared in the show (as they do every year) had considered pulling out, an act which would have required substantial changes to the show with only a few hours to make them, a virtually impossible task. Apparently the administrators could not tolerate any controversy and wanted to avoid any risk that Ram's Head might confront the activists.

The complaining students have done no good for anyone with this action. Rather then fight actual discrimination and prejudice, they have allowed themselves to be distracted by a joke. The character in Gaieties was not intended to disparage Native American culture, and by failing to appreciate that distinction the agitators have hurt their credibility to attack genuine offenses toward their culture. Many, perhaps even most students on campus recognize how absurd it is to be offended by a satire as silly as Gaieties; they will remember this the next time a similar complaint surfaces.

Another satirist, Tom Lehrer, once remarked that "When correctly viewed/Everything is lewd/I could tell you things about Peter Pan/And the Wizard of OZ: there's a dirty old man." The same can be said of offensiveness, but the solution is not for everyone to walk on eggshells trying to avoid saying or doing anything which might be taken to be offensive. Instead of interpreting everything in the worst light, people should try to find the best view of things. Was the Gaieties scene disparaging to Native American culture, or just good-natured teasing? If it was not intended to be an insult, then it is a mistake to act like it was one.
