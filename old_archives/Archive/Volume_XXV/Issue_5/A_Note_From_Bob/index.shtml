A Note From Bob
It's been an odd three weeks. I never thought I'd see the day when Republicans would be protesting at Embarcadero and El Camino, right outside Stanford, waving "Honk for Bush" signs. The honking was so loud I had to close my car window. Meanwhile, in Miami-Dade county, Republicans were behaving like Democrats, chanting while banging on doors (but not people) in order to get the canvassing board to return to public view. Alas, despite what some Congressional Democrats said afterward, it never amounted to full-scale intimidation - Republicans are still too staid for that. The police watching didn't even have to ask anyone to desist.

If anything, though, the sight of Republicans adopting Democratic tactics just means that the divide between the parties is getting deeper. According to the Washington Post, 90% of Republicans want Al Gore to concede, while only 70% of Democrats want George W. to back down or are unsure. While some like Robert Wright of Slate argue that this shows that Democrats are more open-minded, it could also show that Al Gore just has a weaker case. After all, two thirds of independents also back Bush.

Back on the Farm, however, the left is still the left, even if the right is becoming more like it. Identity politics is back with a vengeance, at the one place we'd all expect to be immune - Gaieties, the hilarious and always offensive comedy intended to rouse Stanford spirit while making fun of ourselves. As we discuss on the front page, a group of Indians complained to the administration and to the Gaieties board of directors that Gaieties made fun of them (along with everyone else). With a push from the administration, the producer was fired and the show censored, as it will be forevermore. Check out Henry Towsner's editorial on the subject.

Of course, identity politics has been with us for years. Its most important triumph may have come in the ending of the Western Culture program and its replacement with CIV. Well, the CIV program too has been replaced with IHUM. In Western Culture, everyone read from the same canon; in CIV, there was at least a core list of books everyone read. In IHUM, however, the gates are thrown open with few requirements on the readings, course structure, or anything else. The lack of any sort of canon means not only that students won't be guaranteed to know any particular knowledge coming out of an IHUM - indeed, the focus of IHUM is the rather more amorphous 'critical thinking skills.' It also means that the range of good and bad IHUMs will be large. Someone needs to review them in order to separate the wheat from the chaff and give praise or blame as deserved.

The Review has taken that challenge upon itself. Scott Rasmussen explains the perils and possibilities inherent in the IHUM program on the front page. On the page 3, we give you a list of this year's IHUMs with a review. The IHUM reading lists range from Hamlet and Oedipus Rex to History of Sexuality and People of the Earth.

Of course, identity politics doesn't play out only in reading lists or University courses. It also plays out in affirmative action, social programs, and personal beliefs. On page 4, we cover Shelby Steele's recent talk at the Law School. Mr. Steele is a black conservative at the Hoover Institution, and his work inspired George W.'s fight against the "soft bigotry of low expectations." Mr. Steele argues that by expecting less of minorities, we merely pretend to equality as opposed to achieving a real equality of skills that would make minorities truly competitive. We here at universities have a special duty in that regard, because it is at universities that those skills and standards are taught.

We took the chance to interview President Hennessy about these subjects and more; the interview appears on page 7. He became president only this quarter with a background in engineering and also in business. I think you'll find his answer to our questions quite interesting.

Also, on the back page, editor emeritus Johnny Openshaw makes an appearance to talk about Chilean culture from his vantage point in Santiago. Or, rather, he talks about the lack of it. Apparently, he's gone abroad only to find himself at home again. Well, you may not be able to be sure what the difference is between culture at home or abroad, or between Democratic protests and Republican ones, but one thing you can always be sure of is the difference between the Review and the rest of campus - we will always be conservative. Read on!
