Gaieties Bows To Political Correctness
This year's Big Game Gaieties spawned a controversy that pitted sensitivity to minority cultures against a tradition of off-color comedy. In the performance, a brief portrayal of a Native American character prompted outrage among the Native American community on campus, ultimately resulting in the dismissal of the show's producer and the removal of the offending character from the final performance. .....Full story in Front Page.....by Jim Carlson

Fall IHUM Review: Area One Under The Microscope
"IHUM sucks." Perhaps it's not the most common refrain heard in freshmen dorms, but at this time of the quarter it's at least a close second behind similar comments about Branner. It makes sense: people develop distaste for large institutions that they can't do anything about, and when they can't take it anymore, they turn the institutions upside down. This explains the French Revolution, the inevitable historical cycle of Stanford's humanities requirement, and might even provide insight to the coming downfall of Branner. .....Full story in Front Page.....by Scott Rasmussen

A Note From Bob
It's been an odd three weeks. I never thought I'd see the day when Republicans would be protesting at Embarcadero and El Camino, right outside Stanford, waving "Honk for Bush" signs. The honking was so loud I had to close my car window. Meanwhile, in Miami-Dade county, Republicans were behaving like Democrats, chanting while banging on doors (but not people) in order to get the canvassing board to return to public view.

Interview with President Hennessy
TSR: Many see a trend in higher education that university presidents are becoming increasingly alienated from the academic affairs of their institutions in favor of fundraising and management. Does this correspond to your own assessment of your job at Stanford? Do you see yourself as a CEO?

Mr. Hennessy: I see myself first and foremost as a member of the faculty. I think there has been increasing tension between the president's focus on academic affairs and the president's focus on a variety of external affairs. Fundraising certainly is a part of that, but not the only part of it, I think.....Full story....by Brian Ball

Shelby Steele Decries Redemptive Liberalism
In a talk hosted at the Stanford Law School, Hoover Fellow Shelby Steele decried what he saw as "redemptive liberalism": the social policies of the past thirty-years which he argues have hurt the very minorities they were meant to help. Mr. Steele, who studies race relations, multiculturalism, and affirmative action, contended that too much of what has been done in the name of black rights has more to do with the self-satisfaction of whites than any real improvement in the lives of blacks. .....Full story in News.....Justin Diener
