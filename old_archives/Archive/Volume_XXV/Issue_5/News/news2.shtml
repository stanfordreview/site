Shelby Steele Decries Redemptive Liberalism
by Justin Diener
Staff Writer

In a talk hosted at the Stanford Law School, Hoover Fellow Shelby Steele decried what he saw as "redemptive liberalism": the social policies of the past thirty-years which he argues have hurt the very minorities they were meant to help. Mr. Steele, who studies race relations, multiculturalism, and affirmative action, contended that too much of what has been done in the name of black rights has more to do with the self-satisfaction of whites than any real improvement in the lives of blacks.

Mr. Steele, an expert on racial policies of the post civil-rights era, is the author of A Dream Deferred: the Second Betrayal of Black Freedom in America, and The Content of Our Character: A New Vision of Race in America. Mr. Steele contends that, prior to the civil rights movement, America as a nation found itself with a crisis of moral authority. Democracy, freedom, and equal rights under the law, virtues that Americans felt they personified were distributed unjustly between Caucasians and African-Americans. This led to a crisis in moral authority. Redemptive liberalism arose to redeem America from its centuries of injustices. As Mr. Steele puts it, how could a country founded on the principles of equal justice under the law commit and justify such unjust acts as wholesale slavery and discrimination without the need to redeem itself? Out of that need came the Great Society programs in the late 1960's.

Here, Mr. Steele contends that the many programs went wrong. "Redemptive liberalism is the second betrayal of black freedom," he said. "It has failed to help the people it is interested in, and that appears to be its central contradiction." Becoming involved in a government outreach program 1960's, he observed many of the plans that people thought could reverse the problems that the poor and minorities faced failed. Mr. Steele argues that many of the programs undermined the values of hard work and individualism that make people successful in America.

Mr. Steele spoke extensively on the failures of affirmative action to meet the needs of making individuals competitive members of society. "When you cut people down, you cut them out of the mainstream of American life. For centuries they have had no familiarity with how it works, no sense, and not skills in many cases. By and large a deficit in skills should have been addressed to make these people competitive in a modern society." During his speech he dwelled upon the need for a "no excuses attitude toward excellence" in our educational system and in society. He asserts that once a parity of skills can be achieved there will be no need for programs such as affirmative action.

Mr. Steele discussed the need to ask people to be the best that they can be. Citing examples of many minorities excelling in the areas of music, entertainment, and sports he asked why so many don't challenge themselves to excel in the area of academics. "When a people decide to do something, ... nothing stops them, racism doesn't, oppression doesn't, they succeed!" While his speech was well received by the members in attendance at the Law School, he admitted that he did not have an effective means to turn back the tide of redemptive programs.

The speech was hosted by the Federalist Society.
