A Postcard From Santiago
Postcard From Santiago de Chile
The Mysteries of Chilean Popular Culture

According to ancient legend, when the creator conjured up Chile he had only the extremes left in his knapsack of geological trinkets. The result is a country which must be the laughing stock of geographers: one of the world's driest deserts to the north and arctic conditions in the south; the snow-capped Andes in the east and the Pacific in the west. The popular culture of Chile is similar to the geography: it makes very little sense and no one seems to know how it ended up that way. Chile, or as Pablo Neruda fondly christened his home, "the thin country", is an anomaly.

Examples of the Chilean popular culture peculiarity stretch from food to entertainment. The first thing that is obvious upon arrival is that the majority of Chilean popular culture is not actually Chilean. A famous Chilean is someone who flies into Chile from LA, Miami, Rome, New York, or Paris but is, unfortunately, carrying a return ticket. It is an eclectic mix of other Latin American countries, the United States, and Europe. The result is a baffling combination that even most Chileans find hard to appreciate.

Most Chileans watch only American movies. Poorly received and even more poorly funded, the Chilean film industry has been in a constant state of torpor for the last twenty years. A gaggle of Hollywood's worst monstrosities pass through Santiago each month. American movies are at their worst in translation--the subtitled versions rarely convey the same theme as the original. All scenes which any Chilean may find offensive are cut: translators have eliminated swear words and risque references and not replaced them with their Spanish equivalents. Reducing the spoken text of an R rated movie to that which would earn a PG rating, Hollywood becomes much less offensive in translation. This results in a climactic visual sequence with very anticlimactic lines. A nasty fistfight in a bar in English becomes the near equivalent of painting a fence in Spanish.

Though Chileans may watch American movies, they watch only Argentinian soap operas. These works often do what many Spanish conquistadors thought impossible: they cross the Andes. One very popular show features an unfortunate women named Betty who sports glasses which resemble the bottoms of beer bottles and a whine which only an aquatic mammal would be able to comprehend. She is joined by a crew of gorgeous coworkers who mumble stupidly for the camera while lounging about and pretending to flip through important manuscripts in the workplace. Invading countless family homes every Wednesday evening, the show's purpose is to demonstrate endlessly how Betty's beauty pales in comparison to her coworkers. No one in the show can act well enough to save the script, and it is somewhat ironic and unfortunate that several fine Chilean actors (or so I am told) are now without a job because Betty moved into their time slot.

Teams of men and women regularly face each other in a battle of the sexes in another popular TV show. Judged by the opposite team (thus, the men judge the women and vice versa), all contestants sing and dance for a raucous audience of peers. By the end of the game, the women, left with a winning score due to the generosity of their male judges and the presence of a host who has an obvious bias, cheer wildly. The men, whose score suffers due to large penalties heaped on by the host and poor scores from the women's team, heroically accept their fate: a negative score and harsh criticism from nearly all involved.

Popular music draws from nearly every country except Chile, although the United States clearly dominates the industry. As far as fast food goes, McDonalds is the obvious winner, easily edging out local SuperPollo which serves up gastronomical distress daily. Ironically, however, McDonalds does not serve fast food in Chile, but rather the "most complete of meals". The McDonalds in Chile proudly claims that the meat in their Big Mac is "healthy". Each time I step into a McDonalds, I am reminded that their "Combo C" offering includes foods from all of the food groups. The reasons why a Big Mac is the healthy choice for the entire family can be found in the color pamphlet at the exit.

What is so odd about the Chilean style of popular entertainment, fast food, and music is that not many people, even those of the younger generation, seem to enjoy it. Life in Santiago is marked by the daily consumption of international popular culture and the resounding lack of anything distinctly Chilean. Some claim that the overwhelming deluge of international products and the lack of Chilean products is a combination of the open economic policies of the dictatorship and the harsh dampers set on artistic expression for nearly 20 years while the military ruled Chile. Whatever the cause, the result is a culture which is being borne away on a tide of international commercialism from Hollywood's tasteless Scary Movie, to the Back Street Boys, to the Big Mac, and to Betty and her mousy appearance. Combined, the effect is one that few can stomach, and it certainly does not qualify as the "most complete of meals".

Johnny Openshaw has been escaping international commercialism in Santiago by slipping away to the wild sanctuaries of Patagonia, where no one has ever heard of McDonalds.
