Election
The Republican Philosopher King
"One of you -- one of you -- is about to be elected the leader of the single most powerful nation in the world -- economically, financially, militarily, diplomatically, you name it," stated moderator Jim Lehrer at the beginning of the second presidential debate. "Have you formed any guiding principles for exercising this enormous power?" George W. Bush's and Al Gore's answers to this question reveal a difference in political philosophy that underlies the rest of this campaign's programs, politicking, and posturing. Mr. Bush believes in the American people; Mr. Gore believes in American government.

Mr. Bush answered first, "The first question is what's in the best interests of the United States? What's in the best interests of our people? ... I also understand that an administration is not one person, but an administration is dedicated citizens who are called by the President to serve the country, to serve a cause greater than self."

Mr. Gore responded, "And I see our greatest natural -- national strength coming from what we stand for in the world. I see it as a question of values. . . . So I think first and foremost, our power ought to be wielded to -- in ways that form a more perfect union."

Discerning concrete differences between the proposals of Messrs. Bush and Gore is difficult, intentionally so. In an effort to reach out to critical blocs of the electorate in this too-close-to-call election, both candidates present centrist solutions to the common problems of the nation's common demographic groups. Reform Medicare to appeal to seniors, reform Social Security to appeal to baby boomers, reform education to appeal to young families, reform the government's finances to appeal to workers. The salient difference between the candidates is not the programs they propose or the people they want to appeal to, but rather the philosophies of government that will shape their every action as chief executive. And this difference is clear, as the candidates stated for themselves in the second debate. Mr. Bush stands for American interests, Gore stands for American values. Mr. Bush defers to the American people, Gore wants to make their government more perfect.

Using the lens of the candidates' underlying philosophical differences sheds light on what appear initially to be indistinguishable proposals. Yes, Messrs. Bush and Gore want to address the same issues to appeal to the same voters, but the problems that they perceive and the ways they plan to solve them are dictated by their ideas of the purpose and practice of government.

Mr. Gore sees rising health care prices for senior citizens and believes that government can do better, so his solution is to increase the size and the cost of the Great Society Medicare program. Mr. Bush sees in the current Medicare crisis an opportunity to harness the free market principles of choice and competition, so his solution is to give seniors access to the same set of collectively bargained health plans that members of Congress have.

Mr. Gore sees Social Security's projected financial imbalance as a test of New Deal big government, so his solution is to expand the federal government's role in the program by feeding it more taxpayer dollars. Mr. Bush sees Social Security as a promise to the American people, so his solution is to give them the choice to meet their needs through a mechanism that is indisputably more efficient and more reliable than government handouts in the long run: the free market.

Mr. Gore sees education as a new arena for big government, so he seeks to expand federal control by sending standards and teachers to states. Mr. Bush sees education as the responsibility of parents and of communities to provide for children, so he seeks to strengthen them through choice and incentives.

Mr. Gore sees the projected budget surplus as the government's money to spend, and seeks to use it to expand the role of the federal government in individuals' lives by creating new pet projects and shoring up the finances of old entitlements. Mr. Bush sees the surplus as the taxpayers' money, and seeks to return it to dedicated American workers to invest in the same economic activities which produced the wealth in the first place.

One need only look so far as the candidates' repeated mantras to see whom, or what, they put their trust in. As an advocate of "Reinventing Government," Mr. Gore believes that he can solve individuals' problems by making the federal government better at interfering in their lives. As a "compassionate conservative," Mr. Bush believes that the most compassionate thing a government can do is to allow individuals to decide for themselves how they will pursue their own happiness. In a campaign of nearly identical proposals and positioning, the candidate whom you put your trust in may well hinge on a simple philosophical question: who trusts you?

Scott Rasmussen is a sophomore, considering a major in Mathematical and Computational Science. Scott was in second grade the last time a Republican won the White House.
