Ralph Nader Criticizes "Corporate Control" In Stanford Speech
by Matthew Barrett
Senior Staff Writer

Ralph Nader condemned corporate control of the government and media in a campaign speech at Memorial Auditorium on October 22. Speaking to a near capacity crowd, he addressed issues ranging from sweatshop labor to space exploration.

Stanford in Government, a nonpartisan campus group, organized the event. The purpose, according to a press release, was "to promote political awareness and involvement." Despite a "votenader.org" banner gracing the podium on stage, Courtney Carter, public relations chair for SIG, insisted that the event was not designed to endorse Mr. Nader.

Medea Benjamin, California's Green Party senate candidate, spoke before Mr. Nader. She set the tone for his anti-corporate rhetoric by attacking California senate incumbent Diane Feinstein. Benjamin pointed out that Mrs. Feinstein recently went to China for trade negotiations which involved Mrs. Feinstein's husband's company.

"Hello!" quipped Benjamin, "You do not have to be a Stanford law professor to know that this is a conflict of interest." She later pointed out that Mrs. Feinstein's office is located on the premises of her husband's billion dollar investment firm and complained, "[The American political system] has lost its soul. It has become part of the corporate government, doing the bidding for the corporations. It is supposed to be a government of 'we the people' and not 'we the corporations'".

Mr. Nader, who polls around 5% in California, followed Ms. Benjamin and spoke for roughly 90 minutes. He began by describing the American political system as one that is dominated by corporate interests. Early on, he criticized Stanford for feeding into corporate culture. "We all grow up corporate É you're growing up corporate É [Stanford's curriculum] makes you think that a University is nothing more than a trade school with a very high tuition."

He cited Stanford students as being not on a "ladder to success", but rather as being on an "escalator to success", and he suggested that too many students treat Stanford as a vocational institute. Specifically, he criticized computer scientists and engineers for caring primarily about stock options. He called his own alma mater, Harvard Law School, a "high priced tool factory."

"It's amazing how people with your talents all over the country come with such low expectations of your life to make a systemic difference," he said.

Mr. Nader referred to the "golden handcuffs" of the Stanford student that will prevent him or her from witnessing the problems that plague the disadvantaged sectors of society. He provided his own statistics, observing that one third of American workers do not make a living wage, that 20% of Americans live in abject poverty, that 46% are in near or deep poverty, and that 20 million Americans are too poor to have a checking account. He noted, "Congressman just voted to raise their pay again and forgot to raise the minimum wage."

He then expanded his scope to include the entire American public, referring to "the great commonwealth of public assets that people own and don't have any control over". He said, "Every major religion warned society about giving too much power to commercial ventures." Mr. Nader spoke of Amercians who must submit to, and cannot participate in, the government structure. He also talked about the legal system. "If you're wrongfully injured, you will not have your full day in court."

Furthermore, Mr. Nader said, citizens are victims of corporate abuse. He cited Americans who die of lead poisoning and who are in bad health because of environmental conditions caused by, Mr. Nader claims, corporate neglect. Mr. Nader suggested that the Department of Justice needs a "Ten Most Wanted Corporate Criminals" in addition to its usual list of violent criminals.

Neither major party, according to Mr. Nader, can avoid these interests. He displayed a recent edition of Business Week with the question "Too Much Corporate Power?" on the cover, and he claimed that the magazine was more progressive than either the Democratic or Republican nominees. Mr. Nader claims that the major parties are basically the same, in that they are both fundamentally controlled by major corporations. He responds to the slogan, " A vote for Nader is a vote for Bush" by saying, "A vote for Gore is a vote for Bush."

Mr. Nader criticized Bush and Gore equally. He asserted that Bush doesn't really understand the issues and he quipped, "The reason [Bush] is interested in education is because he needs so much of it." Mr. Nader said that Gore made too many unfulfilled promises. For instance, he says that Al Gore hasn't fulfilled any promises in his book on environmentalism, and that Gore hasn't advocated finding a reliable source of renewable energy in eight years. He also criticized Gore for attempting to advance the causes of the middle class while completely neglecting the working poor.

He also broadened his criticism of government to include members of the Congress. He complained that not one congressman has his or her voting record cohesively presented on his or her website. He suggested that not one member of congress really understands the WTO, NAFTA, or GATT agreements because not one member of congress has even read them.

Mr. Nader finished his speech with a commentary on "the late evening news", which he thinks is not news at all. Mr. Nader said that 18 years ago, 50 corporations controlled all of the news media, and that today that number has dwindled to seven.

After his talk, he responded several student questions. Students addressed concerns such as sweatshop labor, Middle East peace, human population growth, and abortion. At the end, Mr. Nader emphasized his belief in the importance of citizen involvement in democracy. He said the only way to make citizens more powerful was through access to the media and through public funding of companies. He encouraged participation in politics by everyone and said, "I'd like to urge you over the next two weeks to put a moratorium on small talk and replace it with progressive political talk."

Although an early show of hands indicated that the crowd consisted mostly of Democrats, the crowd was overwhelmingly supportive of Mr. Nader. They applauded often, and gave him a standing ovation at the end of his speech. One member of the audience seemed to capture the sentiment of the whole crowd in saying, "Nobody deserves to be president more than Ralph Nader." Still, not everyone was in favor of Mr. Nader's candidacy. In front of Memorial Auditorium, before the event began, a professor holding a "A Vote For Nader Is A Vote For Bush" sign became involved in a bitter argument with a student.

This was Mr. Nader's last scheduled campaign stop in California.
