Front Page
Bush. Bush. Bush. Bush. Bush. Ben Stein Campaigns For The Governor
by Kurt Berglund
Staff Writer

Typically, most Stanford students only get to see Ben Stein on his Comedy Central game show, Win Ben Stein's Money. However, on Thursday, October 19, Mr. Stein appeared at the Manzanita Park courtyard. Mr. Stein held the event not to give away money, but rather to try to convince students to vote for Texas Governor George W. Bush in the upcoming presidential election. The speech was sponsored by the Stanford Republicans and Castano.

Ben Stein graduated from Columbia University with honor and from Yale Law School as valedictorian of his class. Before gaining fame in movies and on television, he served as a speechwriter for Presidents Nixon and Ford and taught at UC Santa Cruz, Pepperdine, and American University. Mr. Stein also has ties to Stanford. Years ago his parents lived in the area and his father worked on campus.

After telling some funny stories and some jokes about President Clinton, Mr. Stein began to talk about George W. Bush and why he likes him.

The first reason was because Mr. Bush is pro-life. According to Mr. Stein, it is "extremely important to recognize that every innocent life must be protected."

"[The] tax burden on this country in peace time is just astoundingly heavy and burdensome," said Mr. Stein. Mr. Stein doesn't believe that in a time of prosperity and good fortune "dentists or medium level corporate officials should have to pay 50% of their [income]" to the government.

He also argued that not lowering taxes in order to pay off the national debt is unnecessary. People should have the choice, he said, as to whether they have "more of their money to invest themselves, or do they want the government to invest it by paying off the debt."

Mr. Stein also likes Mr. Bush because he has been the governor of the second largest state for six years, and has kept a clean record. In a state that is known for "very violent, partisan politics," Mr. Stein argued that this display of Gov. Bush's character made him different from his opponent. "No one been able to lay a glove on him for ethical misconduct."

But the main reason why Mr. Stein likes Bush, he says, is because "no one has ever charged Bush with trying to short-circuit the Constitution or thinking that he is above the law." Mr. Stein pointed to "the many, many lies [Clinton] told and the many ways he tried to subvert the legal process."

"The law is everything in this country, the Constitution is everything," said Mr. Stein. "[W]ithout the Constitution, this country could just be any other miserable hell hole on this Earth. It's very dangerous when the President of the United States doesn't believe that the law applies to him."

Mr. Stein said that there is no evidence of George W. Bush "behaving as if Constitution did not make him subject to the law" while, as far as he can tell, "Mr. Gore has followed the practice of his boss."

"If we lose the Constitution we lose everything," he says.

After giving reasons why he likes Bush, Mr. Stein took questions from the audience. Many students had questions about Bush's intelligence. Mr. Stein felt this is more due to the impression Bush gives off than due to his real intelligence.

"[He] graduated in the top half of his class from Yale and has an MBA from Harvard [but] he's extremely uninterested in seeming smart," says Mr. Stein. "[Mr. Bush] is a smart, quick witted guy," says Stein, who had to tone down his intelligence to run as a "country boy from midland Texas."

Whether the talk will change students' votes is questionable. Many students went for entertainment rather than to learn about the candidates. According to David Abrahms, a graduate student, the presentation was "incredibly entertaining," but did not affect whom he will vote for. Mr. Stein was also asked questions about the school classic Ferris Bueller's Day Off, in which he is an economics professor, and about his popular game show.

The talk may not sway votes, but it did give many students new ideas and opinions of Bush. Brian Worth, a sophomore, found the "main point about law being everything" very important and agreed with Mr. Stein that "a president is under the law." Another student had "a better opinion of Bush" after the talk. Mr. Stein urged students to vote in the upcoming election - especially for Bush because, "in the final analysis, he's just an honest, decent man."
