A Note From Bob
One afternoon at the beginning of this quarter, I dug out a Bush 2000 button from the primaries and put it on the corner of my door. That evening, while I was working on homework in my room, there came a knock at my door. When I opened it, I saw about six people clustered around my door.

"Is that your Bush button?" they asked.

"Yes," I said.

"Are you ... serious?"

Well, I was. But somehow I think that there's not going to be much Stanford turnout for Bush. I'd guess the Stanford vote will go Gore, Nader, Hagelin, Bush. At least the Governor can take comfort that he'll probably be ahead of Pat Buchanan and Harry Browne.

Nevertheless, this is the Review's special election issue. On the front page, with articles on both Ralph Nader and Ben Stein campaigning on campus, we offer you two different ends of the spectrum: Ralph Nader is worried about those who are giving too much money to candidates, and Ben Stein is looking for someone to whom he can give his money away. Inside, Henry Towsner and Scott Rasmussen duke it out over whom to vote for and decide they don't like anybody likely to be in Stanford's top three. The Review brings you an interview with Condoleezza Rice, former Stanford Provost and current top foreign-policy advisor to Governor Bush. We also bring you some choice tidbits from a panel on just why anyone would ever want to go into public service in the first place.

In a different but not unrelated vein, however, this issue features a center-spread essay by John McCarthy, the inventor of LISP and one of the founders of computer science. Against all the environmental pessimism and bad math, he offers a technologist's approach to sustainability - hard facts combined with optimism about the ways that human beings will continue to overcome the challenges that nature offers us.

Indeed, that contrast infuses the election: from left to right, look at what each candidate offers. We have from Nader, fear (of corporations), from Gore pessimism and distrust (of America), from Bush optimism and trust (of the American people), and from Browne wild optimism and hope (for a truly free society.) What matters in making the decision is which view you hold of human nature, and what facts back each of the candidates up. Read the Review and choose wisely.
