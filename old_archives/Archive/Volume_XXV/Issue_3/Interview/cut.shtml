An Interview With Condoleezza Rice
Condoleezza Rice was a National Security Council advisor to former President George Bush, a former Stanford Provost, and is currently the top foreign affairs advisor to Governor George W. Bush.

TSR: Has the election played out as you expected?

Ms. Rice: Yes. It is playing out as I expected - very close, state by state. I've stopped watching the national polls; I just watch the state by state polls to see what's going on there. But it's very exciting. I expected it to be a close election from the very beginning. I think, in fact, that it's surprising that it's so close, given how the economy is doing so well and so forth. It's amazing that Governor Bush's message has been as resounding as it has.

TSR: After the second debate, many people said that the candidates' positions are very similar. In terms of broad divisions between the candidates on foreign policy, where do you see the differences lie?

Ms. Rice: They were similar because Vice-President Gore chose to agree on everything - I thought it was quite amazing, actually.

Bush would be much more focused. You have a sense with the Vice President that he thinks that every problem in the world is of equal importance, that every problem in the world requires American intervention. Now, the United States is the remaining superpower, and everybody would like us to think that way about the world, but I will tell you something. If we really do find ourselves trying to solve every problem in the world, rather than relying sometimes on others in coordination with us to solve them - or for others to solve them - we're going to lose the will of the American people to be engaged abroad, we're going to dissipate our own power, and eventually people are going to come to resent us.

One thing that Governor Bush made very clear in his debate comments is that he thought that we should not be arrogant. If I could paraphrase, I would say that we should not be everybody's great aunt - that we can't have a solution to everybody's problems no matter how small.

TSR: The Balkan peacekeeping issue changed some minds about the candidates' similarities.

Ms. Rice: I thought the Vice President, in that debate, said he also thought that American troops should eventually leave the Balkans, so we were all a little surprised when he changed his mind the other day. I guess now he favors a permanent peacekeeping role for the American troops in the Balkans. Five years ago, he thought it was only going to be one year that they were there, because when they went there in 1995, he and the President promised the American people they'd be out in a year. So I'm now not quite sure where the Vice President is on Balkan peacekeeping.

I know where Governor Bush is on Balkan peacekeeping, which is that the United States will stay and fulfill its commitment - that we will not leave until political stability is capable of supporting our withdrawal, but that we can't be there permanently because we have a lot of other military responsibilities that we have to attend to.

TSR: Do you feel that your background as a Stanford professor has made you look at things in a different light than someone who didn't come from academe?

Ms. Rice: My background as Provost has really changed them. As Provost I had to be chief operating officer for a very big institution that was dealing with issues like the role of the Internet and intellectual property rights.

When foreign delegations would come to visit, they never wanted to talk about nuclear policy - what they wanted to know was "How can we do what Silicon Valley has done?' And Stanford, of course, has had a very big role in that. I think I've come to understand the values in the United States that make us so dominant in this particular environment - our risk-taking propensity, the importance of venture capital, the importance of universities like this which are essential to the constant creation of new ideas, as well as the creation of people who go out into the Silicon Valley and bring those new ideas. I see the role of the United States in a quite different light as a result that, I think.

TSR: Why should a Stanford student vote for Bush?

Ms. Rice: Stanford students should vote for Bush because he is someone who really understands where the strength of America lies. He understands that it is in us as individuals. He has talked about the importance of community-based institutions or organizations that do a lot of the important work for people who are less fortunate. He is someone who has faith in the American people to make their own decisions.

In the narrowest sense, if you are a Stanford student, you may never see Social Security unless someone reforms the system now. I know it may seem a long way away for a Stanford student, but those entitlements are not going to be there unless somebody bring the American polity together, both Republicans and Democrats, to make reforms to these important entitlements.

Governor Bush is committed to education - and to the education of the poorest kids as well as the wealthiest kids. If you are a Stanford student, you've certainly had the experience of being in school with someone who is maybe the first person to go to college in their family. Well, unless the public schools get better, subsequent Stanford students are not going to have that experience. We're going to become more and more elitist because the public education system is not working for upward mobility. Governor Bush wants to deal with that issue.

And if you knew him, you would know that he is a person of great integrity, somebody who is a man of his word and who is not arrogant and puffed-up.

TSR: One final question: the National Review has said that, if Bush were to win, you could be "rock-star big." Have you ever considered going into politics yourself?

Ms. Rice: Since rock stars flame out, I don't quite know... I don't know - I would never say never. I don't think I'm particularly cut out for elective office. I've now been on the campaign trail quite a lot and these are special people - what they're willing to go through, the way they keep going, hour after hour after hour. It's been instructive, and I think it's not me.
