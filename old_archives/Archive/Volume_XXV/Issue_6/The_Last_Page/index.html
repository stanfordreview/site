The Last Page
A Postcard From Patagonia
In Search Of A Wild Kingdom
Sandwiched between two businessmen discussing woodchips in a plane with engine failure on the runway of a nameless Chilean town, I am completely preoccupied with the idea of an aristocratic Frenchman. The plane was bound for Patagonia, and the Frenchman was Orelie-Antoine de Tounens. Our Frenchman, who must have been quite an adventurous and dashing dandy, abandoned his country in 1859 while only in his early thirties. He left behind his law practice and family in Perigueux. Ahead lay nobility, fame, betrayal, and a pathetic end. Completely irrational and a megalomaniac, the Frenchman's dream was to become king of Patagonia, the stretch of land at the southern tip of South America. Very well received by the native population, Orelie-Antoine proclaimed Patagonia a constitutional monarchy with himself at the head by the end of the year. His government consisted of one living person and three characters: His Majesty, the Secretary of State for Justice, and the Minister for Foreign Affairs. Sporting three characters in a performance which must have been worthy of an Oscar, Orelie-Antoine annexed the whole of South America from Latitude 42 degrees to the Horn.

Welcoming parties and generous land giving must no longer be this area's forte. The natives are hardly willing to give up the entire southern tip of South America if one has a foreign accent. In the airport, I was greeted by an abundance of poorly translated messages. One sign declared: "Do you have nothing to do this Sunday? Come join us for the Catholic Mess" (what a difference an "a" makes). After eating a dessert which was presented on the menu as a "cup of ice cream topped with mange" (which was surprisingly good), I set off to find my hostel. Upon arrival at my shelter, I was greeted by the owner's vivacious cat who, after rubbing against my leg, sank his teeth into my left arm. Unfastening the creature became a constant challenge. Lady Dixie, the owner of the cat and the hostel, did supply me with a bundle of unwelcome entertainment--a twenty year old collection of the British tabloid "Hello!". With the help of these magazines, she had started a hostel which has the atmosphere of an english teashop in the middle of nowhere.

Transportation in Patagonia has also seen better days. Our Frenchman and his two phantom ministers paraded about on horseback with well paid servants (Orelie-Antoine's only charm was that he had trouble with foreign currency and supposedly overpaid everyone). Immense distances and poor roads separate populated areas. On most trips, I found myself alone in a van with a driver who smoked at an alarming rate and listened to the most dependable radio station in the middle of nowhere-a never ending recording of a preacher dragging on about fire and brimstone. On one such occasion, as the cabin of the van was filling with smoke and the preacher was starting his most graphic description of hell, we stopped to pick up a clown with baggy yellow pants and a red nose in the middle of a sheep pasture. For two hours, we bounced along in a smoke-filled van: the driver, myself, the clown, the monotonous Priest, and, thanks to Lady Dixie, ten copies of "Hello!" magazine with pictures of the royal family flying out of them.

Orelie-Antoine's spectacular welcome was due to a prophecy made by the late ruler of the native people of Patagonia. A gathering of native tribes had been told that they would be delivered from slavery by the arrival of a bearded european. Being French, Orelie-Antoine easily satisfied the latter requirement. Months without adequate grooming during his sea voyage assured that the former was also true. I, however, being rather obviously American and clean shaven, had quite a few problems-even with the fauna. Guanacos showered me with saliva (they have an incredible biological spitting system at their disposal that is able to surpass the water pressure in Lady Dixie's quasi-english hotel), flightless birds became airborne when they spotted me, and one misstep sent all the penguins diving into their burrows.

Unfortunately, the story of our Frenchman has a sad ending. The kingdom broke apart when the king and his two ministers were betrayed and arrested (all at the same time), thrown in jail in Chile (all in the same cell), and later sent home in the hull of a French warship (where they realized that they were all the same man). The adventurer would return three times until Orelie-Antoine died in a French town while holding the very un-kingly position of village lamplighter.

Sandwiched between two businessmen discussing fish meal, my return trip was slightly more pleasant. The land which Orelie-Antoine fought over and (perhaps) died of grief over loosing is a desolate, cold, and windswept place. Few places, however, can match its spectacular beauty and expanse of glaciers, mountains, and rivers. Its people are as interesting as the landscape: mavericks and daredevil explorers populate the isolated valleys of the Patagonian countryside. Despite the flying of the Chilean flag over the western territory of Patagonia and the Argentinean flag over the eastern portion, the area is very much its own country. With their own unwritten laws and customs, the region of Patagonia presents an isolated population living in an area of cold beauty with the harsh realities of freezing temperatures. I can understand why Orelie-Antoine kept trying to come back. His Majesty would have been proud of his former kingdom.

Johnny Openshaw is junior majoring in Political Science and Biology. He is a conservative neo-Darwinist.
