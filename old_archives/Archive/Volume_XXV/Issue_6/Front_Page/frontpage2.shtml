Front Page
Cowell Waits For RU-486
By Scott Rasmussen
Senior Staff Writer

RU-486
In the wake of the approval of the controversial abortion drug RU-486 by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration last September, overseers of health insurance plans across the country face a difficult decision. If plans provide the drug, pro-life subscribers will object to their money being used to fund a procedure they find objectionable. If plans don't provide the drug, pro-choice subscribers will object to not having access to a procedure they view as necessary.

Nowhere is the debate hotter than on the nation's campuses, where the subscribers are politically-conscious college students and the overseers are administrators concerned about alumni donations--and where liberal sexual mores and policies have created a ready market for a drug like RU-486. According to the Chronicle of Higher Education, Yale will offer RU-486, as part of the basic student health plan while Harvard will refer students to one of its teaching hospitals and will reimburse students for the portion of the fee used to finance it.

The issue is still unresolved at Stanford, but it appears that there are no substantial obstacles to RU-486 on campus if Cowell Student Health Services decides to introduce it. Cowell Director Ira Friedman states that "We are not currently prescribing [RU-486]," but "We will continue to review it as the medical community get more experience with this alternative." However, Cowell's current philosophy and policies leave the door open to an approach toward RU-486 more like Yale's than Harvard's.

Cowell does not currently perform abortions, but it does offer a full range of women's health services, including counseling and planning, in which Dr. Friedman explains, "The full range of pregnancy options are discussed, which includes pregnancy termination." Cowell refers students to specialists in the community who perform abortions but it does not pay for them.

Some students feel that although the university presents abortion as one choice among many, it is a privileged choice. Stanford Students for Life member Arnold Saha comments, "Choice ordinarily involves multiple alternatives. My problem with Cowell becoming involved in abortions is that too often abortion is presented as the only alternative, instead of other, healthier options like adoption."

If RU-486 were to be offered at Stanford, Dr. Friedman speculates, "I would expect [RU-486] to be included within the existing plan benefits, without additional premium students." Like Yale, and unlike Harvard, students under the CardinalCare plan are unable to withhold the portion of their fee paying for a specific procedure.

There also are no medical obstacles to the prescription of RU-486 at Stanford. The FDA lists three requirements for providers of RU-486: "Ability to assess the duration of pregnancy accurately; Ability to diagnose ectopic pregnancies; Ability to provide surgical intervention in cases of incomplete abortion or severe bleeding, or have made plans to provide such care through others, and are able to assure patient access to medical facilities equipped to provide blood transfusions and resuscitation, if necessary."

The cost of the surgical equipment and skills to meet these requirement, combined with the drug itself, which costs $300 to $400 (about the same as a surgical abortion), has prompted many colleges to opt out of offering RU-486. Dr. Friedman indicated that this was not a concern for Stanford since "Cowell has physicians on staff now who do all of this."

Given the bipolar nature of the abortion debate, is there room for a compromise on the Stanford campus? As Stanford Students for Choice member Bryan Choi argues, "RU-486 should be an optional part of an individual's insurance plan; other students shouldn't have to pay for an individual's abortion. You shouldn't have to answer for a decision like that to anyone but yourself."

RU-486, sold under the trade name Mifeprex by Danco Laboratories, was first approved for use in 1988 in France, but the F.D.A. has been slow to approve it in the United States in large part because of political pressures. An estimated 500,000 European women have had medical abortions with the pill, and it has been touted as an alternative with more convenience and less stigma than going to an abortion clinic.

Mr. Saha believes that this ease of use also carries troublesome implications. "Because abortion has become so much easier with simply swallowing a pill, it becomes more difficult to see the consequences of the decision. People don't see it as affecting a human any more, but as a simple medical choice to kill some cells."
