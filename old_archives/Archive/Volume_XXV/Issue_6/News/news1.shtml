University Plans "Diverse" Celebration of MLK
A week of celebrations commemorating the life of Martin Luther King Jr. began January 18th on the second floor of Tresidder Union with a birthday party thrown in honor of the late civil rights activist. The planned celebrations will relate to the diversity of thought and experience in the civil rights movement, with events ranging from "Living History: Women from Civil Rights to Black Power," a forum of female civil rights activists, to a screening of the documentary film A Place of Rage, which describes the aspirations civil rights leaders shared with contemporary movements in feminism and sexual orientation. The week presents a united front by several historically divided strands of the civil rights movement, including representatives from King's own Southern Christian Leadership Conference and the more militant Black Panther Party. Stanford University is the home of the King Papers Project, an ongoing effort spanning decades devoted to archiving documents relevant to King's private and public life. The project is headed by Stanford Professor Clayborne Carson, who edited The Autobiography of Martin Luther King, a compilation of King's autobiographical sketches published in 1998.

Study Shows TV Watching Causes Violence
Researchers at the Stanford University Medical Center have found that a reduction in the amount of time children spend watching television may create a corresponding drop in their levels of aggression towards their classmates, according to a report published in Archives of Pediatrics and Adolescent Medicine. The study was conducted at two San Jose elementary schools roughly comparable to each other in critical factors such as number of televisions in the house, televisions in the room, and general socio-demographic patterns. One school's set of students underwent training over six months in reducing the amount of time spent at videogames and televisions, while the other students were left uninstructed. At the end of the study, researchers found the children who were instructed had cut their level of television consumption by a third, and that displays of aggressive behavior on the playground were halved. Though previous studies had also shown a link between television and aggression in children, this study is the first to show that the level of aggression in children can actually be reversed by reducing the amount of time children spend at the television.

Sunken Diamond Renovated
Despite opposition from the elements, the Sunken Diamond is scheduled to be ready for the first Stanford home baseball game on January 26. Since last summer the legendary baseball field has undergone a $2.3 million dollar renovation. The costs have gone toward replacing the old bleachers, dugouts, and press box with 2,113 stadium-style seats, a multi-level press box, improved facilities for the disabled, and modern dugouts. Construction was stopped briefly over the past month for rain, but nevertheless the Sunken Diamond should easily accommodate the turnout for Stanford's opening series against Fresno State, which lasts through January 28th.
