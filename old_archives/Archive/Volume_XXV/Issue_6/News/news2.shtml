John Rawls vs. Adam Smith: Amartya Sen On Justice
By John Gibbs
Senior Staff Writer

On Monday January 15th, Nobel Prize winner Amartya Sen spoke at Kresge Auditorium on "The Limits of Contractual Reasoning".

Nobel Prize Winner
Amartya Sen
Mr. Sen's speech laid out what he views as two different ways of thinking of justice: the notion of "contractarian" justice promoted by John Rawls, and a type of justice defined by its dependence on a sole "impartial observer", proposed by free market proponent Adam Smith. Contractarian justice is based on a hypothetical contract established by society under circumstances intended to ensure that the contract would be fair. Adam Smith, on the other hand, defined justice as that which would be considered just if viewed by an impartial and fair independent observer.

Mr. Sen argued that Rawls' popular theory of justice has a number of problems which are not faced by Adam Smith's notion of justice. In particular he criticized the assumption made in the conctractarian definition of justice that it would be possible to reach a unanimous agreement as to the right contract. The impartial observer method of justice is more flexible because it demands less and assumes less, according to Mr. Sen.

Mr. Sen claims that justice not only "needs to be done, but needs to be seen to be done" in order to be truly just. Democracy plays a key role in allowing justice to be seen to be done, in that it allows a wide variety of viewpoints to be expressed, and that it tends to allow public access to a wide variety of information which can be used to aid in our calculation of justice.

Though his talk was rather theoretical and aimed at people with a strong background in political philosophy, others less knowledgeable about the subject picked up on key ideas in the field.

Mr. Sen received his Nobel Prize for work on welfare economics. In particular, Mr. Sen showed that shortages of food are not likely the cause of famine, but rather such factors as poor food distribution methods and dramatic increases in food prices with a corresponding decrease in wages are what probably cause such disasters. Mr. Sen has also created indexes of welfare and poverty which attempt to quantify income distributions in different countries. Also included in his Nobel Prize citation are his papers on "Social Choice, Welfare Distributions, and Poverty", and on "Individual Values and Collective Decisions".

Mr. Sen was born on November 3, 1933 in West Bengal, India. He received his B.A. in 1956 and his Ph.D. in 1959 from Trinity College in Cambridge, England. Mr. Sen was the Lament University Professor and Professor of Economics and of Philosophy at Harvard University and is now Master of the College at Trinity College. Mr. Sen is married to Emma Rothschild and has four children.
