Editor's Note
Welcome to Stanford and The Stanford Review
Welcome to Stanford! Although it may seem like nothing from the outside world ever penetrates beyond our palm-lined driveways, politics sneaks into Stanford to foment protest rallies in White Plaza or spark impromptu debates over Presidential candidates. As our story in Opinion shows, all sorts of political organizations from the Stanford Republicans to the Stanford Labor Action Coalition find a home under our red-tile roofs. Given that Stanford is in Northern California, most are radical, some are liberal, and a small but visible few are conservative.

What does it mean to be conservative, or, for that matter, radical? A radical sees society as a building to be constructed; he has a blueprint for the perfect society and spends his life trying to force society to conform to it. Conservatives, on the other hand, see society as a living thing, evolved from the pressure of generations of experience, guided, not by some abstract, bloodless ideal, but by the preferences and desires of the millions of its members. Conservatism is not an ideology but the negation of ideology. It is the acceptance that, as both the French and October Revolutions showed, the unbounded pursuit of an ideal ends in disaster.

Pragmatically, of course, that means that conservatives support free markets over government dictates as the best way to transmit and fulfill the desires of consumers and traditional morality over individual relativism as the irreplaceable evolved wisdom that allows us to live together in society. Beyond that, however, conservatives come in all shapes and sizes. Until this year, the campus hosted both the Stanford Republicans - an explicitly moderate organization - and the College Republicans, an explicitly conservative one. Last year, the two merged to form an inclusive organization that, as you can read in Opinion, still boasts moderate ideals.

Conservatism is more than a matter of practical politics, however. The Review cover Stanford news from its distinctly conservative point of view, one that rarely finds a home on the rest of campus. As documented in News, the Review was forged in the debates over the junking of the Western Civilization core in the '80s, matured during the years of Stanford's speech code, and, stiffened by stolen copies and the occasional Review burning, remains proud to be Stanford's conservative voice. We cover stories of national importance that affect Stanford, like our article last year on the Bush-Hoover connection, but also seek to speak out for the students' interests, as in our article on Tresidder's sad fall from grace.

The Review is always looking for new writers and layout artists. Stanford classes will definitely fill up your time if you let them (see our article on classes for advice on how to choose them), but your life will feel empty without something else. If you are a conservative - or even a political moderate - and you like to write or have journalism experience, come to the Media Orientation on Monday, Sept. 25 or our first staff meeting on Friday, Sept. 29 to find out more about working with us. No experience is necessary - only one of the last five editors had worked at a paper before the Review . Come join us - keep Stanford's conservative voice strong!
