Opinion
What Student Union?
Dateline: January 17th, 2000
This article was written soon after Tresidder's hours were extended past midnight last Winter Quarter. Little has changed at Tresidder since. Aside from politics, the Review is a voice for the students when it comes to improving life on campus. Unlike the Stanford Report, which is wholly owned by the university, or the Daily, which rarely steps outside the bounds of university press releases, the Review has never hesitated to make its position known when student interests were on the line. The next time you notice that the administration has dropped the ball on cheap food or campus parties, you can be sure the Review will be there to pick it up.

Tresidder Memorial Union, Stanford's only student union, currently contains Jamba Juice, Tresidder Express, Pulse, Fitness Center, three restaurants, and two banks. Some students consider it indispensable for those otherwise forced to use off-campus facilities and face the inflated prices of the Bay Area. Compared to the student unions at other universities, however, which often serve as student social centers, late-night hangouts, and favorite lunch and dinner destinations, Tresidder seems far less valuable.

"Tresidder? Tresidder has served three basic functions for me: one, as a landmark to navigate by, two, a place to pay my Stanford bill, and three, a place that has a Wells Fargo bank. Other than that, neither the presence or absence of Tresidder makes much of an impact on my life," said David Lee, a senior majoring in Biology, pointing out the conspicuous lack of facilities such as a pool hall, arcade, or bowling alley, in Tresidder when compared to its counterparts in other universities.

The ongoing dissatisfaction with Tresidder Union remains because Tresidder hardly qualifies as a student union, in the sense that it offers few places where students can socialize and entertain themselves without having to leave campus. As a result, few Stanford students consider Tresidder as their hangout place on weekends.

Dean of Students Marc Wais was quick to point out that such a reaction from the students should come as no surprise.

"One must remember that Tresidder is not a 'student union' in the sense that we often think of," said Mr. Wais. "The reality is that Tresidder is a 'community union,' and was designed to support Stanford's decision long ago to base a lot of its social and intellectual activity around our very strong residential house system. Therefore, Tresidder was not designed and does not serve as the singular hub of student activity on campus."

Mr. Wais added that Tresidder does not have the physical size to house many services such as a campus bookstore, food court, movie theater, student organization offices, etc., that are often found at other student unions. "I think this aspect has a tendency to frustrate some students - especially if they have visited their friends on campuses that have huge student unions."

To be sure, the idea of making Tresidder a more "hang-out friendly" place is hardly novel. According to Mr. Wais, Tresidder at one time contained a small bowling alley, located where the present day Tresidder Fitness Center and Treehouse stand. The back dining area of the Treehouse, formerly Pollo Rey, was the wood floor of the original bowling alley. The site of what is now Pulse once contained an arcade and pool room. This arcade/pool room was vacated about three years ago due to low use.

"I believe Tresidder has improved dramatically in the last three years in terms of its appearance, occupancy, and overall student utilization," continued Mr. Wais. "When I arrived at Stanford in 1995, almost one-half of the first floor was vacant, due to the reluctance of businesses to invest in Tresidder. They simply thought the facility looked dreadful and did not generate enough student traffic to present even a break-even business proposition. Now when vacancies develop, we generate much greater interest from businesses interested in coming to Tresidder."

Mr. Wais's account of the history and mission of Tresidder raises an interesting point: if there was once a bowling alley and an arcade/pool hall in Tresidder Union, it indicates that students initially demanded these facilities and that the administration considered establishing and maintaining such facilities within the spirit of Tresidder's mission as the 'community union.' What merits our attention is that, for some reason, the demand for these facilities dwindled until they became an unprofitable business.

Why would a business that began in order to satisfy clearly existing demand become gradually unprofitable? Mr. Wais cited the increased popularity of home computers and video games as one reason for the reduced demand for the arcade hall. There is, however, a much more fundamental and obvious cause for the disappearance of the bowling alley and the arcade/pool hall in Tresidder: that they were owned and operated by the University whose profit-seeking motivation would have been much weaker than that of a private provider of those services.

Just as public monopolies have little incentive to respond to market change, the University-operated bowling alleys, arcade, and pool halls probably had little incentive to make the prompt adjustments necessary to remain profitable. My guess is that in the absence of a profit incentive, the same old Pac Man probably remained for months in the Tresidder arcade even though no one was playing it; the pool tables and cues abused by quasi-sharks were left uncared for; and the bowling balls, pins, and alley were all probably left in shambles after a few months of profitable operation. A profit-seeking private provider would have never run its business in such a shameful manner. As a result, gradually, students stopped using the facilities in Tresidder and began looking for off-campus alternatives. Once it started losing money, the University, without realizing the fundamental flaws of its management, probably suspended the operation of those facilities without much hesitation, blaming it on declining demand without bothering to know why.

If the above hypothesis is correct, then the administration is out of excuses for not reviving the bowling alley and the arcade/pool hall in Tresidder. Only this time, the University should leave the management of the facilities to private contractors and let them profit from Tresidder. So far, in its recent attempts to revive Tresidder, the University has done the right thing by leasing out the spaces to private companies, and not surprisingly, as Mr. Wais admitted, they have served the students well. Let us give the arcade and bowling alley one more chance at Tresidder, but this time, let us do it right.

Daniel Maeng graduated last year with a degree in economics. He probably would have made frequent trips to the Tresidder arcade and spent his entire salary from the Review on Pac Man.
