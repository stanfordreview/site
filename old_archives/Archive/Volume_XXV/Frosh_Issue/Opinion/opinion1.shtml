Opinion
Moderation in All Things: The Journey of a Conservative
by Chris Desmond
Staff Writer

George Prescott Bush, the dashing and racially mixed young star of the dashing and racially mixed Republican National Convention last month, made something of a faux pas during one of the many interviews which his uncle's campaign arranged for him over the summer. Asked how his uncle could appeal to young voters, he replied (correctly) that younger voters were largely fiscally conservative and socially liberal, and (incorrectly) that his uncle shared those views.

Governor Bush instantly and firmly denied that he was softening the traditional conservative social agenda, but his nephew was on to something. People our age are growing up in a time of comfortable security and prosperity, and as a result we are mostly fiscally conservative; we are also young, and hence socially liberal. In the current language of politics, this is called "moderate," a label that goes a long way towards explaining why finding students at Stanford whose views mirror George P's is not as easy as it should be.

Moderates are apathetic, almost by definition. If you want things to stay as they are, the logic goes, there is no reason to protest and no reason to make yourself heard. The minority opinion with something to fight for, on the other hand - the extreme right or left - must be louder to gain exposure. Stanford is a case in point. Any corporate recruitment visit by Philip Morris (or for that matter Kraft) is liable to be loudly protested and may even be canceled due to a hostile campus atmosphere. And when I arrived two years ago, the College Republicans boasted four active members and were called "brownshirts" and "bigots," though this is no longer the case. While the Stanford Democrats were then, and remain, an impressive, professionally run organization that can be proud of its track record, they are a bit too polite for my taste; besides, in my heart I could really never be a Democrat.

Republican Youth Majority caught my eye later that year for two reasons. First, the literature they handed out in White Plaza listed Bill Weld, Christie Whitman, Fred Thompson and several other moderates who I have always liked, and the mission statement screamed "Pro-choice, pro-environment" in huge letters. Second, the name Republican Youth Majority sounded so ominous and cult-like that I simply had to see what it was about. Several friends and I founded the Stanford Republicans as a result of that meeting.

RYM is a not a cult (sigh); instead, it organizes groups like ours in colleges around the country. It seems that the polarization at Stanford isn't unique: we have made contact with groups at Harvard, Berkeley, UCLA and Columbia to name a few. Their participation has been limited at Stanford, but they have introduced us to several prominent groups of moderate and, ahem, rich Republicans who have given us more tangible support. The Stanford Republicans, as we fashioned ourselves, were up and running almost immediately: in our first quarter we traveled to a moderate junket in Santa Barbara (all expenses paid) and hosted a televised meeting for Tom Campbell. We've since helped at a half dozen events for the Lincoln Club and the WISH (Women in the Senate and the House) List, and last spring held a reception and talk for Condoleeza Rice, ex-provost and probable soon-to-be National Security Advisor. We have sixty members, of whom around twenty are active at any one time. These numbers are not staggering, but they're an improvement over zero.

George P. may have correctly characterized the attitudes of this generation, but it is important to realize that our opinions will only matter if we make them heard. George W. Bush could downplay our views because he knows that thirty million eligible people under the age of 25 didn't vote in 1998, and those that did mostly picked a candidate based on personality. Obviously I would like every incoming freshman to join the Stanford Republicans and budget an hour or two every Thursday to come to our meetings, but regardless of your political views, don't ever think that simply voting is enough to get a message across. If you honestly care about the future of the country, then there's no reason not to sign up for a political student organization at the activities fair. And if you don't like one you can always start your own.
