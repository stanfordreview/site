Opinion
Hey hey! Ho ho! The New Post Office's Got To Go! Why is it so hard to get students excited about anything significant these days?
This year marks the first election cycle in recent memory where the election genuinely has yet to be decided in the beginning of October. So it is hardly a surprise that 52 million people were tuned into the television networks on the night of the first debate, as opposed to only 46.1 million for the debates in 1996, right?

This is what Governor Bush would call "fuzzy math." Unfortunately, for the first time ever, two of the networks decided not to broadcast the debate live. It therefore seems that 17 million from that 52 were watching Dark Angel on Fox, James Cameron's series about a genetically enhanced young woman fighting, well, whomever it is that genetically enhanced young women fight. This leaves 35 million debate viewers, 11.1 million less than in the so-called 'contest' four years ago. Coincidentally, the 17 million that were watching Jessica Alba fight evil operatives rather than watching the debate exactly overlap the college demographic. This makes this the least-watched-ever Presidential debate among students, by catastrophic margins, reflecting the lowest registration rate under 25 years old ever recorded.

This is especially surprising because the two candidates have sharply differing views on a large variety of issues that students genuinely care about. Governor Bush is strongly pro-life, whereas Veep Gore is just as strongly pro-choice; with as many as three Supreme Court seats probably becoming vacant in the next President's tenure, one candidate or the other will see his views on abortion become policy for decades to come. The candidates differ similarly on hate-crimes laws, campaign reform, the balance between protection of the environment and the free markets, and a dozen other fundamental domestic policies.

While both candidates are decidedly internationalist, much to the dismay of the short-sighted and isolationist Green People's Party, Bush and Gore disagree on specific hotspots, including China's handling of Taiwan and Tibet, and the United States' position in the Middle East and Israel. Considering the volume of Free Tibet protests that appear at the arrival of any Chinese government representative, the lack of interest from our generation is stunning.

So what do students get excited about? The post office, janitorial working conditions, and California Grape Workers are the examples that leap to my mind. Ignoring the impact that the candidates' budget proposals would have on the amount janitors take home every year, we must find a way to get students to view an election with the same sort of fervor that they view a union protest. We need to show students that Bush could directly affect a woman's right to choose, but Gore could directly affect our right to watch entertainment of our own choosing. We need to show, in other words, that elections matter.

Fortunately, the electoral system has a built-in mechanism to take care of this problem. In four years, half of our generation is going to wake up in a country they detest, and realize that they have, and have always had, the power to change it.

And then they'll vote.

Chris Desmond is a junior majoring in Symbolic Systems. He is planning to vote.
