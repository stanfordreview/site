Book Review
A Story Of Two Joes: Stalin and McCarthy
Can the cold war be viewed as a political battle between two distant superpowers, or was it a battle between two sets of beliefs about the nature of government, one morally superior to the other?

When CNN ran a mammoth twenty-four-episode documentary on the cold war, it at least suggested that many unsavory American actions were comparable to Soviet actions which were orders of magnitude worse. Perhaps the most surprising thing about Arnold Beichman's collection of articles on the topic is that it becomes a dispute, not over whether it is correct to equate the actions of the US and the USSR, but whether or not the video actually made such a comparison.

Mr. Beichman's book, CNN's Cold War Documentary: Issues and Controversy has eighteen articles, most critical of the television series and the companion book, written by several people involved in the production of the documentary. The remaining articles are defenses of the documentary, mostly by the producer, Sir Jeremy Isaacs, and one of the historical consultants, John Lewis Gaddis. For full disclosure, it should be known that Arnold Beichman, the editor and a Hoover fellow, is a long-time friend and helper to the Review.

The criticism centers on an accusation that the series finds a "moral equivalence" between the action of the two superpowers. The favorite example is a particular episode, "Reds," which discusses the way the US and USSR attacked supposed traitors internally. In doing this, it gives half of the air time to the Soviet gulags, forced labor camps which consumed millions of lives, and the other half to Senator McCarthy's crusade against communists. While some of the critics are too willing to write off McCarthyism as a trivial aberration barely worthy of notice, they are correct to point out that no interpretation of a McCarthyism can make it compare to the systematic imprisonment and murder of millions perpetrated by the Soviet government.

The film's makers, in their responses to the attacks, make no attempt to argue that McCarthyism compares to the gulags. Indeed, both Mr. Isaacs and Mr. Gaddis deny that anyone watching the show could get the impression that the US and USSR were morally equivalent, using several quotes from the show which do clearly indicate the moral differences between the sides, for example a quote Mr. Gaddis offers (from the book), "What in the United States was an aberration was in the Soviet world the system itself." The rebuttal, however, raises the question of whether a few lines can make up for a structure which does seem to balance what one critical writer describes as "two cold war Josephs: McCarthy, the reckless junior senator from Wisconsin, and Stalin, the genocidal maniac from Georgia."

Several other similar criticisms are raised in Mr. Beichman's book, and the arguments for both sides are largely the same. Indeed, the most serious criticism of the volume is that after a hundred or so pages it feels as if the same attacks on the same points are being repeated (a necessary deficit of collecting articles which were originally intended to stand alone). Despite this minor flaw, the book is an interesting read, even independent of the background. Certainly to really understand the issues raised would require watching the entire twenty-hour series, and given that some of the articles go deep into the historical details of the cold war, far more knowledge of the events of the cold war than most college students possess.

Still, even without that background, Issues and Controversy raises important questions not only about the dangers while popularizing history, but also about the additional complications raised by the audio-visual nature of CNN documentary. A producer does not have nearly the control over the emotions raised by a movie, especially one which, like this one, depends heavily on original footage and interviews. Indeed, the first article in the book faults the portion of the series on conflicts in the third world for representing the Soviet side with "attractive-looking, articulate spokesmen" and the American side with "a gaggle of cynical ex-CIA men who not only look like thugs from central casting, but speak like them as well."

While it may be unfair to accuse Mr. Isaacs and CNN of doing this intentionally (and Mr. Isaacs denies that the people speaking for the CIA are less convincing than the ones speaking for the KGB), the fact remains that an illegitimate cause can always benefit from charismatic spokesmen, and the television medium is particularly prone to corruption in this manner. If Mr. Isaacs includes an interview with a convincing Soviet who makes claims about the motivations of the USSR which can be proven false with additional information, presumably he has some obligation to present that information in the name of accuracy (something he is accused of failing to do), yet there must be limits or any endeavor will be forced to include every fact known about any aspect of the cold war. Similarly, anyone creating a document for the general public (and one which needs, or at least wants, good ratings) may be tempted to make things more dramatic and simpler by putting together events which don't quite go together; Mr. Isaacs is justly criticized for going so far as to rearrange the order.

While those without substantial knowledge of the documentary and the cold war in general will not be able to appreciate the entire book, that does not make most of it any less interesting. It is an important cautionary tale about how any historical work can include substantial unintended messages, and how the desire to create a powerful and moving product can lead to twisting the way things are presented (let us hope without malicious intent) sometimes, but not always, to the point of deception.

Henry Towsner is a sophomore majoring in Math and Computational Science. He is not, and has never been, a member of the Communist Party.
