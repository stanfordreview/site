Lawyers Discuss Internet Privacy and Government Regulation
by Dave Myszewski
Senior Staff Writer

In a surprisingly light-hearted discussion, four well-known lawyers and legal scholars - Richard Epstein, the Hon. Abraham Sofaer, Dorothy Glancy, and Ian Ballon - discussed Internet privacy and Internet law at a recent panel discussion on September 21st.

One of the problems, the panelists agreed, is the attempt--or lack thereof--to apply existing laws to the information superhighway. The Honorable Abraham Sofaer, advisor to the U.S. Department of State from 1985 to 1990, maintained, "You don't give up 2000 years of legalism to accommodate the Internet."

In order to decide whether one should apply existing laws, he argued, one should see if there is an appropriate analogy. Second, one has to ask, "Is there something within the nature of technology that would justify an alteration in the rules?"

Stanford's Federalist Society, which organized the event, asked the panelists several questions which are unique to issues in Internet policy.

One of the many legal dilemmas the Internet presents is whether the states or the federal government should be making privacy policy. Dorothy Glancy, professor at Santa Clara University's law school, who rarely weighed in with her opinions during the discussion, argued that, "In most cases, privacy laws are state statutes." Another panelist pointed out that California's constitution, Article 1, Section 1, states that people have an "inalienable right to privacy." This right to privacy applies to such things as personal e-mails and financial records.

Another question posed by the moderator was simply, "Who owns it?" Richard Epstein, the James Parker Hall Distinguished Service Professor of Law at the University of Chicago, used an analogy of medical records. For a period of time in U.S. history, there was a debate whether the patient or the hospital owned that information. The typical medical database, he asserted, is used an average of one hundred times by doctors, specialists, insurance coverage, and research. Patients would choose to give up their right to the information for the medical attention they need.

The panelists discussed Internet Service Providers (ISP's) placing devices on their servers which filter out information based on content, who an e-mail is from, and who the e-mail is to. Mr. Epstein cited the fact that there are many ways around devices which filter material based on content, saying that people will send encrypted messages or use variations on words rather than the words known to be filtered out.

The discussion's most prominent privacy analogy, brought up by Ms. Glancy, was the difference between virtual life and real life. The question was asked, "Which would individuals fear more, someone physically following them or someone following them online?"

After conducting an informal poll of the audience, Ms. Glancy informed the audience that most people agree with them. In general, people are much more afraid of an individual physically following their movements than they are of someone "following" them on the Internet.

The reasoning was summed up succinctly by Mr. Epstein. "The person on the Internet can't kill you." Despite the lesser fear of being followed on the Internet, Mr. Sofaer still takes precautions. "One of the things that I did was say that no one is going to get into my writing. I have two computers, two hard drives, and nothing is connected to one of the computers."

By not getting an Internet connection on the computer on which he does his writing, Mr. Sofaer eliminates the possibility of a malicious person hacking into his files on that computer.

The panelists cited privacy policies as important contracts between consumers and companies. Privacy policies state which information a certain Internet company collects and how they will or will not use it.

Ian Ballon, a corporate lawyer who concentrates on intellectual property and Internet-related litigation, cited Amazon.com as an example where the provider-client relationship are being eroded, since Amazon.com is no longer adhering to their privacy policy.

"That is potentially problematic."

In discussing unsolicited e-mails, Mr. Sofaer stated that it is "unbelievable that this society tolerates someone phoning you at six who you've never heard of before."

Mr. Epstein replied that "Most people have an intuitive sense that e-mail is less intrusive than phone calls." He also pointed out that as commercialization plays its role on the Internet, the idea of inviolable privacy becomes difficult to sustain. "Think of the most embarrassing questions [Internet sites] could ask you but don't."

Mr. Ballon replied, "People will answer those questions for a five-dollar T-shirt."
