Front Page
The New Cultural Orientation:Whose Values Are Stanford Values?
by Scott Rasmussen
Staff Writer

"I pledge allegiance to the United States of America," student actors recited to listening freshmen in a series of different languages during "Trajectories," the latest addition to the New Student Orientation program lineup.

Along with "The Real World: Stanford" and "Faces of Community", the University intended "Trajectories: A History of Stanford" to introduce new students to Stanford values. This program focused on certain parts of Stanford's "historical trajectory" which it defined as highlights: the inclusion of Asian-Americans and women as students from the beginning, the founding of the Black Student Association, students' 70% community service participation rate, and the 520 active student organizations.

"Trajectories," a premiere of writer, choreographer, and director Venus Opal Reese, gave new students an hour-length overview of Stanford history and culture. Dean of Students Marc Wais presented a top ten list of events in Stanford history ranging from the first Big Game to the development of Silicon Valley, a video presentation captured memorable student moments, and student groups played and danced in an attempt to introduce students to Stanford's traditions.

Even though the majority of students supported each individual message in "Trajectories," some were concerned that identity politics overshadowed the main message of Stanford's traditions. Freshman Justin Diener commented, "'Trajectories' ' purpose was to teach about Stanford history, but it didn't entirely accomplish this. Instead, it focused on pieces of Stanford history."

Ms. Reese had a different perspective. "'Trajectories' showed students that their actions have meaning and consequence," she commented. "It handled situations that have been touchy. It handled them with finesse."

Native American alumnus Margo Kerrigan talked about her role as a student activist in removing the Indian as the Stanford mascot and then proceeded to criticize the treatment of Native Americans by the federal government and by American society in general. "How many of you know that Indian bones were next to rodent bones in the Calaveras County museum?" she asked the incoming Stanford class, 40% hailing from California. "How many of you know that women had the right to vote before Indians?"

Students were then shown a drama where a woman was telling her female friend about a date with a handsome campus man. "The problem is, I didn't like it," she explained about a kiss. "I'm sexually attracted to girls."

The final drama was a dialogue between three students: one African-American, one Asian-American, and one white. In a typical conversation about school and exams, the white student insulted both companions with language that will not be repeated here, adding, "Everyone knows that Asians are the smartest minority and blacks aren't." "Faces of Community" continued developing the cultural themes introduced in "Trajectories." Dean Tommy Lee Woon said that the purpose of "Faces" was to "introduce Stanford students to diversity and community."

The show that followed delivered on this promise with speeches and performances from a plethora of identity groups. A white man spoke about the difficulties of being disabled at Stanford. A student spoke about the difficulty of being gay in the socially conservative Latino community. Cultural a capella and dance groups performed.

In a somewhat more theatrical performance, ASSU President Seth Newton enjoined the audience to "deny your whiteness" and abandon privilege, casting off his giant white puppet costume to emphasize his point. After the performance in Memorial Auditorium, students returned to their residences for staff-moderated discussions of the issues brought up in "Faces."

Students were receptive to the performances so long as the represented communities stuck to spreading awareness about their groups. Freshman Andrew Dumitrescu-Solomon commented that "the purpose of 'Faces' was to show us all the different sides represented at Stanford and how beautiful they are." However, students were less receptive when some went beyond providing information to telling others what to think. Freshman Mary Haw noted, "Everyone can have their own beliefs as long as they don't condemn others."

According to students, several "Faces" presentations flirted with crossing this line. Ms. Haw commented that a Native-American speaking on behalf of minority faculty tenure used an "irrelevant statistic" to make his point. Freshman Brendan Tarlow described a poem with a racial emphasis presented by an African-American man as "better fit in the 1960's genre."

Another program, the "Real World," was intended to provide health and safety information for this new time in students' lives which the Orientation Calendar describes as "when people leave home and start getting real." Student actors traced through a series of episodes that incoming students may have to deal with at Stanford: parties, drinking, eating disorders, sexual activity, and respect for others, to name a few.

The speakers gave information about the issues, and discussed alternatives for dealing with them. Sophomore Jenny Hildner presented a health fact that may very well make other students think twice about their behavior: "Seventy percent of students will be sexually active in their time at Stanford, and one third would be willing to lie about their sexual history in order to have sex."

As in "Faces," students then had the opportunity to discuss the information afterward in their residences. Most students approved of the format because it allowed them to think through the issues themselves. Brendan Tarlow commented, "I liked "Real World" because it offered multiple perspectives and described the consequences of behaviors."

Although the program necessarily presented certain unpleasant facts, students were happy with the positive outlook that "Real World" put on the student experience. The overriding message was to "take care of yourself, and to take care of others," commented Mr. Dumitrescu-Solomon. "We are so privileged to be in this position at Stanford that it would be a shame to ruin it.

Freshmen and transfer students are now hitting the books, the extracurricular activities, and the parties with the advantage of having been introduced to the full range of social and cultural opportunities at Stanford. Not everyone thinks the programs' choice of topics was correct; as Freshman Chris Bruner described the Orientation programs as being like "doing a play on the topics canonized as acceptable topics to do a play on at Stanford."
