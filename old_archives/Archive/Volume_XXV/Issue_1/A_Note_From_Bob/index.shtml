A Note From Bob
It's a new year, with new students, new issues, and a new volume. Fortunately, though time passes and circumstances change, some time-hallowed institutions remain as important as ever. The Review is one of them. For fourteen years, the Review has been a lone conservative voice on campus, telling truths others would choose just as well not to hear, seeing its criticisms of President Kennedy, the CIV program, and the speech code slowly but surely recognized and acted upon.

Like our model the National Review, whose editor we interview, the Review exists to cover news from a conservative perspective, not merely to act as Socrates' gadfly to unsettle the University's state of mind. First comes our front-page coverage of the University's substantially revised cultural Orientation program, featuring an entirely new event: Trajectories, a "history of Stanford," told in a dramatic format. As we document, however, the story of Stanford can be told many different ways by emphasizing some things and leaving out others. And it's not particularly surprising to see what symbols of Stanford the administration thinks are important.

Symbols of themselves can mean many things to many people. Last year, staffers debated whether the Confederate flag should come down over South Carolina, a debate that ultimately hinged on whether the flag was a symbol of Southern tradition and states' rights or a symbol of slavery and oppression. In News, we cover the strange saga of the Stars and Bars at Stanford. A stylized Confederate flag, as a symbol of the television series Dukes of Hazzard, made a brief appearance over Xanadu before morphing into an inoffensive blue X under pressure from the ASSU. Who ultimately should decide the fate of the banner: the members of the house on whose walls it hangs in a free and open election, or the passers-by who see it every day?

Ultimately, these questions are little different from the questions of property rights and privacy which lawyers including Richard Epstein debated at Stanford recently, as we report on the front page. But the relative controversy in the ASSU over a stylized Confederate flag, a symbol of a symbol of a symbol, raises the question Chris Desmond asks in Opinion: why is it so hard to get students excited about anything bigger than a two-foot-square patch of banner?

The Review is a conservative newspaper, but first and foremost it is a newspaper of ideas. We may cover issues from a conservative perspective, but because we do so because we present things as we see that they are. Indeed, bending over backward to attempt an ideologically neutral perspective can cross the line when reality is not ideologically neutral - as it rarely is. The essay collection Henry Towsner reviews in Book Review discusses exactly that issue in the context of Ted Turner's documentary on the Cold War. In the case of the Soviet Union and America, reality clearly favored one set of ideas over the other.

The Review's conservative ideas can inject some debate into the stale politics at Stanford and maybe play the gadfly to the famously apathetic student body as well as to the administration. The first issue is here: read it, discuss it, destroy it if you wish, but by all means think about it. Your settled state of mind may depend on it.
