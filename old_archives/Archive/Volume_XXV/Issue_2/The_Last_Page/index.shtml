The Last Page
A Letter From Across The Pond
I regret to report that the Brits are not laughing at us. I thought that if there were one nation to scoff at how seriously we Americans take our painfully elaborate and lengthy campaigns, it would be the nation that has an official party called the Monster Raving Loony Party. But, much to my chagrin, the British are just as absurd as we are.

Before I left for the Motherland, I was intent to write a piece on how silly our presidential race appeared from across the Atlantic. My English friends laugh at us (my Scottish friends cannot be arsed) and I seem to recall some snide remarks about our election cycle from previous visits. However, the popular media here assure me that I am mistaken. Not only do they not care about our presidential election - they are equally bizarre about their own upcoming election.

As our election draws formidably closer, the standard "Dead Heat in the American Election" article advances one page per day in the London Times. No caricatures of Al "I want to pump...you up" Gore or the tired-eyed Dubya. No sketches of our obsession with drug scandals and non-existent subliminal messages. No comments on how this campaign has been going on in earnest for over a year now.

Just the occasional aside in The Spectator on how Al Gore may or may not be from outer space. Jonah Goldberg from the National Review has suspected Gore to be an alien for some time now but nobody seems to be listening.

Both the Labour and Conservative parties have held their annual conventions in the past few weeks, partaken in much mud slinging and scandal mongering, and conducted a plethora of polls and focus groups. Sound familiar? Here's the difference: the Brits do not even know when their next election is to be held. But the rabid campaigning meanders on.

I always thought it so civilized that the British Prime Minister calls for the election when he is good and ready and that the election follows roughly a month after the announcement. Four weeks of hard-core, in-your-face campaigning and then it's over. Like tearing a band-aid off quick and easy. Like wham-bam-thank-you-ma'am. You get the idea. But I was wrong. Instead of a predetermined election date, the British campaign constantly and aimlessly. Instead of cramming it all into the allotted four weeks, they commence the mud slinging early, when there are fewer rules of engagement.

However, the drug scandals are where it gets really interesting. Just like us Americans, the British want to know which of their politicians have experimented with drugs. Just like us, they want to know of any past drinking indiscretions. The difference is that we are Puritans and they are not. Hence the whole Mayflower thing. Yeah, yeah, that was a long time ago but centuries later, they fess up to their human flaws (if sometimes a little too eagerly) and we dig ourselves into holes trying to bury ours.

Nowhere is the distinction more vivid than President Clinton and Prime Minister Blair's respective answers to the drug question. After Clinton's infamous cop-out, Blair stated that he had never tried drugs (and by all accounts this is true) but that if he had, he would have at least had the decency to inhale. It is a little creepy that young Tony managed London rock bands, wore bell-bottoms and grew long hair without ever smoking up but nonetheless, the man appreciates that you do not waste perfectly good weed. If Clinton's joint had been filled with holy pot that magically ended all human suffering when lit, he still would not have admitted to inhaling.

The British Conservatives are even more down with their bad selves. Opposition Leader William Hague claims to have downed fourteen pints per day as a younger man. This while fighting like a bulldog (literally--the man looks like a pit bull) to prove that he is "ready for Government." Not to mention that eight members of his shadow cabinet have just confessed to experimenting with drugs in their college days, one of which - heaven forbid - claims to have enjoyed it. Where does this leave the Conservative party's bid for government? In just the same place as before. In fact, eyes are starting to turn to the seemingly prudish Labour party with vigilance that they did not do what every other normal university student was doing in the seventies.

You have to ask yourself whether Bill Clinton would have ever been elected if he had admitted to inhaling. And would Dubya be winning this election more if he had admitted to trying cocaine? I think the difference is that the British want their leaders to be human and we still want ours to be dancing around in funny little hats and shiny shoe buckles.

So in case you were up nights worrying that we Americans look silly, rest assured that the British are not laughing at us. They are too drunk to notice and we are too sober for own good.

Brooke Brody-Waite is a junior majoring in Political Science and an editor emeritus of the Review, currently abroad in Scotland. She heatedly denies ever having any past indiscretions.
