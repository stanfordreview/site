Book Review
I'll Cry If I Want To: The Truth About The Republican Party
It's My Party: A Republican's Messy Love Affair with the GOP
By Peter Robinson
"Spendthrifts such as Nelson Rockefeller, suspicious characters such as Richard Nixon, bumblers such as Gerald Ford, self-satisfied rich people such as the ones I encountered at fundraisers, patricians such as George Bush, time-servers such as Bob Dole. There was always so much in the Republican party of which I disapproved."

Hoover Institution Fellow and Reagan speechwriter Peter Robinson describes the many faces of the Republican party with honesty and humor in It's My Party: A Republican's Messy Love Affair with the GOP. Released in August 2000 in time for the presidential race, the book attempts to answer the question of why people are Republicans. As Mr. Robinson points out in his own case, "I grew up Republican. There were extenuating circumstances."

Mr. Robinson uses his experience as a journalist to investigate the background of the people and places that call themselves Republicans. He begins with a historical look at a subject that even he admits he knew little about before writing this book: the origins of the Republican Party. Republicans like to emphasize, as they did in this year's national convention, that their party is the party of Abraham Lincoln and of the crusade against slavery. But Mr. Robinson points out that "when it appeared in 1854, the Republican Party drew much of its support from the same regions, economic classes, and ethnic and religious groupings as had two parties that preceded it, the Federalists and the Whigs. The name of the party may have been new. The party itself was old."

The possibility that the traditions of one's ancestors have as much bearing on one's political orientation as the issues of today prompts Mr. Robinson to explore what he terms "tribal politics." To his surprise, he realizes that having been born into an English-descent, white-collar, suburban family, he "took the Republican imprint before I was old enough to understand what was happening."

Mr. Robinson discussed the subject of tribalism with Stanford colleague David Brady, who was born into the Democratic fold in an Irish family in a working-class neighborhood. Mr. Brady suggested that ethnic loyalties in fact are what keep the American political system stable because they guarantee that a political party, no matter how poorly it fares in an election, will at least remain a viable opposition voice. Mr. Brady commented, "As far as I'm concerned, every American should get down on his knees every night and thank God that people like the Irish are loyal to the Democratic Party while you WASPs can't stop being Republican if you try."

Mr. Robinson devotes a chapter to the Republican stronghold that until recently could not stop being Democratic: the South. He argues that the South has become a solid Republican supporter in presidential politics both because it has changed and because it hasn't. Its traditional conservative values of military pride and regional honor have found a home in the Republican party as the rest of the country has moved politically leftward. And migration accompanying recent economic growth hasn't hurt: "It happens that the three kinds of Yankees who moved South - retirees, members of the military, and managers - are as Republican as any three demographic groups you could name."

The Republican demographic best exemplified by the South is losing political sway, Mr. Robinson fears. "At times I find myself imagining that the GOP represents the past, its members, the last remnants of an America that was once overwhelmingly white and Protestant, now living in the interior of the country as they make, so to speak, their last stand, steadily dwindling as a proportion of the population." Mr. Robinson identifies the groups that have historically had a difficult relationship with the Republican party - women and minorities - and outlines the steps that it would take to bring them into the Republican fold.

Economics is the main explanation for the gender gap, Mr. Robinson explains, because married women and married men support the Republican party equally, while single women - both young mothers and widows - are disproportionately dependent on the government assistance championed by Democrats. Mr. Robinson notes that "what the GOP preaches is self-reliance. What single mothers and widows want is help." Marrying the two antithetical concepts together, Mr. Robinson mentions examples of successful Republican initiatives to provide a helping hand to people while lessening their dependence on the government. Welfare reform assisting single mothers with child-care and transportation and faith-based initiatives serving the needs of the elderly are teaching Republicans that "showing a little heart would do the party good."

Republicans could likewise benefit by showing more heart than they have in the past with minorities. African-Americans supported the GOP for decades after the Civil War, and Mr. Robinson points out that even "as late as 1932, black people gave a majority of their votes to the Republican presidential candidate, Herbert Hoover, even as Hoover was losing to the Democratic candidate, Franklin Roosevelt, in a landslide." But the big government of the New Deal provided the help that African-Americans wanted, and Republicans have since given up on the black vote. Hispanic-Americans are voting Democratic, particularly after Proposition 187 in California convinced them that the Republican party was the party of anti-immigration and racism. Mr. Robinson states that "of all the immigrant groups who came afterward - the Irish, the Italians, the Jews, the Slavs - the GOP has failed to make even one - just one - a loyal part of the Republican constituency."

Mr. Robinson believes that George W. Bush represents the solution to the demographic difficulties of the Republican Party. Gov. Bush's proposals appeal to groups that the GOP needs to reach out to: "His welfare reforms include 'faith-based initiatives' ... an instance of compassionate conservatism that Catholics and single women find reassuring." His fluency with the language and culture of Hispanics make "Bush appear so much at ease with Hispanics that they might as well be members of his family (which, for that matter some are)".

Gov. Bush also unites Republicans-social conservatives and economic conservatives, southerners and northerners, claims Mr. Robinson, "George W. Bush was raised in Texas. Yet he was educated, so to speak, abroad, attending Andover, Yale, and Harvard. When southerners look at him, they see not a Yankee but one of their own. When northerners look at him, they see not a redneck but the product of a New England education." Mr. Robinson's analysis: "George W. Bush demonstrates how the GOP can win." At last.

Scott Rasmussen is an undeclared sophomore. He was born into the Republican party and hasn't regretted it since.
