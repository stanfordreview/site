Indigenous People's Day And The Demons Of Columbus
by Keun Lee
Staff Writer

This year's Columbus Day went hardly noticed by many Cardinals. Aside from postal workers taking a day off, business continued as usual for most of the Stanford community. Stanford's Native American community, however, saw the federal holiday as an opportunity to strengthen their solidarity and celebrate their heritage. SAIO, the Stanford American Indian Organization, observed Indigenous People's Day at White Plaza at 8:00pm on October 9th.

Theresa Downey, one of the senior members of SAIO, explained, 'People from our community decided that there should be a day to remember all the indigenous people throughout the world, to celebrate the survival of all those people.'

The event was a candlelight vigil held in an open mic format. While the pouring rain of the day and evening ruled out any possibility of a protest as had occurred in previous years, participants, mostly Native American students, took turns sharing stories about their lives as Native Americans today. Joe Stone, a senior at Stanford, was one of many Native American speakers who discussed social and cultural identity crises Native American communities around the nation are going through.

Referring to the afflicted adolescent years of his life, he discussed elements of today's mainstream American society that are at odds with traditional Native American values. "All those demons that were brought over with Columbus torment us, conflict our lives, and make us think ourselves not worthy and to place blame on ourselves [sic] and those around us who should be most valuable."

After years of government assistance, the great majority of Native American communities around the nation remain poor and plagued by alcoholism and drug abuse. Such socioeconomic ills, coupled with the increasingly individualistic lifestyle younger Native Americans lead, pose threats to Native Americans' traditional way of life.

As one of the few white speakers of the night, Seth Newton, the ASSU president, spoke about what he deemed as mainstream America's attitude towards Native American issues. Mr. Newton said:, "Guilt, I believe is the thing that allows my people to not consider their role in the oppression. Guilt allows them not to even think that these structures of oppression continue because of them."

Mr. Newton's rhetoric is based on the notion that Columbus was not a heroic man who discovered America but a murderer responsible for the genocide of millions of indigenous people and the poverty in which Native Americans live today. Such view is fairly common around the nation, especially on campuses.

Many activists have pushed for officially replacing Columbus Day with Indigenous People's Day around the country. The Columbus Day holiday was renamed Indigenous People's Day in Berkeley and in South Dakota. Such movement has put Native Americans at odds with America's large Italian population who identify Columbus as an Italian hero.

When asked how she feels about Columbus Day as a federal holiday, Ms. Downey answered, 'Columbus is celebrated in America. You know, post office closes, the banks close, kids don't go to school. That's what everybody sees. Nobody sees the indigenous people who are still alive who still sing their songs and still do their dances. If you silence people so much that they are holding it all in, it turns into anger.'

Patrick Lewis-Jose, also a senior member of SAIO, added, 'We live in a world of symbolism. And to a lot of people Columbus symbolizes the whole idea and concept of colonization. I think each indigenous people around the world can probably pinpoint their own oppressors, their own Columbus's. But because Columbus is so much heralded than any other, that's why he's most symbolic of all European oppressors as indigenous people see it.'

Abolishment of Columbus Day, however, was neither the focus of the night's event nor on SAIO's agenda. Ms. Downey said, "What we can do is educate ourselves first and then try to educate other people. When we educate ourselves, that's when we can start let go of the anger, that pain."
