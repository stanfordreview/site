A Good Party Never Dies: Reviving The Greens
by Matt Barrett
Staff Writer

The Green Party has joined the Republican and Democratic Parties among the parties represented by Stanford students. At a "teach-in" at Synergy on October 13, over 50 students showed up to support the newly re-formed Stanford Greens, who have not had an official presence on campus since 1992. The purpose of the event was to educate the participants about key issues. Excited by the success of this first "teach-in", the group is planning a second "teach-in" for November 3, to take place on the front lawn of Columbae.

Shacar Link, president and co-founder of the group, initiated interest in forming the Stanford Greens in late September by e-mailing lists of various student groups, including the SLAC, the Stanford Labor Action Coalition, and SEAS, Students for Environmental Action at Stanford. The Stanford Greens' first meeting, which Mr. Link organized, included just four people. Sophomore Tarek Ghani was one of the four and he responded favorably to Mr. Link's efforts and helped rally interest in his own house, Synergy, and the neighboring co-op Columbae. The organization then sponsored a booth at the activities fair on September 24 and subscribed roughly 100 students to the Stanford Greens' mailing list.

Mr. Link, a graduate student in history, describes the organization's purpose as primarily "to educate ourselves and others about the issues the Green Party stands for. Education is so important to the members of the group largely because they feel the Stanford Community is basically unaware of the views the Green Party represents."

"A lot of people are attracted to the Green Party," says Mr. Link, "because they're sick of the other two [political parties], not necessarily because they know what it stands for."

The Green Party has several different platforms organized around the same common themes: 10 essential values, including social justice, nonviolence, decentralization, community-based economics, and various environmental issues. One platform, adopted by the Green Party delegates at their annual Green Congress, includes the abolition of the Senate, a $12.50 per hour minimum wage, and a confiscation of all income earned over 10 times the minimum wage. Another platform, endorsed by the Nader/LaDuke campaign, instead calls for the abolition of the Electoral College, a "fair" minimum wage, and "aggressive steps to restore a fair distribution of income."

The 10 essential values are represented at Stanford unequally; students have chosen largely to emphasize three values in particular. According to Mr. Link, those values are the environment, citizen involvement in democracy, and fair trade.

Mr. Link says the environment could be the Stanford Green Party's number one priority. "Democrats don't see the environmental issue as major as it needs to be," he says. "[The Stanford Greens would like to be] involved in open space and Foothills issues." The Stanford Greens were not directly involved in organizing the recent protest hike to the Dish, but the group did bring people to the rally.

But the Stanford Greens are not limited to environmental issues. Although that is the issue the Green Party is best known for, both on and off campus, an even bigger issue, according to Mr. Link, is the effort to diminish corporate involvement in the political system. "If there is one thing about Ralph Nader," Mr. Link says, "it's his stance on corporate influence in democracy." The corresponding key value, Grassroots Democracy, is the first one mentioned on the Green Party's official platform.

Delving deeper into a specific issue, the WTO, he says, "[Ralph] Nader would like to renegotiate NAFTA and GATT...the WTO should not be closed the way it is now. The motto is 'fair trade not free trade'."

Mr. Link also notes that members of the Stanford Greens vary in which issues they consider most important. "There's a broad range of issues that concern people."

The Stanford Greens have also tried to fulfill a different need in addition to education around election time: voter registration. Members of the Greens have distributed voter registration forms around campus, though not necessarily advocating their own party. Getting people to vote "is in itself important," says Mr. Link, especially for third parties. Of course, the members of the organization mostly support Mr. Nader. Tarek Ghani was instrumental in arranging for him to speak at Stanford.

The Green Party is a relatively new political party anywhere, let alone at Stanford. According to the organization's official website, "In 1984, inspired by the German Green Party, American Activists formed the Green Committees of Correspondence." Its first "fully delegated Green Congress was held in Eugene, Oregon in 1989." Shortly thereafter, Stanford students became more interested and more active in Green Party issues. The last time a group of Stanford Green Party Advocates registered with the Office of Student Activities was 1992. According to the Stanford Daily, students represented the Green Party at a political rally in 1996, although not officially.

This year, members of the group are pleased with its initial success. Mr. Link says, "We've all been pleasantly surprised by the interest. I think 50 people for a political event on campus is pretty good." Unlike the Stanford Greens of 1992, this group plans to remain active after the Presidential election. Precisely what they will do is unclear.

Says Mr. Link, "That's something we need to think about. It's in the back of everyone's mind. We'll try to get involved in local issues."
