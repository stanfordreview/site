A Note From Bob
People should be able to choose what kind of speech their money supports. Conversely, people should be able to use their money to support whatever kind of speech they choose. For instance, the ASSU rightly allows students to request back their special fees - that $52 levied on each student to support student organizations. While a large portion of these fees go to community service groups like the Stanford Community Carnival or generally inoffensive organizations like the Chappie, some of the money also goes to political organizations like SEAS - Students for Environmental Action at Stanford - as well as occasionally political organizations like the Black Student Union and the Asian American Students Association. Each year, as we supported on the back page of our last issue, students can get refunds from any group that they wish.

It's perfectly natural that students should wish not to subsidize groups that don't help them. In fact, requesting a refund back from a group that doesn't benefit you will encourage them to try to benefit you for next year. And I think we'd all prefer a campus where groups try to benefit the entire campus as opposed to their narrow membership. That is even the justification for the special fees - to promote diversity of opinions and opportunities for everyone on campus. So requesting back your special fees will actually help to make the special fee system work better.

It's amazing how much vitriol this simple assertion invokes on campus. The Monday after the Review was distributed, the editor of the Chaparral, Gideon Lewis-Kraus, sent a letter to the Daily decrying "the blithering idiocy" of the Review's back page and personally attacking the editors. Referring to the Review, "the local bastion of unabashed classism," and to the Stanford Guardian, an anonymous student who fliered to inform students about the deadline for their special fee refunds, he wrote: "Even if they offered their names, they would only be recognized by the other residents of the unlit hobbit holes they call home." Apparently, the threat of students taking back their fees from his magazine struck a little too close to home - even though we had specifically exempted the Chappie on our second page. Of course, this was the editor of the local humor magazine, so perhaps he was not entirely serious.

Still, the special fees problem crops up in unexpected ways. Unbeknownst to most students, the ASSU recently sponsored the Workshops on Action for Change - billed as a program to promote radical activism on campus. But because it was funded through the ASSU Program Board, students can't get back their special fees without being barred from dozens of unrelated events. And is it really within "the educational mission of the University" - the ostensible guideline for ASSU funding - to learn about press tactics for sit-ins or hear stories about the strategies used in the '94 hunger strike? Even aside from that issue, students deserve the ability to not fund speech that they disagree with. But people also deserve the ability to fund speech that they agree with. One way to do that is to give money to a candidate - with the caveat of full disclosure for that funding. This issue, we used that full disclosure to bring you the full picture of what goes on the the 94305 zip code, at least as far as campaign money goes. Not surprisingly, the majority of the money went to the most liberal Democratic candidate - Bill Bradley. Perhaps surprisingly, second place was Bush, not Gore.

One candidate conspicuously missing from the campaign funding report was Ralph Nader. This issue, we bring you the story of the revival of the Green Party on campus, as well as Henry Towsner's reflections on voting for third-party candidates. Hint: he's not voting Green.

Elsewhere in the issue, you'll read about Indigenous People's Day at Stanford, formerly known as Columbus Day. You'll also read about Reagan speechwriter and Hoover Fellow Peter Robinson's troubled relationship with the GOP.

The Review too is political speech, though funded by subscribers rather than students. So, to the students, read the Review anyway. You'll get far more than what you paid for it.
