Opinion
Don't Throw Your Vote Away: Vote For A Third Party
It is, in the worst possible sense of the word, conventional wisdom that voting for a third party is a "wasted vote." Everyone knows that the only viable candidates are Bush and Gore, and that the only way to influence the election, and therefore to have any meaningful voice in the American democracy, is to vote for a candidate nominated by a major party, holding your nose if necessary.

Many people seem to sense that there is something fundamentally undemocratic about this, but when asked to defend their position they resort to vague statement about voting on principle, which sounds weak against the crippling fear that the hated worse-of-two-evils might win. Nader has tried to defend votes for himself with the claim that it makes no difference whether Bush or Gore wins because they are so similar; this argument has made little headway, largely because most people find it to be self-evidently wrong.

The reason the conventional wisdom is wrong is because it assumes that the sole purpose of voting, exclusive of all others, is to select the next President (or Senator, or City Clerk, or whatever). Certainly selecting the President is important, but choosing one politician for the next four years pales in comparison to changing the entire playing field. Elections, and the Presidential election in particular, guide the nation's politicians towards policies they think will be popular. A President who wins by a large margin can talk about having a "mandate from the people" in a way a narrowly elected President wouldn't dare. If a third party candidate does well, both parties will rush to adopt their ideas in the hopes of getting those voters during the next election.

Third party nay-sayers love to point out that the Republicans and Democrats have dominated American politics since the Civil War, so the chances of a third party succeeding (by the narrow definition of selecting a President) are small. But selecting a President is a limited definition of success: selecting policies is far more important. The classic example is the Progressive party at the turn of the century. Once it became clear that they were attracting votes, the Democrats lusted after their support to the point that they co-nominated the Progressive candidate, William Jennings Bryan, effectively merging the parties. To the close minded, the Progressives failed because they ceased to exist, but to the more realistic, the Progressives succeeded in co-opting one of the major parties. Surely the Progressive supporters who voted for the Progressive party candidates enough to make the Democrats change their platform had far more impact in the long run than the Progressive supporters who voted for "the lesser evil" just to keep one opponent out of office for four years.

It is, of course, necessary to compromise at times. Political parties depend on people working as a coalition to select a representative, suppressing areas of disagreement in exchange for pushing common viewpoints. Any decision to vote for a third-party must be made by balancing the need to work together against the need to push better policies. The coalition argument is weakened, however, by the lack of a meaningful discussion of common viewpoints. A primary is supposed to give the members of a party a chance to select a representative who represents the members of the party as best as is possible, but this year's primaries were both more like coronations. The Democratic primary was openly corrupt: one-third of the votes are from unelected "superdelegates," who were promised to Gore by the Democratic leadership without any say from mere voters.

If voters who disapprove of candidates foisted upon them still vote unquestioningly for their party's nominee then they have surrendered their vote. Why should a party listen to people who have no choice but to vote for whoever the party nominates? The only way to retain some control over the party is to be willing to withhold your vote on occasion when the nominee is too far out of line with your beliefs.

Henry Towsner is a sophomore majoring in Math and Computational Science. He is planning to vote for Harry Browne.
