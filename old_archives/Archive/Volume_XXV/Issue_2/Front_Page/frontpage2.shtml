Front Page
ASSU Sponsors Training Seminar For Radical Activists
by Jim Carlson
Staff Writer

Last week, Stanford students, largely unknowingly, funded a two-day seminar for liberal activists. The two-day event, named Workshops on Action for Change, or WAC, was a replacement for the Grassroots Organizing Weekend (GROW) which has taken place on campus over the previous three years. The WAC offered a series of workshops aimed at improving the effectiveness of Stanford activist groups. The workshops were led by Stanford alumni with experience in campus activism who returned to offer insight and share their experiences. According to student organizer Sarah Faye McMullen, the goal of the WAC was to make sure that "every generation of Stanford activists is better prepared than the one before."

The WAC covered a variety of topics, including general organizational concepts, power analysis, strategies and tactics, member recruitment, press skills, and coalition building. The workshops generally involved alumni presentations followed by group projects and discussion. In addition, there were opportunities for the participating members of various organizations to get to know one another and network in anticipation of future collaboration. Story-telling activities gave the participants a chance to get personal.

Over a dozen different activist groups were represented at the seminar, from SLAC (Stanford Labor Action Coalition) to SEAS (Students for Environmental Action) to MEChA (Stanford's Chicano/Latino student group). In addition, while much of the information provided during the WAC would have been beneficial to any community organization, emphasis was placed on progressive agendas which have been the source of recent and perennial agitation. Among these were the furtherance of ethnic studies programs, funding for community centers in East Palo Alto, resistance to the commercial development of EPA, and the grape boycott.

To achieve a historical perspective on these issues, participants watched a video from the '94 hunger strike and listened as alumni reminisced over the tumultuous events of that spring -- a recent high point in Stanford activism which gave rise to both the ethnic studies program and the grape boycott.

The assembled activists then turned to current issues, working together to develop effective strategies to amend the status quo. Recent controversies, such as the difficulty of attaining tenured positions for professors in the Comparative Studies in Race and Ethnicity program, were the focus of group brainstorming. Considering a hypothetical scenario involving the denial of tenure to a popular Asian American Studies professor, participants considered the value of various countermeasures, such as protests, media exposure, and the application of political pressure. Throughout this process, the Stanford Disorientation Guide, an annual pamphlet on "injustice" at Stanford, was frequently referred to as a valuable resource in struggles against the administration.

In another real-world case study, activists discussed how best to stem the ongoing "gentrification" of East Palo Alto, whereby commercial and residential development threaten to replace endemic conditions of poverty. The participants considered as countermeasures various forms of political pressure, such as rallies, petitions and protests, and policies such as rent control.

The discussion of an off-campus campus issue, prison reform, provided perhaps the clearest insight into the political and social mentalities which dominated the WAC. Holmes Hummel, a Stanford alumnus who is currently pursuing the issue, asked participants to identify who is subject to incarceration under the American judicial system. The immediate response, "minorities," offered by ASSU Vice President Malia Villegas, was deemed close, but not exactly right. After turning down the suggestions, "criminals" and "human beings," Holmes laughingly remarked that "it's like I've stepped back into Palo Alto."

She proceeded to explain that the correct answer is "poor people and people of color." According to Holmes, these groups are "captured by security forces, "in order to have their productivity "seized by the state for sale to corporations" in a system that "looks a lot like slavery." This description was well received by the participants, who proceeded to analyze the roles played by corrupt politicians and corporate donations in this power structure.

On Saturday the WAC concluded with a discussion of the pros and cons of increased unification between the various ethnic and social activist groups present. While obstacles and potential sources of discord between the organizations were noted (such as the perception of the Asian American Students Association as representing a singularly "privileged minority"), a commitment to greater collaboration was reached.

This series of workshops received financial support from the ASSU, using funds derived from the undergraduate student body through the special and general fee process. Events such as the WAC do not appear on the list of volunteer student organizations whose funding is voted on by the student body every spring, and from which students may request refunds for individual organizations. Instead, the WAC, like many other student events, received its funding from a discretionary fund controlled by the Undergraduate Senate of the ASSU. Students may also request refunds of their contribution to this discretionary fund, but in doing so would also retract their support from, and possibly lose access to, over 500 other student organizations. While the ASSU was unable to provide the figures for its contribution to the WAC after four business days, last year's Grassroots Organizing Weekend (for which this year's WAC was a replacement) received $1,492 from the ASSU Program Board.
