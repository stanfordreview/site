The Newest Skeptics: James Cameron, Gnostic Novels and Faith’s Trajectory

by John Komkov

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In December 1945, two peasants on the outskirts of the Egyptian town of Nag Hammadi were digging for fertilizer at the base of a cliff. Muhammad and Khalifah ‘Ali of the al-Samman clan had tethered their camels to some nearby rocks and had proceeded to wrestle aside a boulder in search of decent soil. Beneath that rock, they instead found several clay jars, all 60cm or more in height and sealed at their tops with bitumen. Despite Muhammad’s admonitions that the jars might contain harmful “jinns”, Khalifah cracked open one of the vessels with his pick, claiming that it was equally likely that they contained gold.

So went the discovery of the Nag Hammadi archive. And while we may not comfortably equate the afternoon work of a few Egyptian farm hands to an event with considerably wide-ranging impacts, Khalifah’s decision to break open that first jar constitutes the impetus for the most internally divisive movement within modern Christianity.

The mere mention of Gnosticism in a contemporary Christian setting can be enough to spur an argument. Ascribing to its views and values can be enough to merit anger and ostracism. Of course, many of the ideas contained in Gnostic texts are contentious and some are clearly heretical. Still, though, throughout the history of the church, fringe groups have cropped up time and time again, most to little notice. What exactly is it about Gnosticism that makes it particularly venomous and what ramifications does Gnosticism have for the future of the Christian faith?

Before it is possible to address the issues at hand, we must first go back to 1945 and to Egypt—we must first look a bit more into the origins of the 20th century iteration of Gnostic thought. Shortly after returning to his village with the red slips of papyrus comprising the archive, Khalifah divided his find among seven camel drivers who had assisted in the transport of the documents. Muhammad and Khalifah passed on their scrolls to a Coptic priest, Basiliyus ‘Abd al-Masih. Al-Masih in turn distributed various pieces of the work among his friends, where some portions of the archive were burned and others were lost. Even after these tenuous transfers, the majority of the codex wound up in a Belgian antique dealer’s exposition in Cairo in the early 1950s. There, James Robinson, an American theologian, stumbled on the corpus, and it was shortly thereafter when Robinson was able to secure UNESCO funding for his intended research that the fate of the archive was sealed.

Robinson, as it turns out, is a proponent of the “historical” Jesus. Now a Professor Emeritus at Claremont College, he argues essentially that Jesus was little more than a Jewish teacher who managed to attract a small following of Galileans. After several years of this preaching, Jesus was apprehended in the Iudaea Province of Palestine and was executed. In general, Christians have traditionally opposed such “secular” renditions of Jesus’ past, and even the first advocates of a “historical” Jesus faced staunch criticism. By imbuing in Jesus’ works a clear historical narrative, these Historicists seemed to want to belittle Jesus’ role as a Messiah. It gets harder to say that a man is the Son of God, after all, when we have accounts of that man’s every day life.

Armed with “historical” documents like the Dead Sea scrolls and the Nag Hammadi codex, the Historicists have succeeded in upping the ante in recent years. By couching his new trove as a substantive validation, Robinson was able to make a career out of defending his “historical” Christ—a Christ that countered the spiritual Christ of Sunday church services and a Christ who could allegedly be known absent of the “structures of religion”. In one of his essays, The Real Jesus of the Sayings Q Gospel, Robinson summates the importance of history in the religion:

“To be sure, the Evangelists themselves have already tailored their narrations of Jesus’ sayings and healings to focus on the kerygrna, making the gospel of cross and resurrection the quintessence of the whole ministry of Jesus. So can one then be spared the details? Yet for modern people, a person who remains historically inaccessible is somehow unreal, more fancy than fact, indeed a myth. It would boil down to a kind of modern Doceticism if, moved by awe before the exaltedness of Jesus, we were to declare his historical reality to be academically unattainable or religiously irrelevant.”

Similarly, figures like Michael Baigent, Richard Leigh and Henry Lincoln, authors of Holy Blood, Holy Grail and more famously Dan Brown, author of the Da Vinci Code, have all cashed in on the modern American desire for a historical justification of Jesus. Not all of these alternative Christ stories purport to be wholly factual, but by implicating themselves deeply in conflicting accounts of past events, almost all of the stories ironically underscore the idea that nothing can be certain. Even the Bible, the god-breathed nexus of Christian faith, is not beyond controversy, as the obvious question becomes, “Why weren’t aspects of these other archives that keep cropping up, many of them propagating conflictive doctrine, included in the original biblical text?”

In this context, Gnosticism itself may not necessarily be as angering to traditionally practicing Christians as are motions people have put forward in recent years that have been substantiated with Gnostic texts. Certainly, a comprehensive understanding of theological phenomena has its place in modern America, but when that understanding must inherently undermine the foundation of the church, its relevance is shakier. Most recently, James Cameron, the famed director of Titanic, has been generating press about the release of his movie, The Lost Tomb of Jesus. In the film, Cameron claims to have found the legitimate site of Jesus’ burial, and, perhaps to little surprise considering the film’s production values, Jesus’s body is still onsite. It goes without saying that when Cameron is able to point to skeletal remains on camera and he is able to certify via leading scholars that the remains likely belong to the historical Jesus, the small matter of the Resurrection falls by the wayside. Moreover, the film implies that Jesus was likely romantically linked with Mary Magdalene. The evidence for this, Cameron insists, is that Jesus’ remains were found in the same ossuary as Mary’s, and that such a pairing would only be customary for family/spouses among ancient Hebrews. The Talpiot crypt, the spot in question, is on the outskirts of Jerusalem and has no affiliation with the Church of the Holy Sepulcher, the conventional site of the “tomb of Jesus” and a place of worship and pilgrimage for thousands of years. Anticipating backlash, Cameron is the first to say that “nothing is certain”, but when he has Andrey Feueverger, a professor of mathematics at the University of Toronto, declare on film that there are 599/600 odds that Talpiot represents Jesus’ true familial tomb, Cameron’s assertion is far from tentative.

What is to be made of this new breed of attacks on the routine Christian faith? To what extent do these “questioning” Christians-- these critical practitioners with a penchant for history and “alternate” theories—mean harm through their skepticism? Depending on who you ask, you are likely to encounter a full gamut of explanations, from the innocent Religious Studies professor who contends that an emphasis on the facts is really another method of reiterating faith all the way to the fundamental preacher who figures any such dialogue to represent nothing more than a direct attempt to diminish committal to beliefs and spiritual certitude. As increasingly major players like James Cameron enter the debate, it will be interesting to see how Christianity responds. After all, back in the initial centuries after Jesus’ death, Gnosticism itself first gained momentum when it claimed to possess “secret” knowledge absent of the vacuity of typical Christianity. Its early followers put forward stories from non-recognized gospels like that of St. Thomas that give “holy” proof of debatable knowledge. In one story, for example, it is revealed that God keeps no soul in Hell for eternity, but that only elect believers can be permitted to know this fact. God, as the story goes, has to make it believed among common people that eternal damnation exists in order for devotion to function.

Ultimately it is this “exclusivity” of Gnostic knowledge that has appealed to many people and enabled it to survive for so long in relative obscurity. But in relative obscurity it has never yet had the opportunity to effectively impact Christian thought. Should men like James Cameron and Dan Brown continue to carry out these effectively Gnostic assaults on faith, putting forward “facts” for their theories against the unprovable background of spiritual truth, it is entirely possible that some people may leave the church. It is entirely possible that when stacked up against allegations that they are rationally misguided for following a dogma based in the unverifiable, people may head for more attractive interpretations that incorporate the proper range of footnotes. But for each person who abandons the church by virtue of his more literal interpretation of belief, there may very well be three who are only reassured in the soundness of their faith. There may very well be three who need not have substantive evidence of what Jesus did on this day or that day or who require hard or factual proof of why this scripture was incorporated into the doctrine where that one was not—those who will not demand a Hollywood production that divines the “proper” sites of worship in order to continue to commit themselves to a spiritual endeavor that they instead see vindicated in its moral track record.
