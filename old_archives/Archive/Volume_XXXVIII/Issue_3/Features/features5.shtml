Obama on Race

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Many Americans consider Barack Obama to be a “fresh face” in U.S. politics and a welcome change from the traditional D.C. politicians that we distrust. Of late, Obama has carefully crafted a public persona that suggests he is above race—that, as the son of a Kenyan father and a white mother, his politics transcend the black-white racial divide. As The Economist wrote about Obama in August 2004, he is a “post-racial candidate” who “appeals just as strongly to white suburban voters as he does to blacks.”

Obama’s ability to position himself as being “above race” has enormous appeal across the political spectrum. Indeed, if genuine, his brand of politics would represent a welcome change from politicians like Jesse Jackson and Al Sharpton, whose more overt, “in-your-face” styles tend to alienate middle-class white voters. To many Americans, an Obama presidency would mark the point where America transcends her old, ugly racial politics and finally achieves Dr. Martin Luther King’s ideal, where our descendants will “not be judged by the color of their skin, but the content of their character.”

However, a little skepticism is wise when evaluating politicians, no matter how charismatic. After reading Obama’s first book, Dreams from My Father, which he wrote in 1995, I had the disappointing feeling that in spite of his public image as a “post-racial” politician who will help this nation transcend its racial divisions, Obama is, at heart, a deeply expedient person who has no qualms about using race to pursue his own ambitions.

Despite Obama’s current focus on post-racial rhetoric, Dreams from My Father suggests that Obama is not averse to identifying himself solely as black when it suits his purposes. In his original 1995 introduction, he proudly titles himself “the first African-American president of the Harvard Law Review”—his half-white identity goes unmentioned. He mentions too that “I ceased to advertise my mother’s race at the age of twelve or thirteen, when I began to suspect that by doing so I was ingratiating myself to whites.” Moreover, although Obama confesses that his life is not fully “representative of the [average] black American experience,” he also adds that part of his reason for writing the book was to “embrace my black brothers and sisters, whether in this country or in Africa, and affirm a common destiny without pretending to speak to, or for, all our various struggles.”

Of course, Obama is right to be proud of his father’s black heritage. But Obama’s insistence on identifying himself solely as black is particularly odd given his unique background. Why couldn’t he just fully acknowledge his mixed background—both black and white—and be proud of both? Given that Obama today is a politician whose public image relies heavily on his ability to “transcend race,” it is difficult to see how this account reflects any attempt to transcend any racial divides that have plagued our nation.

Moreover, an unfortunate partisan and racial-activist streak occasionally manifests itself in Dreams from My Father. Writing of his decision to commit greater time to political issues, Obama recalls: “In 1983, I decided to become a community organizer… When classmates in college asked me just what it was that a community organizer did, I couldn’t answer them directly. Instead, I’d pronounce on the need for change.”

What sort of change? Obama writes: “Change in the White House, where Reagan and his minions were carrying on their dirty deeds… That’s what I’ll do. I’ll organize black folks. At the grass roots. For change.”

This tirade against one of America’s most loved presidents (Reagan won a 49-state landslide in 1984) comes across as highly divisive. It carries with it an unspoken assumption that the Reagan administration was somehow anti-black. Moreover, Obama’s past activism seems reminiscent of the overtly racial Jesse Jackson-style politics that present-day Americans believe Obama is supposed to transcend. With this background, it is difficult to view Obama’s current “post-racial” image as anything, but a politically smart move to appeal to white voters for the 2008 presidential elections.

History will remember Obama’s 2004 speech at the Democratic National Convention (DNC) as one of the party’s greatest moments, where he said that “There is not a Black America and a White America and Latino America and Asian America — there’s the United States of America.” By and large, Americans recognize that unlike our Forrest Gump president, whose speech patterns are constantly parodied, Barack Obama is a gifted and articulate speaker. Unfortunately, although Obama’s current rhetoric calls for America to transcend her racial differences, his past views on race, as indicated in Dreams from My Father, suggest a character that is deeply compromised, shrewd, and expedient. While these ugly qualities might make him a good D.C. politician, they should not make him president of the United States.


Barack Obama. Dreams from My Father. 464 pages. Crown, 2007 (first edition 1995).
