A Friend in Need is a Friend Indeed:
On the importance of keeping our Pacific allies

by Grant Everett Starrett

Guest Contributor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The media often reports Great Britain as our greatest ally because the United Kingdom remains the strongest among America’s allies and because our two nations share similar struggles. But I would question whether we might have closer friends in the world, who are willing to make greater sacrifices for less credit. Whereas British Prime Minister Tony Blair has been generally supportive of our efforts, he always seems to have hidden reservations, particularly with regard to climate change and, now more than ever before, Iraq. On the other hand, Prime Minister John Howard of Australia has been a steadfast proponent of our actions overseas; Prime Minister Junichuro Koizumi of Japan and his successor Shinzo Abe have also made notable commitments to the causes we consider valuable. Such contributions are particularly important given the increasing importance of Asia in the world today and need to be realized by American leaders so that they may feel justly rewarded.

Nevertheless, problems loom on the horizon for executives who support the policies of the Bush Administration. The tenure of John Howard, who has successfully managed Australia for over a decade, is now endangered by a young, fresh, and popular Minority Leader who wants to distance his country from ours. In Japan, Abe’s approval rating has dropped significantly from an initial high of nearly 70%. While his numbers are still robust, the trend is worrisome. Hopefully, it can be attributed to an originally inflated approval rating associated with the departure of former Prime Minister Koizumi, whose decisive leadership (he continued to visit a shrine that controversially celebrated Japan’s colonization period over the repeated objections of China and the Koreas) and quirky habits (he is famous for his Elvis impressions) made him practically a rock star in the country. For the moment, Abe is secure, but Howard is not.

The reason, however, that losing either of these leaders would be so much more catastrophic to American foreign policy than the loss of any supportive international executive has everything to do with rising concerns in Asia over an overly aggressive People’s Republic of China. Prime Minister Abe has traditionally been described as a hawk in the Japanese government, and surprised many when he broke tradition by making his first trip outside of Japan not to the United States but to China. The Chinese official media, who lambasted Koizumi and expressed skepticism about Abe, praised the Japanese premier up the ying-yang for his efforts to smooth over relations. In fact, however, Abe has been demonstrating some Realpolitik, and exercising that old maxim that one should keep his friends close and his enemies closer.

Abe’s principal foreign policy objective is to nurture a quadrilateral alliance with the United States, Australia and India. Such an alliance would be significant not only because the four countries are shining examples of modern democracy, but also because they are the significant power-brokers in Asia capable of containing potential Chinese aggression. Current treaties commit the United States, Australia and Japan to defending the democratic Republic of China at Taiwan from totalitarian Chinese warmongering. But Abe’s ambitions are not limited to diplomatic intrigue. He also wants to revise Article 6 of the Japanese Constitution, the provision that prohibits Japan from having anything more than a self-defense force. Japan currently possesses one of the three biggest economies in the world, and it is often joked in the international community that it has the world’s most expensive police force. Abe believes that Article 6 is a dated and obsolete stipulation: Japan is no longer an imperial power with designs on Asian hegemony. In fact, precisely the reverse is true: Japan is now a democratic and capitalistic state pledged to halt a totalitarian power with designs on Asian hegemony.

In order to do this, Abe needs to continue to ‘play nice’ with China so that Beijing’s objections are kept to a minimum. But he also needs the support of both the United States and Australia; if the Australians elect someone who wants to distance themselves from the United States and embrace their largest trading partner (China), Abe’s crucial plan will fall apart. Similarly, if the United States returns to the Clinton era policy of treating China as a ‘strategic partner’ rather than a ‘strategic competitor,’ (the current Bush Administration policy) all of Abe’s work will be undercut, undermined, and ultimately usurped.

Support for the United States comes at an expense for leaders around the world. Just ask former Spanish Prime Minister José María Aznar, who lost an election directly after the Madrid bombings due to his support for American efforts at retaliation to terrorism. In fact, you may witness the popularity associated with opposing President Bush that is clearly evident with the rise of Hugo Chavez in Venezuela and Mahmoud Ahmadinejad in Iran. In fact, strangely enough, one of the few places in which support for America is not crippling a candidate is in France, where conservative Nicolas Sarkozy currently leads in the polls. As America moves forward, it must find a proper balance between maintaining sovereignty over its actions and providing cover for the leaders who bravely support its usually righteous actions. The United States can do much as a beneficent world power, but its effectiveness is limited if it cannot back up its allies.
