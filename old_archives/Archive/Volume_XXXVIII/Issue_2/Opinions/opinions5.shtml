Islamofascism

by Daniel Slate

Opinions Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The intellectual history of fascism shows that our common conception of it in America is dangerously misguided. It is imprudent to think of fascism as a catchall pejorative meant to characterize policies or governments we find repulsive, and it is dangerous to have such misconceptions about a powerful social and political idea, especially one that is still alive and well in the twenty-first century. The reality is that fascism has a coherent political philosophy and presents a powerful critique, as Marxism did, of bourgeois democracy and capitalism. Its intellectual roots trace back in the West to the atomization and disintegration of social life that began in the late Renaissance and Enlightenment, moving up through modernity through the Romantic period and into the twentieth century. The thought of Hegel and parts of Marx, as well as that of Nietzsche, Henri Bergson, Georg Sorel, Freud, and the Futurists all served as threads in the weaving of a coherent fascist worldview crafted by continental writers and harnessed by charismatic nationalists.

The film “Obsession” does not aim to find all the elements of fascism present in radical Islam, but they are nevertheless there to behold. Beyond the theory and intellectual history, fascism is characterized in the world by (1) strong populism with a captivating leader at the helm; (2) mass rallies and public demonstrations designed to bring about a psychological shift, in which the individual transforms into a member of a crowd and defines himself as part of a whole; (3) a concept of the nation or people as a living body; and (4) the idea that by overcoming society’s atomization and instead integrating individuals through the mechanism of the transcendent state, the nation will be renewed and—with an added Futurist twist—will bring about a golden age that rockets the people into the future and brings them the respect they believe they deserve.

Thus, when seeking out a term to characterize radical Islam as a movement, the term Islamofascism is not inappropriate. Indeed, all the key elements of fascism are there: a broad, populist movement with charismatic leaders like Mahmoud Ahmedinejad of Iran, Osama Bin Laden of al-Qaida, and Hassan Nasrallah of Hezbollah; mass demonstrations with all speaking, chanting, and moving as one, each individual existing as an individual only insofar as he is a member of the whole; the idea of a renewal and overcoming of problems in the Islamic world if all the alienated and dispossessed join together as a borderless umma, reverse their societies’ atomization and excise Western decadence, and grow together as a transcendent, organic being embodied in an all-encompassing Caliphate; a Futurist element, exemplified by the radicals’ willingness to use technology—even if it’s Western—to achieve their ends and move into the golden age promised by the Future. The radicals then go two steps further to create a new breed of fascism, one which blends all the essentials together with the religious ingredient of Islam, as well as with the element that characterized Nazi fascism—antisemitism. Thus, while Americans may overuse the word “fascist,” the term is appropriate when applied to the extremist strain of Islam.

.
