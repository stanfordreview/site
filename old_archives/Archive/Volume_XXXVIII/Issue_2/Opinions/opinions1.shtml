Stanford’s Failed Trickle-Down Policy

by Stuart Baimel

Managing Editor for Content

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Stanford University is currently benefiting from an unprecedented array of financial successes. The endowment grew by an impressive 15.4% last year, surpassing Princeton’s endowment and bringing its total to $14.1 billion, third place behind Harvard and Yale. It was recently reported that Stanford also raised an incredible $911 million last year, shattering the previous worldwide record for university fundraising by more than $300 million (also set by Stanford). 40% of alums contributed to the fundraising drive, and most of the fundraising campaign was based around the new $4.3 billion Stanford Challenge capital campaign.

The financial largesse of Stanford has not been met with increased student services or benefits, however. And despite the impressive financial numbers produced by President Hennessy and Provost Etchemendy, the squeeze on students seems ever more acute. The Board of Trustees recently voted to increase tuition by 5.46%, bringing it from $32,994 to $34,800. Total expenses for undergraduates will total $45,608 in the 2007-2008 school year, compared to $43,361 now. The official press release is keen to point out that the “money the university collects from tuition covers only about 60 percent of the costs of educating an undergraduate.” Most of the increase is going toward “operating costs,” that vague category with which it is hard to determine benefits to students.

Just a couple days after the tuition increase, Stanford announced a new financial aid program for “middle-income families,” committing $5 million annually. Stanford defines middle-income as those with incomes between $60,000 and $135,000. Total financial aid has been increased from $66 million to $76 million annually.

The example given in the press release is indicative of Stanford’s inadequate financial-aid program. It says that for a family with accumulated home equity equal to $480,000 and an income of $103,000, parent contributions will be reduced from $25,000 to $22,000 and the student loan component will be capped at $2,000 annually, down from $3,500 currently. Never mind that Princeton and several other schools have a no-loan policy. And never mind that Stanford seems to weigh home equity highly when most parents, logically, are unwilling to liquidate their house (i.e. taking out a second mortgage) to pay for college.

It is unclear why Stanford is so financially tightfisted in an era of astounding financial prosperity. Whatever the reason, Stanford has failed to ensure that the benefits of its wealth trickle down to students in a way commensurate with the university’s financial situation. For example, our “bumper crop” year of fundraising has yielded more than $300 million more than the previous year, while the total income from undergraduate tuition will be $226 million. Despite the scale of such impressive fundraising numbers, only 3.9% of the increase in fundraising revenue from last year would be needed to cover the tuition increase. So despite making $2 billion on the endowment and an extra $300 million from fundraising, we are still being charged more for tuition.

It is logical to argue that Stanford’s operating costs will rise, year-by-year. It is perhaps logical that the increase will be between 4-6% a year, more than double the rate of inflation. But somehow, the burden of those operating cost increases seems to be borne by students, when alumni and the investment team at the Stanford Management Company have proven themselves perfectly willing to do so. Instead, students are forced to pay increased real costs every year without any substantial increase in the quality of the services. The food at the dining halls is not getting 5% better every year; most students seem to think it has gotten worse. Educational quality is of course very good, but it would be hard to argue it’s getting substantially better in a way that would merit increased tuition every year. And dorm budgets are being reduced, not increased.

Stanford needs to either better articulate why students are being squeezed or change its policy to better reflect the burden students and their parents have in regards to paying for one of the most expensive schools in the nation.
