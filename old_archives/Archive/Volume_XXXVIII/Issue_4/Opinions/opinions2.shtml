The ASSU: A One-Party System

by Chris Nguyen

Guest Contributor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In my four years at Stanford, including two in the ASSU Senate, I’ve heard the Students of Color Coalition (SOCC) called many things. To its supporters, SOCC is an organization that exists to improve “campus life for students of color and other minorities,” “the issues faced by students of color on campus,” “improve diversity,” and strives for “justice and equality” for ethnic minorities. To its critics, SOCC is a “detractor to the electoral process that surpasses all others,” “an engine of discrimination, racism and democratic regression,” has “faulty logic and [a] veneer of inclusivity,” and has “demagogic” tendencies.

To me, SOCC is Stanford’s dominant political party. A political party’s purpose is to run candidates for election, use their resources to get their candidates elected, and expect their candidates to advance the party’s goals and interests once in office. Isn’t that exactly what SOCC does?

For years, SOCC has consisted of the Black Student Union (BSU), Movimiento Estudiantil Chicano de Aztlán (MEChA), Asian-American Student Association (AASA), and Stanford American Indian Organization (SAIO). In the last couple of years, they added the Muslim Student Awareness Network (MSAN). Adding MSAN to SOCC is clearly a political move. Blacks, Latinos, Asians, and American Indians are clearly students of color. Muslims are not inherently students of color. Are there not white Muslims? The addition of MSAN clearly shows that SOCC seeks to expand its political base.

In the Senate, SOCC generally seeks to protect and enhance the funding of their member groups, scream at the administration about faculty and student diversity, complain about wages for campus workers, and divestment.

Blasting SOCC as some evil, corrupt organization that attacks democracy is not an effective method of dealing with them. What is necessary to compete with SOCC is a rival political party. For years, the political parties have been SOCC versus ad hoc coalitions. Under this system, the ASSU is essentially what political scientists call a dominant-party system; laymen call it a de facto single-party system.

In other places where one-party rule exists, the ruling party is corrupt and gives money to its favored factions. SOCC has previously used funding for its candidates, who in turn (when elected) ensure that SOCC gets more Special Fee money collected from all students by the ASSU. Under one-party rule, people fear criticizing the party for fear of official retribution. At Stanford, people fear criticizing SOCC for fear of being called “racist” or “bigoted.”

To handle SOCC, there needs to be a viable political party. A one-party system results in complacency, corruption, and self-centered policies implemented by the ruling party. When SOCC controlled the ASSU Senate funding process in 2004-2005, they drove the student group system into the ground by spending 165% of student fee money.

A competitive political party system would produce more productive, healthy ASSU policies than the current one-party system. Multiple parties must compromise, thereby reducing extremist policies, making government more likely to adopt policies that have broad-based support. In 2005-2006, when I chaired the ASSU Senate, an ad hoc coalition of moderate Democrats and Republicans managed to gain control of the ASSU and delivered a 20% surplus in order to pay down the previous deficit.

Even SOCC itself would benefit from a competitive party system. With other parties forcing it to compromise, SOCC would look more moderate to both the student body and the administration. When the administration sees advocacy issues being supported by a broad-based coalition, it is more likely to take it seriously than when it appears an issue is being backed by one kind of group (e.g. SOCC). No one seriously opposes SOCC’s calls for greater diversity, people simply oppose SOCC’s methods and modus operandi.

Multiple-party systems exist in other student governments. These parties aid student voters in better understanding what candidates stand for. At UC Berkeley, the moderate Student Action and the liberal SOCC-like CalServe are the major political parties that battle for control of the ASUC, along with some minor parties. At UCLA, the moderate Bruins United and the SOCC-like Student Power are the parties seeking control of the ASUCLA. Power swings back and forth between these parties just like in any normal competitive democracy. When one party veers too far into the extreme, the voters simply turn to the other party in the next election. Multiple parties serve to keep everyone in check from making too many extremist policies and from gaining too much power.

SOCC’s power comes from people’s mistaken belief that SOCC speaks for all students of color on campus. As an Asian-American and student of color, I say that they do not speak for me and that there are many students of color that I know do not believe in SOCC’s political agenda. They are just a political party. I say this again: Stanford students and administration must realize that SOCC is not the representative of all ethnic minorities on campus, it is simply a political party. SOCC should not be above criticism; when people realize it is simply a political party, people will criticize it when appropriate and give it credit when credit is due, just like any other political party. The time has come for a viable alternative political party to compete with SOCC for seats in the ASSU.
