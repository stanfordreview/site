Good Cop, Bad Cop:
How a Democratic Congress may be of help in Iraq

by Blair Nathan

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The recent elections were not, in general, good news for the right. Minimum wage and stem cell legislation have been early priorities for the new legislature, for example, and we can expect further action on the Democratic agenda as the 110th Congress proceeds over the next two years. Despite the much-touted fact that many of the newly-elected Democrats come from fairly conservative districts and will thus have to tread lightly, the party will also have to show its loudest—and most liberal—supporters that it intends to be assertive.

There is at least one silver lining to this state for conservatives, however. A divided government could allow the US to prod the government of Iraq in ways that would not have been credible before. It has been argued that the Iraqi government has been expecting the US to be eternally patient and accommodating. If the Iraqis fail to meet a milestone for sectarian reconciliation, suppression of the insurgency, or training of indigenous forces, well, these things can always be done tomorrow. The US, they believe, would never withdraw from its commitments to the Iraq project, so the prospect of American departure has not inspired much of a sense of urgency among Iraqi politicians. The Bush administration has shown a great deal of forbearance with Nouri al-Maliki’s progress--or lack thereof—in stabilizing and securing his country. On the home front, the administration has insisted that Maliki’s government needs time to make things work. The ascendancy of the Democrats in Congress, however, affords an opportunity for President Bush to get tough and effective with the Iraqi government. “We are willing to give you all the time you need to suppress strife and insurrection,” he can say to the Iraqis, “but I can’t speak for them.”

Indeed, Bush cannot speak for the Congress on the matter of Iraq. Many senior Democrats have expressed extensive discontent with his proposal to send over 20,000 additional troops to Baghdad and al-Anbar and fully intend to try to stymie the president’s efforts to enact a troop “surge.” Sen. Kennedy has said, “We cannot simply speak out against an escalation of troops in Iraq. We must act to prevent it.” Sen. Boxer has stated, “This is reminding me more of Vietnam every day.” The Democratic leadership has been singing a similar tune. The Minneapolis Star Tribune reported on the eve of the president’s recent State of the Union speech that the Senate and House’s top Democrats were already set against his proposal: “House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, D-Calif., and Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid, D-Nev., have left little doubt that Congress would take steps to block the plan if Bush insisted on adding troops.”

The White House, moreover, faces wariness from more than the usual suspects. Senate Majority Leader Reid believes that at least nine of his colleagues across the Senate aisle are against the escalation. Moderate Republican Olympia Snowe put it this way: “I have deep skepticism about it, about a surge addressing the root causes of the mistrust and hatred that sects have for each other. That’s what I expressed. The fact of the matter is that the American people don’t support this war and the way it has evolved because they see the Iraqis fighting among themselves instead of for themselves.” The nation as a whole seems to share Snowe’s skepticism. Just 17% of those polled by the Washington Post before the president’s address wanted to see a troop surge in the Iraqi theater.

On the whole, then, President Bush cannot guarantee that the citizenry or even the government shares his viewpoints and his dedication to the government of Nouri al-Maliki. Is this a bad thing? Not necessarily, according to Newsweek’s Fareed Zakaria: “The one action that might be forcing the Iraqi leadership to make some compromises has been the threat that Congress would force a withdrawal of American support. One month ago, the White House was criticizing Congress as being borderline treasonous for suggesting such a thing. Today its strategy in Iraq rests on the fruits of that assertiveness.”

National Review’s Ramesh Ponnuru brings up a similar point, pointing to a 1995 John Chettle piece in The National Interest for precedent of the good cop, bad cop routine in US foreign policy successes. According to Chettle, the success of the US in pressuring South Africa and the Soviet Union to reform (fundamentally, in both cases) is owed in large part to the divisiveness inherent in American government. In the case of South Africa, the right was somewhat willing to overlook the human rights issues of a Cold War ally, while the left wanted to sever ties with the apartheid state. In the case of the USSR, it was the left that wanted to entertain a more friendly dialogue, while the cold warriors of the right were loath to accept the “Evil Empire” for what it was. Chettle argues that in both cases, foreign leaders had to yield to the soft power of the United States. If they did not, then they risked alienating opinion in the US and handing American foreign policy over to thoroughly anti-apartheid or anti-communist sentiments. Chettle stresses that divergence in opinion within the US needed to be genuine in order for it to effect change: “It’s success in the cases of the Soviet Union and South Africa came not from its use as a negotiating tool but from the perception -- and the reality -- that there were indeed powerful forces in the United States that were deeply hostile to both countries and wanted to use punitive measures against them.” Chettle suggests that US failure to provoke reform in Cuba and North Korea can be conversely traced to an historical absence of divergence in US opinion.

Of course, the Shiite-led government of Maliki is not viewed as an adversary of the United States (or its values), as were the Soviet Union and South Africa. Still, it is not always evident that the government is a friend of ours either. The Iraqi government depends on the support of overtly sectarian forces such as followers of cleric Moqtada al-Sadr. Many of these Shiite partisans rival the Sunni insurgents in their anti-American rhetoric (and action), and they do just as bad a job at getting Iraqis to form a national, rather than a narrow and exclusive, identity. The government in Iraq, for its part, has all too often been beholden to such extremist voices, counterproductively vetoing the US-led coalition’s considered decisions on where to go and whom to arrest, for example. National Security Adviser Stephen Hadley summed it up this way in November: “[T]he reality on the streets of Baghdad suggests Maliki is either ignorant of what is going on, misrepresenting his intentions, or that his capabilities are not yet sufficient to turn his good intentions into action.”

Congressional Democrats will surely continue to manifest their dissatisfaction with the situation in Iraq. At the least, Democrats will pass a resolution documenting their lack of confidence in the president’s plans for Iraq. Sen. Kennedy and others want to go further. Will the increasing dissatisfaction among Americans and their leaders help to prod the Iraqi leadership into action? The Iraqi government surely does not want to be left in the lurch by the US while the Sunni insurgency still rages (with plenty of foreign help). If the Iraqis see this as a distinct possibility, they just may realize that the addition of four-plus American brigades to the country is as good a time as any for cracking down on Shiite militias, making earnest efforts at national reconciliation and reconstruction, and prioritizing the training of an army that would ultimately be proud to call itself Iraqi.
