Tear Down This Wall

by Tucker Herbert

Executive Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The debate on immigration policy in the United States is not polarized along party lines. The issue is fragmented such that there are alignments across ideological boundaries that are not usually crossed. On the right, business interests are pitted against Buchananesque-nationalists; while on the left, worldly-liberals find themselves in stark contrast to the party’s old guard protectionist labor union base. The mistake is to assume that one group’s gain is another’s loss. A pragmatic approach to immigration policy can maintain America’s historical commitment as a melting pot of upward mobility for the world while building a nation and economy that is stronger for all.

Business interests, particularly California’s agricultural industry, rely on immigrants to fill many jobs that no American desires. This availability of low-skill labor helps to keep America’s economy dynamic and competitive, while reducing the inflationary pressures that would result from a labor shortage. For quite a while, these business interests have succeeded in their efforts to promote policies that allow for low-skill migrant workers to come to the United States. But government policies have neglected to recognize the tremendous benefit that high-skilled laborers could bring to this country. Though many nationalists claim that such workers would be taking high-paying jobs away from Americans, I ask: where do these jobs come from, anyway?

New businesses, whether they are in industry or the services sector, are the source of jobs. Immigrants with entrepreneurial skills start businesses that create jobs to employ both native-born Americans and other immigrants. In fact, according to a report cited by the New York Times, immigrants in New York City start new businesses at a higher rate than native-born Americans. The competitiveness of New York serves as an incubator to develop small businesses started by immigrants into national and international enterprises that employ thousands of Americans. All of this occurs despite a legal framework that is not favorable to skilled immigrants, generally hindering their ability to obtain licenses to work and open businesses in the United States. Reducing government regulation in this sphere would increase demand for small business support services as many of these entrepreneurs lack knowledge of local markets, legal skills, and English proficiency for marketing. These new businesses would also increase demand for the skills of America’s union members and increase America’s capacity to absorb the world’s tired, poor and huddled masses - thus lifting all boats.

Congress has every right to keep our borders secure to prevent illegal immigration. What is simply a security concern should not be confused with immigration policy. However, when a security policy has the byproduct of reducing the availability of immigrant labor, a complimentary policy is necessary to rebalance this force with greater channels for legal immigration. As bad as it may be for America’s security to have porous boundaries, it is far worse for those innocents who are left to traverse the desert in search of a better life.

To respond to the Buchanan nationalists, we will spread American ideals by allowing more people into our country. Those who immigrate to America believe in the American dream and can re-energize our commitment to our ideals. Assimilation to America does not mean eating hotdogs, practicing Christianity, and living in suburbia - it means a commitment to liberty, individual rights, and equality of opportunity. It is true that immigration quotas must be set at a reasonable balance, but many economic indicators point to an optimal level greater than it is now. In states with high levels of immigration, growth has remained high, unemployment is low, and the minimum wage commanded by market forces is generally greater than the national average, due to the positive effect that immigration has on labor demand.

I do not necessarily advocate tearing down the physical barrier that divides the United States from its southern neighbor, but the legal barriers should have a good sledgehammer taken to them. In 1987, President Reagan proclaimed in Berlin, “if you seek peace, if you seek prosperity for the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, if you seek liberalization: Come here to this gate! Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate! Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!” Twenty years later in debating immigration policy, America should follow its own advice.
