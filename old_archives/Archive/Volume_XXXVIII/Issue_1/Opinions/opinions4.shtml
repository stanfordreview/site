Editor’s Note: A Renewed Commitment

by Luukas Ilves

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

This past October, President Hennessy announced the Stanford Challenge, a 4.1-billion dollar push to, in his words, “galvanize the Stanford community to meet the commitment made by Jane and Leland Stanford “to promote the public welfare by exercising an influence on behalf of humanity and civilization” - to forge new academic understanding, but also to educate students to be leaders in this ever-changing world.

Stanford already leads the world in the strength of its dynamic and creative inter-disciplinary programs. Not only do Stanford’s students graduate with a world-class education, Stanford leads the world in imparting a spirit of service – Stanford students are constantly engaged not only in scholarly enterprise but in community service with countless non-profits, NGOs, foundations, and planning and implementing their own initiatives.

Yet, if Stanford students are to become global leaders, they must leave here with more than just the technical skills and experience they need to affect change. They must leave with a sense of their place in the world and the ends to which they wish to effect this change. They must be able to answer not only the question of how, but also what and, most importantly, why.

In order to address these questions, Stanford graduates will need to participate in public discourse, synthesize conclusions from many different sources, types and disciplines of information, and act upon these conclusions. The ability to participate in sustained and open dialogue, and combine different types of information into a coherent picture, are acquired skills, but they cannot be learned in the classroom. They come from experience, from having participated in such dialogue and being socialized into an environment that values these discussions and questions and imparts a sense of their relevance. If Stanford students are to become leaders in local, national, and global marketplaces of ideas, then their exposure to a marketplace of ideas must begin with their Stanford education.

Unfortunately, Stanford students’ lives currently lack such intellectual engagement. Long and involved dinner table discussions, late night bull sessions on the meaning of life, back and forth repartees between different student publications, public student debates, student activists and volunteers critically evaluating their civic engagement, a true love of learning outside of the classroom and the lab – these are all too rare at Stanford today.

The Review’s Role

Within a general milieu of increasing intellectual engagement, the Review has a distinct role to play. We are the skeptic and the gadfly, the intrepid critic who shines a light into the darker crevasses of university life.

The Review and indeed all who care about discourse on campus bear a burden heavier than the past’s. For much of its life, the Review has created debate through a dialectic. We have responded to left-wing hot air and senselessness with good arguments, we have shown people a different, wiser side to the pressing issues of the day. Today, however, we often lack a thesis contra which to present an antithesis. On important matters like the presence of ROTC recruiters, our humanities core curriculum, the administration’s opacity, the role of ethnic identity should play in a university, etc., people do not hold extreme positions – they simply lack positions altogether, or hold weak and uncertain positions.

If the university is akin to John Stuart Mill’s marketplace of ideas, then we once argued for the unpopular but ultimately right ideas whose existence Mill so vehemently supported. We now face a far more formidable task – to create a marketplace of ideas where there is none. We must not merely enjoin students to take more informed positions on politics and university policy, we must enjoin them to take positions at all. To this end, the goal of the Review is not necessarily to bring over all our readers to the conservative side or get everyone to agree with us (though that would be nice!). Rather, we seek intellectual engagement. We would much rather face thoughtful and intellectual leftists, progressives, greens, and Democrats, with thoughtful and intelligent ideas, than none at all. By the same token, we would rather the Stanford Progressive and the Roosevelt Institution were powerful engines of debate and policy proposal than not at all.

Our engagement with the intellectual will always be on a pragmatic and tangible level. The Review is not a journal of abstract philosophical or literary thought. Our activities on campus have always occurred on a target level, engaging with specific policies and specific practices. We apply our ideologies and our beliefs to university policies and campus politics, and take sides. We have always cast a skeptical eye upon ethnic and gender identity-based politics and self-identification, including ethnic theme dorms and identity-based student groups; upon restrictions of student speech, including of “offensive” speech; upon an over-politicization of university policy, including banning ROTC recruiters; upon multiculturalism, including frivolous courses and attacks upon a liberal education in Western culture; upon financial largesse, including gargantuan spending projects and high tuition; and upon an opaque administration, including an authoritarian University president and incompetent mid-level administrators. Most of these standpoints are unlikely to change. Though they are not catechismal statements of faith, they ground our location on the university’s intellectual spectrum and help form our intellectual pedigree.

Volume XXXVIII

We practice an applied intellectualism. The ways in which Volume 38 will engage with our campus derive directly from our motto of “Fiat Lux.”

We will shine a light onto our campus. A few areas will interest us in particular. We will investigate the inner workings of the Stanford administration and how our university is run, with an eye to how students can increase their voice in the governance process. We will document the roadblocks Stanford throws up that impede student groups, inhibit our freedom, and help make Stanford a nanny state. We will continue to expose ideology, partisanship, and politicization in student groups and the administration, and we will investigate both the good and the bad of how Stanford connects to larger national and international affairs.

As we look at the problems of today, we will do so with a view to the past. Many of the conflicts of the past twenty years remain active issues today, and we will do well to remember the arguments of yore in formulating today’s. We hope that this perspective will make for a richer debate, and a more considered tomorrow.

Though our goal may be enlightenment, the “fiat” is as important an aspect of our creed. It conveys the active nature of our task. Bringing about greater debate and intellectual engagement requires not just words, but deeds.

We will be looking for ways to actively encourage debate. Within the pages of the Review, we will now have a regular feature titled “Friendly Fire”, in which two writers will take opposing stances on a pressing issue of the day and attack each others’ arguments head on. We will also sponsor debate outside of our pages. We hope to organize several debates between prominent conservatives at Stanford with their leftist counterparts. We will help organize and participate in student panels and debates. We also will sponsor other events on campus that will challenge the prevailing intellectual orthodoxy. For example, this past Wednesday, we co-sponsored a showing of “Obsession”, an award-winning film about radical Islam’s war against the West.

None of what we will accomplish in the next Volume will have been possible without the Review’s incredibly capable and dedicated staff. We owe a particular debt of gratitude to Ryan Tracey, the previous Editor-in-Chief, who has done a tremendous job building momentum, recruiting new writers, increasing the quality and regularity of our publication, and bringing about important changes in the behind-the-scenes functioning of our publication. I won’t dwell on our writers, whose contributions you can see for yourself on our pages, but I would like to thank those who make the Review run behind the scenes. Our layout team, distribution team, copy editor, and business team are indispensable in ensuring our writing makes it to your eyes. And, finally, I thank you for your attention and your consideration. It is only with the thoughtfulness of our readers that our renewed commitment can bring about lasting change.

Fiat Lux!

Luukas Ilves
Editor in Chief
