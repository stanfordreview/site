Ralph Nader: Exclusive Interview

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The Stanford Review: Are there any cultural or social pre-conditions to participatory democracy?

Ralph Nader: Yes, there have to be. We’re fairly deficient. First, there is very little continuity in our parental cultures. The family is under tremendous pressures – time pressure, space pressure, commercial pressure. Children are watching far too much TV. They’re not socializing or connecting with their neighborhoods. When you add it up, computers, screens, and video and television, you’re at about six seven hours a day for the average family.
Second, you have a dearth of civic skills. Granted, you learn civics in school. But that is all rote memorization. Civic skills entail connecting the classroom to the community and learning things such as how you use the Freedom of Information Act act, how you build coalitions, how you steel yourself from being demoralized, and how you tackle a real-life problem.

SR: You certainly participate in the civic life of your country. Is there a certain ethos you have to cultivate as an outspoken and often unpopular public figure? Does your distance from the mainstream bring with it any challenges?

RN: The views that I advance are majoritarian views. They’re just not corporatist views. So when you fight for a genuine universal health care plan that the HMOs and the hospital chains don’t like, who is mainstream and who isn’t?

People are also very demoralized. They don’t enough of their public institutions and get cynical, withdraw, and don’t vote or show up at hearings and councils, and so public institutions are turned against them by people who do show up, largely corporate interests.

The problem starts with low expectations. You have, in contrast, high expectations of how a car should be produced. That moves right into a critique of the auto companies. That moves into the regulatory arena, the product liability, motorists’ tastes and how much they would like in a car.

SR: You’ve often been characterized as anti-globalization because globalization favors corporate interests, yet globalization is often characterized as lowering trade barriers, which helps consumers by lowering prices. You’re a big consumer advocate; how do you reconcile these two interests?

RN: It’s corporate globalization. There could be a thing known as democratic or civic globalization. The trade agreements we have now are basically drafted by corporate lawyers, lobbied into facilitative administrations and straight through congress, with no amendments and a simple up or down vote. They are systems of governance. They are hundreds of pages long, they are not free trade in any pristine sense, because if they were free trade, it would only take one page. They are corporate manuscripts, and all those pages contain the rules. And who writes the rules? It’s not workers, environmentalists, or consumers.

What you have is this brilliant, ingenious scheme of transnational governance that increasingly bypasses our own democratic institutions like legislators, regulatory agencies, and courts.

You have this situation where these trade agreements are pull-down agreements. You don’t get in trouble with the WTO as a nation if you mistreat your workers or allow a lot of pollution. You don’t get in trouble if you raise standards of pollution control, labor standards and then are accused of violating the WTO agreement. Most of the reporters including Tom Friedman never read these agreements, they read the summaries, and have an Econ 101 knowledge, you know, Portuguese wine for British wool, from the 18th century. It’s absolute advantage now - it’s not comparative advantage! You can go to China with capital, intellectual property, managerial know-how, and dirt cheap labor that works its head off. So it isn’t wine for textiles, but students are not learning this, it’s a new ball game. And it’s severely destructive of a democracy because it pulls higher standards of protection countries down lower. Example: when we were fighting WTO, it had a provision that said that the only thing that you could challenge as a signatory nation is the way a product is produced, which is defined as prison labor. So you could say “We don’t want any prison labor products from Indonesia”, for example. Child labor is acceptable, in fact if we tried to pass a law prohibit products made by child labor abroad, it would violate the WTO. So you see the pull-down nature of it? Because you can’t buy U.S. child labor products because it’s illegal here.

You can’t believe how many professors of economics have bought this propaganda, probably because they don’t see it as a system of enforceable governance but as knocking down trade barriers. It also extends the IP monopoly of patents and copyrights, which isn’t exactly free trade. But it is a brilliant conceptual system of control that completely leapfrogged standards that we built up like regulatory agencies and fair labor laws, so you can’t improve upon it. You try to get a bill through congress or Sacramento and they say “No, that violates the WTO.” After a while, the chilling factor leads good legislators to not even try because the US trade rep says “NO, you’re going to lose in Geneva, why try?”

SR: Many analysts point to the US economy’s strong performance – low inflation, low unemployment, high growth. What do you think the causes of this are?

RN: If I told you you wouldn’t get into Stanford unless you were in the top 10% of High School students who could get on their knees and push a beach-ball down a field faster than anyone else, what do you think all the kids who wanted to go to Stanford would be doing in their senior year?

Whoever controls the yardstick controls the agenda. So when Greenspan and Bernanke say the economy is in good shape, they are using business indicators. They aren’t saying the economy is in poor shape because there are fifty million people living in poverty and thirteen million children going hungry, and unaffordable housing and huge consumer debt. It is sheer propaganda saying that “GDP is going up, corporate profits are going up.” They are embarrassed, but they can’t say that labor and real wages are going up, because the top year in real wages for majority of workers in this country was still ’73, inflation adjusted.

It’s now getting close, but it’s about time. They don’t say that one out of three workers makes less than $10.50 an hour. The economy is in terrible shape for the majority of people; it’s in terrific shape for people who don’t care about their descendents and are in the top 10% of the income and wealth.

SR: Many of your concerns clearly require a political solution. What strategies would a thirty party need to adopt to gain real power given our electoral system?

RN: First, they need to be able to recruit thousands of young people – people in their 20s are the ones who change societies. On the local level, you can really win on the first round – there are two and a half million elective offices in the US, and a lot of them, like school boards, city councils, don’t have any opponents to the incumbent. And you move up to the state and national level.

Whether you engage in this struggle comes down to your own self-respect. Do you just want to make a pile of money and stock options and spoil your descendents? Stanford has a poor environment in this respect because so many here have hit it big – there’s almost a gold rush, a Google rush.

But you must ask: “Who is doing the most important work in the world?” – it is not corporations. Talk about famines and infectious diseases, and how the drug companies evade their responsibilities. If you talk about arms control, just list the main problems in the world – we have a ridiculous mis-deployment of brainpower in our country. We should have no famine etc., we have so much know-how and wealth, but too much greed.
