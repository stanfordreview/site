Secret Democracy

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Students recently decided their dorms’ publications distribution policy—door-to-door or centralized. Didn’t know? That’s because ResEd didn’t tell you, and continues to resist openness.

Last year, The Stanford Review went through a protracted battle with the Stanford administration and Residential Education over the door-to-door distribution policy for Stanford publications. For years, the university had propounded a confusing and inconsistent policy that allowed Resident Fellows and dorm staff to decide their dorm’s policy without needing to solicit student opinions. In most instances, small publications were forced to leave their publications in a stack in dorm lounges, from which they were cleaned away by custodians or students. In effect, small student publications like the Progressive, Review, or Chaparral couldn’t make it to their audience.

After months of wrangling and negotiating, the Review finally brokered a compromise solution. The administration would require every dorm to vote on whether they wanted door-to-door distribution for student publications, either by an online polling system or in their house meetings. Residential Education (ResEd) would also install individual dorm mailboxes so that student publications could still distribute to students in dorms that voted against door-to-door.

Unfortunately, The Review quickly discovered this fall that the administration and ResEd had simply dropped the ball and failed to uphold their end of the bargain. Students were able to log-on to the ResEd website and vote until October 13, but they received information on their ability to vote from precious few sources. The ignorance of student voters underlined a larger truth, that there had been nearly no advertising of the vote. With some exceptions, RAs did not send out emails to their residents. The turnout, thus, came as little surprise. 678 Stanford students voted, around 10 percent of the on-campus student body. Of 65 dorms, more than half – 33 – had just zero to five votes (see the table for some notable dorms).

The dorms that voted in particularly high numbers had a particular reason for doing so. Junipero, a freshman dorm in Wilbur, which had 60 votes, representing nearly two thirds of the dorm’s population. Lasse Thorenfeldt, a member of the staff at Junipero, explained to the Review: “I think we probably got the highest rate because we actually told residents several times over email that they needed to complete the vote. Nothing more involved than that.” A number of the other dorms with high turnout, including Rinconada, Soto, Serra, and Twain, received visits from Review staff members and further encouragement to vote over email lists.

The outcome in Roble is perhaps most notable of all. Roble had long been the icon of opposition to door-to-door distribution. The Resident Fellow at Roble, Christine Gabali, had in past years maintained a ban on door-to-door distribution in Roble. When asked why she didn’t allow Roble students to vote, she famously responded: “Voting is wonderful. It’s a great right we’re given. But sometimes voting is unnecessary — like, let’s vote if we should eat or not — it’s common sense.” This year, the tables turned on Gabali: Roble had the highest number of votes – 69 (which is made more impressive by the fact that Roble is half empty due to construction). And 64% of Roble students voted in favor of door-to-door distribution. Among the factors that contributed to the outcome in Roble were the continued efforts of former Review executive editor Milton Solorzano. Milton patiently spoke to individual students and sent many emails to his dorm listserv. He would explain that for small publications, door-to-door is the best way to reach students, and noted that those who were irked by receiving a publication under their door could simply “take it and put it in the recycling bin—not that big of a problem.”

Unfortunately, dorms like Roble were the exception—one, two or three were the most common number of votes—hardly a democratically representative outcome. And the low turnout is compounded by other problems. ResEd gave dorms a choice between voting online or in house meetings, but then seems to have neglected to tell students which policy their dorm was following, and has also failed to collect information from dorms that did vote in their house meetings. This failure to communicate has yielded chaotic outcomes of the kind witnessed in Global Affairs theme dorm Castaño. The ResEd website reports 60% of students voting in favor of door-to-door, but Castaño staff claim they voted for centralized distribution in dorm meetings. Other dorms’ information was similarly not forwarded to ResEd. ResEd director Jane Camarillo claims not to have received information from Florence Moore Freshman dorm Alondra, though Alondra staff confirm that their dorm did in fact vote.

All of this confusion has been further compounded by ResEd’s obscurantism. The specific numbers of votes in individual dorms upon which much of this article is based upon were initially posted on the ResEd website, but have now been taken down and replaced with a simple statement of the outcome of the vote in each dorm. Nowhere does the ResEd website now indicate that only 10% of Stanford voted. But ResEd has gone beyond simply covering up information—it seems to have used this cover-up to wantonly manipulate the outcome of the vote. Initially, ResEd posted results and policies for each individual Row House (notably, nobody in the French house voted). The majority of Row houses had voted for door-to-door, albeit with low turnout. Now, however, the ResEd website simply lists “The Row” as a residence and door-to-door distribution as its policy.

It’s not clear why ResEd has acted with so little transparency. It’s doubtful that they have a hidden anti-door-to-door agenda. After all, a number of the questionable decisions are against centralized distribution, including the listing of Castaño and the entire row as door-to-door. But your concern should not be with the specific outcome of the vote, but the transgression against the student body inherent in the poor turnout numbers. Under the guise of democracy, the informed and interested few have decided for everyone. This leaves students feeling frustrated and exasperated.

The only tenable solution to this problem is to hold another vote at the beginning of next quarter. After all, with 10% turnout from ignorance, voter fatigue will hardly be a problem. This time, ResEd needs to make a concerted effort to advertise the vote, which actually shouldn’t be too difficult. A few short reminders to RAs to send out emails to their dorm email lists, prominent fliers, an announcement in the Daily, would at least give student the information to freely decide whether or not to vote and then vote wisely. Until then, students are free to decide their dorm’s distribution policy in form, but not in truth.
