Eliminated in the Playoffs

by John Komkov

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Having played high school football in Texas, I can tell you that one of the first things a prospective player will hear before his first practice before his first game before he first breaks through a butcher-paper pep banner is that if he hasn’t got something to bring to the table, it’s better that he doesn’t show up at all.

With the premier of NBC’s Friday Night Lights on October 3rd, I have the rare opportunity of justifiably applying this same rubric of coach-logic to prime time television. Set in the fictional town of Dillon, FNL centers on a community that, you guessed it, eats, drinks and breathes the gridiron pastime. Stars on the Panthers squad are regaled as heroes as they mosey to and from their remedial classes during the school day and to and from local burger joints and tract houses at night.

Jason Street, Dillon’s proud quarterback whose wardrobe knows little other than wingtip boots, Wranglers and a bulky letter jacket, neatly splits off-field time between sating his main squeeze Lyla and maintaining an endearing “aww shucks” persona in his interactions with the town’s older residents. Even Coach Eric Taylor, the new guy on the block with the odds stacked against him to “win or else…”, seems to know his place well. Accordingly, he tugs just enough of his forelock while in the presence of the mighty University of Texas coach, Mack Brown, so as to appear humble, and speaks with just enough of the straight-edged twang of an established field general that the parents of Dillon know that he knows his stuff.

I approached Friday Night Lights expecting some clichés. After all, when we consider the genre of sports dramas, certain plot dynamics are best kept constant. I knew before I turned on the show that there would be a Jason, the established Captain of the team with the integrity of five boy scouts rolled into one, and a Matt Saracen, the likable but jittery backup quarterback, and even a Gaius “Smash” Williams, the enigmatic African-American running back who is the thankless dynamo of the squad. Moreover, I knew that as far as many of the other characters go, the writers could only play around with a limited number of variables before the show would start to lose credibility. When we’re talking high school football in Texas, your “Coach Taylor” figure better match one of a few archetypes. You can have your bible-beating good ol’ boy with the perfect scripture for every situation, you can have your psychotic performance-at-all-costs drillmaster or, in the case of Friday Night Lights, you can have the vulnerable outsider come to town oblivious to the full spectrum of expectations that lord over his livelihood. Sure it’s a bit trite, but it’s understandable.

What irked me instead were the characters that the staff writers took the liberty to invent in full. Julie, for instance, is Coach Taylor’s quick-mouthed daughter always ready with an analogy for how football is a lot like Moby Dick or a diatribe for how in the end, “it’s just a game”. Granted, the “Julies” of Dillon are few and far between, but given that the show’s creators were willing to experiment with the small-town docudrama formula even a little, it’s frustrating that they did so in such saccharine, hollow ways.

Thematically, too, the show often seems underdeveloped and even pre-packaged. The first episode portends racial tension between the team’s Black and Anglo players. The strain takes the, again, predictable form of shoving matches prior to practices and cheap shots during tackling drills. I won’t pretend that there aren’t such clashes in actuality, but I will insist that the producers are skipping over a much more significant subplot in the drama of Texas football by opting for the more nationally recognized Black/White anxiety. Rather, West Texas football teams typically have large numbers of Hispanic players, and the truer conflict involves this demographic. Again, while these small points don’t necessarily ruin the show, they certainly nag the more knowledgeable of viewers. In its own right, Friday Night Lights is enjoyable, but as it pertains to be based on truth, it could be much more powerful if it pursued that truth at the expense of the safer modes “expected truth”. To someone in New York, a football team full of Hispanics may not seem plausible, but the scenario is very often so.

Still, while the show caters to industry standards outside of the stadium, the on-field action is superb. Several other football programs, including Remember the Titans and the cinematic version of Friday Night Lights, have tried to accurately capture the essence of the high school game. Sadly, the action sequences in these productions are typically sensationalistic and choreographed. Blood-and-flesh 17-year-olds don’t have the physical coordination and poise of NFL superstars, and this is a fact that directors have largely ignored in the past. One need only watch the final game portrayed in Remember the Titans to see high school football at its over-produced worst. Every hit is a bone-crunching spear tackle, each run ducks and weaves through falling linemen for at minimum thirty yards, and quarterbacks release every pass in coiled spirals set dead-on for the back corner of the end zone. Friday Night Lights does things better by showing more how play actually unfolds. It is more common to see a lanky fullback pronate his way to a three yard gain than it is to see a lean powerhouse break linebackers like balsawood, and the show’s action coordinators account for this. As long as you are willing to deal with a bit of rumble-camera—my term for the new television norm to shake the camera during quick-moving sequences—the game footage is highly enjoyable.

All in all, Friday Night Lights has quite a bit to offer provided you approach it the right way. Drawing back to the nervous player sitting in his first team meeting, a good coach understands that the kid needn’t be the next Deion Sanders to be put to efficient use. He can just as effectively be a solid journeyman—someone who learns his position well and crafts his art to something not quite high enough to be called greatness but something far from being mediocre. Despite Friday Night Light’s writer Peter Berg’s indication there will be no actual football footage in the shows next few episodes, I still feel it has the potential to be, in a coach’s words, “a good utility player”. So for the next few months, when you find yourself with nothing to do early on a Tuesday evening, stop by at NBC and enjoy a perfectly entertaining drama with perfectly entertaining plots, dialogue and characters. But know that this same perfection belies truth, and know that higher forms of accomplishment do not come from flawless reiteration but from subtle changes to the forms themselves.

Late in the first episode, Julie consoles her nervous father by reminding him that things won’t be any different if Dillon doesn’t win the state championship and by criticizing Dillon’s citizens for insisting that they will stand for nothing less. People, she says, can still take joy in watching the game and people can certainly take joy in experiences that fall somewhere short of what they had originally hoped.

One day, someone will make a documentary chronicling Texas football for what it is, but it is doubtful that many people outside the state will really appreciate it. Instead, chock up Friday Night Lights’ run as another successful shortcoming—one that doesn’t quite make it to the championship game, but plays a brand of football much more exciting to the general spectator along the way.

“Friday Night Lights” airs on Tuesdays, 8PM EST/5PM PST on NBC.
