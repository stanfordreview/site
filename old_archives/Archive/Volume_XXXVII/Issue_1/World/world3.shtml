Is Peace Possible?

by Tucker Herbert

World Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

This has not been the most serene of summers on the global political stage. A massive terrorist plot uncovered in London has added one more security check to students’ flights back to school this fall; Israel deflected a double front of terrorist incursions from the Gaza Strip and Lebanon as Syria threatened war and Iran sustained its pursuit of nuclear technology; a coordinated attack on the mass transit system of Mumbai, India reeked of al-Qaeda; Thailand suffered continuing attacks in its far south; and prolonged UN flaccidity has emboldened the Sudanese government in its continuing oppression of minorities in its western province of Darfur.

The most extreme Islamofascists strive for nothing less than the elimination of the infidel and a world order under Shriara law. In such a world, is peace possible? Ultimately there are only three possible resolutions to such a situation: one, the Islamofascists succeed, two, they are eliminated, or three, they learn to live in peace on the same planet as Christians, Jews, Hindus, Buddhists, and the other groups with which they are now at war.

The question is how to achieve that third and most desirable outcome. The largest conflict this summer, that surrounding Israel, did not turn out well. Its parallels to the larger war on terror do not bode well for a quick solution to the world’s problems. Like the war on terror that the United States faces, Hamas and Hezbollah employed terrorist methods to target civilians in Israel, and there is an absence of solutions through traditional diplomacy. Terrorists are free to use all their might to cause destruction but the West must use restraint with regard to civilian casualties in order to not lose the public relations battle. The use of human shields was extremely successful for Hezbollah in its recent confrontation with Israel, and the West should be prepared for the repeated use of this maneuver. It is the epitome of irony that such a tactic helps groups such as Hezbollah to win the public relations war. The burden to prevent civilian casualties is placed entirely on Western forces. Hezbollah targets the West’s own values of human rights by subjecting the public to images of dead bodies that Hezbollah placed in harms way.

The resolve of Muslim fundamentalists appears to be getting stronger rather than weaker, and their base of popular support appears to be expanding rather than contracting. Hezbollah fills the dual roll of terrorist group and aid organization as it steps in to provide food and medical assistance in the aftermath of the bombings that they provoked. The West has had more difficulty spinning its actions in the Middle East. The Iraq War has not fashioned the beacon of freedom that neo-cons sought to establish. Nor have attempts at democracy in the Middle East produced the results that would have been desired.

Iran poses the most significant threat to the West, because the terrorist groups that it funds are ready and willing to use all the firepower available to them to maximize human casualties. The weapons technologies that Iran develops as well as its oil revenues are routinely transferred to Hezbollah et al. Iran however, is not held accountable for the actions of these proxy militias. Israel knows this, and logically concludes that a nuclear Iran would pose a grave threat. The principle of mutually assured destruction has helped to prevent the use of nuclear weapons in the post-WWII period. But if Hezbollah were to use a nuclear weapon against Israel, Hezbollah would not face the same retaliation as would a nation-state. The dispersion of Hezbollah throughout Lebanon would ensure that the group could not be destroyed without the loss of millions of innocent civilian lives. Likewise, the government of Iran could claim absolution from guilt because, after all, they do not have full control over Hezbollah’s actions.

The West cannot solve these issues one at a time, and must instead keep a broader approach in mind while addressing each individual crisis. The mission in Iraq has failed thus far in part because of the persisting instability of its neighbors. The current regimes in Syria and Iran impede the peaceful coexistence of factions in Iraq by supporting extremists. The Taliban may be rebuilding its strength in Afghanistan after an extended vacation in northern Pakistan. Terrorist organizations are not constrained by national borders—but as simple as that fact is, it is not yet fully reflected in the tactics of the war on terror.

Neither force nor ideology alone can solve the tensions surrounding the Middle East. That being said, your columnist feels at a loss for a concrete solution, but believes that the cure will be discovered in moderate Muslim states. It is not clear what the X factor is that sets these states apart, but more work should be done to foster its growth. Turkey, Malaysia, and Dubai have all exhibited significant progress toward development of a peaceful society that would rather do business with the West than see it destroyed. The idea would be to replicate that priority in the rest of the region.
