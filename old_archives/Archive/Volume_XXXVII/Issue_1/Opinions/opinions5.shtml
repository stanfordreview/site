Editor's Note: The New, Improved Review

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The staff of The Stanford Review labored hard throughout the summer in order to debut an excitingly new publication. The Review’s layout, distribution plan, website, and internal organization have all undergone great changes to appeal to a wider audience from within the Stanford community.

Late last year, the administrative staff of The Stanford Review made the decision to revitalize the publication after realizing the coming change in the university’s distribution policy this fall would expose the paper’s content to a significantly larger population of students. An aesthetically enticing layout, a stronger organization, engaging content, and an interactive website would be crucial to retain the attention of this new audience.

Omkar Muralidharan, Managing Editor for The Stanford Review, subsequently designed the new layout for this volume. Inspired by The Times of London, the new look grants readers easy access to a lot of information from just the front page while allocating room for a large photograph to accompany the issue’s flagship story. New decks of text beneath each headline allow readers to quickly understand the content of the underlying stories. Furthermore, a more compact size makes the new Review more transportable, taking the hassle out of bringing a Review home and helping better conceal the popular practice of reading newspapers during dull lectures. A new printer will present the new Review in more vibrant colors and on higher-quality paper.

In addition to the new look, for the first time in quite awhile, The Review will be publishing on a weekly schedule. In an effort to become Stanford’s “Weekend Newspaper,” our staff is ambitiously planning to publish a Review almost every Friday during this coming volume. Students and Stanford community members can now expect interesting and engaging reading content during their weekends, when The Stanford Daily does not publish.

To support many of these changes, the Board of The Stanford Review made a unique decision to retain the leadership of The Review’s organization over the summer and through another volume. That’s why, unlike previous volumes, readers will not find a welcome message from a new Editor-in-Chief in this first issue. The continuity in leadership allowed The Review to lay the groundwork for this volume early last spring. The leadership has been able to spend time on improving and optimizing many of the processes that drive The Review, instead of working on learning them from the beginning.

Despite all of this change, the principles and vision of The Stanford Review remain the same. The new look, weekly distribution, and retained organization are all part of a broader effort to facilitate a dialogue between students. The chief aim of The Stanford Review is still to provide an alternative viewpoint to that which is generally heard on campus. By reading the paper, students will hopefully feel compelled to reexamine and challenge their own viewpoints while gaining a better awareness of the important issues in the modern world. A new forum established on The Stanford Review’s website will now allow readers to engage in an active discussion on these issues.

My hope is that these changes will not only help foster the political dialectic on campus, but also work to enhance The Review’s credibility. For far too long, students and faculty at Stanford have mistakenly judged The Review as a political propaganda piece. Critics gloss over the well-researched investigative news articles and insightful opinions pieces that frequently fill our pages. Many of these detractors do not even read the paper—they simply consider our viewpoints to be offensive to modern academia. Too many people are beginning to believe that there is only one respectable side to an issue. At a university, more than anywhere else, people need to respect the beliefs of others. Yet, nowadays, the university is one of the least likely places to find a good representation of both sides of an issue. That needs to change.

With any luck, big pictures and colorful headlines will prompt open-minded students and faculty to pick our paper up and judge us by the quality of our content. I foresee a great opportunity for The Review to regain legitimacy and truly make a difference at Stanford with the coming volume.

Fiat Lux!

Ryan Tracey
Editor-in-Chief
