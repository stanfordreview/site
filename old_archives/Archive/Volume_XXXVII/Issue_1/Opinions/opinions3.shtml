Door-to-Door Critical for Publications

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In the coming weeks, Stanford undergraduate students in each dorm must vote on whether student publications can distribute to their doorstep.

A vote in favor of door-to-door distribution in residences could dramatically enhance the readership and relevance of small student publications at Stanford. For several years, small publications have struggled with a prohibition of door-to-door distribution enforced by many Resident Fellows (RFs). The prohibition forced many publications to resort to the use of centralized distribution locations, which students often overlook.

The new university policy on the door-to-door distribution of periodicals takes the decision-making power away from the RFs, allowing residents to decide the policy. Each residence on-campus will be able to decide their policy through a majority vote, conducted through an online system.

The vote on door-to-door distribution resulted from an immense effort over the past few years from student publications and the ASSU Senate. Although various student journalists have made door-to-door distribution an issue for many years, the most coordinated effort began in April 2005, when a measure on the ASSU ballot gauged that the undergraduate population would favor a democratic vote on the issue. Subsequently, several ASSU senators worked to get Residential Education (ResEd) to change their policy to reflect student desires. A refusal to comply prompted The Stanford Review and other organizations to discuss an alternative method for instigating a change in the policy.

Ultimately, The Stanford Review decided to resort to civil disobedience. On January 21, 2006, several staff members of the paper openly violated university policy by distributing papers in dormitories that did not allow door-to-door distribution. After the university indicted and tried The Review for its violation, members of The Review threatened a lawsuit for a targeted violation of free speech. The impending lawsuit prompted the establishment an active dialogue between administrators and student publications that led to the university’s realization that a change in policy was necessary.

Although publications like The Stanford Review originally desired a policy allowing door-to-door distribution in every dorm, without any voting processes, university administrators insisted that dorms must retain the right to opt-out. Eventually, a compromise was reached, which put the measure to vote among student residents and provided for the construction of personalized mailboxes for distribution for dorms that declined to allow door-to-door. The university also agreed to help facilitate the distribution of the first issue of every small publication, along with education information pertaining to the door-to-door distribution vote.

The Stanford Review anticipates support from students for door-to-door distribution, as they believe the benefits of such a system greatly outweigh the inconvenience. Most of the complaints against door-to-door revolve the potential clutter that might accumulate if students refuse to pick-up papers in front of their doorsteps. Many opponents, in fact, simply argue that it should not be their responsibility to pick-up an unsolicited newspaper at their doorstep. Other arguments usually involve fire and safety hazards that are often not adequately substantiated.

On the other hand, the extensive benefits of door-to-door distribution of small student publications have been well established and thoroughly proven. Before RFs began enforcing a ban on door-to-door distribution in 2003, small student publications were able to deliver to nearly every room on-campus. Content was directly available for students to discuss, debate, and enjoy from the convenience of their own dorm rooms. Recently, however, with the ban preventing publications from distributing door-to-door in most residences, potential readers have to be constantly on the lookout for new editions at the centralized distribution locations. The stacks of papers in these locations often go untouched. For The Stanford Review, a malicious student or a misinformed custodian often prematurely trashes the stacks of issues.

If dormitories approve door-to-door distribution in the approaching vote, it will only affect student organizations. As before, outside solicitors would not be allowed to distribute within student dormitories. The Stanford Review would be the only weekly publication to distribute door-to-door. The Stanford Progressive, the Chaparral, and other publications would most likely publish and distribute less frequently—consistent with their historical practices. It is also expected that The Stanford Daily will continue to distribute only to centralized locations, as the resources to distribute a daily paper door-to-door would be too great. Additionally, their daily schedule allows them to establish a routine awareness among students as to the location and availability of their newspaper. Small publications’ lack of such awareness is one of the chief reasons door-to-door distribution is such a necessity.

 

Although the democratization of the door-to-door distribution decision is a major step in improving the political dialogue at Stanford University, the cooperation of community members and support of residents will be critical in determining the success of the publication community’s effort. The opportunity for RFs, residence staffs, and upperclassmen to unduly influence the judgment of others is a great risk. If such individuals restrain from abusing their authority, however, the Stanford community can look forward to collectively deciding the fate of an important aspect of academic dialogue in the modern university.
