Journalist Murder Still a Mystery

by Matt Sprague

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Russian investigative journalist Anna Politkovskaya was murdered as she returned to her apartment in Moscow. A prominent journalist and publisher of the Novaya Gazeta, Politkokvskaya was a vocal critic of the current Russian administration and won international awards for her coverage of the current rebellion in Chechnya.

Politkokvskaya’s death has sparked international outrage and prompted Putin to declare “we must be clear that it was a dreadful and unacceptable crime which cannot be allowed to go unpunished.” However, he angered supporters of Politkokvskaya by degrading her work, as he claimed “this murder has done more damage to Russia — and the current authorities of Russia and Chechnya, which she has been covering lately in her work — than Politkovskaya’s articles.” By this, Putin intended to dispel accusations that his government may have ordered the killing. The reasoning is that it would have been detrimental to the Kremlin’s broader interests to eliminate Politkovskaya; while this logic follows, Putin’s condolences are nevertheless bitter-cold. The Russian government has been at the center of much controversy in recent years, with many suspecting them to be responsible for assassination attempts in Eastern Europe, ranging from the murdering of journalists to the unsuccessful poisoning of Ukrainian President Viktor Yushchenko.

The E.U. has declared its intent to raise the issue of Politkovskaya’s murder at a summit next week in Finland, which currently holds the E.U. rotating presidency. At a press conference, European affairs minister of Finland, Paula Lehtomaki, was quoted as saying “This is a terrible crime which we call on the Russian authorities thoroughly to investigate. We want the perpetrators to be brought to justice, and this is a particularly important test of the rule of law in Russia.”

In what has recently become an alarming trend in Russia, Politkovskaya’s death is the third such high profile assassination this month alone. A quick scan of the Committee to Protect Journalists website, cpj.org, will reveal that since Putin’s assumption of the Russian presidency, over 14 journalists have been abducted or murdered, and none of these cases have been resolved. Many of these journalists had been critical of Russian policies.

Because of this string of journalist-icide, many do not believe that Russia’s police force is putting sufficient effort into investigating these unsolved mysteries or protecting journalists from harm. In a memo on CPJ’s website about the 2004 murder of Forbes’ Russia editor, Paul Klebnikov, CPJ Executive Director Ann Cooper stated “we are hoping for justice in this case but have no way of evaluating the proceedings…Russian authorities are asking everyone to trust them, but their record gives no basis for such faith.” Mishandling and false leads have plagued Russian investigations into the deaths and kidnappings of journalists.

All this added to the recent string of intimidation endured by journalists, from the jailing of German student journalists on the eve of the G8 summit, visas denied to foreign journalists, the many prosecutions for “criminal insults” of Russian government officials, and numerous other mistreatment cases have led critics to suspect that the Russian government itself, or at least rogue parts of it, are responsible for this plague of journalist abuse.

These recent killings are not limited to journalists, either; on October 11th, a Russian bank manager, Alexander Plokhin, was assassinated in what appears to be another contracted murder. This follows the death of Russian Central Bank Chief, Andrei Kozlov, just a month earlier.

Regardless of who is responsible for these deaths, Russia is feeling the heat of the international community and may soon suffer the economic consequences of its relative lawlessness. Attracting foreign capital and investment becomes a more difficult task with the specter of these murders hanging over Russia. The assassinations have caused Russia to lose much respect, legitimacy, and authority to the world and its own citizens, and Russia’s response to this issue will certainly have an impact on the nations’s future standing in the world.
