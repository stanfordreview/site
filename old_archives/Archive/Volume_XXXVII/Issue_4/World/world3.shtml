A Bolder Edo

by Stuart Baimel

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) has ruled Japan consecutively, except for a few months, since the establishment of democracy after World War Two. As such, most politicking in Japan takes place intra-party, rather than between the LDP and the weak and divided opposition. After five years of economic and organizational reform, Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi stepped down to be replaced by cabinet secretary, Shinzo Abe. The succession battle was surprisingly orderly; Abe easily beat Taro Aso, the foreign minister, and Sadakazu Tanigaki, the finance minister.

Koizumi’s term in office was marked by reform and the first glimmers of economic growth in an economy that had suffered in a liquidity trap for fifteen years. His main policy legacy will be the reform of the postal-savings institution, a combination post office and gargantuan national bank, which will be privatized over a long-term horizon. But Koizumi also made his mark on the foreign policy front by drawing closer with the United States, campaigning for a permanent seat on the Security Council, and instituting a hard-line stance versus North Korea. It is likely that Abe will continue these policies, although he will not immediately have the personal rapport with President Bush that Koizumi did. What is less clear, however, is whether Abe will continue Koizumi’s economic reforms. Abe’s budgetary and fiscal policy team is a mix of old conservatives wary of reform and younger reformers eager for change; it remains to be seen what policy will emerge from this combination.

Abe, arguably, will face the most challenges on foreign policy matters. First and foremost, Abe is a staunch supporter of visits to the Yasukuni shrine, a private Shinto temple that enshrines 14 Class A Japanese war criminals. This, naturally, angers China and South Korea, victims of egregious abuses by the Japanese from the early 1920’s to 1945, and strains relations at a time of growing threats from North Korea. Interestingly, North Korea’s recent nuclear test has had the positive externality of drawing China and Japan closer for the first time in years. This is not far removed from violent riots outside the Japanese embassy in China, and marks a slight bit of luck for Japan when the country faces huge national-security risks from a nuclear and belligerent North Korea.

The largest complaint from South Korea and China regarding Japan is that the country has never entirely “atoned” for its crimes during the first half of the twentieth century. Japan never faced the piecemeal occupation that Germany did, and was quickly allowed to reindustrialize, and became the centerpiece of American strategic interests in East Asia. So while it would be completely unacceptable to Poland and Russia for German leaders to visit shrines featuring Nazis, it is a fact of life that Japanese Prime Ministers will continue to visit the Yasukuni shrine. The People’s Daily compared Koizumi’s insistence on visiting to insisting on eating “rat excrement.” The Japanese public remains ambivalent about the visits but so far has not shown massive public opposition to the continued visits and subsequent hardening of relations with South Korea and China. While Abe will certainly continue the visits—his conservative base necessitates it, North Korea’s belligerence might put this issue on the backburner.

Koizumi also pushed the country into a much larger global rule, including the placement of Japanese troops in Afghanistan. Japan has a “pacifist constitution” that includes a renunciation of war. But Article 5 has come under increasing strain and it is likely that it will be removed during Abe’s tenure. Indeed, before his ascendancy to the premiership Abe commented that he would like to shape a new constitution with his “own hands.” Japan’s evolving security will be likely to accelerate under Abe, even as China grows more and more assertive about its own economic interests. The relationship between China and Japan, rather than China and the U.S., will be the one that shapes the region in the future. Let’s hope that Abe can end, or at least mitigate, what some commentators are already calling a “cold war” between the Land of the Rising Sun and the Middle Kingdom.
