Replacing An Empty Mind With An Open One

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In my Structured Liberal Education (SLE) lectures, I was recently exposed to an interesting Buddhist film that reminded me of a question that I used to ask myself: “Is it possible for a man to love a woman so much that in order to be with her for just this lifetime, he would sacrifice his soul to Hell for an eternity?”

This is a difficult question. As a Christian guy who was once in love with a beautiful, but staunchly atheistic girl, I used to spend hours each day searching for an answer. Although Christianity is a deeply liberal religion that permits miscegenation and even intergenerational love, my home church is dead serious about forbidding believers to marry non-believers. Eventually, I came to the sad but necessary conclusion that for love, one could sacrifice money, time, and perhaps even life itself—but never one’s soul.

Pan Nalin’s film Samsara, set in Tibet, is based on a similarly intriguing premise: A young but devoted Buddhist monk named Tashi (Shawn Ku) faces an inner crisis when he falls madly in love with a beautiful village girl named Pema (Christy Chung). Although Tashi loves the woman deeply, he also knows that human love, being temporal, can only result in the perpetuation of samsara, the endless cycle of rebirth, suffering, and death. Thus, the monk faces a painful choice: He must choose between the pursuit of enlightenment and the pursuit of happiness. This is an intriguing philosophical dilemma.

The film, which centers on the Vajrayana Buddhism that is practiced in Tibet, teaches us several interesting things about the religion. Buddhists believe that suffering is caused by the problem of tanha—the endless desire for things like fame, money, power, and yes, love. In the Buddhist mind, only the elimination of desire can bring about an end to suffering. Therefore, a devout Buddhist will seek to eliminate all his desires, even love, in the name of achieving enlightenment.

Initially, the film starts off on a powerful note. Tashi is shown as a devoted monk, so devoted that he meditates in a cave for three years and nearly dies as a result. And when he meets the beautiful Pema by accident for the first time, the scene is filmed so artfully that the audience can almost feel what it is like to be a lonely, lovesick monk. It is one of the most touching scenes you will ever see on film: Watch it and your ticket would have just paid for itself. Aside from the stunning view of the Tibetan landscape, this would be the biggest reason to watch Samsara.

But despite its lofty intentions, the movie falls short in delivering what it promises. Instead of presenting solely the conflict between spiritual duty and romantic love, the story degenerates into a conflict between spiritual duty and sexual desire—a much less balanced contest. As wonderful as it is, sex does not possess love’s moral weight. And in a serious film like this, moral weight is essential to making the sacrifice of the monk’s soul a noble sacrifice rather than a foolhardy proposition.

In spite of its craftsmanship, the movie does not fully succeed in portraying a genuinely deep relationship between Tashi and Pema. The two fall in love at first sight despite never having had a meaningful conversation. Within fifteen minutes film time of their initial meeting, Tashi has abandoned his monastery and the couple is having passionate sex under a tree. Ten minutes later, they are married and she is pregnant. Even in screen time, ten minutes is a little short to get married and pregnant—most on-film relationships need at least an hour of screen time to build sufficient emotional rapport with the audience.

The shallow nature of their relationship manifests itself in other ways: Tashi soon becomes unfaithful to his wife, and begins eyeing an attractive co-worker. After an R-rated romp with the co-worker, he feels ashamed and decides to return to his monastery—pathetically abandoning his wife and son in the process. In the end, the audience sees Tashi not as an upright man facing a complex philosophical dilemma, but as a sex-starved monk who abandons his spiritual calling in order to pursue a woman, whom he then betrays.

Ultimately, this could have been a deeply insightful movie in which an aged Tashi, lying sick in his bed with his elderly wife dying by his side, asks himself whether his decision to choose love over enlightenment was truly the right thing to do. At that point, the film would raise important questions: whether the human experience (and love) is intrinsically meaningful, whether meaning can be found in death and suffering, or whether all human experience is meaningless and spiritual attainment is the only thing that truly counts.

Unfortunately, the film did not actually touch on these questions. Nevertheless, it reminded me of them. I am not a Buddhist and I do not intend to become one, but watching this film and reading about foreign philosophical traditions in my SLE class raises a lot of questions that I believe are worth thinking about. And these are questions that anyone from any religious tradition, whether Christian, Muslim, Jew, or atheist, should be considering.
