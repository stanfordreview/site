Attending an LGBT workshop at Stanford

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Growing-up in an all-straight environment, I had little contact with gay people before I went to college. Much of what I knew about homosexuals was gleaned from strictly academic sources: in secondary school, I learnt from my straight-faced literature teacher that Socrates and Plato may have been gay.

By and large, I grew up with a rather romanticized stereotype of gays: as deeply intellectual, sensitive, and artistic people. In particular, I read about the 1895 trial of Oscar Wilde, who, when facing trial for his homosexuality, eloquently defended his way of life:

“The love that dare not speak its name…is such a great affection of an elder for a younger man as there was between David and Jonathan, such as Plato made the very basis of his philosophy, and such as you find in the sonnets of Michelangelo and Shakespeare. It is that deep, spiritual affection that is as pure as it is perfect.…It is beautiful, it is fine, it is the noblest form of affection. There is nothing unnatural about it. It is intellectual, and it repeatedly exists between an elder and a younger man, when the elder man has intellect, and the younger man has all the joy, hope and glamour of life before him.”

Although I hardly consider myself sympathetic to the gay movement, I do appreciate poetic elegance when I see it.

Then, I recently met some real, out-in-the-open gays for the first time.

A few weeks ago on a cheerful Monday evening, my dorm hosted a sensitivity training workshop called Safe and Open Spaces at Stanford (SOSAS), organized by the Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, & Transgender Community Resource Center (LGBT CRC).

The panel members turned out to be ordinary Stanford students, just like the rest of us. Nevertheless, they were neatly-dressed, thoughtful, and highly articulate. I was deeply impressed by their professionalism.

At the Q&A, I had some questions, and to my delight, the panelists seemed pleased to offer answers.

First, I decided to get the most bugging thought off my chest: “Most religions disapprove of your way of life. How do you reconcile your religious beliefs with your lifestyle?”

A panelist thought deeply for a minute, then answered: “I am a Christian, and I admit that I struggled with my feelings for a long time. But eventually, after talking to my pastor, I came to the conclusion that being gay and Christian are not necessarily mutually exclusive.” He then suggested the Old Testament’s strictures against homosexuality, like the commands against eating pork, could be considered obsolete in the modern church.

Another panelist, a Muslim, chipped in: “Even if homosexuality is a sin, why do religious groups always pick on us while ignoring others?” He then suggested that it was wrong for religious leaders to focus their condemnation on private acts between consenting adults, while paying scant attention to bigger moral issues like innocent people being killed in Iraq.

Then, a Jewish panelist added: “If the Torah is taken word for word, it is virtually impossible, literally speaking, to violate any commandment.” She then suggested, for example, that the prohibition against a “man lying with a man” does not necessarily include a prohibition against a man having intercourse with a man, nor does it include women lying with women.

I sensed that my pastor might interpret the Bible somewhat differently from the panelist. Nevertheless, being straight, I figured that it was not my problem. So I went on to ask my second question: “How would you respond to the conservative argument that if gay marriage is legalized, there would be a slippery slope effect—i.e. people pushing to legalize polygamy and bestiality?”

One of the girls on the panel replied that she personally saw no real problem with polygamy: “So long as all the parties are happy and consent to the arrangement, I personally don’t see why I should go out of my way to deprive them of their free choice.”

Although I personally oppose polygamy, I feel that the case for polygamy is probably at least as strong as the case for gay marriage. Traditionally, many civilizations—including the Islamic, Chinese, and Indian civilizations—have practiced polygamy sometime during the course of their histories. If gay marriage, a relatively modern concept, is legalized, it will be very difficult to oppose polygamy, which has existed for centuries.

Responding to my point on bestiality, another female panelist reiterated the principle of free choice: “The reason why bestiality is wrong is because animals cannot freely consent to sex. How does an animal express consent?”

Although I disagree with the panelists, I think we should give them credit for being honest. They believe that the principle of free consent should take precedence over traditional practices, and they are willing to defend that stance.

Recently, however, someone informed me that the girl’s assumption—that animals cannot give consent—is not necessarily true. Apparently in 2001, the prominent liberal professor Peter Singer wrote that “occasionally mutually satisfying activities may develop” between humans and animals. Shortly after, The New York Times reported that Singer’s remarks were supported by Ingrid Newkirk, the president of People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA): “If a girl gets sexual pleasure from riding a horse, does the horse suffer? If not, who cares? If you French kiss your dog and he or she thinks it’s great, is it wrong? We believe all exploitation and abuse is wrong […] [But] if it isn’t exploitation and abuse, it may not be wrong.”

I feel that if future LGBT panels wish to uphold the pro-gay marriage argument that free consent is an overriding principle, they may need to find an appropriate response to the pro-zoophilia arguments of Singer and PETA. (I hope that most modern liberals will be conservative enough to draw the line at bestiality. Otherwise, it’ll probably be the end of civilization as we know it.)

Moving on, a panelist talked more about gay marriage: “When straight people tell me that we gays already have the right to marry—to marry members of the opposite sex—I feel that it sounds pretty condescending.”

One panelist said: “I don’t think that everyone in society should be compelled to recognize gay marriage, or that every church in this country be forced to perform gay marriages. Rather, I feel that we should allow individual churches and organizations to decide their own policies on gay marriages.”

Finally, near the end, someone chipped in a similar idea: “I feel that gay marriage should be a private issue for individual communities to decide democratically, not the federal government.”
I offer a modified version of his argument: I feel that gay marriage should be an issue for individual states to resolve. Many conservatives believe that individual states should be allowed to democratically decide their abortion policies: we believe that Roe v. Wade, whatever its merits, unfairly infringes states’ rights. Therefore, in the conservative view, it may make sense under certain circumstances to allow individual states to decide their own policies on gay marriage (although most states are unlikely to vote to legalize it). Despite their faults, state-specific laws on gay marriage are probably preferable to a top-down imposition from the federal government (or the Supreme Court).

After the workshop, I thanked the panel leader for her time. To be perfectly honest, I still have deep reservations about the LGBT lifestyle, mainly intellectual and moral ones. Nevertheless, I left that seminar feeling more informed about the issues. Granted, the SOSAS panel may never be in the same league as the odes of Oscar Wilde. But I think that for a bunch of students trying to promote their cause on the Stanford campus, they did a pretty good job.
