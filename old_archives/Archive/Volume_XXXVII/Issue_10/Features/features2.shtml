What I Read Over Winter Break

by Tristan Abbey

Deputy Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Classmates often say they don’t have time for pleasure reading during an academic quarter. The countless hours we spend on Facebook and reading e-mails notwithstanding, there is a great deal of truth to this claim; keeping track of complex plots and characters is no easy task if you can only read a chapter or two per night. So, over break I decided to read for pleasure. These are the highlights:

Harris, Thomas. Hannibal Rising. New York: Delacorte Press, 2006.

Have you ever wondered how something (vice someone) as monstrous as Hannibal the Cannibal could have evolved? Thomas Harris promises to answer that in this prequel to the prequel to Silence of the Lambs. When the Nazis invade Russia, prepubescent Hannibal’s childhood is shattered as his family escapes to a forest hideaway, where they hope to wait out the duration of the war. I can’t get into all the gory details, but let’s just say that something awful happens, out of which the Hannibal Lecter we all have come to know and fear is—quite convincingly—born. What follows is a tragic and bloody tale of revenge, topped off with one of the most bone-chilling surprise endings I’ve ever read. I devoured this gruesome novel in short order.

Crichton, Michael. Next. New York: HarperCollins, 2006.

I then shifted gears to science fiction and picked up the latest from Michael Crichton. He is a master at explaining complex scientific concepts to the layman, as he did so well in State of Fear and his dinosaur books. His new novel continues his recurring theme that we humans often make decisions without thinking about the long-term consequences.In the case of Next, we decide to play around with DNA, modifying and inserting genes without much thought of tomorrow. The cast of characters include a conversational parrot, some kind of orangutan or monkey that can pass for a deformed human child, an idiotic Berkeley leftist, and more scientists than you can shake a gibbon stick at. The novel’s chief downside is that there might be too many subplots (Diane Feinstein makes a cameo appearance, for example) for people to follow over a long period of time, so this is definitely a book to read in single-digit sittings. I stayed up quite late into the night, often telling myself I’d read just a few more minutes only to find what seemed like a split-second later that I had plowed through twenty pages.


Iraq Study Group Report. New York: Vintage Books, 2006.

I won’t say I overdosed on novels (I read three other Crichtons in addition to Next), but it was time for a change of pace. The Iraq Study Group Report is short, under one hundred pages. “Serious” is the one-word description I would give. At least three of the group’s commissioners have a Stanford connection: Sandra Day O’Connor, William Perry (MS&E), and Edwin Meese (Hoover fellow). The report is divided into two sections, an assessment of the current situation, which is “grave and deteriorating,” and a series of 79 recommendations for a new strategy. While the media has focused on just a handful, most notably Recommendations 9-17 (which suggest we deal with Iran, Syria, and the Palestinians), there are quite a few that are difficult to argue with. 67 calls for a single person to be in charge of reconstruction projects, 58 suggests the FBI help more with Iraqi police force training, 35 says we should dispatch an emissary to Grand Ayatollah Sistani, and 18 reminds us not to forget Afghanistan. The most important part of the report, though, is the 40-page assessment that precedes the recommendations. It explains in plain words where we are now and what happens if we pull-out, stay the course, or implement their recommendations (which admittedly have “shortcomings”). Anyone who talks about the war—for it or against it—needs to understand the content of the assessment. A quick, informative read available for free online.
