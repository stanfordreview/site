How Stanford Works: An Introduction

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Nearly every Stanford student has gripes about ‘the administration.’ But who is ‘the administration’, who influences its decisions, who wields power?

At first, this seems like an easy question. The President. Or maybe the Faculty Senate. At any rate, if you don’t know the answer, then it must be written down somewhere, the answer easily available. Stanford is an enlightened institution, after all.

Alas, this is hardly the case. Formal authority is uneasily divided between the President and his various appointees, the faculty, the Board of Trustees. And this list seems to have some glaring omissions. What influence can students have on university policy? How about alumni, big donors, the Federal Government, the Media?

At many of our peer institutions, a lively debate on governance, administration, and the ends of the University animates discussions of school policy. Consider, for example, the recent debate at Dartmouth over proposed changes in their Alumni Constitution and how this might affect Board of Trustee elections. At Dartmouth, Alumni are the ultimate sources of authority and legitimacy in governing the College and setting its policy, with the Board of Trustees administering this oversight. Dartmouth holds Board of Trustee elections, and allows write-in candidates who might represent an otherwise unheard from alumni voice. The administration and a small group of alumni wished to change the rules governing write-in candidates, but a broad coalition of students and alumni opposed these changes. The ensuing debate played out not only on the pages of Dartmouth publications and blogs, but the New York Times and the Wall Street Journal. The prerequisite of such debate is knowing which debates matter, whose decisions matter, which fights are worth picking, and even what the possible range of fights is.

While not all universities are governed as democratically as Dartmouth, the students and alumni of Berkeley, Harvard, and many other institutions at least know who wields power and has influence.

This is not the case at Stanford. Consider two recent examples: Munger construction and the Fraga affair.

Stanford is constructing a large new Graduate residence in the center of campus. Though there has been vociferous opposition to the Munger project, and the disruption of undergraduate lives, traffic flows, and the aesthetics of the campus that it will cause, nothing came of it. Opponents of the project didn’t know who had authority in the matter, what political considerations led the university to allow a donor to dictate many of the details of a construction project, or what kinds of counterarguments leveled at whom might stop or alter the project. The general student body only found out about the project after the details had been decided. They did not know how and where to exercise any influence; any opposition was purely pro forma.

Similarly, The hiring away of Political Science Professor Luis Fraga to the University of Washington was the subject of a student campaign to convince the University to retain him, and occasioned discussion of faculty diversity hiring and promotion of racial minority professors. The level of discussion betrayed a lack of understanding of how tenure decisions are made or who has authority. And, similarly, the chances of affecting the university’s behavior were slim.

Over the next few months, I and others at The Stanford Review will address this knowledge deficiency. In a series of investigative articles, we will take closer looks at the how the University President, administrators, faculty senate, departments, the Board of Trustees, alumni, and the ASSU contribute to Stanford’s governance. For each institution, we will look at both its formal powers and how it informally influences decisions. We will investigate what values undergird its decisions and what policies it most doggedly pursues. For each group, we will examine the role it has played in recent controversies, from faculty hiring and promotion to scholarships and construction. And at every stop along the way, we will ask how much attention is paid to student input and interests, and how students can increase their influence.

Certainly, we are not so naïve as to believe that more information alone will suddenly render Stanford fully transparent and put students in charge. But the inverse is certainly true – without an underlying understanding of how the University makes decisions and who holds power, students do not so much as know where to begin if they wish to influence University policies, programs, and decisions. If politics are as much about power as the issues, then we must know who holds power before we can even begin to think about ourselves wielding power.
