Two Hours of Decadence

by Stuart Baimel

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Marie Antoinette, the great enemy of the bourgeoisie circa 1789, is indulged, feted and revisioned in the new eponymous film by director Sofia Coppola, of Lost in Translation fame. Everything about the film’s aesthetic is a revision of the standard European-history textbook treatment of Antoinette and her husband, Louis XVI, second-to-last of the Bourbons. From the trendy soundtrack (featuring trendy artists such as Air, Gang of Four, and predictably, the Strokes) to the constant quick cuts and long shots, English dialogue (and British accents) this film is very clearly an ahistorical look at one of history’s most notorious rulers.

Marie Antoinette, from the outset, very clearly suffers from a problem of expectations. At the Cannes Film Festival, where it was first shown, it received both a standing ovation and boos, from different parts of the audience. Expectations were high for this film in France, and less so in the United States, but clearly they diverge for different people. Many filmgoers will be looking, or at least hoping, for a serious historical analysis of the time, or even a reasonably accurate biopic of Antoinette, but history buffs will find themselves sorely disappointed. Those looking for a decadent (no other word captures the entire aesthetic of the film as precisely as that word), almost alarmingly shallow, and above all entertaining impressionistic look at one of the most opulent lives in the history of mankind will find their eyes rewarded with all sorts of candy.

Kirsten Dunst seems like a poor choice to take the lead role—there must be someone who would be a better fit after all, but she performs surprisingly well, or at least well enough to keep the film moving along. Her fragility and innocence matches her character’s lack of knowledge of the elaborate strictures and hierarchy of the French court that provides many of the film’s set pieces and the self-contained structure in which the plot both operates and rebels from (notably a sequence with the Swedish count). While she dominates in terms of camera time, she is supported by a strong cast of fascinating characters, including Rip Torn as a mildly carnal Louis XV and Jamie Dornan as the Swedish Count, with whom she has a passionate affair. While historians can only speculate on whether the affair actually happened or not, Coppola treats it as a historical fact. She needs it too; without that affair, the film’s fast-moving plot would have faded by the halfway point.

But what really steals the show, and constitutes the film’s “candy,” is the visual opulence. Almost entirely filmed at Versailles, the camera inhabits a world of ornate wall paneling, elaborate food platters, and overdone clothing. Coppola is artful with the camera, never spending too much time on any one shot but making sure that everything counts. Occasionally a trendy band comes on and the shots speed up, taking much in at once. Considering how much is going on in each scene, few shots are wasted, certainly something that keeps Marie from stalling in the face of repetition. The film’s plot constantly teeters on the precipice of static boredom; because political events are almost entirely ignored until the very end, not very much happens, and even the necessary and overdramatic court drama disappoints in most cases. And although the plot totters on stasis, it never quite does and remains eminently followable. The movie ends long before her death, and even before she is forced to leave Versailles, ensuring that no detail is spaced about the aesthetics of French court life.

But what is far more interesting than the plot, cinematography or relevance to history is what this film says about our society today. Has bourgeois society, which has ruled so effectively since 1789, come so far as to idealize and indulge the number one enemy of the bourgeoisie? Has the materialism of the West reached such a point that we can watch a film about a court that cared little, if at all, about the fate of the millions it ruled, and immerse ourselves in a distracting world of bon bons and petit fours? Do we truly find ourselves sympathizing with the innocent, well-meaning Antoinette versus the loud, brutal and above all “uncivilized” crowd of bourgeoisie outside the palace, fully intent on beheading her?
In a word, yes.

This reviewer is not sure whether to be alarmed or not, considering that he himself enjoyed the film, fully realizing the rich ironies present. Can we truly separate an indulgent and fun portrait of royalty from the historical realities of the time and the great overthrow of the aristocracy by the bourgeoisie? Coppola certainly tries to, and in order to let oneself be entertained, history has to be separated out here. We certainly feel sympathy for the Bourbons, considering how clueless Antoinette seems about the issue of “national finances,” despite her sense of entitlement, something with which we also sympathize. Coppola implies the court’s overspending attributable to France’s invaluable assistance to the American revolutionaries.

Marie is a compelling film, in and of itself. If one knew nothing of the French Revolution, then it would be eminently enjoyable, like eating cotton candy—tasty, although there is nothing to it. And there is a certain value in that—aesthetically the film is beautiful, perhaps even gorgeous at times, and that is something to be valued in and of itself. History, of course, inevitably adds weight to it, even though Coppola self-consciously tries to ignore, or reject it. History majors beware.
