Love in Singapore

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Singapore is a modern, cosmopolitan city whose citizens come from all over the world. She is a wealthy, first world nation with a pro-U.S. and pro-Western foreign policy. And despite having a centralized government that is strong by Western standards, Singapore is a highly democratic republic, one of the freest nations in Southeast Asia.

Singapore, like Taiwan and Israel, is a small nation: She has only 4 million people and an area of only 270 square miles. As a result, her prosperity is heavily dependent on foreign trade and globalization, which has led to the development of a heavily commercialized society. Although the Singaporean people strongly believe in democratic ideals like freedom of speech, their zeal for liberty is secondary to their adherence to the practical and the material. Therefore, given this expedient culture, Singaporeans usually vote for leaders who have similar beliefs.
Few things tell us more about a people than how they fall in love. And in observing the Singaporean love scene, we can gain a deeper appreciation of the highly pragmatic culture that exists in Singapore.

Let me offer one example. In 2004, Singapore’s Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong, in his annual speech to the nation, observed that too few couples were getting married and bearing children. Therefore, he decided to offer some words of wisdom to the unmarried:
“[You] need to have realistic expectations. Don’t look for the impossible. If you go for…soap opera standard, then [there is] no hope. What do I mean by being realistic? I give you a couple of examples. First one is from a very experienced grassroots leader friend of mine who said he counseled his niece many years ago. She was single in her late 20s. He says, ‘You must get a husband.’ She said, ‘But I must find a man who will tell jokes to me, entertain me, pick me up if I am down, [and] look after me in the evenings.’ So, he told her: ‘You don’t need a husband, you need a TV set.’ So, she took his advice to heart, got married and is now happily married with children. I should bring him into S.D.U. (Social Development Unit, the state-run matchmaking agency) council some day.”

Mr Lee then went on to comment on an anonymous young male citizen who rejected a girl because she wore glasses: “So, he’s still single…I think we’ve got to change our mindsets and be realistic and accommodating.”

So, as we can see here, even Singapore’s leaders are able to offer their two-cents worth on how citizens should choose their dates and spouses. One does not often see President Bush offering similar advice to unmarried Americans during his State of the Union Address on prime-time TV. But while an American might see this as government intrusion, the practical Singaporean sees it as a caring government.

Another way to observe Singapore’s culture is to consider its treatment of common phenomena. One such phenomenon might be this: The most beautiful women often end up marrying the most successful guys; less attractive men are thus often unable to marry the women of their dreams.

Here in America, thanks to cultural organizations like Disney, we prefer to gloss over this unhappy phenomenon. We want to feel as though the underdog has a chance, so we have romanticized the idea of the makeover, in which an unattractive person changes in order to attract the spouse of his/her dreams. We see it on TV all the time, from Extreme Makeover to My Big Fat Greek Wedding. Just last year, the film Just Friends was released, a movie about a fat student who loses weight so that he can win over his high school sweetheart.

In contrast, Singapore’s culture, being highly pragmatic, treats love stories differently by choosing to emphasize the stark realities (which most people face) rather than the glorious possibilities (which are available to few). This realism is often reflected in the modern fables that Singaporeans tell each other, most notably in their recent 2005 film, Be With Me.
The film is a complex story containing no less than three sub-plots, but what interested me most was the subplot involving a middle-aged security guard (discreetly named Fatty) who is madly in love with Ann, a career woman who works at the office building he guards. Unfortunately, Ann is way out of Fatty’s league: He is ugly, obese, lonely, and poor; she is svelte, beautiful, confident, educated, and wealthy. Painfully aware of this social divide, Fatty is reduced to hopeless fantasies about being with the woman he dreams about. He tries following her around, watching enviously as she dates far more attractive and successful men.

Moreover, Fatty never quite manages to redeem himself: He remains too shy and self-conscious to ever approach the beautiful woman. He is caught sleeping on the job and gets fired. Worst of all, he remains an overweight, unattractive slob throughout the film. One poignant scene features him eating a dozen dishes of oily food at a restaurant. After the eating binge, the hapless man stares at the stacks of empty, greasy plates cluttered on his table—irrefutable evidence of his self-control, restraint, and willpower!

Too scared to talk face-to-face with Ann, Fatty decides to write a love letter. He therefore sneaks to a bookshop and buys pink, scented paper with lots of flowers and hearts. He then tries to write down his thoughts. Unfortunately, being semi-illiterate, he is unable to write properly: His wastepaper basket is soon full of crumpled letters.
In the end, Fatty does manage to (painfully) finish his letter, and he does succeed in fitting it inside an envelope. But on his way to delivering the letter (which as the film implies, would almost certainly have given Ann a heart attack), he dies in a most bizarre way: He gets crushed to death when a lesbian quite accidentally falls on him.

To some of us, this story might seem rather sad. But the focus of the film is not on what life should be like, but on what it really is. Here in America, Mickey Mouse tells us that “When you wish upon a star, your dreams come true.” But in Singapore, most people simply do not believe in the Disney Doctrine. Therefore, Singaporeans do not preach about underdogs marrying princesses, nor do they preach about underdogs becoming princes.

Does this mean that Singaporean culture is necessarily inferior to ours? No, but it is to say that it is run by different values. Americans believe in certain ideals; Singaporeans choose to believe in what they feel is more practical. Americans choose to emphasize the idea that people can change their destinies; other cultures may think somewhat differently. Ultimately, however, few things tell us more about Singapore’s culture than how it treats the idea of love (they could take a hint or two from Hollywood, though).
