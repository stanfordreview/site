Dinesh D’Souza Delivers, Once Again

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In the aftermath of the 2005 London bombings, one of the burning questions was why affluent, Western-educated, British-born Muslims would ever want to become suicide bombers. If these men were hardly poor or desperate, why would they ever want to become terrorists?

Dinesh D’Souza’s addresses this question in his recent book, The Enemy At Home: The cultural left and its responsibility for 9/11. In D’Souza’s view, while we Americans are accustomed to (and also enjoy) blue state cultural fare such as Hollywood films and MTV culture, it is not difficult to imagine how our blue state culture might disgust people from more traditionally-minded societies, especially the Muslim world. Around the world, millions of Muslims believe that American culture is a threat to their way of life, which is why they may, overtly or covertly, sympathize with Islamic terrorists. Therefore, even Western-born, well-educated Muslim immigrants—those most exposed to our MTV culture—sometimes view us as iimmoral and decadent.

At the same time, many in the Muslim world hate America for backing international organizations that promote ideas that remain controversial abroad (abortion, homosexual rights, etc.) in the name of “family planning,” “human rights,” and “international law.”

D’Souza suggests that the cultural left is responsible for America’s poor foreign image: “I am not making the absurd accusation that this group [the cultural left] blew up the World Trade Center and the Pentagon. I am saying that the cultural left and its allies in Congress, the media, Hollywood, the non-profit sector and the universities are the primary cause of the volcano of anger toward America that is erupting from the Islamic world.”

But this brings about an interesting paradox: If Islamic anti-Americanism is rooted in a hatred of America’s liberal values, then why has the cultural left (and liberal Democrats) been so uncooperative in President Bush’s war on terror? Why do so many of them favor immediate withdrawal from Iraq, which could result in the Iraqi people being oppressed by an extremist government?

D’Souza suggests an answer: “If the left can convert national security—usually a source of political strength for the right—into a liability, then it has vastly improved its chances of winning future elections. If conservatives lose badly, all three branches of government—the presidency, the Congress, and the Supreme Court—could end up in the grips of the opposition.”

As Machiavellian as it may seem, this theory sounds plausible. D’Souza’s idea is that the cultural left opposes Bush’s wars because they hate Islamic extremism less than American conservatism. To a left-wing ideologue, although winning Iraq might help liberate Iraqi women and minorities, losing Iraq would be even better, because it would mean defeat for American conservatism. It would mean future electoral victories for the Democratic Party. It would mean more government spending and more liberal judges on the Supreme Court, leading to an expansion of abortion rights, the welfare state, and possibly even gay marriage. In short, a defeat for America’s war on terrorism would be a political victory for the American left.

D’Souza’s main argument, therefore, is that in pursuing its natural self-interest, the American left ends up in de jure opposition to Bush and in a de facto alliance with, well, the Islamic radicals. His solution? American conservatives should stop trying to seek “bipartisan” solutions with uncooperative liberals, and choose instead to forge more common ground with traditional Muslims. This can be achieved by convincing traditional Muslims that most Americans abide by traditional, Judeo-Christian “red state” values, as opposed to Hollywood, “blue state” ones. In short, he believes that the Islamic world despises Blue State America, not Red State America.

I do have doubts about D’Souza’s belief that America can reduce Islamic hatred simply by showcasing her traditional “red state values” instead of the Hollywood-and-Hillary “blue state” ones. Even the gulf between red state America and traditional Muslims is probably too large a gap to bridge. For example, on the issue of gay rights, a blue state liberal might believe that homosexuals should be allowed to get married, while a red state conservative might argue for tolerance, but not gay marriage. Chances are, however, a traditional Muslim would reject both gay marriage and mere tolerance—he would argue that under Sharia law, homosexuals should be stoned or whipped.

Nevertheless, D’Souza’s The Enemy At Home is a book that should be judged on its own merits: Does it offer reasoned, articulate arguments? Does this book offer a coherent explanation for why 9/11 happened? And ultimately, are D’Souza’s arguments convincing?

The answer to all the above questions is “yes.” Like all political books, this one has flaws. But unlike many political writers, D’Souza is careful to present his arguments in a rational, intellectual manner—one that will appeal to intelligent, thoughtful people rather than true believers. He offers a careful and nuanced portrayal of not only Islamic theology, but also its modern history and its Zeitgeist. Moreover, his analysis of American liberalism and left-wing thinking is extremely detailed—he knows the left better than most liberals.

Dinesh D’Souza is a talented writer, one of the best in the business. Indeed, on a cold Sunday afternoon, this reader read all 292 pages of The Enemy At Home in one sitting—I could not put it down.

There are some books that ought to be borrowed and some that should be bought. Buy this one.
