Shanghai Noon on the Final Frontier

by Grant Everett Starrett

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Space, popularly identified as the “final frontier,” seems to have lost its ability to capture the hearts and minds of the American people. Ever since we managed to land a man on the moon, Americans seem to be content with how far we have come. Sure, there is some idle speculation about what the future holds and a natural wonder enters into the minds of many as they gaze toward the stars, but these moments are brief and usually nothing more than passing fancies. NASA, America’s space program, rarely garners much coverage, and when it does, the press is usually the result of a calamity, such as the loss of the Challenger in the late 1980s or the accidental destruction of a satellite we sent to Mars due to a miscalculation regarding feet-to-meters conversions. Perhaps the lack of interest is due to a lack of national competition, or more specifically, a lack of fear: The Soviet Union has not threatened to overtake our extraterrestrial advancements since it ceased to exist some years ago. If this premise is true, however, I can accurately predict a whole lot more interest in space in the coming years.

Only three nations have independently sent their citizens into space and each conveniently has its own title for their job description. A Soviet cosmonaut and American astronaut both entered space for the first time in 1961 while the Chinese have gotten a late start: The first taikonauts entered space in the 1990s. Today, the Russians have but a nominal program, barely scraping by with the funds that they have; in fact, they have gone so far as selling the opportunity to become a cosmonaut for $20 million. The Chinese, on the other hand, have been stepping up their space program, which, unlike the American version, is run by the military. China has long stated that its official policy is to keep space demilitarized and this, surprisingly, has been largely accepted by members of the international community--that is, until a couple of weeks ago.

On January 11, 2007, the Chinese government demonstrated the capability to knock out a satellite in space. Harvard astronomer Jonathan McDowell said that the test was “the first real escalation in the weaponization of space that we’ve seen in 20 years,” indicating that it “ends a long period of restraint.” Scientists estimate that “space junk” around Earth has increased by 10% as a result. The U.S. State Department issued a statement indicating that “modern life” had been somehow altered while the governments of Britain, Canada, Australia, Japan, and other American allies publicly questioned the motives of the Chinese government. President Hu Jintao, however, seemed to have been caught off-guard. His government failed to respond to criticism quickly and there was some speculation that he may not have even known about the test. More likely, Hu probably overplayed his hand, thinking that the challenge to U.S. space supremacy would go unnoticed and was surprised to have to deal with such a great amount of negative reaction. Most countries became skeptical of China’s peaceful intentions regarding space.

Despite these condemnations, nothing seems to have changed. China issued a statement some time later saying that it did not wish to engage in an arms race--a nice way of dissuading us from militarizing while they keep building their power projection capabilities. Sino-American relations continue largely unaltered, presumably the result of close economic ties and our need for their pressure on rogue states like North Korea and Iran. Americans seemed to take no notice at all. The satirical magazine the Onion seemed to suggest that they only would have had the Chinese knocked out a DirecTV satellite during a football game.

Perhaps if this was a single incident by a friendly nation, we might be able to overlook what has happened. But this is simply the newest prodding of whether or not America is willing to enforce its stated policies. In a related incident, the Chinese have already attempted to block U.S. satellite imaging of its territory. Only a few months ago did a Chinese submarine shadow one of our carriers in international waters. The Chinese recently unveiled plans for a new blue water navy and introduced a new fighter jet available for purchasing on the world market. Finally, they’ve engaged in soft power politics all around the globe, providing aid to some of the most notorious anti-American governments.

With the increasing likelihood that space will be militarized, the United States faces an enormous challenge. Currently, both China and the United States have plans in the works to colonize both the Moon and Mars. So questions arise: Will the first words on Mars be “This is one small step for a man, and one great leap forward for the workers of the world”? Will we allow the red planet to become part of Red China?

No. I may joke when I refer to China’s “communist” self-labeling, but that doesn’t change the central premise: We cannot allow ourselves to defer responsibility from a benevolent multi-party democracy with a proven record of liberating oppressed peoples to an ambitious malevolent one-party dictatorship with a proven record of oppressing its people.

The Clinton Administration had set forward a space policy for the United States that was fairly sheepish in its goals; essentially, space would be a place for scientific research and would be exploited by the entire international community. Luckily, the Bush Administration set forth a new policy this last year: we now reserve the right to “deny, if necessary, adversaries the use of space capabilities hostile to U.S. national interests” and reject a premise of international legislation governing space.

Now we simply need to put our money where our mouth is. We have to do our best to assert sovereignty over space while we still have the awesome technological, financial, and military advantages that we do. Right now, the Defense Department’s special projects division, DARPA, is working on a project called FALCON that will enhance our ability to project ourselves all over the planet; basically, it’s a hypersonic vehicle that will transport our troops anywhere in the world within two hours. Our missile defense system is supposed to be operational within a year. Now may be time to start to take seriously the idea of establishing some sort of space marine corps or similar branch of the armed forces. What we need more than ever is the willpower to assert ourselves, rather than tip-toe around how much power we have until we no longer are able to use it. We need to reject the New York Times’ call for an international treaty banning space weaponry that will surely not be followed by our friends across the Pacific and instead get serious about what we can do in space. On Friday, February 2, the Bush Administration took the first step by suspending all plans of working with the Chinese on space exploration. With any luck, this is a sign of increased boldness in enforcing American space policy that will send a message to the Chinese that we intend to win any “star wars” that are fought.
