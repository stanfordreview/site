Pro-Life Display Sparks Dialogue

by Allysia Finley

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The pro-life demonstration in White Plaza on Jan. 22nd remembering the 34th anniversary of the Roe v. Wade decision sparked little conflict or opposition, reflecting what some students called a surprising tolerance among campus pro-life and pro-choice groups.

The Stanford Students for Life set up 464 white crosses in the grassy patch across from the book store to represent the 46 million abortions that have occurred since the 1973 ruling. The Stanford Students for Life (SSFL) also managed a table where they handed out flyers promoting their position to students who dropped by to discuss the display. While the SSFL admit that it is debatable whether the demonstration converted many students, they believe that it did provoke civil conversation between the two opposing groups.
“Virtually everyone was interested, and even people who disagreed were respectful,” said SSFL member junior Chris Hammen.

The open dialogue surprised some students, who had feared aggressive activists from both sides. The pro-choice response was muted and limited to isolated instances of individual activism. For example, a few students rode their bicycles through the crosses, some uttered expletives as they passed, and one hung a baby doll on a pole in mockery of the demonstration. The Stanford Students for Choice group appeared briefly around lunchtime with a few posters but did not attempt to disrupt the pro-life demonstration.

“We don’t have bad relations with the pro-life group and we don’t have really good relations with them either. We are both very respectful of each other’s events and opinions,” said Mishan Araujo, co-president of Stanford Students for Choice (SSFC).

Despite periodic political upheavals on campus, as evidenced by last year’s vehement protests against President Bush’s appearance on campus, the atmosphere in White Plaza generally remained calm and contemplative throughout the day. The SSFL estimates that about half of the 200 or so individuals who stopped by their table were pro-choice proponents looking to engage in meaningful dialogue.

“The display was very powerful and evoked a lot of confusing emotions that forced me to re-examine my position on the issue,” said freshman Marissa Miller.

Some students, however, complained that the display was overly sentimental and obnoxious, suggesting that the crosses played on feelings of guilt. Araujo indicated that the SSFC’s only major objections to the demonstration were its high religious-charge and its portrayal of women who seek abortions as criminals. Some pro-life advocates echoed similar objections.
“I think there were certain elements that could be construed as overly-sensational. The use of crosses, I feel, imbues the whole thing with a level of religiosity that cannot be denied-- this despite the fact that Stanford Students for Life is purportedly secular,” said self-described pro-life sophomore John Komkov.

SSFL Co-President Dan Cervantes, however, insisted that the group used the crosses because of their universal symbolism and that they did not contain any religious undertones. Regardless of the crosses’ symbolism, students acknowledged that the crosses did draw their attention to the issue. On a predominantly liberal and pro-choice campus, the crosses raised students’ consciousness of society’s dividedness over the issue.

“Conversation rarely comes up because most people assume that everyone on campus is pro-choice,” said Hammen.

Open dialogue over the issue carried over into the evening in a panel discussion sponsored by the SSFC featuring Stanford professors Marcia Stefanick and Margo Horn and the President of Law Students for Choice Dorotha Ocker. About fifty students attended the discussion, which covered topics in reproductive health and the implications of Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey. Araujo reported that there was no pro-life response to or activism at the event.
