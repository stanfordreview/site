Pub Night’s Postponement Protects Students

by Allysia Finley

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Unsurprisingly, the postponement of the traditional senior Pub Night left masses of students griping and grumbling over the administration’s allegedly underhanded efforts to hamper students’ fun. It seems that the Stanford administration has now even outdone the infamous right-wing conspiracy. First, it was the suspension of the Band, then the ban on bikes in the quad, and now the postponement of Senior Night.

Better blame the administration. Or perhaps it was that nerdy chemistry major who complained about out-of-control students disrupting his concentration and sleep. You know those chemistry majors…studying all the time and never wanting anyone else to have any fun. To hell with him and the administration and to anyone else who dares think that students should be respectful of others and responsible for their actions. To hell with anyone that tries to reign in Stanford students’ personal freedom!

But really, do students ever consider that the administration is taking the wellbeing of students into its own hands because many students have proven incapable of doing so themselves? The administration is merely trying to prevent the freedom of the masses from subordinating the rights of the individual. Care to hark back to the Constitution that states an individual can exercise his own freedom insofar he does not impinge on another man’s freedom? Ironically, at a school as culturally and socially aware as Stanford, students often fail to show consideration for their fellow man.

Residential Assistants constantly impress upon students the need to make smart decisions. For example, if you’re going to get drunk, make sure you’re with a friend who can take care of you. And if you’re underage, don’t drink in an area where you can get caught. But these are the exact messages that lead students to believe that they can act irresponsibly and break the rules so long as they don’t hurt themselves. The rationale goes as follows: getting drunk at a party is all right as long as you a) maintain your grades and b) are with a friend who will clean up your mess and bring you back home safely.

However, most students don’t consider the hassle of taking care of a drunk friend or cleaning up after busloads of vomiting students, as occurred after last year’s Pub Night. Nor do most students like to ponder the case of the pedestrian who gets slammed by an inattentive biker racing through the quad to get to his next class. And Stanford students claim to be concerned with the rights of workers and the common man? Before students go off to cure AIDS in Africa, they better be able to clean up their own vomit.

Similarly, most students never think or rather care about how the Band’s outrageous antics offend some people. The Stanford student body prides itself on its tolerance and sensitivity while remaining insensitive to individuals on campus that are not of a specified minority group (i.e. Chicano, black, homosexual). Students who walk rather than bike around campus—they’re a nuisance! Women who squirm at the site of streakers—they’re prudes! Students who choose not to drink at parties—they’re a minority!

It is this kind of thinking that lies at the core of the current clash between students and the administration: students act selfishly and irresponsibly and then resent it when the administration forces them to consider others by imposing irksome rules.

Whatever happened to Stanford students’ sense of moral responsibility and respect for individual liberties? Perhaps they got lost somewhere between the NAACP protest and social justice class.
