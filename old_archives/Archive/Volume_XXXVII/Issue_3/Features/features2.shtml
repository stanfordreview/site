Snow Patrol: Eyes Open

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

I’ve never liked the label “alternative rock” much. It’s not so much that it’s vague and no longer relevant in its meaning. It just doesn’t roll off the tongue as well as “hyphy” or “krunk” or even “electro-emo-new-age” does. I guess that’s why there seems to be a new word for the same, generic, 4-chord alternative rock every year. And so, when I first heard Snow Patrol’s previous effort, Final Straw, I was pretty much blown away. They were melodic, yet not poppy enough to sound like a McDonald’s jingle. Their music was powerful, yet not so much as to sound like every other “she tore my heart out” band of the hour.

Well, Snow Patrol is back with their recently released follow-up to Final Straw, titled Eyes Open. The first single, “Chasing Cars”, is reminiscent of previous singles “Chocolate” and “Run” in that it begins with a stripped down, quiet melody. The song builds to a crescendo of distorted guitars, a four-on-the-floor drum beat, and a quasi-emo repeated hook: “If I just lay here, would you lie with me and just forget the world?” It’s a catchy tune and very much in line with what Snow Patrol fans might expect. Yet the beauty of the album lies is in its more adventurous tracks.

The fifth song, “Hands Open,” is a more straight forward rock and roll tune. With a slightly less melodic and more upbeat feel, it stands apart from the other songs on the CD. Unfortunately, the lyrics aren’t all that uplifting, or even unique to Snow Patrol. The chorus repeats the hook “I want to hear you laugh like you mean it” in the same stereotypical and somewhat depressing lyrical style you’d expect from any Coldplay-esque band.

Still, the album has some high points. Well—a high and a low point, actually. The slow closer, “The Finish Line,” is a well written song on all fronts. Its floating melody catches the ear with an array of synthesizers and ambient type patches similar to something you might expect from The Postal Service. It is also the most depressing song on the album, with lyrics such as “A distant motorcade and suddenly there’s joy/The snow and tickertape blurs all my senses numb.” In contrast, the first U.K. single, “You’re All I Have” has a poppy, upbeat feel and stands out on the album as one of the few songs you might want to listen to while working out at the gym.

The CD has done well in the U.S., selling nearly a million copies as of the printing of this article. The single “Chasing Cars” is receiving good radio play, and I wouldn’t be surprised if a U.S. tour is forthcoming.

If there’s one area the where CD feels a bit repetitive, it is in its lyrical content. Though a couple tracks, such as “You’re All I Have” and “Make This Go On Forever”, indicate some level of growth and optimism, the majority of the CD is a bit of a downer. While other bands in similar genres have grown out of a lyrical hole, Snow Patrol appears to have remained lodged in the “she broke my heart” mantra. Still, the musical complexity and style indicate a progression from Snow Patrol’s last album. In my opinion, it is a change for the better. I’d give the album an 8/10.
