Forum: Is Peace Possible in Israel and Palestine?

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

At the beginning of this volume, Tucker Herbert, World Editor for The Review, wrote a piece titled “Is Peace Possible?” regarding this summer’s turmoil in the Middle East. The implicit hope of that question is that the answer is “yes” – but any solution will be as complex as the historical background that it reflects. The international dialogue continues to be dominated by hostility and a lack of understanding. At Stanford, there are a number of students of Arab and of Israeli background. As Stanford students, we take the same classes, cheer for the same Cardinal, and coexist in harmony, so the cultural and political differences should be far less extreme than on the world stage. Here we present two viewpoints, and the goal is to present these ideas in a forum that is less polarized than the land between the Jordan River and the Sinai Peninsula. The following text was contributed by guest authors David Cohen and Omar Shakir and does not necessarily reflect the views of The Review. David is a board member of the Stanford Israel Alliance and Omar is an active member of the Coalition for Justice in the Middle East (CJME) and Students Confronting Apartheid in Israel (SCAI).

   

The Palestinian people need democratic institutions, not simply elections, to cast off Hamas and enable peace.

 

by David Cohen

Guest Contributor

The vast majority of Israelis and Palestinians support a two-state solution. There is even some agreement on borders: they will roughly correspond to the final armistice lines of Israel’s War of Independence, probably with land swaps. Palestine will absorb all Palestinian refugees, and Israel will offer recognition and compensation for their plight. Sticking points remain, but with the largest issues settled in all non-extremist minds, one might expect peace to arrive shortly. Yet the killing continues. Only the fall of the Palestinian Hamas government, replaced by one which recognizes Israel’s right to exist as a Jewish state with secure borders, will allow these two peoples to force their leaders to make peace. And that will require real democracy in the Palestinian territories.

To understand why, one must understand the desires of both sides. Israel is a haven for persecuted Jews and a homeland for the Jewish people. The desire for a haven originated in response to European persecution, becoming even more relevant after six million Jews died in the Holocaust. Since its founding, Jews have fled or been airlifted to Israel from the USSR, Arab nations, Ethiopia, and parts of Europe. The Jewish need for a homeland goes beyond simple survival, however. “Jewish” is not a just religious descriptor, but a holistic identity involving history, heritage, and ethnicity. As explained by Avraham Infeld, former president of Hillel International, “Jews are a people who have a religion”. Like countless other peoples, the Jews also have a state. Any proposal that does not preserve Israel as a homeland for the Jewish people is unacceptable to Israelis and Jews everywhere.

The Palestinian people also desire a state. The 1948 UN partition plan called for a Palestinian state, but the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem (the Palestinian leader) rejected that plan in favor of a war to crush Israel. In that war, Israel’s territory expanded, and Egypt and Jordan occupied the Gaza Strip and the West Bank (which they lost to Israel in 1967). Also, 800,000 Palestinians became refugees under circumstances hotly contested to this day. Most Arab governments placed their share of these refugees in camps, and their descendents now number more than 4 million. Arab countries still deny them citizenship. Israel cannot accept four million Palestinian immigrants, because soon afterwards an Arab majority would vote to terminate Israel’s status as a Jewish homeland. However, no peace plan without a Palestinian state and a fair solution to the refugee crisis will ever be acceptable to the Palestinians.

All attempts to create a Palestinian state have failed thus far. At Camp David in 2000, Israel offered Palestinian President Yasir Arafat a deal much like the one above, and he walked away without a counteroffer. In 2005, Israel withdrew from the Gaza Strip and the northern West Bank. Like southern Lebanon, from which Israel also withdrew unilaterally, these areas have become staging grounds for kidnapping and rocket attacks against Israel. Based on this experience, no sane Israeli politician will unilaterally withdraw from additional territory, so any solution must be negotiated.

In order for an Israeli government to invest political capital in peace talks, it must believe that the Palestinian Authority (PA) will negotiate in good faith. However, the current Hamas government of the PA calls openly for the destruction of Israel. According to its charter, “There is no solution for the Palestinian question except through Jihad. Initiatives, proposals and international conferences are all a waste of time and vain endeavors.” Hamas refuses to even acknowledge agreements to which the Palestinian Authority is already a signatory. Given these circumstances, the prospects for negotiation are miniscule.

If Hamas loses power, negotiations may become possible. For them to be successful however, the PA must be accountable to its people, unlike Arafat in 2000. The PA has elections, but lacks democratic institutions. Without sufficient law and order to protect political speech and organization, the Palestinian people cannot compel a government to put their interests first.

It is through Palestinian democracy-building that the world can best help this region. Control of the Israeli government has alternated between hawks and doves for over a decade, with the Israeli electorate incensed at the nonstop violence. If there were a Palestinian government responsible to its people and dedicated to peace, no Israeli administration could survive without pursuing negotiations. When a democratically empowered Palestinian public stands beside the body politic of Israel, there just may be hope for peace in the Middle East.

Peace in the Holy Land requires ending institutional discrimination against Palestinians.

 

by Omar Shakir

Guest Contributor

The system of apartheid against Palestinians in Israel and the Occupied Territories stands as the principal obstacle to peace in the Middle East. Since its creation, enabled by the expulsion of the majority of the indigenous Palestinian population, Israeli policy has been dictated by the desire to establish and maintain a Jewish majority.

Playing on the fear of becoming a minority, Israeli leaders openly discuss the ratio of Jews to non-Jews as a national security issue and speak ominously of the ticking Palestinian ‘demographic time bomb.’ Even the 2005 disengagement from Gaza, trumpeted as a bold step towards peace, stemmed from Israel’s desire to separate itself entirely from the 1.3 million Palestinians of Gaza. As former Prime Minister Shimon Peres admitted, “we are disengaging from Gaza because of demography.” While maintaining a Jewish majority is conventionally seen as a liberal aspiration, it is fundamentally racist; imagine, for example, the Texas state legislature undertaking policies to maintain white domination. Such aspirations have long been relegated to the dustbin of history.

Israel most clearly practices apartheid in the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and Golan Heights, lands which it has occupied for nearly forty years in violation of dozens of U.N. resolutions and the Geneva Convention. Even with the withdrawal of 8,000 settlers from the Gaza Strip, hundreds of Jewish-only settlements and over 400,000 settlers remain in the Occupied Territories, where, as noted by an article in The Nation by Michael Brown, “a dual system of law [is] at work… one for Jews and one for Palestinians.” While Jews living in the West Bank hold Israeli citizenship, are subject to Israeli laws, vote in Israeli elections, and freely travel within Israel and abroad, Palestinians living in the same territory lack true citizenship and cannot avail themselves of Israeli law, vote in Israeli elections, or freely travel to Jerusalem, Israel, or abroad. Some would attribute this disparity to the ability of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza to vote for a separate authority; however, the Palestinian Authority lacks true sovereignty, having no army and no control over basic governmental functions like borders, airspace, and water resources. The PA, much like the black ‘homelands’ of South Africa, has symbolic value, but ultimate control over the daily lives of Palestinian remains in Tel Aviv’s hands.

Jewish settlers use, on average, as much water as seventeen Palestinians, filling swimming pools while Palestinians only miles away struggle to find drinking water. Occupying the most fertile lands, Jewish settlers enjoy great economic prosperity, while Palestinians face limited opportunity and unemployment estimated at 40%. With a ratio of three soldiers per settler, the Israeli army maintains tight control over the Palestinians, imposing curfews and arbitrarily detaining thousands, including 2500 children under the age of 18 since September 2000. To enable increased settlement expansion, Israel confiscates Palestinian land and demolishes Palestinian homes at alarming rates.

In addition, Israel has set-up a system of Jewish-only roads to facilitate transportation for settlers and soldiers. Forbidden from using these roads, Palestinians must pass through the hundreds of military checkpoints dispersed throughout the territories, where they are humiliated, delayed and sometimes even prevented from going to work and school. Moreover, Israel’s separation ‘barrier’, built largely on Palestinian land as opposed to the internationally recognized 1967 border, bisects Palestinian towns, separates Palestinian from Palestinian, encircles some villages, and effectively confines Palestinians to small ghettos and bantustans. In 2004, the International Court of Justice deemed the ‘barrier’, which spans 426 miles and rises up to 25 feet--- over twice as high and three times as long as the Berlin Wall---, illegal and called for its dismantlement.

Palestinian citizens of Israel, who comprise roughly 20% of Israel’s population and represent the descendants of Palestinian families who did not flee their homes in 1948, have also endured decades of segregation and institutionalized racism. Because the Basic Law of Human Dignity and Liberty defines Israel as a “Jewish” state rather than a state for all its citizens, state policies consistently favor Jewish citizens and, according to a 2004 U.S. State Department report on human rights, “[have done] little to reduce institutional, legal, and societal discrimination against the country’s Arab citizens.” The Law of Return, for example, grants automatic citizenship to Jews from anywhere in the world upon request, but denies that same right to native Palestinians and refuses to allow Palestinian refugees to return to their homes. Moreover, roughly 93% of the land in Israel is owned by the state or quasi-state agencies and cannot be sold to non-Jews. Non-Jewish Israelis also attend ‘separate but equal’ school systems, which categorically receive less funding than those for Jewish Israelis, and have less access to housing, subsidies, and employment. Palestinian villages, neglected by the government, often lack basic services. Israel has also expressly banned the unification of Palestinian families by preventing Palestinians from the Occupied Territories who are married to Palestinian citizens of Israel from gaining residency or citizenship status.

The deeply entrenched nature of Israeli apartheid indicates that ending the occupation is not enough to resolve the conflict; peace will only come when strong steps are taken to dismantle this unjust system. Given the great imbalance of power that exists today, with the United States firmly behind the Israeli government, only external pressure and an international campaign of boycott, divestment, and sanctions against Israel can bring Israel to the negotiating table and produce peace. While prospects for change seem bleak, Archbishop Desmond Tutu, Nobel Laureate and leading South African anti-apartheid activist, provides us with a glimmer of hope. After a visit to Israel and the Occupied Territories, he wrote, “Apartheid is back, complete with the ‘Separation Wall’ and bantusans. History, it seems, repeats itself. Yet, if peace could come to South Africa, surely it can come also to the Holy Land.”
