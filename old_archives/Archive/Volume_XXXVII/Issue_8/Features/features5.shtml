Time for Harris to Go?

by Stuart Baimel

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Stanford’s recent problems in football—a near-winless season the most prominent among them—began not this year, but two years ago in December 2004. In its infinite wisdom, Stanford somehow decided to hire University of Pittsburgh coach Walt Harris over Norm Chow, who was at the time USC’s offensive coordinator, as the new football coach.

Norm Chow. He coached two Heisman trophy-winning quarterbacks in Carson Palmer and Matt Leinart, the latter leading Chow’s offense to a 55-19 Orange Bowl pasting of highly touted Oklahoma, an offensive display and championship performance unmatched by a football team in recent memory. Some say that Chow, rather than Carroll, deserves credit for building a team that has gone 56-5 in the past five years, last year beating our own humble team 44-7 at halftime. Chow would have been an excellent choice to use the deep-ball talents of Trent Edwards, and knew the Pac-10 intimately, easing the learning curve considerably.

Stanford instead hired Harris, who had compiled a mediocre 56-44 record at Pittsburgh despite playing in what is widely regarded as a weak conference. Most in the Pittsburgh media thought he was going to be fired at end of the 2004 season, but Stanford, for some reason, was happy to take Harris off Pittsburgh’s hands. He replaced the fired Buddy Teevens, who went just 10-23 in his three seasons and is now head coach at Dartmouth. We’ll never know why Chow was not hired, but he was quickly snapped-up by an NFL team, the Tennessee Titans. Ted Leland, Stanford’s Athletic Director at the time, had hired Harris for his first head coaching job 16 years ago at the University of the Pacific, however. The connection between the two is clear.

Harris’s first season actually went pretty well. Stanford amassed a 5-6 record, a marked improvement over Teevens’s tenure. But Harris was working with Teevens’ players, such as the underutilized Trent Edwards, a probable NFL player next year. Stanford managed to win five games despite choking versus Notre Dame (in the old stadium’s final game), UCLA (blowing a 24-3 lead in the fourth quarter), and worst of all, D-II UC Davis, all at home.

This season, however, marked a sharp regression. Stanford did not win for 364 days, 11 straight games, finally beating Washington 20-3 despite another miserable offensive performance. A few statistics stand out from this season: 115th out of 119 D-I teams for rushing yards per game, 118th for points per game, and 118th for offensive yards per game. All of the other teams occupying the bottom of these lists are schools that play in non-big-time conferences and do not have the extensive financial resources that Stanford football possesses. The team has endured some truly crushing defeats this year: 48-10 at Oregon, 31-0 at UCLA, 42-0 to USC and 38-3 to Arizona State, the last two at home. Our defense is not any better than our offense. The USC game reminded one of the German Blitzkrieg on Poland. Our new, gorgeous stadium deserved better than a 37-9 drubbing at the hands of a mediocre Navy team. Stanford has consistently maintained the #2 spot on the ESPN “Bottom 10” rankings, only losing, or winning depending on your perspective, to equally hapless Duke.

The monotony of crushing defeat after crushing defeat has certainly worn on the players. ESPN.com reported that “Linebacker Michael Okwo, the Pac-10’s leading tackler and the Cardinal’s best player, didn’t show up for practice Wednesday and apparently intended to quit the team.” He returned to play in the Washington game, which ESPN.com described as a “humiliating 20-3 drubbing at the hands of winless, woeful Stanford inside a stunned and disgusted Husky Stadium is being billed as the worst defeat in program history.”

Whether or not Stanford even scores a touchdown against Cal (we won’t), Harris should have just one more season to prove his mettle before the firewaltharris.com blogs and websites start going-up. Teevens had three seasons and so should Harris. Harris, 6-17 after the end of this season, will have to win four games next year just to match Teevens, an unlikely event. Harris, termed an “offensive mind” (by the San Francisco Chronicle) coaches an offense that amassed just seven first downs against Washington, albeit a victory. Florida and Michigan can amass seven first downs in their first drive.

But Stanford can never expect to win 11 games, year on year, like Florida and Michigan do, or at least their fans expect them to. We have higher academic standards, a smaller student body, and a nonexistent football tradition that makes it difficult to recruit top players. We don’t accept junior college players, which is how many schools, like Nebraska, build championship-caliber teams. All of these restrictions are necessary and good, but we should be able to compete every week. Harris and Teevens posted very high graduation rates and no real scandals, but at Stanford, perhaps unlike schools like Miami, we should be taking that for granted.

Last year was a perfectly good season from this writer’s perspective; and with a better defense, it could have been a great season. We shouldn’t be expected to lose by 40 points to our rival every year, and we will lose by that much, even more, this year. We should be able to pull off the occasional upset of a ranked team, and we should be able to sell more than half the tickets to our home games. Stanford’s football team should generate enough interest that USC fans do not vastly outnumber Stanford’s at our own stadium.

Harris has one more season to prove that he can make Stanford’s football team competitive. Teevens had three years and so should Harris. This gives him enough time to prove himself by installing his system and recruiting his own players. Both 5-7 and 6-6 are acceptable records, even 4-8 might do, which would be a marked improvement over this year’s tepid performance. Here’s to next year.
