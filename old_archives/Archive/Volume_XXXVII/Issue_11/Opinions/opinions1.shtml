Editor’s Note: A Review Reflection

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

It’s a great honor to preside over a publication like The Stanford Review during its twentieth anniversary. A quick look through The Review’s archives provides only a tiny snapshot of the immense number of things our paper accomplished over the years.

Not only did The Review help take Stanford’s Speech Code to court (and win), but we convinced students to stop funding an ethnic organization with racist values, exposed unfaithful hunger strikers, and forced a change in the University’s distribution policy. Meanwhile, countless investigative news articles and insightful commentaries from Review writers provided an important counterpoint to the liberal political discourse that prevails at Stanford. We’ve successfully indoctrinated The Review into being an important part of the Stanford educational experience.

This 20th anniversary edition seeks to recognize the development of The Review’s voice since 1987. The next ten pages do not necessarily contain the most important articles from The Review, but rather thought to reflect the historical attitudes and idiosyncrasies of the staffers of The Stanford Review.

It’s rather clear that The Review of today confronts many of the same issues dealt with in previous volumes: multiculturalism, organized labor, curriculum politicization, and administrator incompetence to name just a few. Yet, the approach to each of these issues has changed—most likely as a function of the political climate at Stanford and the manners of Review staffers.

Peter Thiel and Norman Book, at the inception of The Review, were crusaders. The first few volumes of The Review challenged the liberal establishment head-on by writing incisive articles on Stanford’s Western Civilization curriculum, the loss of the Reagan Library, and the tyranny of President Donald Kennedy. The strength and vigor of Stanford liberals during that time required great zeal and courage on the part of The Review.

For better or worse, the current Stanford political climate is a lot more apathetic than it was twenty years ago. There are still a fair number of crazy radical liberals—and we still have our fun with them—but the majority of students just don’t care a lot about politics. As Features Editor Luukas Ilves describes in his article on the future of The Review, most students at Stanford “direct their attention to the more pressing concerns of their favorite television shows, their myriad of classes and activities, and landing that job or getting into grad school.” Discourse in The Review has changed to reflect these new attitudes of the average Stanford student.

Stephen Cohen, Editor-in-Chief of the 32nd volume of The Review attacked the problem of political apathy by using humor to generate interest in The Stanford Review. Cohen published articles such as “The Penis Dialogues” (a parody of “The Vagina Monologues”) while jokingly introducing himself as “Donald Rumsfeld’s longtime mentor” in his first issue and sponsoring a “Most Beautiful Conservative Woman Contest” (see pg. 6-7 for original advertisement for the ad). Cohen’s controversial wit was successful—renewing interest, familiarity and relevance for The Review among Stanford students.

David Stat, Ben Guthrie, Alex Medearis and I, as subsequent editors at The Review, each used our own techniques to generate interest in the content of our paper at Stanford. Ben, for example, put a strong emphasis on producing solid investigative news stories. I, as another example opted to adopt a more frequent publication schedule and include more general interest content in an effort to get people to pick-up a paper. Each of these different approaches, along with different staff members, resulted in a great diversity in content for just the past several years. A broader look—at all 20 years of The Review—reveals an even greater diversity of content.

I hope this special anniversary edition of The Review provides some perspective on the development of the Stanford conservative movement. Our archives serve as a great resource for recent Stanford political history. All of the major political issues and developments are notated in our pages. Additionally, as always, the writing is hard-hitting, ingenious, entertaining, and enjoyable. I promise satisfaction.

I’m confident that The Review’s content will continue to exhibit these qualities in the future. The next anniversary edition will, no doubt, contain even more excellent content. I’m very thankful that my predecessors developed such an amazing publication!

Fiat Lux!

Ryan Tracey,
Editor-in-Chief
