Icy Welcome Fails to Ruin Bush’s Stanford Visit

by Carl J. Kelm

May 2006, Vol. 36, No. 7

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On Friday, April 21, President Bush stopped at Stanford as part of his four-day California tour. While here, Bush conversed with Hoover Fellows and university officials about policy ideas in a relatively informal format. Originally, the discussion was to be held in the Lou Henry Hoover Building on campus. News of Bush’s visit spread quickly once released, and protesters gathered Friday afternoon to vilify Bush. Things turned ugly when, during the course of the protest, several demonstrators lied down in the street, preventing the President’s motorcade from approaching. The meeting was then moved to the home of George Shultz—Senior Hoover Fellow and former U.S. Secretary of State from 1982 to 1989.

According to Hoover Institute Director John Raisian, the Bush Administration contacted the institute about visiting Hoover while in the Bay Area. The Bush White House, like previous Republican administrations, has had cordial relations with the institute and holds close ties to many of its members. Several Hoover Senior Fellows, including Condoleezza Rice, Edward Lazear, and John Cogan, have served in positions of great power during Bush’s tenure as president. Rice, for example, is currently Secretary of State, while Lazear serves as the chairman of the President’s Council of Economic Advisers.

While in California, the President intended to meet old allies in a more laid back setting than the typical high-pressure situations of public life. As Hoover Institute Director John Raisian stated during an interview with The Review, the visit was made out of “an expression of friendship, not a feeling of need.” Raisian further stated that the discussion took on a round-table feel. Bush began the conference by enumerating his vision and strategy for his remaining time in office. When he had finished, the President fielded questions and heard the opinions of those present, which included approximately thirty Fellows, Stanford President Hennessy and Provost John Etchemendy, and others. A wide variety of topics were covered, from Iraq and Iran to Social Security and tax reform. When the meeting had finished, Bush stayed for dinner at Shultz’s house with a select number of guests. The entire process amounted to “down-time for the President,” according to Raisian.

But while the actual meeting can best be characterized as pleasant and friendly, the President received a frosty reaction from many in the community. Initial reports were that one thousand protesters turned out to share their objections with the President. The validity of this number, however, is questionable. Many people in the crowd were simply excited that Bush was coming and hoped to catch a glimpse of him. Indeed, there were even a few counter-protesters in the mostly hostile crowd. For example, The Stanford Review’s own Matt Martin—a former deputy editor—and Rahul Kanakia waved signs saying “Stay the Course” and “Thank You, Mr. Bush,” while chanting “Four More Years!” Martin and Kanakia were even interviewed by the media on scene and made it on the local ABC affiliate.

Quite a few of the actual protesters were not Stanford students, but older people from the Bay Area taking the opportunity to disparage the President. While many of these demonstrations were innocuous, some were borderline extreme. One individual was seen handing out “9/11 Deception Dollars” with the names of websites “proving” that the terrorist attacks were part of a giant conspiracy. Another radical protester held up signs claiming that the Neocons and the Jews are scheming to mold the world to fit their machinations. One of this man’s signs in particular said that America is “fighting Israel’s wars”—with the letter “S” replaced by a swastika. None of these protesters were students.

However, members of the Stanford community were not blameless in the affair. Multiple people ignored the barricades and lied down in the street, effectively preventing the motorcade from drawing up in front of the Hoover Institute, where the meeting was scheduled to be held. These protesters then refused to obey orders from the local police and the President’s security detail. Nor would they move when a fire engine was called to the scene, sirens blaring. After about forty-five minutes, the decision was made to simply hold the gathering at Shultz’s house, as the President did not want the situation to escalate or cause controversy. Indeed, police likely would have needed to use force in order to clear a route for Bush.

Fortunately for Bush and his companions, the change in location did not significantly affect the meeting’s quality. According to Raisian, the detour was “a minor disappointment,” but the President remained comfortable convening in the more informal environment. Essentially, “Bush was able to have his cake and eat it too”: the assembly still met the President’s expectations of a relaxed discussion and a sharing of ideas. The only significant loss was time—instead of ninety minutes or more, the summit could only last around seventy or seventy-five, according to Raisian.

While some see the activists who forced the move as heroic, many feel that their actions were unjustified and inappropriate. By ignoring the law, these individuals ceased to exercise the right to voice their opinions and infringed on the liberties of others. Senior Hoover Fellow Thomas Henriksen justifiably told The Stanford Review, “I think that those Stanford students who blocked the president’s motorcade violated my right to hear a speaker. Imagine the campus outrage if Hoover fellows laid down on university streets to block John Kerry from meeting with students.” Indeed, it is unlikely that the protesters in question would tolerate such behavior if the situation were reversed.

Though Fellows like Henriksen were rightly frustrated with the demonstrators’ actions, Hoover Director Raisian put the affair in perspective. He told The Stanford Review that he was “Incredibly proud of Stanford students and community.” Raisian did agree that “To physically interfere crosses a line,” but noted that the vast majority of onlookers respected the boundaries and were, for the most part, well-behaved. This is a welcomed sign, considering the role of universities as forums for discussion; to use physical action against those you disagree with violates the spirit of education and debate that defines a place like Stanford University. One can only hope that this blotch is erased from memory and that our community is remembered for its commitment to scholarship, not its intolerance of ideas.
