“Get the Hell Out of Here”: Zapata Resident Fellow verbally Assaults Review Distributors

by Ed Malone

May 1994, Vol. 12, No. 13

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

According to the Resident Fellow Position Description, a Resident Fellow is expected to promote pluralism and the free expression of ideas. “Resident Fellows encourage students to respect and learn from others whose backgrounds, ethnicity, or life experience differs from their own,” reads the document. “Through programming and by example, RFs actively promote inclusive communities where students of any race, class, gender, or sexual orientation may feel comfortable.” Last week, however, it became quite clear that José Burciaga was not doing his job.

In a blatant assault on the free expression of opposing viewpoints, Casa Zapata Resident Fellow José Burciaga discarded a large number of copies of The Stanford Review and forcefully prevented myself and two other students from distributing copies of the Review in his dorm. Not only did Burciaga throw out the papers meant for distribution in Zapata, but he threw out the papers we intended to distribute at other dorms in Stern Hall. In response to this, the Review has filed a formal complaint to the Stanford Judicial Affairs Office against José Burciaga and is currently exploring the option of filing legal complaints outside the jurisdiction of the University.

At approximately 5:40 p.m. Monday, May 9, Bob Dolbow, Jack Guerrero, and I entered Casa Zapata, the Chicano theme house, intending to distribute copies of the paper to each room as part of our designated distribution route.

Upon entering the first floor hallway in Casa Zapata, we were immediately confronted by Burciaga, who demanded to know what newspaper we were holding in our hands. When Guerrero told him that we were distributing copies of The Stanford Review, Burciaga shouted, “Get that trash out of here! All of you, get the hell out of here!”

Before we could respond, he charged at us, grabbed a bundle of our papers off of the floor, and threw them into a recycling bin right outside the dorm. Burciaga continued to shout, “All of you get the hell out of here!” as he took another bundle of the Reviews to the recyclery.
Burciaga then yanked approximately another 200 papers from Dolbow’s hands, proceeded to throw them away, and further demanded that we “get this trash out of here."

While Burciaga was outside the dorm throwing the papers out, a small crowd began to congregate in response to this commotion. Many of them yelled comments like, “get out of here,” “you’d better leave,” and “this is our home.”

After his third trip to the bin, Burciaga sprinted up to the stairwell and formed a human barricade, spreading his arms and legs in order to prevent Dolbow, Guerrero, and I from distributing the papers on the second and third floors.

Dolbow informed Burciaga that he was breaking Stanford policy in expelling the students. Burciaga yelled, “I don’t give a damn about University policy. I’m out of here.”

Next, Burciaga rushed to the telephone, exclaiming “I’m going to call 911!” By this time, about 50 residents of Casa Zapata – one half of the dorm’s population – had surrounded us in gauntlet fashion and continued to harass us, shouting insults and demanding once again that we leave.

Associate Dean of Student Affairs and Casa Zapata Resident Fellow Cecilia Burciaga then came to join her husband and her residents in their effort to throw us out of their dorm. “This is our home,” she said. “Just go. Please go.”

Burciaga returned from the phone booth and warned us that the police were coming. However, Dolbow, Guerrero, and I decided that we would wait on the police because we knew that Burciaga was in the wrong. I asked Burciaga, “Is this what you do when you don’t agree with a certain viewpoint?” Burciaga screamed at the top of his lungs, “Shut up! Shut up!”

When a police officer arrived at the dorm, he asked Dolbow, Guerrero, and me to step outside for a private conference.

At the request of the officer, we agreed on a compromise. Instead of distributing papers under all residents’ doors, Dolbow, escorted by the officer, placed 75 papers in the Casa Zapata common lounge. The moment that Bob and the officer turned their backs to depart, a Casa Zapata resident grabbed the 75 papers and left the area, with the obvious intent of throwing them away just as Burciaga had done.

Although no physical contact took place, we all felt threatened by Burciaga’s raging anger. Guerrero later exclaimed, “I felt threatened. He could have attacked any one of us.”

The sad irony in this case could not be more obvious: As a Resident Fellow of Casa Zapata, one to whom Stanford confers a great deal of benefits to supposedly foster open discourse and tolerance, José Burciaga could not have been more close-minded and intolerant of our opposing viewpoints. He has made a mockery of higher education at Stanford.
