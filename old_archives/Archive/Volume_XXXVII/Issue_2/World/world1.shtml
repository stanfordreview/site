Thai Coup D’état

by Tucker Herbert

World Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On September 19th, General Shondi Boonyaratglin led a coup against Prime Minister Thaksin Chinnawat in Thailand. The coup follows a long and smoldering conflict between the prime minister and the chief of state, King Phumiphon Adunyadet, and a close analysis of the aftermath of the coup suggests that the event was at the very least tacitly encouraged by the King. Thailand has considerable experience with coups, and so can handle them smoothly. The military knows how to do them right, and the population understands the protocol for “appropriate behavior” following a coup; as a result there has been no bloodshed.

In execution, the events of September 19th resemble the textbook model for a coup. As Wikipedia defines: “Tactically, a coup usually involves control of some active portion of the military while neutralizing the remainder of a country’s armed services. This active group captures or expels leaders, seizes physical control of important government offices, means of communication, and the physical infrastructure, such as streets and power plants. The coup succeeds if its opponents fail to dislodge the plotters, allowing them to consolidate their position, obtain the surrender or acquiescence of the populace and surviving armed forces, and claim legitimacy.”

The above section crisply summarizes the events in Thailand, as such a process has been followed all too many times before. While Prime Minister Thaksin Chinnawat was attending a United Nations meeting in New York, the military under the command of General Shondi rolled tanks through Bangkok and seized control of the Presidential offices, television stations, and main avenues. Thaksin was cut-off in the middle of his address to the public annulling the coup; and the military ran images of the King’s family in his place. Thaksin’s growing unpopularity in Bangkok meant that many greeted the tanks with flowers of gratitude. Some Thais are so lighthearted about the coup that the military had to ban go-go dancers from performing in front of armored tanks wearing camouflage outfits.

Shondi has proclaimed himself head of the Administrative Reform Council under the Democratic System with the King as Head of State. The logic of how a system can be “democratic” when the constitution has been abolished, un-elected leaders have seized power by force, the military is stationed at media outlets, and political gatherings have been banned, remains to be explained by those who rule this “democratic system.” One could suppose that the logic is similar to that of the Democratic Republic of the Congo, the Islamic Republic of Iran, or the People’s Republic of China. In the military’s public address they said that they did not intend to hold on to power for the long term, and apologized “for any inconvenience.”

In Thailand, the King, and by proxy, the military, acts to check and balance the power of the prime minister. Military coups were once rather frequent in Thailand, when the democratically elected executive failed, they would step in to realign the nation. And as the response of Bangkok shows, Thai conceptions of democracy are not quite Western. To many, this event is just a normal part of the political process. Any democratic system requires checks and balances to power—so why can’t that source of balance come from a King, who is a god, via the military?—some would ask.

First, allowing the military to seize power is never a sure bet to improve democracy. While the King has in the past intervened to maintain stability and prevent violence, this coup was unnecessary and counterproductive to Thailand’s democratic and economic aims. Thailand had gone 15 years without a coup and was consolidating its democratic institutions. By throwing a coup, the military squelched the possibility that the system could develop democratic institutions of checks and balances, thus perpetuating the coup cycle. Even if the Prime Minister had, as his opponents claim, overstepped his authority and used his power to advance his own economic interests, a change in power should have been acquired through democratic channels. Finally, there is no guarantee that the military will act upon its commitment to relinquish power in a year’s time.

Thailand’s loss of democracy does not bode well for the stability of other unconsolidated democracies in the region. Or perhaps, Indonesia and the Philippines could take from this experience an urgency to establish democratic institutions to check the power of the executive and establish a framework to keep the military out of politics. For the ruling military junta in Burma, the pressure to democraticize has eased. But once Thailand makes its transition back to electoral democracy, conceivably, Burma could one day follow its model for devolution of military rule. While, Thaksin is taking his rest in London, he is officially planning humanitarian projects. However, some Thais believe that Thaksin, with his capacity and drive, will not so willingly accept this expulsion from power. Thaksin, Shondi, and Phumiphon will continue to vie for authority in Thailand through one means or another.
