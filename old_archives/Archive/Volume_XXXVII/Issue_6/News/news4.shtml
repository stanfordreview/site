Important Controller Race Looms

by Matt Sprague

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

In the face of looming gubernatorial and congressional elections, small posts such as California State Controller can easily get swept under the rug. This forgotten election, however, can have a bigger impact on your life that it may seem.

First of all, what is a state controller? Though not an actual implementer of taxes, the controller has a vote on over 40 boards with tremendous sway on California tax policy, is overseer of cash-flow, and, quite literally, “controls” the state’s money supply. In fact, the state controller has a seat on the State’s Board of Equalization, often made up of two conservatives and two liberals--thus, the controller’s vote is often a tiebreaker on a board that heavily influences state tax policy. However, an equally important duty for the controller is the unlimited power to audit government agencies for fraudulent or questionable practices--a key distinction between controllers of different political inclinations is what direction the controller aims this weapon in.

The two mainstream candidates for the post of State Controller this year are, for the Democrats, John Chiang, a 44-year old lawyer with a finance background and ten years of experience on the state’s Board of Equalization, and for the Republicans, Tony Strickland, a 36-year old with six years of experience in the State Assembly for the Republicans. Many have noted the polarization of these two candidates, who are taking opposite sides on big ticket issues such as illegal immigration, gasoline prices, and education, as well as promoting their plans to impact such matters.

One of the largest differences between these two candidates is their views on oil excise taxes--Chiang favors a tax hike on gasoline that would fund research into alternative energy technologies, put forth on this upcoming ballot as Proposition 87. He believes that California “needs to drive investment into alternative and clean energy sources…The middle class is strained because we’re so dependent on fossil fuels.” This emphasis on spending for new projects, be they some of the bonds on November’s ballot or increased investment in the state’s welfare system seems to be a hallmark of Chiang’s positions.

Strickland takes an opposing view on the oil tax controversy, stating that “That’s a billion-dollar tax increase…when you raise taxes, you’re naive if you don’t think it’s going to be passed on to the consumer.” He argues that this tax will hurt California businesses and consumers, causing a potential economic slowdown. Strickland’s position on this oil tax is also characteristic of many of his other positions, which mainly are mainly for accountability of schools and other governmental organizations and in opposition to any tax hikes.

John Chiang has been endorsed by such notables as L.A. Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa, the San Francisco Chronicle, and a host of other newspapers and elected officials. The son of Taiwanese immigrants, Chiang attended the University of South Florida where he majored in finance, as well as the Georgetown University Law Center, where he obtained his law degree. His position on welfare has earned him a reputation of being a coddler of “California’s corporate welfare queens.” He favors many of the propositions and bonds put forth in this year’s ballot, from the gas excise tax to a variety of public building projects. Presumably, the funding for these projects would in some form or another be derived from residents of California.

On the other hand, Tony Strickland is the favorite of former gubernatorial candidate Bill Simon, State Senator Tom McClintock, and a variety of other people and publications. A political science graduate of Whittier College, Strickland served in the State Assembly for six years before being forced out by term limits. Since then he has run the California Club for Growth, a tax watchdog organization of high regard. He has a firm record of opposing tax increases and government spending, and plans to audit some of the most egregiously underperforming government organizations. He claims that if elected, ``the first thing I’m going to do is audit the L.A.U.S.D. (Los Angeles Unified School District). And we’re going to find out where the money is going and why it’s not going into the classroom.’’ Additionally, he plans to scrutinize the current welfare systems in place and ensure that no money is going to illegal immigrants, a hot button issue this election.

Currently the polls show John Chiang with a significant edge on Strickland, with the latest numbers showing a 36% to 30% lead. However, MercuryNews.com estimates that the Strickland campaign has $160,000 left to spend in these upcoming crucial weeks, whereas Chiang lags behind with $116,000. Both campaigns will be scrambling these next few weeks to get the name recognition that both candidates seem to lack with the general public; change in the current polls is not unlikely by any means.

The Stanford Review has endorsed Tony Strickland in the 2006 race for state controller because of his emphasis on fiscal responsibility and opposition to tax increases. Having just recovered from a serious budget deficit, profligate spending and high taxes would slow down a currently booming California economy and reverse the progress that has been made in the past few years. We agree that a thorough review of waste in the California education system is in order as well, since the state’s schools have consistently placed among the bottom ten in the country.
