Endorsements: State Cabinet

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

These are Stanford Review’s staff recommendations for the 2006 midterm elections. We endorsed a candidate or initiative only if one choice received an absolute majority of the staff’s vote. For votes that ended in a plurality, we made no endorsement.

Governor: Arnold Schwarzenegger (R)

Following ups and downs in approval over the past three years, incumbent Gov. Arnold Schwarzenegger (R) is seeking a full term as governor. His priorities for his next term include addressing the state’s continual budget shortfall as well as reforming the prison system and legislative redistricting. During his first two years, Governor Schwarzenegger has presided over a growing economy with half a million new jobs while cutting taxes. His opponent is state Treasurer Phil Angelides (D). Among other issues, Angelides supports increasing taxes on individuals and corporations making in excess of $500,000 per year and legalizing same-sex marriages. Both candidates have received significant criticism for engaging in negative advertising.

Lieutenant Governor: Tom McClintock (R)

California’s lieutenant governor casts tie-breaking votes in the state senate and theoretically presides if the governor is outside of the state. In this race, we endorse State Senator Tom McClintock of Thousand Oaks, who finished third in California’s 2003 recall election. McClintock is known to stick to his principles and would be a counterweight to the heavily Democratic state legislature. He has a reputation for fighting unnecessary taxes and standing up for his conservative views. The Democratic nominee is state Insurance Commissioner John Garamendi of Sacramento, who has experience as champion of the proposition that created his current job, as a state legislator, and as Deputy Secretary of the Interior in the Clinton administration.

Secretary of State: Bruce McPherson (R)

Incumbent Secretary of State Bruce McPherson (R) of Santa Cruz, appointed in 2005 to fill a vacancy, is now seeking a full term in the position. He previously served as both a State Assemblyman and a State Senator before losing to Cruz Bustamante in the 2002 Lieutenant Governor race for California.. Before politics, McPherson worked as the editor of a local newspaper owned by his family. His opponent is state Sen. Debra Bowen (D) of Marina del Ray, the current chair of the senate elections and reapproportionment committee, who has also served in the state legislature since 1992. We endorse Bruce McPherson for his emphasis on voting accuracy and the competence he has brought to a once scandal-ridden office.
  Treasurer: No Endorsement

California’s treasurer is the state’s banker, managing its investments, paying its bills, and chairing boards related to state finances. Here, the Republicans nominated a member of the state board of equalization, Claude Parrish of Long Beach. In addition to implementing a telephone hotline for people seeking tax help in Long Beach, he also sponsored legislation to put a three-year statute of limitation on parking tickets. As Treasurer, Parrish wants to cut back on waste by reducing out of state involvement in the state’s finances, allow small investors to buy tax-exempt municipal bonds, and limit passage of bond issues. The Democrats’ candidate is Hayward’s Bill Lockyer, the current attorney general, who has served in California government for thirty years. He greatly increased the use of DNA technology and paid special attention to protecting “those perceived as different.” Lockyer, interestingly seeking a horizontal shift in the state cabinet, is running to make investments in the economy and infrastructure, protect pensions, and increase health care coverage to all Californians.
Attorney General: Chuck Poochigian (R)

In the office left by Lockyer, two strong candidates emerged to fill the vacancy in a position that enforces state laws, manages the justice departments, gives legal advice to the state government, and oversees public attorneys. We endorse the Republican candidate, State Sen. Chuck Poochigian of Fresno, an attorney who has advised governors on judicial appointments. He plans to fight identity theft, protect minors from online predators and sex offenders, and work to deliver justice for victims of crimes. His opponent is the experienced Jerry Brown (D), who as Governor in the 80s presided over California’s largest increase in crime. His self-reinvention to win the race casts doubt on his integrity and suggests political motives.
Insurance Commissioner: Steve Poizner (R)

Vacated by John Garamendi for his run for lieutenant governor, the insurance commissioner position ensures insurance companies follow state laws, runs the California Department of Insurance, and helps the public manage their insurance. Steve Poizner (R) is a technology entrepreneur from nearby Los Gatos, whose work has enabled emergency services to track cell phone calls. He wants to use his experience as a businessman to increase competition between insurance agencies to reduce costs for consumers, prevent insurance companies from canceling coverage over legitimate claims, and keep uninsured drivers off the road. The Democratic nominee, Cruz Bustamante, is the former lieutenant governor under Gray Davis and finished second to Schwarzenegger in the 2003 recall election. He wants universal health care coverage and automobile insurance to be based off a driver’s record instead of location. Bustamante was criticized for his involvement in MEChA in the 1970s (a Chicano activist group). More recently, Poizner has gained an edge on Bustamante by refusing to accept money from the insurance industry. Bustamante, on the other hand, has not practiced the same ethics.
Controller: Tony Strickland (R)

The California Controller acts as a watchdog over state agencies by tracking spent money, issuing checks, conducting audits, and reporting on finances of the government. The seat is being vacated by Stanford alumnus Steve Westly, who lost to Angelides in the Democratic primary for governor. The Republicans’ nominee in this race is thirty six-year-old Tony Strickland of Moor Park, a former state legislator and current president of the California Club for Growth, a tax advocacy group. As controller, he hopes to audit state agencies that give benefits to illegal immigrants, roll back taxes on gasoline, and stop waste in the classroom. He has a strong vision for the office and wants to use the position as a bully pulpit to highlight and undermine particularly flagrant abuse of the taxpayers’ trust. While in the State Assembly, Strickland spearheaded the lawsuit against former governor Gray Davis to publicly disclose the amount of money California was spending on energy contracts. The Democratic nominee, Torrance’s John Chiang, is the chair of the state board of equalization. He aims to eliminate loopholes that advantage special interests, audit schools to target waste, and simplify tax filing.
