Endorsements: Propositions

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

These are Stanford Review’s staff recommendations for the 2006 midterm elections. We endorsed a candidate or initiative only if one choice received an absolute majority of the staff’s vote. For votes that ended in a plurality, we made no endorsement.

Proposition 1A: YES

Transportation Funding
Protection

Prop 1A limits the state’s ability to spend gas tax money on projects besides infrastructure. It mandates that money collected by California’s gas tax only fund transportation, infrastructure and road-related projects. We consider this a sensible measure that keeps the state from raiding transportation money to satisfy special interests and cover up for frivolous spending. The measure is supported by both Democrats and Republicans.

Prop 1B: NO

Highway Safety, Traffic Reduction, Air Quality, and Port Security

Proposition 1B authorizes $19.9 billion in general obligation bonds to fund state and local transportation improvement projects to relieve congestion, improve movement of goods, improve air quality, and enhance safety and security of the transportation system. We believe that this initiative provides for little accountability. It saddles the state with a large debt burden to build improvements that should be funded through the regular budgetary process. A fiscally responsible solution would be a “pay as you go” approach to funding much-needed transportation projects that doesn’t saddle our generation with the burden of repaying $32 billion over thirty years.

Prop 1C: NO

Housing and Emergency Shelter Trust Fund Act of 2006

$2.9 billion in bonds to fund housing for lower-income residents and development in urban areas near public transportation. Includes funding for veterans, the disabled, and working families. Proposition 1C won’t make housing more affordable for the average Californian. What it will do is grow government and force the average California family of four to pay over $600 in debt and interest while increasing pressure to raise taxes. Proposition 1C is a poorly managed entitlement program.

Prop 1D: NO

Kindergarten-University Public Education Facilities

$10.4 billion in general obligation bonds to fund repair and upgrade of public schools, including kindergarten through grade 12, community colleges, and state universities. Prop 1D funds expensive entitlements that local districts should pay for.

Prop 1E: YES

Disaster Preparedness and Flood Prevention

$4.1 billion in general obligation bonds to finance disaster preparedness and flood prevention projects at the state and local levels. Rebuilds and improves flood control structures, protects drinking water supplies. Prop 1E pays for necessary improvements that local jurisdictions can’t manage to fund themselves. Prop 1E is supported by both parties.

Prop 83: NO ENDORSEMENT

Jessica’s Law

Proposition 83 increases penalties for violent and habitual sex offenders and child molesters, prohibits residence near schools and parks. It also requires Global Positioning System monitoring of registered sex offenders. Supporters stress the importance of tracking and deterring sex offenders, while detractors cite the program’s $500 million price tag, the inefficacy of similar laws elsewhere, and the unfair punishment of non-violent offenders. Proposition 83 is supported by both parties.

Prop 84: NO

Water Quality, Safety and Supply. Flood Control. Natural Resource Protection.Park Improvements.

Prop 84 Funds water, flood control, natural resources, park and conservation projects by authorizing $5,388,000,000 in general obligation bonds. The initiative includes emergency drinking water safety provisions. This bond was placed on the ballot by special interests likely to receive taxpayers’ money if the bond is approved. This so-called “water and flood control bond” has no funding for dams or water storage and little funding for flood control. This initiative would spend billions without effective oversight. It is pure pork that claims to solve problems already addressed by prop 1E.

 

Prop 85: YES

Waiting Period and Parental Notification Before Termination of Minor’s Pregnancy

Proposition 85 amends the California Constitution by prohibiting abortion for an un-emancipated minor until 48 hours after a physician notifies the minor’s parent or guardian, except in cases of medical emergency or with parental waiver. The initiative also authorizes monetary damages against physicians for violating the law.

Prop 86: NO

Tax on Cigarettes

An additional tax of $2.60 per cigarette pack to fund new and expanded health services, health insurance for children, and expand tobacco use prevention programs. Increase tax revenues next year by $2.1 billion. The proposition is a front for hospitals to make billions of dollars through a tax hike while unfairly punishing smokers.

Prop 87: NO

Alternative Energy. Research, Production, Incentives. Tax on California Oil Producers.

Proposition 87 is ostensibly a $4 billion program to reduce petroleum consumption through incentives for alternative energy, education and training, funded by tax on California oil producers. It will bring in state oil tax revenues of $225 million to $485 million annually. Prop 87 has been widely derided for hurting California oil producers while making it easier and more economical to import more oil from abroad, increasing our dependence on foreign producers. Though the bill supposedly protects against price increases, it is liable to hurt consumers in a time of already high gas prices. The issue has attracted considerable attention from the significant amounts of money both supporters and opponents are spending. Steve Bing, a Hollywood producer, individually donated over 39 million dollars to the “Yes on 87” campaign. Many oil producers have put forth a large share of money to support the “No on 87” campaign, which also has tens of millions of dollars.
 

Prop 88: NO

Education Funding. Real Property Parcel Tax

Prop 88 imposes $50 tax on each real property parcel to provide additional public school funding for kindergarten through grade 12.Certain elderly and disabled homeowners are excluded from the tax. Prop 88 is simply another unfounded tax hike that sets a precedent for further such laws. Most schools won’t receive grants from this law, and it allows the state legislature to parcel out money as it sees fit. Prop 88 is opposed by both parties.
 

Prop 89: NO

Political Campaigns. Public Financing. Corporate Tax Increase. Campaign Contribution and Expenditure Limits.

Proposition 89 provides that eligible candidates for state elective office may receive public campaign funding by increasing tax on corporations and financial contribution/expenditure limits. It will raise $200 million annually in new taxes. Prop 89 allows politicians to finance ever larger and costlier campaigns, limits small businesses’ and nonprofits’ participation in politics and unfairly forces businesses to pay for views and campaigns they may disagree with. You’ve heard of pork for constituents, this is pork for politicians.
 

Prop 90: YES

Eminent Domain - Government Acquisition, Regulation of Private Property

Proposition 90 Bars state and local governments from condemning or damaging private property to promote other private projects and uses, limits government’s authority to adopt certain land use, housing, consumer, environmental, and workplace laws and regulations. Prop 90 is California’s response to the Supreme court’s monumentally foolish ruling in Kelo vs. New London, and reasserts the importance of property rights. The chief criticism of the measure is that it might allow too much room for frivilous lawsuits

  Santa Clara Measure A: NO

Measure A, also known as the Open Land Initiative, would amend the general land use policy in Santa Clara to greatly restrict construction on farmland. There has been neither an economic impact study nor an environmental impact study, and the ramifications of the bill are quite unclear. Though the Measure has been touted as a way to protect our Green spaces, it is more likely to serve special interests, who have modified individual lines in the opaque measure to benefit themselves. While this Measure does not actually affect Stanford, which operates under special zoning regulations negotiated with Santa Clara County, Stanford has disagreed with the spirit of the proposition. Larry Horton, the Director of Government and Community Relations for Stanford, noted that preemptively creating precise zoning regulations in an election complicates future needs for flexibility. “It’s generally unwise to do land-use by initiative, because there’s no way to change it,” he said. We agree; Measure A is an instance of legislation by the ballot box taken to its worst extreme.
