Endorsements: National and Local Positions

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

These are Stanford Review’s staff recommendations for the 2006 midterm elections. We endorsed a candidate or initiative only if one choice received an absolute majority of the staff’s vote. For votes that ended in a plurality, we made no endorsement.

 

Senate: No Endorsement

According to a Field Poll in October, incumbent Senator Dianne Feinstein (D) maintains a comfortable lead of 57% to 29% percent against Republican challenger Richard Mountjoy. The race has been uneventful, with both candidates easily winning their party’s respective primaries. Mountjoy, a former State Senator and current Immigration Control Consultant, is less-known than Feinstein, whose popularity will likely win her an easy re-election. It is worth noting, however, that both candidates endorse polar views on most issues, including the war in Iraq and tax cuts (supported by Mountjoy, opposed by Feinstein), as well as abortion rights and trade with China (supported by Feinstein, opposed by Mountjoy).

 

Congress 11th: No Endorsement

The race for the 11th pits incumbent Republican Richard W. Pombo against Democrat Jerry McNerney. Pombo, a California rancher by trade, has easily won the past 7 elections in this heavily Republican East Bay district. However, recent media exposure of Pombo’s ties with Jack Abramoff and Tom DeLay, his efforts to dismantle the Endangered Species Act, and his interest in restarting off-shore drilling in California, have all tarnished his image and weakened his chances. On the other side, McNearney, an engineer, Ph.D. in mathematics, and CEO of a wind-power company, has little political experience. His agenda includes a call for strategic redeployment in Iraq, as well as national strategies embracing economic globalization. Pete McCloskey, Pombo’s challenger in the Republican primaries, publicly stated that he will vote for McNearney in November.


Congress 14th: Bob Smith (R)

Republican Rob Smith will challenge Democratic incumbent Anna Eshoo for the right to represent the district home to the heart of Silicon Valley. Eshoo has held her seat since 1992, and has very comfortably won her last several elections. Smith, a local Republican activist and small businessman, names securing the borders, denying amnesty for illegal aliens, protecting property owners from eminent domain, and supporting Jessica’s Law as his agenda. Though he is unlikely to win, a vote for Smith is an important step toward making the once competitive 14th contested again.


State Assembly District 21: Virginia Chang Kiraly (R)

Incumbent Democrat Ira Ruskin faces Republican Virginia Chang Kiraly. Ruskin’s activities as an Assemblyman include the Chair of the Budget Subcommittee on Environmental Resources as well as Chair of the Committee on Environmental Safety and Toxic Materials. Kiraly brings commercial experience to the race, as a local entrepreneur, businesswoman, and political activist. She supports market-based solutions to California’s problems that emphasize and take advantage of Silicon valley’s technological know-how and vibrant business community. Kiraly is a fresh face in politics, confounds party stereotypes, and would bring much-needed youth and energy to the State Assembly.

 

Santa Clara District Attorney: No endorsement

A non-partisan race, both candidates have extensive experience in public law. Karyn Sinunu, the current top Prosecutor to the District Attorney, endorses incarceration of violent offenders, and better technology in the legal system to improve accuracy and accountability. Dolores A. Carr, the Superior Court Judge of Santa Clara County since 2000, and the Deputy District Attorney in Santa Clara from 1985 to 2000, would like to improve infrastructure for receiving feedback from the public, as well as improving the efficiency of resolving court cases.
