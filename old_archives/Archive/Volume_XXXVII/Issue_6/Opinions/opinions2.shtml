Policy Interpretation Risks Reputation

I support the distribution of all student publications, including the Stanford Review, which provides a valuable outlet for the conservative voice on campus. However, I believe that an interpretation of Stanford’s non-distribution policy as a surreptitious means of excluding certain student perspectives, including the conservative voice, risks discrediting the Review’s reputation.

The restriction on distribution of student publications in the dorms is meant first and foremost to maintain cleanliness in the dorm. These provisions exist not only for the benefit of those of us who prefer a tidy living environment but also out of respect for our custodial staff who is often burdened by abuse by residents who leave trash and furniture outside of their rooms as if they have a right to infringe upon the shared living space of others.

The Review was admonished for distributing to the dorms for these reasons, not because of their conservative voice. I remember walking out of my dorm finding copies of the Review at my door and at every single other door in my hall. While it may have been convenient that morning, those copies of the Review remained littered across my hall for days before the custodial staff was forced to go around and pick all of them up. By the end of this debacle, I was more than a little miffed by the Review - and I believe that I was not the only one.

The Review jeopardizes its reputation in this battle with the administration. All other campus publications are constrained by the same non-distribution policy, so the argument that the Review is being unfairly silenced because of its conservative voice is a moot point. An effort to spin this issue discredits the Review and its authors, especially at a time when the Review should be working to overcome similar misrepresentation allegations made against its content.

I respect the Review as a publication and would like to continue to read your material. However, know that the continued attention you pay to this issue will only frustrate readers and nonreaders alike. And please do not recast an issue of campus maintenance as one of free speech - the Review risks damaging its reputation in the process.

Theo Milonopoulos
Faculty Senate Student Representative At-Large

 

 

Baha’i Faith is Independent of Islam

John Komkov’s September 22 article on the Baha’i Faith should be commended for drawing attention to a religion that, in spite of its progressive teachings, has yet to reach the awareness of the mainstream American public. Unfortunately, a central premise of the article, that the Baha’i Faith is a form of “Protestant Islam,” is incorrect and misleading. This premise is contradicted by the central claim the Baha’i Faith advances, by the teachings it upholds, by the diversity of the religious backgrounds of its adherents, by the perception the Baha’is have of their own Faith, by the nature of the activities they engage in within their communities, and by the considered views of informed scholars. The Baha’i Faith arose within an Islamic religious and cultural milieu and it recognizes the divine origin of Islam, however, its connection to Islam resembles the link between Christianity and Judaism, which share a similar relationship. The Baha’i Faith is no more a denomination of Islam than Christianity is a denomination of Judaism.

The Baha’i Faith is an independent religion that claims to fulfill the promises of those that have gone before it. It upholds the unity of God, recognizes the unity of His Prophets, and inculcates the principle of the oneness and wholeness of the entire human race. Its claim to an independent divine revelation is one of the key reasons that it is bitterly persecuted in many Islamic countries. It has its own principles, laws and administrative structure that are entirely distinct from those of Islam. While confirming timeless spiritual truths, it introduces a range of new teachings, such as the free and independent investigation of truth, the fundamental harmony of scientific and religious truth, the abolition of priesthood, and the full equality of men and women. It also unequivocally repudiates the notion of holy war. The great majority of the adherents of the Baha’i Faith, which stem from more than 2,000 ethnic and tribal groups, come from non-Muslim backgrounds. The focus and functioning of Baha’i community life bears little resemblance to that of Muslim communities.

The Baha’i Faith has a long history at Stanford. In particular, ‘Abdul-Baha, the son of the founder of the Baha’i Faith, visited the campus in 1912 at the invitation of David Starr Jordan and gave an address to the entire student body. Stanford Review readers may be interested in reading the text of that address, which touches on some of the fundamental principles of the Baha’i Faith, at http://www.stanford.edu/group/bahai.

Sincerely,

The Baha’i Association at Stanford
