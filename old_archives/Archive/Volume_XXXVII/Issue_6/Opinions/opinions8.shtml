’94 Déjà Vu?

by Tim Hyde

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

“The Democrats’ nightmare goes something like this: the party that broke a dozen years of frustration by winning the White House in 1992 awakens Nov. 9 to find itself transformed overnight into the minority part in Congress.” This was an opening line in Congressional Quarterly Weekly, October 22, 1994, on the eve of the Republican congressional sweep in the midterm elections. Now the tables seem to have turned, with Republicans looking vulnerable in midterm congressional races across the country. In light of polling data that suggests Democrats have a chance to retake the House and possibly the Senate, major news organizations and professional pundits are looking to answer the question: will 2006 be like 1994?

There are a number of explanations for what happened on that November day in 1994 when voters nationwide cast out 36 Democratic congressional incumbents and handed control of both houses of congress to the GOP for the first time since the 1950’s. Some theories revolve around President Clinton’s unpopularity at the time, especially in relation to the budget and the failed initiative for universal health care. Republicans successfully painted the Democratic leadership as corrupt; in much the same way, Democrats are trying to portray many Republican incumbents as members of a crooked establishment.

1994 saw a major shift in the voting patterns of the GOP’s base. Exit-poll data published in the New York Times a week after the 1994 election revealed that core constituencies—notably Southern whites and white “born-again” Christians—had turned out for the GOP in large numbers and had voted with an increasingly Republican bent. The data showed that Republican congressional support among Southern whites had jumped from 53% to 65% and among white evangelicals had jumped from 66% to 76%. With this support, Republican congressional candidates surged to victory in the South, unseating eight incumbent Democrat house members and James Sasser, Democratic senator from Tennessee. This solidified a trend of Republican dominance in the region.

Among the issues most important in the races nationwide was the controversial Clinton budget that passed through Congress in 1993 without the support of a single Republican vote. Many Democratic representatives had this unpopular budget hung around their necks. In this year’s midterm election, many Democratic challengers are trying to link GOP incumbents to the increasingly unpopular war in Iraq.

But a crucial difference between this year’s midterm and the one in 1994 is that there were many more open seats in 1994 than there are today. Partisan re-drawing of congressional districts in several states, especially in Texas, California, and Georgia, have made fewer races competitive each decade. Despite the Republicans’ vast “sweep” in 1994, incumbents had a whopping 90% reelection rate. The relative scarcity of open seats this year—only 31 as compared to 52 in 1994—means that Democrats will have to work harder, and against tougher opponents, to retake Congress.

A few examples seem to suggest a political pattern that would imply the country is in the midst of a political realignment akin to that of 1994. These patterns emerge in the progressive Northeast, the Democrats’ analog to the conservative South that came home most resoundingly for the Republicans in 1994. In New York and Connecticut, two states where President Bush’s approval rating is in the high 20’s among political independents, there are nearly a dozen races featuring endangered Republican incumbents who have been surviving in relatively liberal environs for years. In Connecticut, Chris Shays, Nancy Johnson, and Rob Simmons, three moderate Republican representatives, are all in the fights of their political lives. Their challengers are trying to tie them to locally unpopular President Bush and their votes for the war in Iraq. Some pundits are predicting that New York’s congressional delegation could be almost wholly Democratic in the next Congress—only two New York Republican house members seem to be completely safe in their seats for November 7.

Around the country, the “Foley effect” may push several close races into the Democrats’ column. Also underlying several house races nationally is a potential shift among “Reagan Democrats” as noted by Republican pollster Frank Luntz. He recently told the New York Times “the Santorum (Pennsylvania Senate) race shows that Reagan Democrats are returning to their roots. Economic issues among blue-collar social conservatives are now subsuming concerns about social issues.”

The outcome of November 7 will lead to recriminations among the party that does not live up to expectations. If Democrats fall short of retaking the house, despite the favorable political winds of an unpopular war and a Republican sex scandal, they will descend into an argument of missed opportunities and electoral incompetence. If Republicans lose the house, questions will arise as to who lost the base and whether the Republican Revolution has come to an end. But whatever the outcome, the diverse stories of various districts suggest that this is not truly a national wave in the mold of the ’94 election. With fewer open seats and entrenched incumbents, it remains to be seen whether a widespread national political realignment, liberal or conservative, can occur in a midterm election.
