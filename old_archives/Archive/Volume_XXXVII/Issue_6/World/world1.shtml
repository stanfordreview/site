Asian Inclinations for the American Election

by Chris Seck

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

As a student, my father once had to write an examination essay on the question: “Should the world be allowed to elect America’s leaders?” The exam’s point was that the world has a stake in the outcome of America’s democratic process because of the United States’ global pre-eminence.

Asia’s leaders are mostly concerned with how America’s election will impact U.S. trade and foreign policy. This article will examine how several nations—China, India, and Indonesia—might view the 2006 midterm elections.

This analysis relies on several major, but important assumptions about what Republicans and Democrats stand for:

On the foreign policy front, if the Democrats win a majority, they will support liberal internationalism: a deeper commitment to multilateralism, greater deference for global organizations like the U.N., and a general preference for diplomacy over unilateral military action. Should the Democrats win in November, they will revert to a foreign policy similar to that of the Clinton era.

In contrast, should the Republicans maintain their majority, they are most likely to support the Bush Doctrine: promoting democracy in strategic regions, the policy of pre-emptive war, and a preference for unilateral military action when multilateral solutions are found unacceptable.
On the trade policy front, if the Democrats win a majority, they will generally continue to support free trade, but this support will be ameliorated by the pro-union, fair trade, and anti-Wal-Mart lobbies that many blue-collar Democrats support. Many prominent Democrats such as Dick Gephardt continue to promote populist economic views.

In contrast, the Republicans have a slightly stronger belief in free trade. Although President Bush initially passed steel tariffs in 2001, he quickly repealed the tariffs. The Bush administration has negotiated a flurry of trade agreements, including the Central American Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA). Although some top Democrats are pro-free trade, the GOP as a whole has a more Ricardian stance on trade issues than the Democratic Party.

How would this above information be of interest to major Asian nations? By examining the evidence, we can speculate on their leaders’ attitudes. Two major nations, China and India, are likely to favor the status quo: a Republican majority.

First, China’s leaders are likely to favor a Republican victory: China owes much of its prosperity to the Bush administration’s almost dogmatic stance on trade and investment. In 2005, for example, China’s trade surplus with America stood at $716 billion—an all time high. This year, economists predict that it will be even higher. China’s economy has grown by an average of 10% and its GDP has overtaken Britain to become the world’s 4th largest. The Bush administration’s economic policies favor China.

Equally, China favors the Bush administration’s foreign policy, which is largely focused on the Middle East, allowing China to strengthen its security position. In 2001, China signed a “Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation” with Putin’s Russia. In 2003, China settled a major border dispute with India and is expected to become India’s largest trading partner within the next decade.

India’s political ties with America have grown since 9/11, in part aided by the influence of approximately 2 million Indians who live in America, and in part aided by the Bush Administration’s desire to develop a pro-Western ally in Asia. The outsourcing of American jobs benefits New Delhi as millions of jobs have been created in India. In July this year, President Bush signed an agreement to share U.S. nuclear technology with India, even though India refused to commit to U.S. requests to cancel a planned $4 billion pipeline delivering natural gas from Iran. India views the Bush administration and the Republican Congress in a positive light.

Finally, Indonesia, the world’s most populous Muslim-majority nation, faces a balance of goals. Indonesia has suffered many terrorist attacks since 9/11: the 2002 Bali bombings, the 2003 Marriott Hotel bombing, the 2004 car bombing at the Australian embassy, and a second series of bombings in Bali in 2005. Many Indonesians dislike current U.S. foreign policy and believe that Indonesia’s support for the War on Terror has made it a vulnerable target for extremists. And although Indonesia’s economy has done well, in part due to booming trade with America (the Jakarta stock market was up more than 40% in 2004 and the Indonesian economy grew nearly 6% in 2005), the differences in Republican and Democratic stances on trade is small compared to their differences in foreign policy. Therefore, Indonesia is likely to favor a Democratic victory, which could help reduce Indonesia’s unpopular foreign policy obligations to America.

Many Asians believe the invasion of Iraq was arrogant and that America should not impose its values (including democracy) on the world. In 2005, a BBC poll stated that globally, about 58% of people in 21 countries believe that President Bush’s re-election had made the world a more dangerous place.

Fortunately, the commoners’ angry passions do not necessarily determine what their governments do. Asia’s leaders—cold, rational, and calculating—are the ones whose opinions matter most. The leaders do not allow raw emotions to dictate how they view the U.S.; their sole concern is how America’s behavior affects their national interests. And where national interests are concerned, Asia’s leaders obey Nietzsche’s dictum: “The state is a cold monster.”
