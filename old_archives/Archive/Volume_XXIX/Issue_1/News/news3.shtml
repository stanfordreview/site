The Best of Times: The Boom and Bust Years of America Before and After Everything Changed
by Jen Loomis
Staff Writer

Haynes Johnson opens The Best of Times with a gripping account of Gary Kasparov's loss to Deep Blue in 1997. He lets the simple statement of a devastated Kasparov speak for the event's significance: "I'm a human being. When I see something that is well beyond my understanding, I'm afraid." So is Mr. Johnson, and he thinks we should share his fear.

The Best of Times is ostensibly a social history of Nineties America, a period Mr. Johnson declares significant not for its unprecedented peace and prosperity, but for its wasted opportunities. To him, Bill Clinton is symbolic of America as a whole: an abundance of promise lost via a combination of self-indulgence and trivial distractions.

Mr. Johnson divides the Clinton years into four chronologically overlapping themes: "Technotimes," which covers scientific advances and the technology boom, "Teletimes," which primarily examines the O.J. Simpson trial, "Scandal Times," which details the Clinton-Lewinsky debacle, and "Millennial Times," which wraps up the period with Bush's controversial election. Though Mr. Johnson is dedicated to his thesis, identifying sources of missed opportunity and distraction at every turn, his criticisms reduce to a contention with one defining characteristic of the era: an all-pervasive lack of fear. Once this is recognized, The Best of Times seems not so much a survey of the Clinton years as a compendium of Mr. Johnson's fears -- and his struggle to understand why America does not share them.

Mr. Johnson has many fears, but he makes it quite clear that a free market economy tops the list. In "Technotimes," he stresses that the real credit for the incredible advances in science and technology lie not with the recent tech boom and its much celebrated geek millionaires, but with the government's commitment to basic research begun under FDR. He warns that tax cuts and mega-mergers will lead to downturn unless the government intervenes. The theme continues in "Teletimes," where he laments the nation's fascination with celebrity scandals, over real news. Here Mr. Johnson appears to have found an reason for America's apathy: if only they weren't so distracted by O.J. (or JonBenet or Monica) the American people would surely be fretting over the under-regulation of the biotech industry or out picketing Microsoft headquarters. Not surprisingly, he once again finds a lack of government oversight to blame, this time citing the Reagan era deregulation of radio and television, with which "the public-interest standard gave way to the actions of the unfettered marketplace." More interesting is his condemnation of the increasingly popular use of the internet as a news source; his fear that "on the Internet, everyone can play the role of reporter...everyone can claim to present supposedly reliable information as "news" when in fact it may be inaccurate and uninformed" betrays either a basic misunderstanding of the nature of the Internet or a remarkably pessimistic view of the judgment capabilities of his fellow Americans (or, more likely, both.)

From here, Mr. Johnson naturally proceeds into "Scandal Times," in which he spends over two hundred pages discussing the Monica Lewinsky affair in excruciating detail. After denouncing Clinton for this ultimate act of self-indulgence, he spends the bulk of his time distributing blame between the media, the Internet and the "vast right-wing conspiracy." Mr. Johnson inexplicably proclaims the public's continued support of Clinton to be the saving grace of the whole affair, leaving one to wonder how a group so unsophisticated as to require government protection from the excesses of the media could figure out that the media was wrong.

Despite these flaws, Mr. Johnson is a writer of undeniable skill, with a particularly remarkable knack for explaining the background, characters and tone of a scene so well that the reader not only understands what is taking place, but seems to experience it (unfortunately many if not most Americans are not yet eager to relive, say, the Lewinsky scandal.) In the eighteeen months since the first publication of The Best of Times in the Spring of 2001, some of his fears have proven remarkably prescient, above all his warnings of the building hatred of America among Islamic fundamentalists.

Clinton, Johnson frequently reminded us, was the first multi-term democratic president since FDR...and perhaps it is here that we can find the real source of Johnson's missed opportunity obsession. In Clinton he saw not just any president but a potential FDR. He saw potential for legislation of New Deal proportions, returning this society he no longer understood to a more familiar state. His inability to accept that this period was in fact "the best of times" seems to have stemmed from his inability to even consider that the Reagan's laissez-faire principles might have worked.
