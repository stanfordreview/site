Stanford Peace Group, Greens, Muslims Rally Against Congress War Resolution
by Harrison Osaki
Staff Writer

On Thursday, October 3rd, 2002, a coalition of several Stanford groups led by the Stanford Coalition for Peace and Justice (SCPJ) held a "Words Against War" rally in protest about the House and Senatorial votes scheduled to be held later that month on resolution for war in Iraq. The other organizations sponsoring the rally included the Stanford Green Party and the Muslim Student Awareness Network (MSAN). Students were supplied with cell phones and phones numbers for the White House and California senators and House representatives. Although they were told that they could say anything they wished, would-be callers were also supplied with scripts to read criticizing the Bush administration's policies on issues ranging from the upcoming Congressional vote regarding the war in Iraq to Bush's request for a strong U.N. resolution against Iraq. Students who participated in the call-in had their votes tabulated and the overall results were apparently recorded for posterity.

The rally against military conflict began under a large banner reading, "Bush, Cheney, Rumsfield -- Guilty of 9/11". The opening speaker began with a denunciation against America and its involvement in the deaths of "hundreds of thousands" of civilians. The speaker brought up the American-supported war in Columbia against the drug trade, which he described as not being a war on drugs but on "civilians trying to live and fight a right-wing government." Mr. Johnson also described America as waging "at least ten wars. . . .on the rest of the world", including the war involving Israel and Palestine, which the speaker condemned as a war through a proxy government against Palestine for the last thirty years, and the war in the Philippines, again against civilians.

The first Spoken Word artist was a man by the name of Shahid Buttar, a member of the Spoken Word Collective. Mr. Buttar began by nostalgically remembering, to a largely placid audience of ten to twenty people, the 1960's and how the people of that time knew of their "obligation to represent". He then launched into a freeform rap criticizing patriotism with rhymes such as "flags like rags on pickup trucks", and "talk about our loss, having used the world as floss". Over the course of a total of three pieces Mr. Buttar described terror as historically being "the arm of the state used against the populace" and applied that definition to modern-day America, explained the cause of our "paradigm shift in economic exploitation" and our change in attitude to that of a war footing while denouncing both, and compared our government to Nazi Germany, ". . . .at least Hitler was democratically elected." The pieces ended with a criticism of the "yuppies and their social contract", critiquing the passivity that permeats the Stanford campus.

The second speaker, a MSAN officer named Muhammed Abdallah, spoke against the American sanctions in place against the Iraqi regime. Asking the question, "Is it because we care about people?" Abdallah complained that sanctions often indiscriminately hurt people under the regime being attacked. As an example, he maintained that the Kurds' society has been wrecked due to the American sanctions placed upon Iraq. He also censured America's working with Saddam Hussein despite the fact that we "knew expressly and exclusively that Saddam used chemical weapons versus his own people." Accompanied by a cry from one of the rally workers to "Impeach Bush!" Abdallah concluded that America should cease its "unprincipled approaches in everything" and stop "support[ing] the most oppressive regimes around the world."

The next performer, an artist by the name of Xochitl Johnson, attacked the "pervasion of fear and dissent" in America. Johnson's speech attacked many important American political figures, from those who "tore Afghanistan into pieces" to John Ashcroft and Tom Riddle for "rounding people up" and putting them into "detention centers" and deporting them. Johnson compared the American experience to that of a Holocaust survivor's account of life under Nazi Germany, emphasizing the quote from the survivor that ". . . .if I knew what I know today, I would go running through the streets." This was compared to the governmental harassment of the radio channel KABL, which had made controversial comments on the air. In apparent reference to former president George Bush's State of the Union address in 1991, Johnson also criticized the "dark new world order" which America was instituting upon the rest of the world. The speech/rap ended with Johnson's pronunciation that he did not necessarily stand with America--he stood with "the six billion people of this world."

At this point, about forty minutes into the rally, a hundred or so people in total had gathered to watch the spectacle in White Plaza. Most people, however, stood and watched--and walked away. Few people seemed to be using the cell phones, and this would remain constant through the rest of the rally.

The groups also passed out pamphlets, one of which advertised a book by one Nafeez Mosaddeq called The War on Freedom: How and Why America was Attacked on September 11, 2001. The pamphlet begins with the phrase, "Piercing the smoke and mirrors of propaganda, misinformation and the largest psychological operation ever pulled on humanity, the book calmly and meticulously examines the fact and documents the need for an open, public inquiry into 9/11." The brochure calls upon readers to not be fooled by the politicians who have "lied, fabricated, and covered [the 9/11 incident] up", and accuses governmental agencies of providing incorrect and contradictory information. For example, the pamphlet questions how a letter providing instructions which officials used to link the terrorists together was found within the wreckage of two of the planes and in a misplaced piece of luggage; the leaflet goes on to say that, "Later we discover that six of the hijackers are still alive, one has been dead for two years--their identities stolen. Yet their faces continue to appear in the press. . . .The extremely shoddy nature of this investigation clearly confirms that it was put together to put our minds at rest, and to cover-up something they don't want us to see." The pamphlet finishes with the words, "Dare to Ask Questions. . . .An Informed Citizenry IsThe [sic] Foundation of Our Democratic Freedom. (Otherwise it's just Free-dumb)."
