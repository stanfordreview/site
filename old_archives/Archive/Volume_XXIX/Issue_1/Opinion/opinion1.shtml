Opinion
Democrats Should Support an Attack
by Stephen Cohen
Guest Columnist

The Democratic Party has had a long history of political ambivalence and indecision when it comes to matters of war. In 1991, we warned of how the world's oil markets would collapse, thousands of Americans would die, and how Iraq could quickly become the Vietnam of the Middle East. When it came time to vote, a large majority of Democrats voted against authorizing force to backup the UN resolution that would liberate Kuwait. The war was not a Vietnam; it was not even a Korea. After committing troops to the ground it took less than 100 hours to liberate Kuwait and crush what remained of Iraq's once feared army. This political victory gave back 10 house seats to the Republicans in 1992 and left many Democrats looking overly paranoid and 30 years in the past. It is time for us to stand up as Democrats and embrace a war resolution rather than fear it.

Diplomacy should always precede war. Although many have called for renewed diplomatic overtures towards Iraq, we must remember that we have been diplomatically engaged with Iraq since 1991, and it has yielded us nothing but disappointment. The Iraqi government has habitually lied and deceived us and the international community at most every opportunity. A few examples: The same day last month in which Iraq promised to get in line with UN law and readmit inspectors it fired at the allied planes enforcing the same no fly zones it agreed to in 1991. Although over 48 scud missiles, 40,000 chemical munitions and 600 tons of chemical warfare agents were found by inspectors before they were kicked out, Iraq's Deputy Prime Minister Tariq Aziz recently boldly proclaimed "Everyone in the world should no there are no weapons of mass destruction in Iraq." Their pattern of continuous lying and deception makes them a bad partner for political dialogue.

Iraq agreed to the terms laid out by the Security Council to voluntarily disarm, make a full declaration of all of its weapons, and allow a regime of inspections to verify the authenticity of their claims. They have reneged on all of these claims and this unfulfilled promise is the basis of any action in Iraq.

There are larger foreign policy objectives at stake here as well. The Democratic Party has traditionally viewed backing the United Nations as an important foreign policy goal. Believing that a strong international forum will result in long lasting peace, the Security Council has been held as the definitive voice of international resolve. Yet the integrity and validity of the Security Council is at stake in this current conflict. In 1991, America was fighting a war. This war ended only because Iraq signed a contract with the Security Council in resolution 687. Ten years later the contract that allowed Iraq to end a war has been entirely unfulfilled. If Security Council mandates as important as those that define the terms of surrender of another nation are allowed to remain completely unenforced, the UN will cease to serve as an effective diplomatic forum. Why should the dictators of the world fear UN intervention when all that is needed to avoid conflict are a few empty promises and a habit of compulsively lying.

Many argue that it is simply not the right time for war in Iraq. It is bad timing. We should have done something in 1995 when inspectors were denied access to Saddam's presidential palaces. We should have done something more in 1998 when weapons inspectors were forced out of Iraq. But just because we have made the error of inaction in the past doesn't mean we are condemned to repeat it now. Iraq has continued to show a pattern of lying and deceit to the international community. If we choose to reward this pattern through inaction, we will be helping to perpetuate exactly what we should stop. But if we act, we can definitively prove to the world that a promise to Security Council should not be taken lightly.

Stephen Cohen is a Sophomore majoring in Computer Science. He is a California native, an advocate of gay rights, and is pro-choice, positions he holds in common with much, but not all, of the Review Staff.
