Front Page
Stanford Students: "Don't Attack Iraq"
by Mark Zavislak
Editorial Staff

Stanford students are a peaceful group, even relative to the Bay Area, as we see from these charts.
A poll conducted for the Associated Press found that a majority of Americans support future military action in Iraq. Historically, students on college campuses have had views on war widely diverging from the mainstream, almost exclusively in favor of peace. College opinion on the possible war against Iraq is no different in this respect.

Led by Opinions Editor Nels Hansen, Review staffers conducted their own poll of the Stanford campus. The results indicate that a mere 14 percent of Stanford students believed that America should go to war against Iraq, giving an affirmative response to the question "Should the United States take military action to remove Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein?" This is in contrast to 74 percent of Stanford students who say that the U.S. should not take military action against Saddam Hussein. 12 percent of students answered that they did not know or were not sure.

Compare this to the Associated Press poll which concluded that 59 percent of Americans were in support of military action with just 29 percent opposed and 12 percent not sure. The difference between 59 percent and 8 percent is quite significant, even with regards to the small sample size of Stanford students, 50, and the poll error margin of 10 percent.

In the second question, poll respondents were asked if they would "support a ground attack on Iraq if President Bush decided to launch one?" More students answered yes to this question than the last one, such that about 24 percent of the student population would support President Bush. 74 percent said they would still be opposed to a ground attack, with only 2 percent not sure.

Again, this is compared with a large majority of Americans, 64 percent, saying they would support a ground attack on Iraq, with only 28 percent opposed. Since more people would support an attack if President Bush decides one is necessary, it indicates a small portion (approximately 10 percent) of both populations would be swayed by patriotism to support the war.

Stanford students were more evenly divided over why they thought Bush has been pushing for a war in Iraq more recently. 40 percent believe that Bush is doing so for genuine reasons of national security, whereas 56 percent believe is doing so for political reasons. But statistically, students are split evenly.

Students on Stanford's peace-discuss list like Stanford Physic's Staff Mandeep Gill lambasted the Bush administration for what they called its "war-for-oil policy". Other students suscribed to the view that the desire to attack Iraq was politically motivated, and acted as a "distraction from the economy and our own problems." Still others professed a complete lack of understanding for "why people think they have the right to decide who is evil and who is good," and insisted that this "egotistical" view of the world is causing the conflict.

A La Rouche supporter had another point of view on the conflict. "The International Jewish Community and Wall Street conservatives are teamed up to conceal the crimes of Israel, and to make Iraq look bad," to the benefit of both, he suggested to an environmental-action list.

Still more students had other perspectives. Said Stanford Junior Michael Calhoun, a self-described pacifist, "I just cannot support taking the life of another, for any reason." Regardless of the economic cost, he would avoid war, he explained.

The fact that a potential war in Iraq gets so little support on campus may explain why there is a lack of activism, with respect to anti-war protests. The Stanford campus is so overwhelmingly against action in Iraq that protests mostly fall on ears that already agree. Moreover, because this is only a potential war, it is additionally difficult to protest something that is not either happening yet nor necessarily going to happen.

Except for sporadic gatherings, supporters of a potential war have yet to be making a scene on campus. Perhaps the beginning of military action against Iraq will invigorate campus activism. In the meantime, liberal groups are holding rallies. According to Joe Fairbanks, co-refounder of Stanford's College Republicans, Stanford's conservative community is planning to hold a "Liberate Iraq" rally, the details of which are TBA. [see article on page 6, "Stanford's College Republicans"].

The only large public show of support for a war in Iraq that has been visible on campus in recent weeks occurred at a speech by Ehud Barak, the previous Prime Minister of Israel. A vast majority of a crowd 1700-strong replied with thunderous applause to his insistence on the need to deal with Iraq "now, while we can," as "nobody knows how close this dangerous man [Saddam Hussein] is to a crude nuclear device...we have tried peace."
