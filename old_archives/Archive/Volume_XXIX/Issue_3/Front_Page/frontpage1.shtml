Front Page
Student Survey: Israel, US, and Islam
by Christopher Fish
Guest Contributor

The results of the second Review survey of the year are in. Our first survey dealt directly with the issue of Iraq, and was modeled after an AP survey on the same subject. The results of that survey showed that Stanford students, even more than Bay Area residents, are extremely pro-appeasement relative to the nation as a whole.

The majority of Stanford students gave politically correct answers to the Review's second survey. As seen above, 53 percent of students believe that U.S. foreign policy is somewhat or extremely biased against Islam.

This second survey follows up by dealing with the attitudes students have toward Jews, Israel, Islam, and related United States foreign policy. Students were asked ten multiple choice questions.

The first question asked was, "What are your feelings toward the Jewish people?" 56 percent answered "quite positive," 19 percent answered "somewhat positive," 24 percent answered "neutral," and 1 percent answered "somewhat negative." No student chose the "quite negative" response.

Stanford students also reported their response to "Muslim people." The response was markedly less positive than the response to the first question, perhaps reflecting less familiarity with the people and culture. Still, students responded almost exclusively to the positive or neutral. 29 percent felt very positive toward the Muslims, 25 percent somewhat positive, 41 percent were neutral, 7 percent somewhat negative, and 1 percent very negative.

The survey was taken anonymously to obtain sound results; while openly many professed their appreciation of both Judaism and Islam, no students were willing to be quoted on negative opinions of these religions. "I think that while you can interpret Islam as a peaceful religion, and many people do, the Koran and the warrior culture upon which the religion was founded lends itself to being easily manipulated into a doctrine of violence against outsiders," explained a Sophomore. "I'd elaborate, but if you quote me on any of this, I'll have crowds banging down my door."

The second question students were asked was whether they believed "It is excessively ethnocentric for Jews to pay so much attention to the Holocaust when other groups have also suffered hardship." 24 percent agreed, while 63 percent disagreed. In the Holocaust of the second World War, millions of European Jews were stripped of their possessions and butchered in concentration camps, along with others including homosexuals, communists, and gypsies. The Saudi Arabian and Arab press still circulates Nazi propaganda to this day.

Students on many college campuses have called for their universities to divest from companies doing business in Israel, and circulated petitions to that effect. At Stanford, 66 percent would oppose such a petition, and only 9 percent would support it.

Israel, the only democratic state in the Middle Eastern region, has been attacked for purported human rights violations, especially with regard to its treatment of Palestinians. Iraq, another state in the region, has gassed tens of thousands of Kurdish Iraqis and razed many Kurdish villages in northern Iraq. Iran's government is currently engaged in utilizing military police against thousands of student demonstrators, and its police forces have conducted street executions. Some say, though, that as Israel is a democracy it should be held to a higher standard than, for example, Iraq's Saddam Hussein. To determine the attitude of Stanford students in this debate, we asked students to what extent they agreed with the following statement: "To single out Israel our of all countries for human rights violations constitutes anti-Semitism." 34 percent of respondents agreed with this statement, while 45 percent disagreed.

Also, in some circles, particularly in Europe and the Arab world, it is believed that Jews secretly run the affairs of the United States through control of the media, finance, and/or government. Stanford Students, asked whether "Jews have too much influence in such areas as the media, finance, and government," largely disagreed. 12 percent agreed, and 68 percent disagreed, with the rest neutral.

When asked whether Islam is a violent religion, most students said no. 13 percent agreed, and 67 percent disagreed, and 20 percent had no reply.

Some conservative thinkers have criticized the American Muslim community for its lack of condemnation, or a perceived insufficient condemnation, of the September 11th attacks in particular. When asked whether American Muslims have responded appropriately to September 11th, students were divided in their response, though the body leaned toward the affirmative. 27 percent strongly agreed that American Muslims have responded appropriately, 21 percent somewhat agreed, 39 percent were neutral, 10 percent somewhat disagreed, and 3 percent disagreed strongly.

Many, particularly in the Arab world but also on college campuses and in Europe, have accused the US of having a foreign policy biased against Islam. A narrow majority of Stanford students agree that American foreign policy is biased against Islam. 15 percent strongly believe this to be the case, and 38 percent somewhat believe this to be true. 23 percent are neutral, 13 percent somewhat disagree, and 11 percent strongly disagree.

When asked how familiar they are with the Muslim people and with the Muslim religion, most students claimed to be at least slightly familiar. 9 percent believe they are very familiar, 30 percent fairly familiar, 32 percent slightly familiar, 25 percent not very familiar, and 4 percent very unfamiliar with the people and religion.

Interestingly, the percentage of students who feel that Islam is a violent religion or that American Muslims have not responded appropriately closely aligns with the percent who favored war on Iraq in the previous survey. Maybe the percent that favors war simply does not understand Islam; or perhaps the 9 percent of anti-Jewish-power views are affecting and blinding others.
