Many Bubbles to Burst in Iraq Debate
by Piotr H. Kosicki
Staff Writer

On November 5th, a new student-sponsored discussion series called Bursting the Bubble kicked off with a panel discussion on potential US policy toward Iraq. The organizers' attempt to balance the panel politically brought together ultraliberal Computer Science Professor Eric Roberts, liberal Political Science Professor David Abernethy, and moderate conservative Hoover Institute Senior Fellow Abraham Sofaer. Although left-biased, the panel managed both to foster heated discussion and to reach a moderate conclusion.

From left to right: Professor David Abernethy, Professor Eric Roberts, Dr. Abraham Sofaer.

Each panel member gave a 10-minute speech before the moderator, Vice Provost for Undergraduate Education John Bravman, opened the floor for what would be 90 minutes of discussion on 16 questions asked by the audience.

Dr. Sofaer began the evening by offering a legal framework for the use of preemptive force by one nation against another, arguing that such force is legitimate and even necessary when "in pursuit of the aims of the UN charter." According to Dr. Sofaer, in a situation like that of Iraq, where deterrence has failed and where the world is dealing with "a merciless murderer" who "poses a very grave threat to the U.S. and the civilized world," preemptive strike is logical. To keep the goal in the realm of the UN, however, Dr. Sofaer argued, such a strike should be multilateral.

Professor Eric Roberts introduced himself as one of the founders of a new pacifist group at Stanford called Stanford Faculty Against the War. Distributing 100 leaflets with the URLs of on-line literature denouncing any prospect of military strikes on Iraq, Professor Roberts attempted to change the course of discussion by focusing not on legal ramifications but on a political interpretation of the Bush administration.

In the professor's words, the administration's "mad dash toward unilateralism" must be stopped because it has paved the road to the "erosion" of civil liberties under the pretext of national security. Ignoring the legal framework offered by Dr. Sofaer, Professor Roberts limited himself to assessing the Iraqi conflict as an econopolitical tool of the President.

In contrast, Professor Abernethy moved out of the political sphere and back into theory, quoting the National Security Council's National Security Strategy published in September. He also denounced unilateral preemptive strike as, at best, a "dangerous precedent" for other countries.

Abernethy then moved to what he called the "absence of a credible exit strategy," arguing that a potential U.S. occupation of Iraq would be, at best, "an ambitious project doomed by its inherently top-down nature" and, at worst, "another round in the Crusades."

Discussion was sometimes tense: Sofaer at one point cut off Abernethy, while an audience member badgered Eric Roberts on the question of whether the US has an obligation to defend the rights of other states' citizens. However, the discussion ended with smiles and handshakes as the participants recognized a few common beliefs.

Cued by Abernethy's observation that "our government is prepared to act unilaterally," all three men expressed disdain for unilateralism. While Sofaer praised the administration's pursuit of UN diplomacy, Roberts and Abernethy addressed this diplomacy skeptically but not without hope.

Moderator John Bravman's reminder that the tragedy of September 11th cost the United States an estimated $1 trillion inspired all of the participants to agree on the need for something to be done about Iraq, but the only element on which they could agree was the necessity of multilateralism.

Midterm Election Results

The biweekly publication of the Review permits the addition of some hindsight to place the event in the context of what was happening nationally and internationally in the same week. The discussion took place on Election Day: despite the timing--or perhaps because of it--Cypress Room North at Tresidder was packed with an audience of at least 200, enough that the organizers were forced to commandeer Cypress Room South as overflow space.

The elections provide a point of reference to which Eric Roberts referred often when saying that he hoped the audience's votes reflected their views on Iraq. Whether or not voters followed Professor Roberts's advice, the outcome was unfortunate for him: in a poll published in the November 9th edition of Newsweek, 59 percent of respondents cited "President Bush's willingness to use military force, if necessary, to disarm and remove Saddam Hussein from power in Iraq" as a "major factor" in the Republicans' midterm victory. The degree to which Bush's war stance actually helped Republicans is a matter for debate, but it certainly gives pause to reconsider accusations of the administration's "mad dash."

U.N. Security Council Resolution

The events of November 8th further complicate the idea of a "mad dash." On that day, the UN Security Council approved a tough resolution modeled on an US draft threatening Iraq with "serious consequences" should Saddam Hussein fail to readmit weapons inspectors with "unconditional access."

Are then the professors' fears that the administration has forsaken multilateralism still justified? Students' opinions vary, but even the critical seem to agree that November 8th constituted an important show of multilateralism. In the words of freshman Kevin Gao, "When they [the panelists] said they feared unilateralism, they knew that multilateral action would only be a guise, but even that guise was necessary."

Granted, the Bush administration promises unilateral attack should the UN fail to approve explicit action in the event of Iraqi non-compliance, but the Security Council resolution certainly offers some perspective on the arguments of all three discussion participants. As sophomore Charles Hallford stated, "It's definitely multilateral, so any arguments that the anti-war professors raised based on negative aspects of going to war with Iraq unilaterally no longer apply."
