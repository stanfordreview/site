Blacker & Casper Drain Some Suds Out of Liberal Students' Politics
by Piotr H. Kosicki
Staff Writer

Coit Blacker and Gerhard Casper hosted the second "Bursting the Bubble" current-event panel session on Tuesday, December 3rd, discussing the National Security Strategy (NSS) issued by the Bush administration in September. Mr. Blacker, a former advisor to President Clinton and Deputy Director of Stanford's Institute for International Studies, and Mr. Casper, University President Emeritus and Law Professor, both presented themselves as liberal, yet they accepted the program's challenge to burst bubbles even for their ideological colleagues in the audience.

The scholars spoke for about 15 minutes each before the floor opened for 10 questions from the audience.

Mr. Casper began the session by looking at the rhetoric in President Bush's preface to the NSS. While Mr. Casper acknowledged that Bush's rhetoric of defending "the peace by fighting terrorists and tyrants" might seem a sign of imperialistic hegemony on the part of the US, he argued against the idea that has President Bush trying to create a "new world order" driven by an American empire. Mr. Casper pointed out that the United States has always been dedicated to the promotion of democracy throughout the world. After citing historical figures from John Adams through FDR's Vice President Henry A. Wallace, Mr. Casper concluded, "there is nothing original about what President Bush says."

Mr. Casper then turned to some fuzzier issues intended to express his own doubts. As a constitutionalist, he proclaimed concern to find Congress mentioned only once in the 35-page NSS. He suggested that the document might become a springboard for "separation-of-powers violations" in the federal government. When Mr. Blacker suggested that the document is little more than an articulation of the administration's "understanding of American core values," Mr. Casper agreed.

In his opening remarks, Mr. Blacker took care to debunk myths on President Bush's primary motives, arguing that the NSS is clearly the product of a president who knows that his primary constitutional purpose is "to protect the people of the United States," a goal to which "all else is secondary." He denounced several "catch phrases he felt were being used to characterize Mr. Bush's foreign policy, including, "A new order for the ages, a hunting license for the United States," and "a virtual invitation to aggression."

Mr. Blacker echoed Mr. Casper's fears that the National Security Strategy might be blown out of proportion. In his mind, the NSS moves beyond the doctrine of preemption, which he defined as, "the legitimate use of force when you know that an attack is about to take place." Mr. Blacker then asserted the legitimacy of preemption as a doctrine rooted in Article 51 of the UN Charter, commenting on the administration: "They're not making this stuff up."

In defining where the NSS goes, however, Mr. Blacker pointed to the use of "a concert system of great powers" for prevention of "conditions under which country X can do something to me down the road." Mr. Blacker offered the notion that "the US must demonstrate its willingness to subordinate its goals and objectives to the interest of the international community."

The NSS may, as Mr. Casper argued, envision the world as a place where the President "needs more authority to act and to act swiftly and, if necessary, to act unilaterally." More importantly, however, as both men observed, it is "a policy in progress." Although both scholars limited their reference to hypothetical doom scenarios, they did not define what unilateral action really means in a legalistic sense for the UN, nor did they state their objections to unilateralism. Failing to define the scope of their analysis made legitimate concerns appear as unqualified overgeneralizations.

Q&A did force the scholars to define their frame of reference, clarifying the fact that the NSS is just a statement of ideas, like other strategic declarations issued by virtually every administration. Even within this frame of reference, the men continued to voice concerns without telling the audience how to evaluate those concerns, creating an ad hominem effect: Mr. Blacker about the "political self-interest" underlying policy decisions and Mr. Casper on the idea that "there can be no concert of nations when one player is the entire orchestra."

While metaphorically striking, these concerns reflect relatively infrequent overgeneralization on the part of Mr. Blacker and Mr. Casper, who, for the most part, did justice to the administration despite their political affiliation. As sophomore Howard Kao said, "I expected them both to espouse more of a liberal viewpoint."

This may have been the single most important act of bubble-bursting of the evening: showing that President Bush is a dedicated leader who can put politics aside to do what he thinks best to protect the American people. Mr. Blacker admitted in Q&A that, if he were in the president's shoes, he probably would have pursued the War on Terrorism with a similar strategy because "You can't hit us that hard and not be punished." Similarly, he appealed to students not to reject the president's approach out of hand but to put themselves in his shoes: "I want you to think carefully about what you'd do."

Mr. Blacker did come down harshly on the president as a "cowboy" appealing to the American people with "down-home straight talk" rather than factual analysis. He evenutally closed supporting the president, saying that "turning the other cheek is not something [the Bush administration] can or would do, primarily because it would not stop future attacks." Even Mr. Casper agreed, "The administration has so far done everything pretty much right."

This "Bursting the Bubble" session displayed a tone markedly different from the first, in which three moderate-to-liberal scholars debated one-another's policy toward Iraq. Mssrs. Blacker and Casper were much more in concert than their predecessors. This event's speakers offered a broad analysis directed straight at their student audience. They took pains to tell their audience that national security is not just about politics, but about "values" and "protecting the people."
