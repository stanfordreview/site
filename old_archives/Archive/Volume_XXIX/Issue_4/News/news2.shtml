Former Student Body President Speaks about Iraq and Stanford in the 1960's
by Ryan Wisnesky
Staff Writer

On November 18th, former student body president David Harris spoke in Toyon Lounge to students and alums about his experiences at Stanford in the 1960's and about the current situation in Iraq. Mr. Harris was an anti-war activist during the height of the Vietnam War and spent time in jail for draft resistance. After being introduced by his friend Professor Eric Roberts, he spoke briefly about his time at Stanford before delving into the issue of Iraq.

In talking about his experiences at Stanford Mr. Harris drew many parallels between the state of the country in 1965 and the state of the country today. For instance, he noted that both the current Iraqi situation and the Vietnam War reeked of colonialism. However, he also noted that "this is not 1965". Rather, he said, speaking of the potential problems with war with Iraq, "this is worse."

Mr. Harris dwelled briefly on the actual events that changed him into an activist. He said that in the 1960's, men had three options; to be "John Wayne, John Wayne, or John Wayne." He said that he started life at Stanford as a typical American kid, full of ideas about what America stood for, but later realized that "the America we had been told existed did not in fact exist." He gave specific examples of disturbing American policies in Vietnam, included the construction of "44 regional torture centers" and "the use of plastic fragmentation weapons to create more wounded [because it takes more resources to care for a wounded person than a dead one]". Mr. Harris said that once he realized what was truly happening, he could not support the US war effort, and became involved in resistance.

Moving into the topic of Iraq, Mr. Harris said that "our government has openly proclaimed Empire." He vowed that he could not stand by while the Bush administration "makes the US into a little island of fascism." Mr. Harris suggested that it was up to the American people, and students in particular, to "make it clear that this government does not speak for all of us." A movement to stop war with Iraq can only form if "students are fighting for their futures", he said, but because a draft is unlikely it is harder, but not impossible, to rally student support against a war.

Mr. Harris spoke briefly about the current state of politics in the country, noting that it was unlikely that political pressure against the war would come from within the government. "The democrats have rolled over", he said, "those of use who are simply citizens must band together", in order to "speak truth to power". Only then, he said, could this war be stopped. After finishing his speech about the war Mr. Harris fielded questions from the audience. Questions ranged from mundane to conspiratorial. When asked about the feeling of community he felt in the 1960's, he said that "if you're going to find meaning, you're going to find it in communal identity." When asked about the root of American foreign policy, Mr. Harris spoke not primarily of wealth or oil, but said that ours is "a policy driven by nothing but arrogance."

Mr. Harris answered a few question about how a student movement against the war might form. He said that he didn't think there had to be conscription to create an anti-war movement and that "we are crippled by our 15 second attention span." However, in dealing with those who do not oppose the war, Mr. Harris said that "you're not out there to fight against them, just to make them talk." When asked for a suggestion of a concrete action students could take, Mr. Harris said that students "could move into Senator Feinstein's office" until she voted differently.

Mr. Harris was careful to maintain an anti-war but realistic approach to dealing with Iraq. When an audience member claimed that the events of September 11th "were a special operation to get support for the war", Mr. Harris listened respectfully but steadfastly refused to jump to conclusions. Instead, he said that such claims required careful proof. At the end of the question and answer session Mr. Harris spoke a little about his personal beliefs. He said that he's "voted in every election [he's] been allowed to" and voted for "no republicans and few democrats". He said that for himself and a number of his friends during the 1960's, Ghandi was their role model. And in a final protest against the administrations policies, he said that "pre-emption is Evil with a capital E" and that the Bush administration "doesn't do diplomacy". Finally, he said that "I would love to have an uprising in two days in which the government was stopped from this policy." But he said it's up to the students to make it happen.
