Opinion
All Hatred is Not Created Equal
by Aaron Master
Contributing Writer

"I feel like I can't criticize Israel without being called anti-Semitic." This is the charge many of us have heard in recent months and years, especially as debate over Israel and Palestine has grown more intense. In order to know when the line into anti-Semitism has been crossed, one must know both what anti-Semitism is, and how people debate the Arab-Israeli conflict.

Hate attacks on different groups are different. To launch hate against Blacks, one might make the baseless claim that Blacks are born racially inferior to whites, or suggest that slavery was a good idea. To hurt Japanese-Americans, one could exalt internment camps. To voice hatred of Jews, however, a better approach would be to falsely claim that Jews are conspiring to harm -- or fail to prevent harm against -- the non-Jewish world and save or nourish only themselves. Why? Because this has been the basis of centuries of deadly anti-Jewish hate. For example, history has seen claims that Jews kill Christian children and use their blood to make Passover matzos (England, 1144); that Jews are responsible for Europe's 14th century Black Death; that Jews are plotting to control the world's money and government ("The Protocols of the Learned Elders of Zion," Czarist Russian Secret Police fabrication, 1897; stated as possibly true in Egyptian TV miniseries, November, 2002); that Jews are behind international communism and want to use it to take over Germany (Hitler and other Nazis, 1930's-1940's), or that Jews use the blood of Muslim and Christian youths when making Purim pastries (Saudi Arabian government daily "Al-Riyadh," March 13, 2002). Under such banners were literally millions of innocent Jews systematically deported and murdered in Europe and the Near East.

The conspiracy theories have taken hold for a reason. Unlike many peoples, Jews have been a small minority in every country in which they have lived, except ancient and modern Israel. Jews have nonetheless been successful in many societies, due to a huge cultural emphasis on education and family. And that has often upset traditional classist and ethnicist order. Hence, leaders and even much of the populations of places like Russia, Germany, and France claimed that Jews must have been conspiring among themselves to succeed (or to dominate and kill). Anyone who's heard a hate chorus of "Asians only succeed in school because they cheat" knows how this accusation works.

This sort of thought has, unfortunately, found its way into the modern debate on Israel. Specifically, some anti-Israel activists choose to enter into the debate selective truths that can implicitly rely on or motivate anti-Semitism. They begin with presentation of evidence of Israeli actions such as humiliating checkpoints for Palestinians, Israeli military action that kills civilians, and occupation of Arab lands. But this is all without mention of brutal Palestinian terrorism that necessitates security measures, or of failed Arab-initiated "war[s] of extermination" and rejections of peace that result in occupation of land. The activists also fail to mention why the U.S. supports Israel -- ignoring everything from Israeli -U.S. intelligence sharing, to evangelical Christian voters' support of a Jewish state (to encourage the second coming of the messiah). But they allow, and sometimes even claim, that the U.S. supports Israel for a more sinister reason: that Jews control the U.S. media and government.

This suggests that, despite the bitter debates over Israel in Jewish intellectual circles, American Jews in politics and journalism will categorically support Israel -- at the expense of treason against their country or betrayal of their professional journalistic standards. That is a horribly anti-Semitic concept, and it is well rooted in the tradition of anti-Semitic conspiracy theories. It was the Nazi party that tried to place quotas on Jewish representation in the German parliament (before deportation and murder made that unnecessary) and it was Hitler that complained of the "Jewish media" trying to expose him as a fascist. The real factor the anti-Semites don't understand, and never will, is the Jewish culture's value of learning, which has led to many Jews succeeding in college. It is the college-educated that are over-represented in politics and journalism, not "controlling" Jews.

Politicians and activists positioned against Israel can also voice hate quite directly. When they apply language like "the largest ethnic cleansing operation in modern history," "genocide," or "nazism" to Israel without any factual basis, it's clear that the goal is more to foster hatred than to enter into debate. Demonization of Israel's government and population is also furthered by outright fabrications, like the PLO canard that the Israeli government injected 300 Palestinian children with the HIV virus, or the Al-Manar (Hezbollah television station) fictitious "news" that 4000 Israelis working at the World Trade Center stayed home on September 11th, 2001. Purveyors of such falsehoods have not shown similar interest in defaming the peoples of other Western democracies or Near Eastern states; they should not be surprised or indignant when they are labeled anti-Semites. This is especially true in Near Eastern media, where the Arabic terms for "Zionist," "Jew," and "Israeli" are often used interchangeably, and where Israel and Jews alike are so often slandered.

Of course, anti-Israel viewpoints are not necessarily anti-Semitic. The view that Israel's settlement policies and occupation of the West Bank are wrong represents but one example. But a lack of anti-Semitism does not guarantee a lack of ignorance or bias. Tom Wise laments the "IDF's firing on ambulances to ensure that those wounded by their actions will die slowly" without mentioning Hamas's repeated use of the same "ambulances" to carry suicide bombing belts. Whether the source is Jewish (like Wise) or not, severely biased statements and anti-Semitic statements can be two very different evils. Intellectual debate is better without either.

So, in the end, demonization of Israel -- when not supported by facts or context -- is dangerously suggestive of or dependent on conspiracy theories that lie far too close to the backbone of traditional anti-Semitic propaganda. Hateful fabrications that single out the Israeli people and government should also be seen for what they are. But we should never automatically affix the label of anti-Semitism to criticism of Israel, even if such criticism is heavily biased. These basic understandings ultimately can be of great use to everyone who cares about the Arab-Israeli conflict. Palestinians, Israelis, Arabs, and Jews deserve no less.
