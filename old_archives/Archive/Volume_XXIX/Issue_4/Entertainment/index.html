Rent: The Modern La Boheme
by Ming Zhu and Hilary Connell
Entertainment Editors

Rent hit the stage in 1996 to packed houses, raving reviews, and the consensus opinion that this was the "next big thing" in theatre." After watching the Collins National Tour performance at the Orpheum Theatre in San Francisco the weekend before Thanksgiving...I wonder if RENT should pass the title on to someone else.

As a modern adaptation to Puccini's La Boheme, RENT is based upon the lives of several starving artists as they try to live life amidst the chaos of the approaching millennium. They wrestle with issues of poverty, AIDS, homosexuality, and death...but with a glossy cover. In contrast to the Italian opera, RENT is set in present day New York and Mimi comes back to life after a close brush with death after taking a "leap of faith." Those of you who remember my review of La Boheme will recall that my greatest fault with Western Opera's take on the classic was their modernization. In contrast, my greatest critique of RENT was the datedness. To put it simply, RENT was starting to look old.

This isn't to say that RENT is losing its appeal. It is still one of the most intoxicating shows on Broadway...filled with amazing beats and melodies that keep your feet tapping for days after leaving the theatre. Even now, I find myself going through the lyrics of La Vie Boheme in my head.

RENT is one of the few musicals where each lead is given their own moment in the spotlight...and I applaud each and every one of the leads in this traveling production. Starting from the top, Kevin Spencer, an extraordinary Roger. As a fellow audience member commented, "you could imagine this guy as the front man for a rock band." His voice was strong and full of emotion Ð definitely my vote for most dynamic vocal performance. Mimi, played by the versatile Krystal L. Washington, lent a sultry but aggressive voice that perfectly complimented her feisty and resilient character. Ashton Holmes, as the documentarian Mark Cohen, claimed the title of "Most Consistent"--I didn't hear a single musical fault or acting slip-up in the entire performance. Caissie Levy did a marvelous job in the characterization of Maureen, her solo at the protest was one of the most hilarious sequences I've ever seen on stage. If I had to pick a point of improvement, it would fall upon Justin Rodriguez, who portrayed the optimistic transvestite Angel. There is a good chance that it was simply not his night...but Angel's character just seemed flat in comparison to the other leads. There wasn't the pizzazz that one would except out of this purposely over-the-top character, even in his Today for You solo. Overall, however, I would find it hard to critique the musicality of the cast.

Some highlights of the evening included Ashton's unexpected breakdance moves and the electric duet between Joanne (Bridget Anne Mohammed) and Maureen in the famed Take Me or Leave Me. Roger and Mimi's love duet in the first act was spot-on and Tom's (Bruce Wilson Jr.) delivery of I'll Cover You was achingly poignant. Stanford sophomore Willie Liou offered up his opinion that "the gospel soloist on Seasons of Love outshined them all," supporting the observation that this was a stellar cast where anyone could steal the show.

However, regardless of the fact it is still amazing, RENT's "innovations" have become dated. AIDS and "AZT breaks" are no longer culture-shocking topics; rock-band music is no longer a breakthrough in theatre scores. RENT is still one of the best musicals of all time, but it is no longer "the next big thing" of Broadway.
