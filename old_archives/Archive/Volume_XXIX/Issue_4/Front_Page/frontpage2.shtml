Front Page
Stanford Student Enterprises to Provide Financial Information
by Harrison Osaki
Staff Writer

Last month, Stanford Student Enterprises (SSE) decided to release its financial documents to The Stanford Review and The Stanford Daily after several months of stonewalling. The decision to disclose the information came after former Review editor Henry Towsner and former Daily editor Mark Boucher brought the issue before the ASSU's Constitutional Council, representing their respective publications. SSE will be releasing information including income data and balance statements for the ASSU and SSE organizations to the public; separate, proprietary information such as information about SSE's specific business units, will be released exclusively to the Daily and Review under a non-disclosure agreement. The initial information request by the Daily and Review to the SSE was made on April 24th, 2002, when the Review and the Daily jointly sent a letter to the President of SSE Ross Davisson asking for "access to the salaries of all SSE employees, a copy of SSE's financial statements from the past three years, and a listing of SSE's current investments". Despite Mr. Towsner's efforts to negotiate with Mr. Davisson (which resulted in an agreement to limit the information request to SSE balance sheets, SSE student employee salaries and current investments), Mr. Davisson eventually rejected the request, although he did say that he would provide a listing of investments.

At issue in the case brought before the Constitutional Council was a subsection of the ASSU Constitution which states, "All records of any Association entity must be available for scrutiny by the public with the exception of proprietary business information of Association businesses...and personnel records of employees." The ASSU Constitution does not define what information is and is not proprietary.

SSE's primary argument said that the information which the Review and the Daily were requesting was proprietary, and that providing this information to the public would unfairly penalize SSE's business. In his initial statement defending SSE's practices, current SSE CEO and ASSU Financial Manager David Endelman argued that the exact issue at hand was not the release of the documents themselves but the constitutionality of the law limiting their release; Mr. Endelman referred to in defense of this statement the particular section of the Association Financial Policies (which he cited as a section from the Bylaws of the Association) which stated, "The following policies shall be determined by the Board of Directors of Stanford Student Enterprises, or its designee(s)...A specification of what information held by Stanford Student Enterprises may be considered proprietary", interpreting it to mean that SSE and its Board of Directors had the complete and final authority to define information as proprietary.

Mr. Endelman further argued that the Constitutional Council had no jurisdiction to adjudicate the case, since the ASSU Constitution limited the Council to judging cases in which, "...the constitutionality [under the ASSU Constitution] of an act by an Association legislative body, the President of the Association, or any members of the Association is called into question." Mr. Endelman also later argued that SSE's newly created policy of releasing yearly consolidated statements from an independent auditing firm was enough to satisfy the Constitutional requirement of transparency and that it did not need to release any other documents.

Mr. Towsner represented the primary appellant in the case and argued for the Review and the Daily. He drew upon his experience last year as an advisor to the ASSU for constitutional matters. Mr. Towsner responded in a clarification to the original statement that the SSE CEO's relationship to the ASSU did in fact validate a Constitutional Council hearing and that, furthermore, the SSE does not in fact have sole authority to define proprietary information without oversight or process. Although the CEO of the SSE may only be a former student and not therefore a member of the ASSU, Mr. Towsner argued that the CEO is still an official acting on the behalf of the ASSU legislative bodies and thus authorized and acting for the ASSU; since the Council had previously ruled that ASSU legislative bodies may not permit their employees to act as the legislative bodies themselves may not act, the actions of the CEO and the ASSU's legislative bodies remained under the legal power of the Constitutional Council. Mr. Towsner pointed out, furthermore, that Mr. Endelman's claim that the SSE and the joint legislative bodies over it have the ultimate authority to define documents as proprietary without review, "...would allow them to amend away all public scrutiny by simply declaring every document proprietary".

Mr. Towsner proposed instead that the section Mr. Endelman cites describes a process which the SSE must follow in order to define a document as proprietary-- a process which, Mr. Towsner said, had not been followed. In order to declare documents as proprietary a body of SSE officials would have been forced to convene as given in the SSE charter. Since no such body had been arranged prior to the information request by the Daily and the Review, the documents could not have been proprietary at the time of the request.

In addition, a group of former ASSU Financial Managers and former SSE CEOs presented a signed document describing how the release of certain information could possibly hurt SSE, and how the business ventures of SSE were vital to the ASSU.

The hearing before the Constitutional Council ended without a ruling, although the two sides concluded with a compromise settlement. Robert Rudnitsky, a member on the Council, commented that although, "there's a big advantage to a lot of openness," regarding the SSE's transparency, he was not sure whether or not the Council would be able to rule on the propriety of documents.
