Front Page
ASSU Vice-President Pushes New Student Union
by Harrison Osaki
Senior Staff Writer

Tresidder Union is the aging home of the Associated Students of Stanford University (ASSU), and meeting place for many student clubs. Proponents of a new student union believe that students would benefit greatly by the initiative.
For many new students, the Tresidder Memorial Union is one of the first sites they see on campus. Tresidder hosts a variety of student services and stores such as a Jamba Juice, TresEx (a small shopping store) a computer cluster, a pair of banks and the home offices of the ASSU and the OSA. Recently, however, a new student-led movement has risen pressing for the development of a New Student Union (NSU) to either supplement or replace the existing Tresidder facilities and building.

The push for the NSU is led by an exploratory comittee under ASSU Vice-President Nick Rodriguez and Joey Natoli, head of the Student Life Committee of the Undergraduate Senate. They hope to build student awareness and interest so that the project remains in the student consciousness in the future. They also hope to bring the issue into the administration's consciousness and to hasten the planning for the new building.

The idea behind a NSU is not entirely new; in 1999, the Stanford Daily published an editorial calling for the creation of such a union to either supplement or replace Tresidder for student services including food and entertainment. It is only recently, however, that branches of the ASSU have begun to push in concert for the development of a NSU. Mr. Rodriguez commented that it is a "strange and sad phenomenon that Stanford does not have a student union worthy of the name when many, much lower tier institutions do."

Currently, according to Dean of Students Marc Wais, Tresidder is intended "to meet the needs of all community members, including but not limited to: students, faculty, staff, alumni, and visitors", instead of students in particular.

A new NSU would improve upon Tresidder by expanding the available food services and enlarging group storage space, said Mr. Rodriguez. The NSU would also provide more meeting space for campus groups and provide a centralized location for student parties, lessening the pressure on other departments to provide room for these activities. Mr. Rodriguez said if Tresidder were to remain as a secondary student center, the current nerve centers of administration (such as the ASSU office) would be moved.

According to Mr. Natoli, over two dozen student groups have been queried over what they would want in a new union. The project is still in the early stages and, therefore, the precise services which the union would provide have yet to be named. The exploratory committee has yet to establish any means of paying for the new building; Mr. Natoli commented that, "every option is currently being looked into, particularly options that were successful at other universities including having the Development office raise the money, have the students pay for it through a special fee or a combination therein." He did not indicate even a rough estimate for the fee, or for any other mans of fundraising.

The exploratory committee ahas yet to confront the General Use Permit which determines how Stanford University uses its land. Building a NSU would require a significant amount of land, which could restrict and be restricted by other future building projects. As a result, if the NSU were created it is likely that it would be forced to replace Tresidder due to the General Use Permit's limitations on land use.

The possibility of retrofitting Tresidder Union has not to any large extent been seriously considered by the NSU exploratory comittee or the Student Life Committee. Neither group has considered where a new student center could be placed relative to student dorms on campus, heightening chances that the center would be planned to replace the existing Tresidder facilities.

Student reaction according to Mr. Rodriguez has been largely positive. Student groups who have responded have, among many other things, expressed interest in having more and larger rooms available for meetings on campus. However, some students we queried expressed regret that Tresidder would be replaced. When asked how he would feel if Tresidder was replaced by a NSU, Sophomore Rodrigo Munoz replied that "...to lose Tresidder would be to lose a part of Stanford's history."

The ASSU's recent activity in reviving the NSU issue behind a united student leadership may be related to a general push to combat the perceived high prices of University-provided student services in general. The ASSU's "Cost of Living Campaign," championed by Mr. Rodriguez and ASSU President Monica Henestroza has repeatedly criticized several of Tresidder's current food services. Most of the campaign's goals involve straight negotiation with businesses and the University in order to bring prices towards competitive marketplace rates in order to serve students better. Although the home page for the campaign says, "in some cases, a rational approach works" it also calls for stronger measures, such as an elimination of long-term contracts in order to "break up local campus monopolies". The ASSU also wishes to revive the ASSU Book Exchange Program and the Stanford Store, both under the control of the Stanford Student Enterprises, in order to provide alternatives to the Stanford Bookstore and TresEx. Replacing Tresidder with a NSU would most likely facilitate the success of both these SSE businesses. Alternatively, revamping Tresidder as a student-designed student union could greatly further the effort to eliminate the "businesses on campus [that] have a captive market".

Mr. Rodriguez said that he fears that without active student participation in the Tresidder remodeling which may occur due to the General Use Permit, "students could lose the little space that they already have if decisions are made without [their] input", and that the ASSU hopes to raise the issue so that "Stanford students are truly leading the fight for space entitlements for students" in years to come.
