Students Protest Potential War
by Ryan Wisnesky
Staff Writer

Members of the Stanford community protested a potential war with Iraq. Held October 25 in White Plaza, the protest was attended by several dozen.
On October 25, dozens of students and faculty gathered to protest a potential war with Iraq. Sponsored by the Stanford Community for Peace and Justice, the protest featured an open microphone and speeches by prominent faculty members.

Each speaker focused his or her speech on different criticisms of current US policy toward Iraq. Professor Sheehan of the Religious Studies department aimed his remarks at the uncertainties involved in a war with Iraq. "This is an attack of possibilities, not actualities", he said, referring to the fact that the US does not concretely know that Iraq is willing or even able to use weapons of mass destruction. Sheehan based his remarks on a historical precedent, noting that since WWII was fought to decide who would control the 1st world, and the Cold War was fought to decide who would control the 3rd world, "WWIV will be the war for control of Central Asia and the Middle East." The current crisis with Iraq is just the first step in a plan to ensure a global balance of power favorable to the US over the coming decades, he noted. History Professor David Como echoed Sheehan's sentiments.

Lolita Roibal, speaking on behalf of MECHA, tied the potential costs of war to social concerns, noting that "it is people of color that suffer the most when these wars are fought." She encouraged the Stanford Community to protest the potential war not only because it would be intrinsically wrong, but because the potential benefits of the war would not be equally distributed. "A lot of people in the US will not benefit from the so-called profits of war", she noted, suggesting that oil companies and other groups will benefit more than ordinary people.

Also included in the diverse group of speakers was Jim Burklo of the United Campus Christian Ministry and Mandeep Gill, a graduate student in Physics.

Opinions about the potential war with Iraq varied considerably across the audience. Elliot Fladen, a 1st year law student, stated that "I am unsure of where I stand on the war with Iraq. However I think it is important to consider not only the consequences of action but of inaction as well."

Although many of the students at the protest had yet to form opinions about the potential war, emotions from both pro-war and anti-war students sparked a number of debates and scornful looks. Ramji Srinivasan, a senior/co-term student in financial math, referred to the protesters and suggested that "the people who want to help people don't realize just how much damage Saddam is doing. Bush is going to produce less dead civilians." On the other side, Tad Cook, a Religious Studies doctoral student, stated that "The human cost of our continuing war with Iraq over the last twelve years has been too high. Sanctions and repeated bombings have resulted in over a million deaths. If we continue this it's just going to get worse and worse. The moral cost is too high."

During the protest a quilt was placed on the ground and all students were invited and encouraged to write down their feelings about the war. While the statements on the quilt were written anonymously, the overwhelming majority of statements were opposed to not only war with Iraq but to war in general. Among the lines written on the quilt were "Think of the flesh, think of the limbs, think of the caskets" and "A war on terror? Does that mean that the US going to end all terror? All fear in the world? Will fear go away if we kill Saddam?"

A large number of students also implied that the US was only considering war with Iraq because of the US's interest in the free flow of oil in the region. "I love Iraqi's more than oil", wrote one student. "No Blood for oil" wrote another.

As the protest continued, the audience actively participated, with slogans like "we're the ones starting the war" being shouted from the audience and greeted with applause. However, because of time constraints, the speakers were unable to respond to questions from the audience. In an effort to continue a dialogue, students were invited to stay after the protest to continue discussion. Although many students did stay for further discussion, the protest ended quickly and peacefully exactly one hour after it started. In the words of one protestor, all that the protest meant was "you are questioning what's going on."
