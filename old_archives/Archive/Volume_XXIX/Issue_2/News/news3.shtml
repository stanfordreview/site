SCPJ Protests Citizenship Requisite, Federalization of Airport Workers
by Nels Hansen
Opinions Editor

Several campus activist organizations, led by Stanford Community for Peace and Justice (SCPJ), the have led a campaign against the proposal that all US Customs workers must be US citizens. This proposal's reasoning presumably is that non-citizens are unsuitable for these positions due to the degree of trustworthiness and loyalty required. The students of SCPJ vehemently disagree with this idea, apparently believing that there is no unique loyalty from US citizens to their country.

However, the policy will be implemented anyway by the 19th of this month at local airports, according to the Stanford Community for Peace and Justice website (at Stanford Community for Peace and Justice website which appears not to have been updated for some time). It cites many reasons for opposing this policy. Apart from consistency (air-line pilots and the National Guard face no similar requirement, they say) which is hardly relevant as it can be answered by applying the new policy to all such individuals, they cite numerous interesting reasons why the policy should be considered carefully (in their view, obliterated). Said the SCPJ, "The citizenship requirement is intended to make air travel safer, but in fact it increases risk. Government studies have shown that security problems at airports are caused by inadequate training and a high turnover due to low pay and inadequate benefits," denying that citizenship grants any advantage whatsoever to the worth of an airport screener.

The SCPJ then sees a more sinister side to the policy, claiming "Furthermore, immigrants deserve to keep these jobs. The reason many airport screeners are immigrants is that these jobs are difficult, low-wage labor traditionally passed over by U.S. citizens. The [American Transportation Security Administration] excludes immigrants from precisely those jobs they were willing to work for low pay (often under $6 an hour), meager benefits, and under stressful conditions, as soon as those jobs see a substantial improvement in wages and benefits under ATSA," implying the bill reflects an anti-immigrant agenda.

On campus, student organizations have already particpiated in several rallies to this effect. The SCPJ site lists some of these events including a "General Meeting of the Immigrant Airport Workers Solidarity Committee" and some sort of "Action at the San Francisco Airport Commission," which took place on the 6th and 7th of last May.

Dan Anthony, a Stanford sophomore, tended to agree with the SCPJ that US citizens may be less trustworthy than the average immigrant airport screener. "The average foreigner has less prejudices than the average Americans with regards to screening," Mr. Anthony noted, because "After 9/11, people of Middle Eastern descent, Asian descent, and southeast Asian were being targeted and it seems funny and kind of irrelevant," referring to the fact that none of the September 11th hijackers were from the far east.

Mr. Anthony then observed that, "Given domestic and international terrorism history, we should trust the average American less, and thus the decision to block foreigners is just a heavy-handed assertion of lack of trust toward non-citizens." Mr. Anthony here referred to the fact that most acts of terror have been perpetrated by domestic citizens, historically at any rate (e.g., Timothy McVeigh, John L. Muhammad, &c.). Additionally Mr. Anthony referred to what he sees as the motivation of the policy, the "lack of trust toward non-citizens", one of the possible implications of which is best reflected by the next interviewee.

Another student was offended by the implications of the policy. Pablo Schyfter, a sophomore hailing from Costa Rica, told us, "I am a dirty, ignorant Latin American; therefore I am not trustworthy enough to give you an opinion." The policy, as it discriminates against potential and current employees based solely on their citizenship, by definition reflects a bit of xenophobia; that is, the policy is one of mistrust or even fear of all foreigners regardless of the particulars of their situation. That is, Tony Blair and Osama bin Laden alike would be barred from serving in US Customs (well, perhaps Osama bin Laden more so, as he might face some minor difficulties in obtaining a Visa, but the point remains the same).

However, as is the case with so many issues with far-reaching implications and national importance, many Stanford students either don't know or don't care about the new policy. The majority of the people interviewed had not heard of this policy before. In fact, every student spoken with required a detailed explanation of the current events surrounding the change in requirements to become a part of the US customs process.

For example, Emily Wood, another Stanford sophomore, told the Review, "Well, I guess that kinda makes sense... wait, no it doesn't." When further explanation was requested, Ms. Wood replied, "I mean, non-citizens MIGHT be as trustworthy as an American, if not more... I don't know."

Stanford students remain, in general, completely unaware of this ongoing policy change, and the activist community has largely failed to inform the rest of campus of the reason so many emails were sent and protests held. Those few students who were willing to comment, when informed of the situation in relatively neutral tone and language, almost unanimously oppose this policy change, reflecting an untapped resource available to the activist community.
