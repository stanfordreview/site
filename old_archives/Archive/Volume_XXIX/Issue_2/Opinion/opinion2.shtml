Opinion
Confessions of a (Former) Liberal
by William Hudson
Senior Staff Writer

I came to California a liberal globalist. I came back to Texas a conservative American.

For some of my friends, you might still remember a year or two in high school when I had a terrible sickness. I was overly stubborn, patently arrogant, and utterly convinced in my ideological methods to save this country and this world. I was known to espouse my views, quietly yet firmly, and I had very little respectable opposition. Most of my friends agreed with me, many of them in more impassioned overtones, and those that didn't were out of their minds, bigoted, insane, and wholly at contest with all standards of decency and morality. They were less than human, progeny of a confined mindset whose clutches I would not respect.

I was, in short, a liberal.

A liberal in Texas means a very different thing than a liberal in New York -- something that President Bush quickly discovered, much to his own chagrin, I might imagine -- but as I set out on my liberal experiment, which took two years away from my life and more from my reputation, I was unchallenged by my peers. This should have dissuaded me already, because arguing that your logic is supported by schoolchildren of a town known nationally as a rich-kid heroin hotspot is less than academically enticing. In any event, I quickly became the most liberal person in my family, and although I was still extremely conservative and traditional in my dress, conversations, and general demeanor, my politics were far enough to the left for a foul ball.

Much of this was because I was young, overzealous, and easily led by a media that is oft to point out the rapacious quality of business and the dogged post-imperalist crunch of moralists, but I myself was simply too quick to jump on the bandwagon. I envisioned myself saving the world, one tree and one welfare check at a time, and in my inability to see the world, I painted one with my refined rhetoric and broader appeals to human decency. The fact of the matter is I was actually quite good at playing the liberal. I had the rhetoric down, I knew for what and whom I should point and uphold in moments of crisis, and I knew that there were others like me, part of a bold new American experiment to save the world.

I came to college, ready to see the triumph of liberal culture in California and ready to learn. At Stanford I found the latter, but the former was a dismal disaster, and from the get-go there were blemishes on my liberal dogma. One of my friends (and now my roommate) and I were discussing over dinner very early into the first quarter politics -- we started school days after flights resumed following the terrorist attacks, and the topic of missile defense came up. My friend, a set conservative, began posing questions to me regarding my opposition to the shield. I smiled and retorted with my typical answers, polished after two years of practice. He chuckled and asked me to actually answer his question.

I was dumbstruck. At that crucial moment, with a new friend in a new place, I rediscovered myself in the most peculiar way. Day by day my friend continued to question my views -- not out of disrespect or mockery, but out of a genuine desire to meet a liberal who could support his views logically. The trouble was obvious: I couldn't, and it wasn't particular to me, either: liberalism as it is now known (after they usurped the term) cannot be rationally defended. It plays on rhetorical smokescreens, appeals of morality to an immoral crowd, and breaks the individual spirit of its followers by destroying their own ability to synthesize information rationally -- thoughts end and rhetorical memory begins.

After soul-searching, I reached the obvious conclusion: I wasn't a liberal, and neither were most Americans. Most have simply been misguided by arrogant dreamers like I used to be, playing preachers without knowing the true word of God -- or even secular reality, for that matter. Some of course truly believe their views, but the typical American liberal simply continues a tradition that is based on nothing -- flowery rhetoric and arguments without substance.

Read the newspapers. Liberals argue that Bush's disdain for the ICC will hurt our friends and break treaties. The rhetoric and puppy-dog imagery conceals the fact that the ICC destroys American individualism and values, limits military and economic freedoms, and brazenly conflicts with the Constitution. What always struck me as funny was the fact that the only thing liberals could throw against their over-performing, well-disciplined president after September 11th centered on something with which the liberals were very familiar: his rhetoric.

The way he said things. In other words, they had problems with something that had nothing to do with the man's ability to lead his people and defend our country, politically and militarily. This stems from the liberal notion that broad expressions of statements and feelings constitute actual law or theory. To obfuscate the matter even further, these statements are not individual -- they are dogmatic, ritualistic stipulations of a liberal quasi-religion. Morality is being pushed aside, logic thrown out the window, and God dethroned by a brainwashed masses who chant in unison their cult-like blurbs.

I know. I used to be one of them. Liberalism in its current form is rationally indefensible and goes against most conceptions of human decency and individuality. Like communism (or as communism, depending on your perspective), it is destined to fail. And like communism, no amount of government change or thrilling speeches will end it. The people must learn for themselves, and to do this they need to be better educated and able to understand that they are perfectly capable of making their own choices, running their own lives, and developing their own perspectives on matters that are important to them.

In short, more need to confess and step into the light. America and its people will be the better for it.
