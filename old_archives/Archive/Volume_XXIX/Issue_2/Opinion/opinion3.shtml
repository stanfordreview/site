Opinion
The Snipers Lived in My Community
by Nens Hansen
Opinions Editor

By now you have all heard of this (or these?) sniper(s) that murdered many innocent Americans on the East Coast as they went about their daily lives. Turns out, these fellows spent a great deal of time in Bellingham, where I am from. Not only that, they hung out in the same local coffeehouse that I did. Played chess there just as I did. And in general were like many local transients; hanging out in one of the few quasi-public spaces that was open until midnight. Stuart's Coffee had faux-everything. We had psuedo-intellectuals, people trying to act like artists to impress their peers, people playing at paganism and various forms of 'ancient' spirituality which had been 'rediscovered' recently, and lots of people who just liked being in a weird social setting. In a sense, a freak show with extremely expensive milkshakes.

It's not really that surprising in retrospect that these fellows found such a place appealing; it's the mecca for all people who weren't comfortable in the more traditional social settings such as school dances, bars, taverns, and so forth. The thing that really seems unusual about these fellows is something that is common to nearly all the perpetrators of acts of anti-western violence in the recent past. Now, these guys lived at the Lighthouse Mission. They were pretending to be poor. However, they were extremely well-funded (by whom is anyone's guess, though many readers have probably drawn their own conclusions I prefer to refrain from such speculation). They flew to various places in the US and without. Social outcasts they were not. In fact, Mr. Malvo was remembered by his peers at Bellingham High School as being a nice fellow.

However, these guys were definitely not ordinary. They came up with various schemes to kill people and attack the government among other things. They were, at the time, faux-terrorists: they wanted to be a part of Osama bin Laden et al, bur they didn't have the faith in Allah to believe in a future with 40 black-eyed virgins; instead they sought a more earthly reward on the order of $10,000,000. Perhaps this is what drew them to Stuart's more than anything else. In a sense, these serial killers have a good deal in common with the typical patrons of the coffee-house. The people in the coffee house who essentially resided there (as the snipers did) were there displaying a kind of alternative lifestyle image. We had would-be poets, musicians, and authors (and the occasional actual example of each species as well); mainly people who had been repeatedly rejected from mainstream society due to their unconventional ideas and desires. These guys were would-be-martyrs. Or at least they liked the scene. What they settled on was not so entirely faith-based, but certainly of comparable destructive power to your average suicide-bomber. Like most people who spent far too much time in the coffee house, they never quite lived up to their talk (thank goodness).

Greed ultimately must be considered a major motive in the various terrorist attacks against western targets. In the Columbine incident you had desire for recognition, for attention; in the destruction of the World Trade Center you had a desire for recognition of worth and machismo; and in the sniper incident you had a more familiar form of greed. They just wanted money.

I know there are some of you out there who think that, instead, this was some sort of protest against the foreign policy of the US. I counter that this is a ludicrous notion, as foreign policy in the past never inspired suicide bombing on US soil. Now, it may be the case that's why the US was targeted; however, the terror tactic itself comes from no source but greed, just like greed motivated many fighters in the Crusades and the jihad that preceded (and precipitated) it. To these people, $10,000,000.00 was worth the lives of people they had never met. This may be a fair estimate of the greed of the terrorists; they each wanted $5,000,000 worth of public recognition of their machismo, which they certainly got back in the Arab world. Why, though, did these chess-playing bums 'go for the gold' in a sense? My guess is, John A. Muhammad, divorced twice and both times denied custody of his children, felt alienated from, well, pretty much everyone. He never talked to anybody, just sat in the corner playing chess silently. He and John Lee Malvo simply played chess and pondered acts of violence, schemes to punish society and reward themselves financially. Islam was simply a religion that could be easily perverted to justify their desires.

All that being said, their ostracization was entirely self-imposed; many people approached them in a friendly manner and were rebuffed. These men were both educated, literate, and financially doing well, just like the Saudis responsible for the World Trade Center massacre.

One question, as always, remains unanswered: what can we do to prevent such demented people from committing acts of destruction? How can we keep our other John A. Muhammads and John Lee Malvos playing chess silently in a coffeehouse, not bothering anyone save for the female performer Muhammad kept harassing? Hell if I know. However, like nearly everyone, I have a suggestion. My take on this is, it's better to befriend the quiet madmen of the world than to have them go to another coast and slaughter innocents. Clearly people who sat by and did not interact with these people are not responsible for their reprehensible actions.

But seriously folks, could a little kindness really hurt all that much? If it weren't for the people in Bellingham calling the FBI about these characters, they probably wouldn't have been caught so early and more people would have died. And if the people at Stuart's hadn't been friendly and engaged these guys, then they might not have even known to be suspicious. Next time you meet some high schooler who's getting canned by the senior quarterback or a guy who was stripped of the right to visit his children (in this case for completely legitimate reasons, mind you) or any individual who feels wrongfully stripped of their dignity, think about what you might want to say. Social outcasts have feelings too, and it might not take too much to make them think about the consequences of their actions before they take them. Of course, I could be completely wrong. But if I'm not, how will you feel when the guy in the corner of your coffeehouse goes on a massacre in the Beltway?
