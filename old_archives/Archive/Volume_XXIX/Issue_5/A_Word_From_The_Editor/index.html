A Word From The Editor
Editor's Note
by Joe Lonsdale
Editor-in-chief

President Bush gave his State of the Union address tonight. We watched it on TV at my Stanford residence.

"Yeah, I thought it was pretty good," my father told me afterwards. "Pretty good?!" To myself and my conservative friends living where we are, to hear the President so strongly defending our beliefs to all of our scornful friends, to hear him stand up as the leader of the American people and reiterate with eloquence and power some of the very same points we've been insisting upon to the derision of our liberal peers, was amazing.

Here at Stanford, we're living in an ultra-liberal environment where it takes courage to publicly admit you're conservative, and only the truly strong of heart will speak out in favor of non-liberal viewpoints on controversial issues.

Recent College Republican surveys suggest over 20% of Stanford students are conservative or libertarian, but a recent question by a freshman girl as a College Republican officer passed out surveys was very telling: "Are there actually Republicans on campus?"

"I didn' tell her I was Republican," noted Travis. "Somebody's got to date the blond bombshells." While many students on campus harbor conservative views, the pervading atmosphere of intolerance means many liberal students aren't even aware there is another legitimate viewpoint.

Some people thought the liberals of the sixties were impressive for standing up and banding together against the Establishment. I tell you it takes far more to stand up to the intolerance of modern day Universities and proudly proclaim that you are pro-America, pro-capitalist, and that you disagree with the Stanford-supported "untouchable" liberal system of beliefs.

Openly ultra-liberal professors are celebrated and speak at rallies. Moderately conservative professors keep silent out of fear, or are not hired in the first place because of their bad beliefs.

No matter how strong your beliefs, when surrounded by large groups of otherwise intelligent peers with entirely antithetical viewpoints, you are forced to constantly question yourself. Many of us unconsciously start to feel guilty for holding dear beliefs considered evil and strange by those around us. But the strength and the perfect moral clarity with which President Bush systematically addressed the path of the United States of America was enough to reassure any wavering conservative students that much of the rest of the nation is sane, and he or she is not alone.

The United States is the most powerful nation in the world, and arguably the greatest civilization of all time. In our current state we are unique among other nations throughout history--we do our best to use our power for good.

The average Stanford liberal will scoff at this, having been taught a million "bad" things America has done. They will tell you about the evil of our profit-hungry capitalist system, dictators and evil men who arose in countries with whose governments we'd interfered. They toss out sound bytes like "Save the environment" and "War is bad". Recently, a liberal professor at Brown University compared Truman to Stalin for the "military industrial" complex he built, and called every President since Truman a "jerk" or "stupid."

There are different ways of looking at the world. When people evaluate a situation the best they can with a respect for the high ideals of life, liberty, property, and justice, and make the best choice they can about whether or not to get involved, and how to do so, they are doing what they can to make the world a better place.

It is easy to criticize, as history never works out without several bumps in the road, but the best you can ask for is a powerful and just response that keeps the best interest of the world in mind, given what principles you have.

President Bush stood up in front of the nation this evening, and he pledged to address the serious issues that are facing us and the world with strength and resolve, and a coherent plan of action. He was not afraid to discuss our support for dissidents in Iran, and to delineate between right and wrong. Around the nation, including in the capitol, I got the sense that despite partisanship, respect for our nation and the principles we hold dear made many Democrats applaud sincerely. Not so at Stanford. The Democrats I watched with not only did not express pleasure for anything the President said, they spent the whole time making flippant remarks and laughing at his Texas accent.

"Ha ha, nu-cu-lar! HA!" How hilarious to pronounce Ônuclear' like that! "Ha, he said Peninchula! Hilarious!" As the President sincerely addressed the grave issues our nation is facing and the path we will take, they found room only for sarcasm and remarks of "bullsh*t."

After President Bush addressed our support for those trying to bring freedom to Iran, only one of twenty Democrats in the room expressed approval. "Great, let's make more people hate us now," said another of the liberals in reply to Bush. If you stand up for good, evil people will not like you! Does that mean you do not proudly say that you support good? Where is the moral strength and righteousness at this University?

President Bush is not perfect, and I happen to disagree with him on a number of issues, especially when it comes to government involvement in community service and "faith-based" initiatives.

But thank God we have a President who realizes that national healthcare is a joke, that liberal buzzwords alone do not an argument make, that the environment is important but does not take precedence over reason and opportunity cost analysis. Thank God we have a leader that cares enough about our future to stand up to the rest of the world, who will insist that history's course will not be dictated by UN dictators, thugs, and an ultra-liberal Europe that lost all moral strength after 50 years of America playing Daddy-Security to its infantile socialist mewlings.

Tonight, President Bush reminded me that I was proud to be a conservative, and even more proud to be a liberty-loving American.
