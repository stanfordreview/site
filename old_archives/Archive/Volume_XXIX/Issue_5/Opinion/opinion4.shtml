Opinion
Students Forced to Support Groups?
by Charles Hallford
Staff Writer

As part of the bill for tuition, student fees, room, and board, every Stanford student pays ASSU general and special fees each quarter that go to various student groups, such as the Stanford Band, Club Sports, and the Sexual Health Peer Resource Center. However, not very many students know that they can request refunds for these fees. Furthermore, even fewer students realize that all of these groups will get the same amount of money regardless of how many people request refunds.

By visiting the website http://assu.stanford.edu/groups/index.shtml and clicking on the "Request a Fee Refund" link, any student can view how much of their money is going to each group. The student can then request a full or partial fee refund based on which special fee groups the student does not wish to support financially and whether or not he wishes to pay the general fees.

There are three different general fees: the community service general fee, the programming general fee, and the publications general fee. While at this time students cannot request partial refunds of these fees based on which groups they do not want to support, according to ASSU Appropriations Co-chair Josh Shipp, "You can request a refund for the general fees in whole."

When asked about this policy, David Endelman, ASSU Financial Manager, said, "The group receives the amount of funding they were approved for in the spring general election. The office of the financial manager predicts the refund rate for each group based on historical trends, then collects the appropriate amount of fees from the student body so that after the expected refunds, there will be just enough money left to give each group their allocation. None of the money collected (or not refunded) is intended for or belongs to the ASSU. "Right now refund rates are at about 7-8%. When the expected rate differs from the actual rate, the difference closes to the Special Fee Buffer. The amount that each group receives is approved in a general election in the spring in which every student is given the opportunity to vote. This is an entirely democratic process that gives each student an equal voice."

The ASSU uses the same method to calculate the special fee for each special fee group. For example, the Stanford NAACP, an undergraduate group, will receive $27,400 from special fees. However, historically, 7% of students request a refund of this group's special fee. So, the ASSU adds $2,062.37 (about 7.5% of $27,400) getting $29,462.37 total and divides this amount between the quarters and between the members of the student body to determine the special fee on each student's bill, which for winter quarter is $1.63.

At first, everyone pays, and the ASSU withholds the extra income. Then as people request refunds they are paid out of this extra income. If the extra income is exhausted by more than an expected amount of refund requests, then the Special Buffer Fund is used to pay the additional refunds. As a result, students who oppose certain groups can choose not to financially support them but cannot affect the amount of money they receive which was pre-determined in the spring elections.

However, not everyone who requests refunds from certain groups oppose them. For instance, David Garfield, a Stanford undergraduate, requested a refund from the Stanford News Readership Program, the program that delivers newspapers to various cafeterias across campus. "Every weekday so many newspapers are dropped off by freshman dorms that many of them simply go to waste, while the News Readership Program does nothing to bring even one newspaper anywhere near my house on the Row. For this reason, I request my money back."

According to ASSU President Monica Henestroza, most students do not ask for refunds along ideological lines. Most ask for a full refund of all fees or no refunds at all.

According to Mr. Shipp, some people request refunds along valid and/or noble ideological lines, e.g. not having been able to vote in the spring elections. Some people request refunds because of monetary problems they may be having. Others just request refunds because they're cheap.

However, Mr. Shipp said that refund-requesters should be warned, "The bad side of [requesting refunds] is those groups can ask for lists of students who asked for refunds." According to the ASSU website these groups "are allowed to deny their services to those students. (For example, this policy is enforced by ASSU Legal Counseling, which will deny service, and by the ASSU Speakers Bureau, which charges an entrance fee to students who have requested a refund.)"

When asked why the ASSU instated their policy concerning special and general fees, Mr. Endelman replied, "Fee groups have to submit a budget for the upcoming year, and many events they plan require a level of certainty regarding available capital that would not be possible if the flux in the refund rate directly impacted their funding. This is especially true for groups that spend money in the fall and winter, since the spring quarter sees the greatest amount of refund requests. The system was carefully constructed several years ago with everyone's best interest in mind, and remains the best funding policy for our campus."
