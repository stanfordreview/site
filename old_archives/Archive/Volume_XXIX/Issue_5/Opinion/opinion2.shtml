Opinion
Abortion: Depoliticizing Death
by William E. Hudson
Staff Writer

This week is the thirtieth anniversary of Roe v. Wade. Depending on your political perspective, this is either a source of great frustration or considerable celebration. While my own opinion on the matter will be readily apparent, I do take issue with the increasing politicization of abortion; the party divisions over a subject as important and final as abortion only serve to obfuscate the essential character of something that, whether you defend it as the woman's right to choose or a ghastly justification for infanticide, it is still a matter of life and death. Part of the problem hindering intelligent debate on the subject of abortion is the manner in which one party has made it a matter of women's rights and constitutional individualism. This will be discussed shortly; in the present, the more serious subject is the argument that a newly conceived child is not a person, and therefore not the benefactor of the same rights as his or her mother.

When you conceive a child, you create a human life; that's the definition of conception. In the act of copulation, a new human life is created from the combination of two human beings. I have never, in my years of discussion with people on this contested subject, ever encountered someone who would describe himself or herself as pregnant with a fetus; this is an important semantic distinction because so much of what partisan groups supporting the right of abortion base their conclusions on is simple semantic divisions. Personally, I find the practice of basing an entire lobbying and activist culture around the definition of a word somewhat absurd, though perhaps I am alone in that conclusion.

Outside the realm of dictionary debauchery, it is also tremendously obvious to all but the pathologically insane that it is unacceptable to destroy human life at whim unless acting in the primal context of self-defense. Self-defense is an eminently important principle rooted in nearly all religious liturgy and moral traditions. There must be just cause and self-defense should be non-fatal as far as realistically possible. Yet there has been a butchery of the term by activists who advocate abortion as self-defense against social or fiscal depression by unwilling mothers, whether by rape, incest, or other circumstances.

This argument is particularly nigh reasoned, as remitting the death penalty for social or financial ruin hardly seems justified if the mother is still free to live, a point on which many Democrats agree yet in their moral relativism cannot apply equally to underrepresented -- and untaxable -- unborn children. Certainly a mother's life can be radically altered by the birth of an unwanted baby, but there are plenty of alternatives, namely adoption, which is a far underdeveloped system wherein the waiting lists for adopted children is tremendously long. Barring the situation wherein the mother's mortal life is directly in danger, arguing that the mother has a right to kill another human being because the child will be a nuisance, inconvenience, psychological disaster, or financial burden strikes me as morally dangerous. It is a direct contradiction to the principle of self-defense on which this nation and its myriad secular and religious moral traditions is grounded.

Force has its legitimate use when other options have been exhausted and there is sufficient mortal danger to the applicant of force's bloody hand, but in doing so the applicant is prudent to remember the bloodiness of the act involved. It's no small matter to take the life of an unborn child even when not doing so would mean death for the mother, any more than it is a small matter to wage war against a dangerous and patently reckless regime halfway across the world. The simple fact of the matter, however, is that sometimes force is justified, but only upon considerable reflection and a direct presentation of danger unless acting in self-defense. It is in this test abortion, no matter how you play the rhetoric roulette of partisan politics, fails, and fails completely.

The first step to saving millions of lives in this country is to stop bickering about when life begins and realize the common humanity of us all. To live in a society that condones the murder of its most undefended inhabitants seems to be more than a political issue to me. As your week ends, ponder what are your criteria for the use of violence. I suspect that regardless of the specifics, it doesn't include the wholesale slaughter of children in the name of a woman's right to choose.

If it's possible to concretely separate abortion from the political thunderstorms that stir up at the issue's mere mention, it's fairly obvious to notice the gross injustice done to human life in the act of making it as political as it's become. The Democrats have made abortion integral to their three winning party platforms (the other two being Bush is dumb and economic crises are better solved through taxation). Republicans, to their credit, have recognized the shockingly elusive fact that life is precious and should be saved, but themselves are the victims of some internal division on the subject, resulting in concessions and setbacks.

This has to end. Children's lives should not be held captive by party platforms or the politics of legislators; their lives should be the same as all other life in the country -- protected, encouraged, and respected for individual worth and beauty. The Supreme Court forgot this thirty years ago, but now we can work towards dismantling the right to murder while rebuilding our nation's venerated respect for human life and liberty.
