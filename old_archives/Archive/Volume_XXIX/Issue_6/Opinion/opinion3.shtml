Opinion
Pro-War: Putting Hindsight and Foresight Together to Pressure Iraq
by Piotr. H. Kosicki
Opinions Editor

With the extent of the case presented by the Bush administration as to the imperative of forcing Saddam Hussein to surrender his weapons of mass destruction, it may seem tedious to continue belaboring the need to justify a potential war on Iraq. Unfortunately, recent media coverage of the British interview with Saddam Hussein published on February 4 and Secretary of State Colin Powell's case to the UN made on February 5 demonstrate that media sources -- and through them many Americans -- still don't perceive the essence of the problem.

A continuous barrage of rhetoric from renowned figures who happen to be idealistic pacifists hasn't helped either in clarifying the issue. South African ex-president Nelson Mandela was recently quoted describing the American effort to pressure Saddam as follows: "One power with a president who has no foresight and cannot think properly is now wanting to plunge the world into a holocaust."

It's easy enough to shake one's head and sigh in frustration at the accusations of ethnocentrism, imperialism, and rashness launched at the Bush administration, but the only way to combat idealistic dogma is to continue reinventing US and world security imperatives in new explanatory contexts.

Perhaps the most persuasive context in which to understand why it may be necessary to launch a war against Iraq is one of recent history. Mandela claims that our president lacks "foresight." I counter that not only does he have a clear vision of the imperatives facing the world's future but that his vision is uniquely informed by hindsight.

The key consideration on which foresight and hindsight converge is that of "national security." The term is sometimes used loosely, but it has clear meaning in the context of recent history. The Cold War saw the birth of peacetime threats to the very existence of the American nation. The term was inaugurated into public discourse with the 1947 creation of the National Security Council, which has since become a staple of executive politics. During the Cold War, successive administrations defined interests of national security as issues that shaped the government's ability to guarantee the stability and survival of the United States.

The Cold War may have ended, but the WMD race it inaugurated has increased the number of threats as rogue states and terrorist networks that benefited from the chaos of the Soviet Union's collapse have gained access to WMD materials and secrets. Very little divides the United States from being susceptible to attacks ordered on the whim of a mad dictator or a misguided zealot. It may seem counterintuitive that a nation like Iraq or a brigand of marauding desert-troopers like Al-Qaeda could pose a real threat to the stability and survival of U.S. citizens, but weapons of mass destruction have made that threat both possible and very real.

When the Bush administration issued its National Security Strategy last September, it was keenly aware of the historical connotations of "national security" conceptions. The serious effort made by President Bush to construct an international coalition hinged on the United Nations reflects his understanding of the fact that national security is no longer defined by a bipolar conflict between the US and the USSR. Rogue states and fanatical terrorism are worldwide phenomena, and the most effective way to combat them would be to facilitate a coordinated global crackdown. When Nelson Mandela criticizes our president, he ignores the fact that President Bush has demonstrated his understanding of the potential boons of UN cooperation by choosing to encourage the UN to boost its own legitimacy by embracing a role for the future.

The care taken by Bush and Powell to offer a measured representation of our understanding of the evidence against Iraq shows particular foresight on Bush's part. Terrorism is a phenomenon unpredictable at best: although the current trend in rogue states and terrorist networks is one of explicit hatred for the US, that trend could easily turn on many other nations and forces in the world.

France may say now that it doesn't really see the magnitude of the international threat posed by Saddam, and, for the purposes of its narrow, short-term vision, it may be correct: the threat is to us, not to them. But when the day comes on which some terrorist network or rogue state poses a threat that includes France, the French will rue the day that they balked on UN cooperation to force Iraq to surrender its WMD. National security may once have focused exclusively on the US, and it still does revolve around the government's ability to guarantee our stability and survival. What President Bush realizes, however, is that it's in the interest of the whole world for the US to make its national security an international issue. Should the world choose to follow him, it will be demonstrating its "foresight." At the end of the day, though, we are the only ones responsible for safeguarding our own survival, and that is why we ultimately reserve the right to make war unilaterally.
