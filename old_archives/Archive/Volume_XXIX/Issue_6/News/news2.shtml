Friedman Speaks to Review Staff
by Silvia Cruz
Staff Writer

Milton Friedman
Milton Friedman, world renowned economist and recipient of the 1976 Nobel Prize for economic science, graced Stanford students with his presence at an open lunch meeting on Tuesday, February 4th. Born in the United States in 1912, he is perhaps most distinguished for his book Capitalism and Freedom, in which he presents his view of an ideal economic system founded upon the principle of private enterprise operating in a free market.

Mr. Friedman broached a plethora of issues in Tuesday's discussion. From the privatization of Social Security to support of President Bush's proposed tax cut, one underlying stream of thought surfaced throughout all of his opinions: the sanctity of individual freedom achieved through the apparatus of free market operations.

This underlying principle was apparent in his discussion on the drug war and the legalization of marijuana. Mr. Friedman said he finds it hard to understand how anybody could be in support of today's drug laws. Billions of dollars, he explained, are wasted every year on a war the United States is clearly losing. His solution: Get rid of the laws and treat drugs the same as tobacco or alcohol.

Mr. Friedman was careful to point out that his personal objection to the drug war stems from moral grounds, referring to the idea shared by many that it is not the government's business to interfere with the private activities of individuals.

"Stick with John Mill's dictum," he suggested. "'What anybody does to him or her self is their own business.'" However, he also noted that the pragmatic reasons for quelling the drug war reinforce the moral: the private and legal distribution of drugs would be more econoomical, and safer for society.

Aside from reforming of current drug laws, his current efforts are focused on promoting the spread of school vouchers in the hopes to make education run more efficiently, along the lines of a well-functioning free market.

"We are making real progress," he optimistically stated, "especially with the recent Supreme Court decision." Drawing upon the principles embodied in a free market, he argued that providing vouchers to parents that allowed them to send their children to public or private schools of their choice would lead to a widespread emergence of private educational institutions far surpassing in quality those of the current public education system.

"Anything the government does, private industry can do for half the cost," he noted.

In discussing Bush's tax plan, Mr. Friedman explained that he'd "...never met a tax cut I didn't like." But what about deficits?

"Do I think deficits are good? Depends what sort of decificit." Deficits, explained Mr. Friedman, are not good in and of themselves. However, not all deficits are bad; whether or not you have a deficit is not the most important thing. What is important is for the government to take in the least amount of money possible. Money spent on tax dollars is not just worthless, but often has a negative utility, due to its ability to create programs that will need to be sustained with further money in the future, he noted.

Because the government will spend whatever it takes in anyway, he continued, the less money it takes in, the better of the economy will be in the future. So while "having a deficit isn't wonderful," said Mr. Friedman, "it's superior to more taxation."

Following the discussion, several students approached Mr. Friedman for autographs and handshakes. When asked what he thought of the discussion, Stanford College Republicans President Joe Fairbanks replied, "I think every student should be required to read his works."

Mr. Friedman's visit is part of a weekly event created by Senior Hoover Fellow Dr. Beichman in collaboration of The Stanford Review, known as the "Hoover Lunch." Dr. Beichman is an accomplished journalist and scholar, who is still active in both of those fields despite being senior to Mr. Friedman. He coordinates the Tuesday lunches and gets speakers for students, in return for a Diet Coke and student lunch guests. The students at the Review and others who attend the lunches are greatful for Dr. Beichman's kind efforts in organizing the event.

Every Tuesday, a Resident or Visiting Fellow from the Hoover Institution and members of the Stanford community sit down and discuss various topics ranging from the focus area of that week's featured Fellow to current domestic and international issues confronting the United States and the world. Past lunches have included discussions with experts from West Point, International Relations experts on multiple regions of the world, the inventor of the flat tax, famous journalists, and several other distinguished scholars and accomplished guests.

Few Stanford students are aware of the event, but those students who consistently attend the lunches speak very highly of them. "The Hoover Lunches are incredibly interesting and informative," said one attendee. "In the heat of all these ridiculous petitions and requests to sever ties from the Hoover Institution, it's refreshing to see students interacting and learning from this great institution."

Joe Lonsdale, another lunch attendee, agreed. "The high-caliber individuals we meet at Hoover has rounded out my Stanford education," he said.
