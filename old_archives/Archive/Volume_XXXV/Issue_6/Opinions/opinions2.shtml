Editor's Note

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The term “limited government” is not nearly as clear of a mandate as one might expect. It has been a rallying cry among conservatives for years and yet, when faced with the task of defining the ideal “limited government,” I think one would find significant disagreement, even among Republicans, as to where the boundaries of government should lie. This past week I watched “The Corporation.” The “documentary” amounts to a 3 hour tirade against global capitalism, which, in its climactic summary, boldly declares that “fifteen corporations would like to control the conditions of our lives.” The documentary is an example of dogmatic leftist thought taken to the extreme. Its utopian “power to the people” conclusion is about as solid as Enron’s accounting. Yet, it has an undeniable gut-instinct appeal. Nobody wants to be controlled by a big, evil corporation. Thus, in creating an enemy of big-business, it appeals to the intrinsic human desire to be individualistic and to live in freedom. The not-so-subliminal message is that liberals are the voice of the underdog, standing up to evil money grubbing conservatives. This is a sentiment that I witnessed this past weekend in Tahoe.

As I drove along highway 89, I had an interesting discussion with a liberal Democrat about how he sees Republicans in our era. According to him, there is one reason to vote Republican and that is to “keep the rich richer and the poor poorer.” I didn’t find the comment particularly surprising since it is certainly not the first time such a criticism has been leveled. Yet, it serves to illustrate what I think is a broader misconception about the liberal-conservative divide. Liberals are supposedly the bleeding hearts – the ones who do the most for those who have the least. Conservatives are cold hearted pragmatists. The charge is no more accurate than Michael Moore’s statistics, yet perhaps it is an indication of the way we, as conservatives, come across. The truth is that capitalism is neither intrinsically good nor evil. However, Conservatives tend to favor free enterprise because it has generally proven to be the most effective means of generating wealth. Wealth, in turn, creates prosperity. Prosperity alleviates poverty. It is an intellectual and somewhat complex argument – one which does not appeal to those seeking a quick and intuitive solution. As economist Thomas Sowell wrote in a recent column, “[w]hile the creation of wealth may be more effective for enabling millions of people to rise out of poverty, it provides no special role for the political left, no puffed up importance, no moral superiority, no power for them to wield over others. Redistribution is clearly better for the left.” And so the myth may be perpetuated that Republicans have no heart, since our argument does not appeal to the masked teenager carrying a red and black flag. At its heart, however, our vision for America is every bit as humanitarian as the approach of the left.

Still, in many cases, we are unable to win the war of gut-instinct politics. What can we do to reverse the stereotype? I think we can do a better job of directly confronting supposedly altruistic liberals with the reality that years of transfer payments and entitlement spending have not done a darn thing to eliminate poverty. Our vision of welfare is that of a limited safety net and not that of an experiment in socialism. I also think we can do a better job of clarifying our positions. Ronald Reagan famously said in his inaugural address, “government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem.” Though I agree in many respects, I also disagree with the somewhat broad generalization he makes. I had the opportunity to work in government this summer with engineers dedicated to making sure Palo Alto is a safe place for its citizens. By and large, my coworkers were passionate about their work and took pride in serving the public. Let us not be dogmatic in our thinking like the left. Let us continue to carry the conservative movement forward with an open-mind.
