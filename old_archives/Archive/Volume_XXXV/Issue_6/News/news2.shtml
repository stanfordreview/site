Gov. Bush Addresses Education Policy

by Tristan Abbey and Carl J. Kelm

Deputy Editors

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Florida Governor Jeb Bush addressed Hoover Institution scholars and education policy experts on January 12 at the Arrillaga Alumni Center, outlining to the audience the various reforms and improvements his state made in recent years to its schooling system.

The Republican governor’s visit coincided with a meeting of the Koret Task Force, a public policy group focusing on education reform sponsored by the San Francisco-based Koret Foundation. The research group supports and advocates policies such as the No Child Left Behind Act, school vouchers, and accountability for schools and teachers. Gov. Bush implemented many of the Task Force’s ideas in Florida.

“Those handwringers have been proven wrong,” declared Hoover Institution director John Raisian as he introduced Bush, referring to critics. “The governor’s plan is working.”

Raisian contrasted Florida’s rapid economic growth and its “revolutionized” state education system with California. He explicitly described the purpose of the dinner: “to honor Governor Bush,” who he pointed out is the only historical two-term Republican governor in Florida.

Bush received a standing ovation upon his 6:47 PM entry into McCaw Hall. The audience, made up of scores of distinguished Hoover Fellows and hundreds of staffers and others, routinely interrupted the governor’s speech with enthusiastic applause.

Aside from one television crew and The Stanford Review, working media were largely absent from the dinner event.

Gov. Bush began his pre-dinner speech by praising the Koret Task Force. “I’ve been accused of being a policy nerd,” he said, adding he believes in “improving the human condition” through education reforms. Because the world is moving so fast, he argued, “the greatness of America will subside” if the school system does not perform. “There are a lot of people that actually read your work,” he said of the Task Force, adding, “Ideas have consequences.”

Bush structured his talk around five rules he says he learned over the course of his work as governor. First, “Say what you’re going to do, and do what you say you’re going to do.” He argued that the left-wing is “void of ideas” and that conservatives need to forcefully defend their convictions. He cited how Florida is “ending social promotion.”

Second, “If you don’t measure, you don’t care.” He touted “discernible gains,” particularly among minority students, in state-mandated testing. Schools need to be “willing to measure the results accurately and consistently,” he argued. This requires mandated state-wide tests, comparing schools against each other, and tracking academic performance over many years.

Third, “You have to stay in it to win it.” In order for his reforms to succeed, they require a long-term commitment from politicians that will succeed him. The battle “requires constant vigilance,” he warned, and accountability reforms must become “embedded in the culture” if they are to continue their effectiveness. Vouchers, which enable parents to use money that would otherwise have paid for their children’s attendance at repeatedly failing schools to attend others, constitute “cornerstones of accountability.” Opponents distort the issue, he claimed, with their “rhetoric and jargon,” and argued against the “tortured notion that competition is a bad thing.” He stated over 90% of school vouchers have gone to Black and Hispanic students.

Fourth, “If you’re involved in advocating big reforms, communicate, communicate, communicate.” Bush said he wants to get the message out that his reforms will allow children to “move beyond their circumstances” and will “teach them to dream and empower them to make it real.”

Fifth, “Success is never final, and reform is never finished.” One is either “in ascendancy or in decline,” he argued. Urging the audience never to “play defense” but to always “play offense,” he said bad teachers should be fired and replaced by teachers who have “zeal” for proper education.

After speaking for roughly twenty minutes, Bush also answered half a dozen questions from the audience. He wants to make sure parents are aware of how their students’ schools are performing, how their children are performing, and how that performance measures up against other schools and standards. He lamented Florida’s “industrialized approach” to education, which entails sixty-seven large school districts, and therefore large schools. He explained that the state should be able to fire badly-performing teachers just as it is now able to fire badly-performing bureaucrats. He condemned bilingual education, declaring that it has had no benefit and advocating “total immersion” in English.

“We underestimate the genius of young kids,” he said. “Why would you want to hold kids back in order to be politically correct?”

He also outlined what his state has done to support exceptional children, such as those diagnosed with mental or physical disabilities. He said one reform has been empowering parents of such children with the right to judge whether or not their children are getting the best education available. Even the American Civil Liberties Union and the powerful National Education Association have hesitated to criticize this, he said.

Gov. Bush has suffered a political hit in recent weeks, with the Florida State Supreme Court ruling that one of his voucher programs is unconstitutional. Addressing the State Supreme Court’s ruling against one of his voucher programs, Gov. Bush pointed out that there is a human side to the problem: over six hundred mostly minority students who are affected by the ruling will have to return to failing schools as a result. He mentioned the thousands of demonstrators who gathered outside the courthouse in favor of vouchers.

Gov. Bush was presented with an advance copy of A Wealth of Ideas by Bertrand Patenaude, to be published later this year by Stanford University Press.

The Koret Task Force consists of eleven Hoover scholars: John Chubb, Bill Evers, Chester Finn, Eric Hanushek, Paul Hill, E. D. Hirsch, Caroline Hoxby, Terry Moe, Paul Peterson, Diane Ravitch, and Herbert Walberg. Dr. Hoxby, a Harvard professor, was also honored at the event. Also in attendance were Hoover scholars from a wide variety of fields, including foreign policy, economics, national security, and the environment.
