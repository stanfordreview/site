24-Hour Strike Fails, Despite Strong Support

by Ryan Tracey

News Editor

 

Update: On January 20th, Stanford Hospital and SEIU 715 reached an agreement for a new contract. The Hospital gave in to many of the workers' demands. Details can be found at SEIU's website. On January 31, another concession was made with the University's service workers.

 

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Although 2,600 workers participated in a 24-hour strike on December 12, 2005, Stanford University adequately retained important student services and, thus far, has refused to meet the union’s demands.

State Assemblyman Ira Ruskin, a democrat representing Los Altos, asked the university to meet the union’s demands during a rally held at noon during the day of the strike. 1,200 workers and many students attended the rally, held outside the Board of Trustees meeting. Workers continue to demand improved family healthcare coverage, better wages, and job security.

The university hired 75 one-day replacement workers to operate the dining halls. Non-union workers also helped the university keep their dining halls running during the first day of finals for Fall Quarter. Paper plates, plastic silverware, and disposable cups were also used to lighten the load on the reduced staff.

The University service workers are still under a contract that does not expire until August 2006. In contrast, the 1,300 hospital workers that also went on strike during December 12 currently do not have a contract. On January 9, Stanford Hospital workers rejected the latest contract offer by the university. Hospital workers also voted on January 9th to allow the negotiating team to call for another strike, if deemed necessary. Overall, the December 12 strike proved ineffectual.

Various reasons have been given for the short length of the strike. The union’s chief negotiator, John Vellardita, stated that the strike only lasted 24-hours in order to allow a federal mediator to help with the negotiations. A credible source for The Review also speculated that a longer strike might have endangered the benefits of the workers, especially with Winter Break approaching.

Many student groups—led by the Stanford Labor Action Coalition and Coalition for Labor Justice—have sympathized with the workers’ grievances. An e-mail composed by the two groups attacked the University for not participating in “good-faith bargaining” during the negotiations process.

Nevertheless, a group called “Stand by Stanford” supports the university in the bargaining process. The group asserts that “ Stanford University is an enlightened employer” on its website (http://www.standbystanford.org), asking people to “send a message to the administration of this great university: Stanford University, we of the Stanford community stand by you!”

The Stand by Stanford group—composed of four undergraduates, one graduate student, and two alumni—choose to remain anonymous. According to their website, the group received “several e-mails with conspiracy theories, blackmail and even threats.” The group claims that several of their members “take severe offence to being called republican,” as they “support gay marriage rights, are pro-choice, and don’t believe much in the trickle down effect” and “voted against George Bush, and Arnold Schwarzenegger.” The group hosts a petition on its website that visitors can sign in support of their cause.

The Stanford worker strike occurred just weeks before 30,000 New York transit workers went on strike for raises and a stronger pension plan. Like the Stanford strike, the New York transit strike did not last long—workers returned after just three days. The transit worker strike also ended without a new contract.

The current contract between Stanford and its workers guarantees an extensive list of benefits. Full-time workers receive eleven paid holidays, 10-24 days of paid vacation, travel insurance, and at least 75% of the cost of their family health coverage. In addition, Stanford will also pay for four years of the college tuition for workers’ children—up to one-half the cost of Stanford tuition. Uniquely, the contract also provides for a one acre site on campus and a half-acre site near SLAC that workers can use to grow crops. Water is provided without charge.

The union continues to argue that workers should receive 100% of the cost of their family health coverage. A promise of extending benefits to new employees is also being sought.
