Hoover Lunches: A Personal Perspective

by Luukas Ilves

Deputy Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

If I asked you what the bastions of conservatism on campus were, what would come to mind? Columbae and CASA? Or would you say The Hoover Institute and the Stanford Review? I know most of my friends would probably choose the latter. So what happens when the two collaborate? A conspiracy to take over the world, you might say. Surprisingly, no.

Nearly every Wednesday at noon, ten or so Review staffers and a number of friends stream into a conference room on the second floor of the Lou Henry Hoover building. There we greet a Hoover Fellow, a prominent member of the Institute who is here to discuss his research and scholarship. As we greedily devour pizza, he regales us with his experience and wisdom. Then, we spend the second half of lunch asking questions of our speaker. All in all, these Hoover lunches are not much different to the scores of lunchtime seminars that occur at Stanford every week.

Not even the opinions themselves are particularly unusual. As junior Tucker Herbert, an Economics major in Murray explains, “I have been impressed that the speakers have very well developed nuanced opinions that do not pander to one specific ideology - quite distant from the Stanford undergrad urban legend that the Hoover is run by extremist neo-cons and war hawks.” Past speakers have included:

•Jonathan Moore, a career diplomat in the Foreign Service

•Arnold Beichman, an expert on the post-Cold War security situation

•Carla Robbins, chief diplomatic correspondent of the Wall Street Journal

•Michael Doyle, an award-winning local journalist who now works in Washington

•Milton Friedman

•Larry Diamond, the Stanford professor who is now suing the Bush administration

•Col William Hix, a chief strategist in Iraq

The Hoover lunches afford at times rare opportunities. Consider Colonel Hix, who in addition to his capacity as Chief of Strategy for Multinational Force-Iraq has also been spending time at the Hoover Institute. The week before he was slated to return to Iraq, he gave a Tuesday afternoon presentation to Hoover institution that was packed at the seams. Senior Fellows were sitting on the floor. Later that week, he held another presentation for the Review. Five of us listened to his presentation and then asked multiple rounds of questions of the man who was helping run the Iraq war strategy. My Political Science professor had talked about his Iraq-related correspondence with Colonel Hix, so it was nice to relate my in-class knowledge to his experience, and ask Hix questions in so intimate an environment.

We also had the opportunity to listen to Larry Diamond, a senior fellow at Hoover and a plaintiff in the ACLU case against the Bush administration. He held a commanding presence in the room as he explained the situation in Iraq. Diamond, who has served as a senior advisor to the Coalition Provisional Authority, quite bluntly admitted that in retrospect going into Iraq was a foolish decision and not necessarily the best way to promote democracy. But he also pragmatically maintained the importance of staying in Iraq and maintaining order. His candor and criticism where it was due surpass any other appraisal I have heard of the Iraq situation in its rigor and credibility. After his presentation, the fifteen or so of us who were present had him to ourselves for questioning.

The best part about the Hoover lunches is that they’re open to everyone. No RSVP is necessary. Just show up at room 238 of the Lou Henry Hoover building (next to the Hoover tower) Wednesdays at noon for a fun hour of pizza and politics. And in case you are wondering, no, we won’t make you join the staff of the Review.
