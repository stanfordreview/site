Lebanon : A Chance to Make a New Friend

by Boris Hanin and Kevin Richards

Deputy Editors

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

There is an old saying that every cloud has a silver lining. This adage seems to be true in the case of the assassination of Lebanon’s former Prime Minister, Rafik Hariri. His death, while ultimately a tragedy, has at least some shimmer in that it presents a unique opportunity for the West to strengthen its position in the Middle East and win an Arab ally in the War on Terror. To do this, however, the West must take positive steps to rid Lebanon of the Syria’s controlling influence, which has been pervasive for decades. The threat of total destabilization in Lebanon has already forced Syria to withdraw its troops. Now both the US and UN are officially blaming the Syrian government and specifically the military-intelligence chief Asef Shawkat, brother-in-law of the Syrian President, for Hariri’s assassination.

But this hasn’t ended Syrian dominance in Lebanon. Pro-Syrian forces still envelop the Lebanese government and Syria’s dictator Bashar Al-Assad has no interest changing the status quo. Even with the removal of Syrian troops from Lebanon, Hezbollah’s more than 20,000 men-in-arms are officially loyal to Syria. Moreover, the pro-Syrian government in Lebanon is delaying elections so that the Christian, Druze, and Sunni opposition will have a chance to self-destruct. Why should the West let it?

Several things must happen to curtail Syria’s power over its weaker neighbor. First, the international community needs to apply strong international pressure for immediate elections. Second, U.S. and U.N. diplomats need to meet with the anti-Syrian coalition. Tacit acknowledgement of their cause will go a long way in maintaining their unity. Next, the West must deal with Hezbollah, a Syrian-funded terrorist organization akin to Hamas. Hezbollah is many things - a political party, a terrorist group, and a social services organization. In the past Syria has always curbed Hezbollah’s political power to prevent any one party from growing too strong, but has encouraged its terrorist activities as a reaction to Israel’s occupation of southern Lebanon. But with Israel’s pullout in 2000 and the possibility of unfettered political sway, Hezbollah has been more reluctant to follow Syrian commands. The West can capitalize on this trend and in time transform Hezbollah into an exclusively political organization.

All this wishful thinking is based on the premise that the West can have some influence in Lebanon. Is that an unreasonable premise? Frankly, no. Before Syria’s occupation of Lebanon nearly three decades ago, there was strong international, especially American, presence in the Levant. To this day, noted institutions such as The American University of Beirut, the Lebanese American University, International College, and the American Community School are symbols of Western involvement in Lebanese society. Furthermore, the experiences of Lebanon’s bloody civil war have taught many Lebanese the necessity and importance of tolerance in keeping peace.

Why should helping Lebanon even matter to the United States? A parable about Middle Eastern relations may clear things up. A scorpion asks a frog to carry it across a river. The frog is anxious for his life, but the scorpion points out that if he stings the frog, they will both die. When they are at midstream, the scorpion stings the frog. The drowning frog asks the drowning scorpion why he did this. “Because this is the Middle East,” replies the scorpion with his last breath. In a region so unpredictable and so hostile to the US and the West, building a genuine partnership with Lebanon would boost security and begin to move the region towards prolonged stability. Namely, making a strong democratic ally today may prevent being stung by an enemy tomorrow.
