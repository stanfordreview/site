Editor's Note

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

As I sit down to write this, my final editor’s note, I must admit I am somewhat tired. It has been a long week for me. Between the onslaughts of school work, preparing and teaching a class, applying for summer jobs and, of course, dealing with Stanford’s latest attempt to silence The Review. I was barely even able to make time to watch The Daily Show with Jon Stewart last night. But I don’t want to talk about any of that right now. This volume has been an exceptional experience for me. I am very proud of the final product and feel that it is something to be celebrated.

It has been challenging. From my first meeting with Milton last spring, I knew that there was a lot more involved in running this paper than meets the eye. We sought to change a number of things about the way our paper operated and I believe that the results speak for themselves. The challenges sometimes meant longer nights than I would have liked. There is nothing quite like debugging inDesign with the printing press at 2 am. Milton actually drove all the way to San Jose to pick up Issue 5 on a Saturday, so that we could offer commentary on the worker strike before The Daily could pick up the story. In talking with our next Editor-in-Chief, Ryan Tracey, I have little doubt that he will take-up the challenges whole-heartedly and continue to improve The Review in the future.

It has been rewarding. When I was a freshman (way back when Stanford did not enforce the door-to-door distribution prohibition), I found some level of solace in receiving The Review on my door stop every-other week. Here was a group of people that not only shared my political views, but also spoke about them openly on a campus where Democrats outnumber Republicans 10:1 among faculty. I don’t claim to be some marginalized victim – what I’m saying is that I looked up to Gary Raichart, even though I didn’t know him, because he was willing to speak his mind. I would like to think that we have done a good job of filling Gary’s shoes. We still seek to present a conservative viewpoint in a constructive and intellectually rigorous manor. I think we have done a pretty darn good job this volume, and our entire staff deserves credit.

It has been a learning experience. I think I would be lying if I claimed to have been consistent in all of my op-ed’s since my days as a lowly staff writer. The reality is that politics is dynamic. I used to sport Anti-Flag garb and read anarchist literature in my free time. Now I make fun of kids who do exactly that. The funny thing is that when I saw Anti-Flag headlining the Warped Tour a couple years ago, I’m pretty sure that 90% of the angry punks stomping around on pier 32 would have never heard of the band if it weren’t for the literally hundreds of corporate Warped Tour sponsors. Still, I think I have moderated a bit this year and that’s ok. If nothing more, I have learned from The Review that the moment that we refuse to change our opinions is the moment that we close our minds.

It has been fun. I had a lot of fun distributing our first issue door-to-door. Perhaps equally fun was witnessing some uptight RA’s whip themselves into a frenzy over a few sheets of paper. I had a blast going head to head with anarchists on camera last summer when making our fall recruiting video. I enjoyed the discussions we had at staff meetings and on our email list; one email quoted from Leno, “Al Sharpton is getting his own show on CBS. I believe it’s called “The Amazing Race Card”… and people laughed! Send that same email to communidad and see how many chuckles you get. I enjoyed Milton’s going away party – he has been missed the last couple weeks, and did an excellent job as Executive Editor. I could not have done this job without him – or any of the incredibly dedicated editors and contributors that made this volume what it is. So to the Editors of this volume – Ryan, Nick, Diane, Navin, Omkar, Milton – thanks for putting in the time. I really appreciate it.

So that’s it. And I can’t help but feel a bit nostalgic, but I suppose it is someone else’s turn. I concluded my first Editor’s note with an open invitation. “If you would like to become part of a community that welcomes intellectual challenge over apathy, contrarian thought over conformity and most importantly, believes in individual liberty over collectivism, I encourage you to join us and to consider writing for The Stanford Review.” At the time I wrote it, I didn’t really think much of it – it was sort of an embellished bit of prose that I hoped would recruit some closet conservatives out of the woodwork. Yet, when I look at it now, I think it really does represent the aims of our publication. Though we may sometimes fall short, we truly attempt to be open-minded and intellectually rigorous. We value individualism and we value those who are willing to speak out. So long as there is a Review, there will be such an invitation.
