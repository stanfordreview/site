Silver Anniversary of a Golden Leader

by Carl J. Kelm

Deputy Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On January 20, 1981, Ronald Reagan was sworn in as the fortieth President of the United States. Twenty-five years later, the lasting stamp The Gipper left on the nation and the world is clear to see. From economic and governmental reform, to America’s Cold War victory, to the restoration of America’s confidence in itself, Reagan righted the course of history and is surely one of our greatest leaders.

When criticizing Reagan, many immediately attack the economy; they do so wrongly – Reagan was an excellent steward of the economy. Reagan’s implementation of supply-side economics led to the largest peacetime economic expansion in history, as well as set the foundation for American economic growth in subsequent years. As Mallory Factor, chairman if the Free Enterprise Foundation, notes,

“With a few notable setbacks, he created a foundation for prosperity based on sound money, low marginal tax rates, and efficient, minimal regulation that has remained in place to this day. The results have been stunning. When Reagan took office, payroll employment stood at below 90 million; it’s now over 135 million -- that’s 45 million more working Americans. Gross domestic product, the size of the U.S. economy, stood at about $5.2 trillion (in chained 2000 dollars); it has since more than doubled, after adjusting for inflation, to well over $10 trillion. The stock market in 1981 had been stagnant for decades, the Dow Jones was under 1000 and still buried on the business pages; the market has since skyrocketed, with the Dow topping 11,000 last week, as stock ownership has become an engine of prosperity for well more than 50 percent of American families.”

The Wall Street Journal also points out that, “Perhaps the greatest tribute to the success of Reaganomics is that, over the course of the past 276 months, the U.S. economy has been in recession for only 15. More wealth has been created in the U.S. in the last quarter-century than in the previous 200 years.” As Reagan himself noted when the economy boomed in 1983, “You know our policies must be working because they don’t call it Reaganomics anymore.”

The one area where Reagan did not meet his goals was immediately taming the budget deficit. He realized that he could not both carry out his defense plan and cut all spending simultaneously, and he followed his priority of winning the war – a goal well worth the cost. Despite his budget deficits, the economic framework he put in place ended up churning out surpluses in the Nineties; the deficit did end up being big enough to take care of itself. Some would argue that Clinton’s 1993 tax hike was what changed the economy, but the Wall Street Journal provides evidence to the contrary. The economy continued to decline in the aftermath of the Clinton’s cut, and then “…both the stock and bond markets turned upward precisely on Election Day in 1994, beginning a whirlwind six-year rally. By 1998, growth and fiscal restraint delivered a budget surplus for the first time in nearly 30 years. In 1997 President Clinton signed a further reduction in the capital gains tax, which propelled investment and the stock market to even greater heights.” Had not Reagan championed fiscal restraint and lower taxes, not only would the stagflation of the 1970s lasted even longer, the economy likely would not have boomed as it has.

Besides being an economic reformer, Reagan was the seminal Cold Warrior, one who championed “Peace through Strength.” Some counter this notion though recalling Harry Truman, not remembering that President Truman did not take any substantive action until Republicans took over both houses of Congress in 1946 by calling Truman soft on communism. Truman was instrumental in that he did vow to contain the Soviets; the problem was, he and his successors largely failed. While campaigning in 1980, the Reagan Doctrine was formulated, vowing that communism would be opposed wherever found and replaced with free and democratic societies. Truman and others lacked both the vision and the resolve to defeat communism once and for all. No other president dared say or do what Reagan did, for fear of upsetting the Soviets. No one else called the Soviets “strangers to morality” (as Reagan did in his first press conference as president) or dared term the USSR an “Evil Empire.” Reagan promised victory in 1981, announcing, “The West will not contain communism, it will transcend communism. We will not bother to denounce it, we’ll dismiss it as a sad, bizarre chapter in human history whose last pages are even now being written.”

This was a sharp departure from other administrations’ tactics, such as the Kissinger policy of securing the second-best place in the world for America, or Carter asking America to abandon its “inordinate fear of communism.” Indeed, the notions of détente and coexistence with the Soviets had crept into America’s strategic outlook and did not instill confidence of victory. Reflecting on this phenomenon, Reagan once wrote, “I am amazed that our national leaders had not philosophically and intellectually taken on the principles of Marxism-Leninism. We were always too worried we would offend the Soviets if we struck at anything so basic. Well, so what? Marxist-Leninist thought is an empty cupboard. Everyone knew it by the 1980s, but no one was saying it. I decided to articulate a few of these things.” He challenged the Soviets not only in his strong rhetoric, but in his strong actions as well. No other American president ever would have initiated the Strategic Defense Initiative, or would have put Pershing II missiles in Europe (despite domestic and European opposition), or could have outsmarted Gorbachev at Reykjavik.

From the October Revolution in 1917 to Reagan’s election in 1980, communism spread nearly uncontrollably around the globe. An even better comparison between Reagan and his predecessors is the level of communist expansion during each presidency. In Ann Coulter’s powerful work, Treason, she reflects on the presidencies from Wilson to Carter, concluding that under each administration America witnessed the spread of communism.

But according to Coulter, Reagan halted and even reversed the global communistic trend: “For the first time since the Russian Revolution, Communist countries began to become free. Not one country fell to Communism on Reagan’s watch. Three Soviet-backed regimes began to crumble, one was taken back outright, and by the end of Reagan’s presidency, the USSR was negotiating the terms of its surrender. In 1980, months before Reagan, would be elected president, Harvard Sovietologist Adam Ulam ruefully remarked that the Soviets’ expanding empire could not be stopped unless faced with ‘a power strong and determined enough to make Soviet foreign adventurism too risky and expensive.’ Under Ronald Reagan, America was that power.”

Back at home, Reagan won other major victories. Politically, he rescued the GOP from the wilderness. Author Craig Shirley points out,

“The GOP had hit rock bottom by the time of the fall elections of 1974. The GOP lost countless House and Senate seats and hundreds of state legislative races. In fact, the situation was so bad, the Democratic Party by January of 1975 had near or total control of 49 state governments. Only in Kansas did the GOP hold sway. With no money coming in, the Republican National Committee fired dozens of staffers and closed its headquarters in December of 1974 for three weeks, just to save on the electricity bill. The GOP was bankrupt in every sense of the word.”

Republican influence today seems to be closer to the opposite position; the Reagan Revolution still lives on in America.

Reagan also restored America’s faith in itself. Frank Donatelli, former Reagan official and current secretary and treasurer of Young America’s Foundation, writes, “Ronald Reagan ended America’s short romance with self doubt induced by the 70s legacy of presidential malaise, Watergate corruption, economic stagnation and failure in Vietnam. He believed strongly in American exceptionalism, the idea that America had a special purpose in the world to preach freedom and be the ‘shining city on a hill.’ Contrary to the leftist critique of America which held that America was a racist, immoral and imperial power, he believed Americans were a moral and decent people and that American involvement in the world affairs made things better, not worse. Renewed self confidence, even more than military power, explains the final triumph over Communism.”

No longer was America resigned to live with the communist threat or a second-best seat in the world. Rather, Reagan helped us remember the values that made us great. Preaching of virtue, hard work, dedication, and freedom, Reagan helped Americans refocus on things that had been set aside during an era of strife. Thanks to our great president, it is no longer midnight over the republic; once more, it is “Morning in America.”
