Germany's Iron Lady?

by Luukas Ilves

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

It would appear that the right’s grasp upon Europe’s large countries is strong. France and Italy have conservative governments. Fifteen years ago, Labour would have denounced Tony Blair’s pro-marketeering. Poland has just brought a conservative government and President to power. Now Germany’s new chancellor is the conservative Angela Merkel, heralded as the Iron Lady of Germany, a Margaret Thatcher for our age. Her nickname, Angie, presents an intentional parallel to Thatcher’s ‘Maggie.’

Yet Germany’s situation is far from rosy. We reach the current situation after nearly six months of German political stasis. In the aftermath of a string of defeats in local elections, chancellor Gerhard Schroeder of the Social Democratic Party (SPD) in June called for a vote of no-confidence against his own government, claiming he no longer had a mandate to govern. He lost the vote (or did he win?), resulting in special elections scheduled for September. At the time, Angela Merkel and her centre-right Christian Democratic Union (CDU) were predicted to handily win, but the SPD narrowed the gap, leaving both parties without the majority necessary to form a government. Unwilling or unable to form coalitions with any of Germany’s smaller parties, the SPD and CDU have been forced to form a grand coalition with a vast majority of the seats in both houses of the German legislature. This has left Germany in a situation where the people have not so much made known whom they want to govern as whom they do not want to govern, leaving no one with a clear mandate.

Merkel has now won the chancellorship, but only after weeks of tussles between parties and at great cost: the cabinet will be split in half. The SPD’s ministerial portfolios will include foreign affairs, finance, work and social affairs, and development. Still, Merkel’s rise is quite an achievement. In fifteen years the former physicist has risen from obscurity to become Germany’s first chancellor from the East and its first woman chancellor. Her candidacy was built on a platform of simplifying the complicated tax code, overhauling the very generous pension system, reducing job protection that makes it almost impossible to fire employees and curbing the unions that so heartily support it: all steps necessary to make Germany once again competitive.

Her coalition government will probably make her most ambitious goals difficult. Though Schroeder has publicly declined a cabinet post, Merkel has to contend with several heavyweights, including Franz Müntefering, head of the SPD fraction in the Bundestag and Günter Verheugen, Germany’s member of the European Commission. If the only other ‘Grand Coalition’ in German history, between the SPD and CDU in 1966-69, is any indication, the coalition government will be capable of making some headway. Ironically, many of the programs Merkel must now reform were brainchildren of that coalition. Thankfully, the SPD does acknowledge the need for reforms: Schroeder received much flack from his own constituencies for pushing through the Hartz IV labor reforms, which worked toward streamlining German labor practices.

But Merkel and company also face a formidable set of problems, including a budget problem that rivals the US’ for sheer gravity and a stagnant 1.2% growth rate that has underperformed the EU average almost since reunification in 1989. The biggest risk for Merkel lies not in failing to pursue reforms, whose necessity both parties acknowledge, but in allowing her more ambitious projects to become tangled up in a web of partisanship. She will have to reckon with SPD ministers and delegates who virulently oppose her reforms and members from both parties whose attitude toward Merkel’s agenda is at best lukewarm. And should necessary reforms fail to take hold within the next two years, Merkel will quickly find herself in a situation where both parties, in anticipation of elections, are most concerned not with structural reform but laying claim to the title of having exacted the least blood and the fewest tears from the German people.

Though domestic reforms are difficult, there is one arena where Merkel can unquestionably alter Germany’s course: EU and foreign affairs. No, Merkel is not a Neo-Con’s dream-come-true. She will not prioritize trans-Atlantic relations over the EU, nor will she place the interests of new EU members over those of Old Europe. However, she will be less susceptible to the personality politics Schroeder has played. She will not feel at home in the old boys’ club of Chirac, Berlusconi and Blair. She will be less tolerant of flagrantly unfair practices like the CAP and will hopefully come out in favor of the Bolkenstein Directive, which creates a free movement of services in the same way that the EU currently allows the free movement of labor, but which has run into fierce opposition from old EU member states. And she will take a harder line against Putin and Russia, adopting a critical stance of human rights abuses and the weak rule of law.

Angela Merkel will not be Germany’s Margaret Thatcher: too many obstacles stand in the way. Yet, hopefully she will follow a course that will steer Germany back toward economic robustness and international rationality. That would be a worthy legacy for the girl from the East.
