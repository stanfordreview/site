Expensive Quad Project Underway

by Ryan Tracey

News Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

As the Arrillaga Family Recreation Center opens its doors and the athletic department prepares for the renovation of Stanford Stadium, the Stanford community can also look forward to the design and completion of a new Science and Engineering Quad (SEQ 2) before 2010.

The new quad, which will consist of four different buildings, houses a new home for the School of Engineering—replacing the Terman Engineering Center as the nucleus of Stanford’s new technological ventures. The Environment and Energy Initiative building (an interdisciplinary program to “promote an environmentally sound and sustainable world”), a replacement Ginzton Laboratory, and a new Bioengineering/Chemical Engineering building will fill the other three buildings in the SEQ 2 design.

The Department of Project Management estimates that SEQ 2 should cost the university between $375 million and $420 million. Donations, debt, and school reserves will fund the new project. SEQ 2 costs significantly more than both the Stanford Stadium renovation (estimated $85 million) and the Arrillaga Family Recreation Center ($27.5 million according to 2003/2004 budget plan).

The idea of constructing SEQ 2 is not new. “The SEQ 2, in an important sense, has been planned for a long time,” said project manager Maggie Burgett when asked about the conceptual origins of the new buildings. “[A] Science and Engineering quad was envisioned in the original campus plan developed by Frederick Law Olmsted west of the main quad. In the Plan for the Second Century developed in 1991, the University recommitted itself to the principles of the original design.”

According to Burgett, SEQ 2 will help fulfill the university’s vision of a “Science, Engineering, and Medical campus.” In addition to SEQ 2, this campus will consist of the new Astrophysics Building (currently under construction), a new biology building, the Learning and Knowledge Center, and the Stanford Institute of Medicine 1. Like SEQ 2, these buildings should also be completed before 2010.

In order to make space for the SEQ 2, contractors will destroy several buildings on campus. The Terman Enginnering Center, Gravity Probe B building, Hansen Experimental Physics Laboratory, and the Ginzton Laboratory will all be destroyed before SEQ 2 is completed.

The new SEQ 2 buildings should provide Stanford engineering and science students with a variety of new resources. In addition to containing the most modern classrooms on campus, state-of-the-art laboratories in the Ginzton replacement and the Environment and Energy Initiative building will allow students to participate in what Burgett describes as “cutting edge engineering in cutting edge facilities.”

Other top tier schools in the nation are also completing major construction projects to provide more modern resources for science and engineering students. Harvard’s Laboratory for Integrated Science and Engineering is set to open in fall of 2006. Meanwhile, the University of California at Berkeley hopes to open the Stanley Bio-sciences and Bioengineering Facility in mid-2006. However, few other projects rival the breadth and size of Stanford’s SEQ 2.

Construction of the first SEQ 2 building, the Environment and Energy Initiative building, should begin in June 2006 with completion scheduled for December 2007. The Environment and Energy building is in the “schematic design” phase. The Stanford’s Project Development Plan describes this time period as “a critical phase where expectations are set, budget and schedule are established, and the project is submitted to the County for Architecture and Site Approval.” Stanford has already selected Boora Architects of Portland, Oregon and Hathaway Dinwiddie Construction Company to complete the Environment and Energy Initiative building.

Designs of SEQ 2 put important emphasis on incorporating with the rest of campus. “A priority of the SEQ 2 Master Plan was integration of the new quad into the existing campus architecture and infrastructure,” said Burgett. “The Master Plan defines site limits, massing, connective elements, fenestration and color and material palettes as well as circulation for both vehicles and pedestrians, service and delivery and landscape design.”

The construction of SEQ 2 should not interfere with development plans for the other non-science or non-engineering departments on campus. “The construction of the SEQ 2 does not preclude other projects elsewhere on campus,” said Burgett. “The schools of Humanities and Sciences (chemistry and art) and the School of Business have or are developing long range plans.”
