A War We'll Never Win: But that doesn't mean we should stop fighting

by Nick Hoy

Opinions Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Upon hearing of a “credible and specific” plan of attack on the New York City subway, New York Senator Charles Schumer advised that “the threat, while specific in terms of location, does not have the highest level of credibility or corroboration.” Still, he was quick to warn that, “nonetheless, in a post 9/11 world you cannot be too careful.”

An interesting notion, this, the “post 9/11 world.” As far as platitudinous phrases go, the “post 9/11 world” has quite expertly invaded common lexicon. (We observe this, most clearly, when a US Senator recalls it for a sound bite). I am immediately inclined to label this a byproduct of our Amero-centric global world view.

Insofar as terrorism goes, 9/11 was certainly not the first major attack of terror, not even the first major attack of terror on American soil. Al-Qaeda had successfully targeted American interests in the past; elsewhere, the leftist FARC and ELN have terrorized the Colombian public for decades, Islamic organizations of various spots and stripes have wreaked havoc on Israel, and the ETA continues to disrupt peace and governance in Spain. To suggest that 9/11, however pseudo-apocalyptic, represented a new dawn in the age of terrorism, is simply to ignore the facts of terror throughout the history of the late 20th and 21st centuries.

Nonetheless (I imagine quite inadvertently), Senator Schumer managed to nail the actual distinction between our terrorism outlook pre- and post-9/11. That it is to say, it’s not terrorism itself that has changed (although it may have undergone a quite inevitable and deadly evolution), but rather our response to terrorism. Attacks in Israel or on embassies in Kenya failed to arouse any serious concern in the American public or all but the most diligent policymakers.

Now, even those politicians spoon-fed clichés by lobbyists from down the road are loathe to inform the public that everything will simply be okay, that they can pick up their transfers and saunter through the turnstiles. Thus, though the threat on New York will likely never become more than a threat, we collectively descend into paranoia at the turn of an abstract traffic light from saffron to mustard. In a post 9/11 world you cannot be too careful.

Maybe, just maybe, in some cave very far away (probably in Afghanistan) Pandora’s Box was opened and the United States is doomed to fight the War on Terror for all eternity. Though a gloomy diagnosis, we may have to face the facts and agree that terror is interminable. Ultimately the War on Terror is not a war, per se, not even a war of attrition. It is yet another silly phrase that was coined by speechwriters and spin doctors. The War on Terror involves no real soldiers, and forces us into combat with an abstraction. It is a war that we really have no hope of winning. We will lose many lives while terrorism, personified, will live on.

Still, and unlike the War on Drugs, the War on Terror is a war worth fighting. We have observed that in the post 9/11 world even a concerted effort to counter terror is often not enough. But we have also shown in the post 9/11 world that a concerted effort can be enough, and that makes all the difference. There is no reason for optimism, but at the same time there is no room for pessimism.

The right response to terror, unfortunately, is not a series of blinking lights, or a mass panic. Last time I checked, panic was a loose synonym of terror, which makes me think that the terrorists have already won. Normally this opinion is met by a delirious dissonance of rabid patriots convinced that I (in this case) am advocating pinning an enormous white flag to my lapel and parading around chanting odes to al-Qaeda. Far from it, needless to say. The terrorists of this world are bargaining on this sort of response. The War on Terror should instead be fought on ideological battlefields. The hatred of extremist Islam and the oppression of blind autocracy are our clearest enemies and represent our most actionable battles.

In the post 9/11 world, these are the things worth fighting against.
