Miers: A Political Mine

by Carl J. Kelm

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Not long after John Roberts had been nominated to the Supreme Court, I read a column by Ann Coulter, in which she started off by listing quotes by President Bush, his chief of staff, Newt Gingrich, and others, all of whom defended the judicial nominee as a staunch conservative. Yet, these quotes were not about Roberts; rather, they were about David Souter, who was appointed by the first President Bush and has since become one of the most liberal members of the Supreme Court. Now we Americans find ourselves facing another stealth candidate; one without an openly conservative background or a well-defined ideology. Yet, this woman is supposed to be the most qualified potential nominee in the entire country.

This assertion is itself a major point of contention. Miers allegedly has a great legal mind, having graduated from SMU and Virginia Law, then serving as the first female president of the Texas State Bar and as Alberto Gonzales’s successor as White House Counsel. But what qualifies her for a lifetime appointment to the nation’s highest court? A résumé like Miers’ perhaps makes her a good choice for the Justice Department or maybe even an appellate court, but not for a position interpreting the supreme law of the land. Why then was she the one chosen?

Many feel that President Bush was under great pressure to replace Sandra Day O’Connor with another woman, or, failing that, at least with a minority. This sort of affirmative action is ridiculous, especially in a case such as this. To put it bluntly, this is not quite the same thing as letting a girl play in the local tee-ball league; we’re trying to decide the state and future of American law. For such a responsibility, we must appoint the greatest minds our nation has to offer- regardless of gender, race, skin color, or creed- so long as they are devoted to protecting the Constitution.

However, let us assume for a moment that there is a compelling rationale to putting a woman or minority on the bench. Why Miers? Janice Rogers Brown was recently appointed to the D.C. Court of Appeals- renowned for producing future Supreme Court justices- and before that served with distinction on the California State Supreme Court. Growing up in the South with both racial and economic disadvantages, Brown overcame these obstacles to become a distinguished jurist, one who wrote the plurality of majority opinions on state Supreme Court. Besides her, there are other skilled appellate court judges, such as Edith Jones and Priscilla Owens, who seem like more qualified nominees. Moreover, both Brown and Owens were declared “not extreme” by the “Gang of 14” during the filibuster compromise earlier this year. Another individual who was named as a possible successor to O’Connor was Viet Dinh. A refugee from communist Vietnam in the seventies, Dinh served as Assistant Attorney General from 2000-2004 and was a major writer of the Patriot Act. He now teaches law at Georgetown.

But we should not forget that being white and male should not preclude one from sitting on the bench. Ironically, Bush was previously hailed for flouting convention and naming who he thought was the best mind, John Roberts, to the bench. However, Roberts and Miers are leagues apart. One criticism made by the Right was that Roberts had never been openly conservative, but all agreed that he was a talented jurist with impeccable credentials. Essentially, Republicans took the President at his word when they agreed to support Roberts. Miers, conversely, has no such credentials to justify a leap of faith. In fact, one of the few known things about her past is that she donated the maximum amount to the Democratic Party during the eighties, including Gore’s ’88 Presidential run, and then to Sen. Lloyd Bentsen, who became Dukakis’ running mate.

Charles Krauthammer recently wrote that, “When in 1962 Edward Moore Kennedy ran for his brother’s seat in the Senate, his opponent famously said that if Kennedy’s name had been Edward Moore, his candidacy would have been a joke. If Harriet Miers were not a crony of the President of the United States, her nomination to the Supreme Court would be a joke, as it would have occurred to no one else to nominate her.” I personally don’t believe that this is an act of sheer cronyism. Rather, I believe the President when he says that she has a great mind and that twenty years from now her opinions will be the same. My issue is with whether her mind is in fact the greatest, and what precisely those unchanging principles are.

We are still left with the same question: what would make President Bush choose a nominee who has never served on a bench or apparently ever publicly held an opinion? Rush Limbaugh recently stated “This is a pick that was made from weakness.” It appears then that Bush has made a choice that will win him few brownie points with the left and will only alienate his conservative base.

As the Senate has the role of granting advice and consent, Bush asked for the opinions of over three-quarters of senators. One note, perhaps a telling one, is that Miers was one of the people suggested by Sen. Harry Reid (D-NV), the Senate Minority Leader. Even Sen. Charles Schumer (D-NY) has been uncharacteristically quiet about the pick. Is Bush simply trying to appeal to the left? And what does he hope to gain from it? I seriously doubt that Reid and the Democrats will trade another Souter for Social Security and tax code reforms. As David Limbaugh has stated, “Of all the things I thought [George Bush] had learned in spades, it was that his father’s overtures to the Left not only were unappreciated and rebuffed- they also wounded his presidency and opened the door to eight years of Clinton…By acting apologetic about conservatism in this and other recent actions, e.g., post-Katrina, he’s sending the wrong signal at the wrong time. If the Republican Party’s standard-bearer acts tentative about conservative solutions and fighting for them, how can we expect the base to get fired up, especially as we go into 2008 without a natural successor to replace him?”

Limbaugh brings up an excellent point. Not only is this stealth pick probably not the best selection for the Supreme Court, it has potentially far-reaching political consequences as well. First of all, it sends the message that conservatives must bury their heads in the sand, rather than confess their true beliefs. Pat Buchanan writes, “Yet we are now told by the White House that Harriet Miers is an ideal candidate because she ‘has no paper trail.’ But what does that mean, other than that Miers has never declared herself with courage and conviction on any of the great issues from 1965 to 2005. This is now a qualification for the U.S. Supreme Court? To have been AWOL in the greatest social and moral conflicts of her time?... Is this the message that we want to send to the next generation: Don’t let anybody know where you stand on gay rights, affirmative action, or Roe v. Wade? Is this what conservatism has come to? By the standard of “no paper trail,” we would never have nominated Scalia or Bork, or Ronald Reagan, who, with his thousands of radio and TV commentaries, had the longest paper trail in American history.” Buchanan is right; this is a lowering of standards. Can that possibly be healthy for our democracy?

What is truly demoralizing, however, is the effect that Miers nomination will have on the conservative political base. Conservatives have waited for years to stop judges from overriding the Constitution with their own activist opinions, only to be let down by the appointments of our own side. More relevantly, American conservatives have waited since 1994, when Clinton chose Ginsburg after he had appointed Breyer, for the balance of power in the court to shift in our favor. Roberts will likely be a mainstream conservative, but that only replaces the voice of Rehnquist. With O’Connor’s seat open there is now an opportunity to effect real change and it appears that the President is squandering it. What is more degrading is the fact that, unlike when Bork and Thomas were nominated, we have the majority in the Senate.

To the rank-and-file, it can only appear to be another let down in a long line of judicial betrayals. We have seen such liberals as Earl Warren (Eisenhower), Harry Blackmun (Nixon), John Paul Stevens (Ford), Sandra Day O’Connor (Reagan), and David Souter (Bush), as well as a host of others in the last fifty-odd years, become justices and then jerk to the left. All were essentially stealth candidates as well. Demoralizing grassroots conservatives is hardly the best strategy for maintaining our current majorities in Congress, as well as keeping the Presidency in Republican hands.

In short, this could be a reversal of all that has been struggled for; especially for those who oppose Roe v. Wade. But why should we even be forced to worry?
