Strike Provides Opportunity for Reform

by Ryan Tracey

News Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Nearly 1,500 Stanford workers and 1,300 Stanford hospital employees plan to strike this coming Monday, December 12, after negotiations with the University over health care, job security, pensions, and other benefits failed.

As a result of the strike, four of the eight student dining halls will be closed, many custodial tasks interrupted, and maintenance prioritized as students take their Fall Quarter final examinations. The Stanford workers, organized through Service Employees International Union (SEIU) 715, are, among other things, asking for full family health care coverage. Currently, Stanford pays the full cost of health care for the worker and 82 percent for their families

Although the strike will aversely affect students during finals week, it also provides an important opportunity for the university to reconsider its dining infrastructure. Many consider the present system to be monopolistic. Stanford requires students living in many undergraduate residences to subscribe to one of Stanford Dining’s meal plans. The meal plan cost $4656 per year regardless of the option chosen.

Large-scale strikes, like the one planned for Monday, only augment the need for reform of Stanford Dining. This Monday, students will have no choice but to submit their appetites to a marginally functional organization, greatly deprived of workers. Lines will be longer, food will likely taste poorer, and students will be hungrier because Stanford leaves them no choice but to eat at one of their affected locations.

Although there are many private eating establishments in the Stanford area, student dining plans are pre-paid. Thus, choosing an alternative place to eat effectively wastes the $8 or so that students pay for each meal at the beginning of the quarter. Also, leaving the Stanford campus requires a great deal of time and effort for freshmen and car-deprived upperclassmen.

One solution to the Stanford Dining “monopoly crisis” is to allow private contractors and businesses to compete for students’ meals. Perhaps, then, the university should allow its workers to continue to strike—without compromising—and instead lease its dining halls to adventurous third-party contractors that can provide good service, food, and eating hours to students. In the process, Stanford rids itself of a great deal of liability, overhead, and responsibility.

Dining for a select group of undergraduate students on campus is already privatized. Many row houses, fraternities, and sororities hire their own chefs. Not surprisingly, the quality of food at these locations is often held in high regard by students. The university does not have to worry about the benefits or quality of the chef, as the students can simply choose to hire a different chef if the food is unsatisfactory. Similarly, if private businesses provided the food for all Stanford students—worker issues could be easily resolved. There would not only be a free market for student meals, but also for service workers. If one dining establishment did not provide adequate benefits, workers could simply migrate to one that did. A more competitive dining market would therefore benefit both the worker and the consumer. Unions like SEIU 715 would become obsolete, shifting control of the labor market to small businesses and individual employees.

An added benefit is that prices would inevitably drop. A substantial meal plan can be purchased for $4340 at Yale, $4153 at Princeton, or $3890 at Columbia. If Stanford’s superfluous prices are, in fact, a result of unnecessary operational costs, increased competition would provide incentives to cut-out “fat” where possible.

Liberalized regulation of meal plans for university students is not unrealistic. At George Washington University, students can spend their “Colonial Cash” at many restaurants in Washington D.C., as well as a market, several Starbucks, and several other delectable locations. Hence, the university components of the meal plan have to compete with outside establishments. The result is better food and lower prices for the student. There are many similar examples of “student-reign” food plans at other universities.

The best solution, however, may be to cut-out the university regulation of student meals entirely. Stanford should forget about resolving the strike and take it as an opportunity for reform. They should simply play the role of the liaison between students and established dining options. Entrepreneurial catering companies, restaurants, and contractors should take over Stanford’s dining halls and give students and their parents’ pocketbooks some long-anticipated relief.
