Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire Sizzles on IMAX

by Navin Kadaba

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Many Harry Potter fans would cringe to know that my obsession with Harry Potter began not with the books, but with the first film. When the books came out, I took what I thought to be the high road and stayed clear, justifying my choice with the reasoning that the books were meant for young children. When the first film was released, I was dragged to it by some high school friends, and by the time the credits were rolling, I was hooked. Immediately I went home, purchased the first novel, and began to indulge in the magical world that has ensnared so many people, both young and old, throughout the world.

By the time the second film was released, I had finished all of the books to date (more than once) and was eager to watch its rendition on the big screen. Needless to say, having read the second thoroughly, it was somewhat difficult to get over the plot holes and poor CG.

When the third film came out last year, it was clear that as the books got longer, more details had to be left out. However, the third film was the first to show great cinematography and insight into the growing actors, and it was a welcome sight to see the three young wizards beginning to act a little older and surer of their abilities. While I expected the abilities of the actors and the CG to improve vastly for the fourth film, I was wary of the fact that the fourth book was nearly twice as long as any of the previous books, and I was worried that the screenplay adapted from the fourth novel would be unable to convey the magic of J.K. Rowling’s text. Luckily, my worries were wiped clean away during the 2.5 magical hours I spent in front of the IMAX screen at the Sony Metreon.

I returned from the theatre in San Francisco having been very pleased with Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire (GOF). The screenplay worked very well, despite the numerous holes in the plot (including several characters left out and side plots deleted). The acting was very good; it has clear that age has helped to solidify the skills of the three main young actors. The computer graphics were especially stunning, having corrected many of the flaws displayed in the previous films. And last, but not least, the skills of the new director Mike Newell were artfully displayed, mostly living up to the standards set in place when Alfonso Cuarón (Y tu mama también) was at the helm. The movie did have its faults, primarily with direction; Newell implemented a rushed feel to several parts of the film, and some of the more mystical and emotional elements of Harry Potter’s world were left out while the film progressed at a breakneck pace. The musical score was also somewhat disappointing, which I expected from start ever since I’d found out that Patrick Doyle was to fill the shoes of the great John Williams.

The main reason many Harry Potter fans hold the films in such contempt is the noticeable simplification of the plot itself. While the first two films did their best to stay true to the book and resorted primarily to deleting what they deemed to be superfluous, the third film cut much more important material out and added new material that was, for lack of a better word, useless. This was illustrated in the somewhat non-existent explanation for the connection of Remus Lupin’s werewolf identity to those of the Animagi that were Peter Pettigrew, Sirius Black, and Harry’s father James Potter, a concept crucial to the third volume and to the entire series. While the movie still depicted the general storyline well and was arguably far superior to the previous two, the egregious plot deletion was irritating. GOF, however, did a surprisingly good job conveying the important elements of the plot and deleting supporting characters and superfluous scenes. Considering that GOF is about twice as long as the previous novels, I am impressed. Many fans complained about the absence of Dobby the house-elf, or Ludo Bagman, but realistically their role and the subplots associated with them contributed little to the main storyline. The same goes for the rather rushed opening segment (where over 200 pages of text were converted into about 20 minutes of film time). By looking at the film’s plot with a realistic approach, I strongly believe that the writers and producers did a great job.

The biggest improvement demonstrated by the film lies firmly in the acting talent of the three youngsters who play Harry Potter (Daniel Radcliffe) and his two best friends, Ron Weasley (Rupert Grint) and Hermione Granger (Emma Watson). The actors are maturing and becoming more confident in their roles, and as a result are much more convincing than in the beginning. While it is a common belief that they are outgrowing their characters, the three only average about two years older in real life than their respective characters. I believe that the older age gives them a greater ability to understand the emotional stress their characters are under, thereby making their acting much more believable. The supporting cast also does an excellent job, with the addition of Brendan Gleeson (Braveheart, Troy) as the new Defense Against the Dark Arts teacher Alastor “Mad-Eye” Moody. Ralph Fiennes (The English Patient, The Constant Gardener) also does an excellent and chilling job as the revived dark wizard Lord Voldemort.

No fantasy movie would be complete without great CGI and a solid score, and even though GOF lacks in the latter department, it is no slouch at demonstrating the marvelous advancements in movie technology. The music in the first three movies was was excellent, with the riveting and enchanting nature one would expect from a pro like John Williams. It is clear that Patrick Doyle tried to fill his shoes, and while the music is decent, it doesn’t quite get there. However, the first and second movie demonstrated less than impressive CGI technology (e.g. the choppy looking Neville Longbottom falling from a tower to the ground), while GOF dazzles the viewer with Dragons, Merpeople, big flashy spellwork, and a particularly spectacular view of the Quidditch World Cup. Even though all of it was obviously not real, the graphics technology makes the viewer forget that fact entirely and instead does an amazing job of demonstrating the magic of Harry Potter’s world.

Mike Newell’s directing is solid, with a few minor issues. The first two movies, directed by Chris Columbus, were very rigid and focused on plot, at the expense of some of the more exciting and fantastic elements one would expect from a story such as Harry Potter. Alfonso Cuarón took the series in a new direction, with darker tones and far more emotional drama than Columbus ever had, while excluding some of the basic plotline. Mike Newell does things very similarly to Cuarón, however with more of a British touch. He focuses on the emotions of the three young actors quite a bit, doing a great job illustrating the fight between Harry and Ron, and the evident sexual tension between Ron and Hermione. His use of cinematography is excellent and the scenes he focuses on are breathtaking. Besides the major events however, some of the other emotional elements felt somewhat detached. Harry’s infatuation with Cho Chang is barely evident, while Viktor Krum and Hermione Granger’s budding connection is given little attention. The death of Cedric Diggory was handled decently; when his father finds out his son is dead, he runs over and sobs “My son! My boy!” in a manner that felt real in the novel but somewhat clichéd in the film. He spends a lot of time on several key events, and then races through other scenes a little fast for my tastes. Newell also needs to work on avoiding cultural stereotypes. The Beuaxbatons girls look like France threw up all over them, while the Durmstrang boys march coldly into the great hall like it was a revival of The Red Army. I also cannot forgive the Yule Ball, with Cho Chang clad in overly Chinese-styled dress robes while Parvati and Padma Patil are clothed in sari-like outfits, complete with garish dangly earrings typical of the stereotypical Indian Princess.

Cultural and other issues aside, Newell nonetheless delivers a strong and convincing film. Somewhat darker than the previous movies, he does very well with advancing the series from a very young, immature feel to a much more complex adventure worthy of the older demographic. J.K. Rowling received quite a bit of flak when her novels turned darker, starting with the fourth book, but she retorted that as the characters grow, the complexity must grow as well. As a member of the somewhat older demographic, I heartily agree with her, and apparently so does Newell. My biggest compliment to him is that this film is clearly the best of the series to date. With superb acting, stellar visuals, solid directing, and most of all a rich plot, Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire is a great film for everyone, from die-hard Harry Potter fans like me to those who just appreciate an excellent movie.
