The Seoul Train Comes to Stanford

by Diane Raub

Foreign Affairs Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On November 16th the Stanford student group NK Focus hosted a screening of the documentary Seoul Train, followed by a question-and-answer session with producer Jim Butterworth. Seoul Train is a unique compilation of interviews with North Korean refugees and Underground Railroad activists, together with covert footage of public executions and other atrocities within North Korea. Today 2-3 million North Koreans have died of hunger since 1995, and roughly 200,000 live in concentration camp-like prisons. It is estimated that 250,000 North Koreans have attempted to flee the country to neighboring China. This number is uncertain, however, because refugees are usually ferried through the country by underground organizations due to China’s policy of forced repatriation.

Seoul Train makes a mockery of the international attempts to engage North Korea. For the past two years, the U.S. has participated in 6-party talks over the North Korean nuclear program that have led nowhere. North Korea only agreed to come to the bargaining table after the other five participants promised to remain silent on the issue of human rights. Seoul Train reveals the moral corruption of countries, such as China and Russia, which are willing to act as if North Korea deserves a place among the world’s civilized nations.

Together with co-producer Lisa Sleeth, Butterworth left his job in 2003 and spent two months in China and South Korea collecting footage and testimony by people who personally experienced the harsh reality of Kim Jong Il’s regime. After returning to the States, he compiled the best clips onto a DVD and Seoul Train was born. Since November 2004, the documentary has aired at film festivals worldwide, provoking a wide range of reactions. Butterworth’s patience and complete dedication to his project speak volumes.

Before beginning work, neither of the producers had any kind of film experience. After buying cameras and How-To books from Amazon.com, Butterworth and Sleeth took a plane to Seoul in October 2003. By taking out second mortgages and working night shifts, the two personally financed the film, from travel expenses to designing, producing, and distributing the DVD. Here are the highlights of Butterworth’s interview with the Review.

Review : What percentage of refugees who escape from North Korea make it out of China successfully?

Butterworth : I don’t think anyone could (accurately) give you that answer, but what I could tell you is that many of the refugees who try to get out end up in the hands of illegal or illicit human traffickers that take advantage of them in any number of ways, whether it’s financially or sexually, or what not. And a lot of times they’ll take their money and just drop them off somewhere, and they’ll end up getting caught and sent back. So it’s very, very difficult for the refugees, who have no experience in this kind of thing, to do their intelligence on who it is that they’re dealing with. That’s why a lot of times it is the church-run underground activists who the refugees end up with, because they have a lot more credibility.

R : Since you first screened the DVD, you’ve been around the country and around the world. What kind of reactions have you encountered?

B : One of the things that we didn’t know when we made this film was what a controversial subject this would be. We didn’t understand what we were stepping into, that this is an incredibly divisive and polarizing issue when you start talking about North Korea. So you go to places like Prague, where they knew nothing about this, and they have an intense interest in human rights because of their own personal experiences. Especially Eastern Europe—Kiev, Belgrade, the former Yugoslavia—in these kinds of places, the people start crying because they can identify…I had a Sudanese, one of the Lost Boys of Sudan saw my film last week, in Salt Lake City. And he was in tears because of his own experience escaping Sudan. And he could totally identify with what these people were going through. And, so these are the kind of experiences that have just blown me away. But on the other hand, there are a lot of people that react very viscerally in the opposite direction. That say, this is a right-wing attack on North Korea. We’ve been accused of being CIA-funded by so many people, it’s been crazy. And I love that controversy. It usually happens in New York City.

R : So these are Americans that are accusing you of this?

B : No, Korean Americans, or North Korean plants.

R : How do you know—just by what they say?

B : Yeah. They’re there to cause trouble. And usually there’ll be a person there, and then someone there will stand up, and then someone over here will stand up, and then all three of them will start firing at me…which is fun. I like that.

R : Do you feel that the issue of North Korean human rights has gained international and national attention over the last five years?

B : Yeah, absolutely. It’s gone from zero, to right now, it’s probably a three. It obviously needs to be at about a ten, but it’s really gone from nowhere to become something that people are starting to pay attention to.

R : It seems like Kim Jong Il’s regime is so repressive that North Koreans don’t have any kind of organized opposition, and so they really don’t have any hope of overthrowing him on their own. Given that other countries haven’t shown much interest in confronting North Korea over its human rights abuses, what action would you most like to see from the U.S.?

B : Well, we have to continue to give North Korea humanitarian aid, but only…if the distribution of that aid can be monitored all the way to its endpoint, to make sure that it gets to the people that need it, that’s number one. Number two, we have to encourage Kim Jong Il, whether through carrot or stick, to open up the country economically. Because it’s only through economic reform are you ever going to get any kind of human rights reform or political reform inside North Korea. Right now, on the DVD, there’s a great discussion actually where Gordon Flake says that totalitarian regimes tend to fall when the expectations of the people rise well above what the government can deliver, and then there’s that arbitrage and the system collapses. But right now, even though the North Korean government delivers this, (the citizens’) expectations are that (motions lower with hands). How do you change that? You get information in, and you do that through businesspeople going there and bringing information about the outside world, some of the people may be traveling to China or South Korea and seeing how other people live; and this is all going to create dissention, and then maybe you can get the infrastructure, the underpinnings, of dissention: a free media, or at least an underground media, internet, things like that allowing people to communicate, organize, and at least be healthy enough to where they can rise up.

R : What’s been your most rewarding or interesting experience so far in working to increase recognition of this issue?

B : It’s the hundreds and hundreds of people that have come up to me and said “I knew nothing about this; I want to get involved, I want to help” or have joined an organization because “your film taught me about this”…That’s just a very, very humbling experience, but ultimately the most rewarding.
