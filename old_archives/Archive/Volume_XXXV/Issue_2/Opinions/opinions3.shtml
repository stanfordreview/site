Still at War

by Jack Greer

Deputy Editor

 

The great historian Robert Conquest once wrote that a plural society “means allowing troublesome ideas to be presented.” Occasionally, he continued, these ideas are “a mass of demonstrable falsehood.” He was, of course, writing more about broader ideologies (Communism and Socialism) than about specific examples (the claim that Saddam Hussein was a CIA stooge), but this quotation remains relevent nonetheless—especially at Stanford, where Communism may not be so popular (knock on wood), but “troublesome ideas” about the Global War on Terrorism (GWOT) may be found in abundance.

Four years after 9/11, many Stanford students still don’t understand the GWOT. They can’t identify the enemy or the enemy’s aims. Similarly, they don’t understand what America is doing to confront this enemy. Instead, campus lefties bemoan the quagmire of Iraq, radicals like Dr. Phil Zimbardo ironically sound the alarm about a “culture of fear” being imposed on us, and well-meaning human rights activists (of whom we have no shortage) demonstrate their disconnect from reality by railing against “torture” in American-run detention centers while ignoring actual atrocities committed by the brethren-in-terror of those detained.

The enemy was clearly identified and the enemy’s aims clearly explained four years ago by a man viewed by liberals as stupid and conniving—an odd combination. A PWR instructor once gently chided me for citing President Bush in a paper. “You’re making a statement when you do that,” she said. For better or for worse, our commander-in-chief explained this war in plain, simple, and accurate words on September 20, 2001, and his words need to be repeated.

Identifying the enemy is crucial. Opponents of the liberation of Iraq asserted that particular campaign was a diversion from the real GWOT, that al Qaeda was the true target. That may have been their idea of the GWOT, but President Bush’s was different from the beginning: “Our war on terror begins with al Qaeda, but it does not end there. It will not end until every terrorist group of global reach has been found, stopped and defeated.” There was never any doubt about Saddam’s links to other terrorist groups—and even links to al Qaeda were confirmed by the 9/11 Commission, contrary to misguided opinion.

Equally important is an understanding of the enemy’s motivations. Critics of America blame us for our support of Israel, accuse us of exploiting the Third World, assail us for our stinginess with regards to foreign aid, and blast American imperialism. President Bush gave us an accurate explanation: “They want to overthrow existing governments in many Muslim countries, such as Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and Jordan. They want to drive Israel out of the Middle East. They want to drive Christians and Jews out of vast regions of Asia and Africa.” A pan-Islamic caliphate, a Muslim empire from Andalusia to the Philippines, is their goal.

America’s response to this threat has been consistent. Rabid Bush-haters claim we have no strategy in the GWOT and that it was only after the absence of WMD was discovered that the focus shifted to bringing freedom to the Middle East. In the same speech just over a week after 9/11, the president stated “the only way to defeat terrorism as a threat to our way of life is to stop it, eliminate it, and destroy it where it grows.” President Bush has used international alliances and the US military from the beginning to stop and eliminate radical Muslim terrorists, but the way to “destroy it where it grows” has been consistently to bring freedom to oppressed peoples, to give them a choice and a chance.

And so the war continues, pursuant to the grand strategy. The commander-in-chief addressed the Naval Academy last May, declaring: “Across Central Asia and the broader Middle East, we are seeing the rise of a new generation whose hearts burn for liberty, and they are going to have it.” At the same time, American troops are deployed in West and East Africa and elsewhere, training counterterrorist units and battling insurgents. Working with international alliances like NATO and through agreements like the Proliferation Security Initiative, the soft walk of freedom is on the march and the hard stick of armed force are working hand-in-hand to defeat the enemy.

Our job, as students, is to better understand the struggle, support those who wage it, and contribute as best we can to the cause. We might give money and time to groups like the American Red Cross as the United States aids Muslims in tsunami-torn regions of the Pacific. Or we might confront those who Blame America First here on campus with facts and figures to shatter their unfounded lies and mass of demonstrable falsehood about our great country. There are ways for us to contribute.

Lastly, we must honor our heritage and fallen heroes by living our lives in freedom and enjoying the liberty with which God has blessed us.
