Editor's Note

 

Hello and welcome to issue 2 of The Stanford Review’s 35th volume. I must say that the past week or so has been particularly exciting for me. It has been exciting because I spent the past week distributing and promoting The Review. That is, I got to see people’s reactions, face to face, when the paper physically transferred from my hand to his or her hand. And, for the most part, the response was positive. It was incredibly rewarding to see individuals across campus, liberal and conservative, taking an interest in our paper… even that eccentric guy who keeps shouting “Woohoo! Stanford Review” at me in the dining hall.

Yet, there are those that do not like discussion. There are those that would rather close their ears and shout “LALA… can’t hear you.” One such group of individuals is the Soto staff, which is apparently about as much fun as their last “party.” Unlike ResEd, we believe in the first amendment, and so henceforth, I am making a pledge to do everything in my power to make sure that everyone who would like to read our paper has access to our paper. Yet, I am not “The Review.” Our publication would not be possible if it were not for the principled insight, dedication and hard work of The Review staff. Thus, I would like to offer a special thanks to the individuals that stayed up late this week to help get our volume off the ground, regardless of the ridiculous regulations our mini-bureaucracy at Stanford has come up with in regard to distribution.

My goal is to make our ideas tangible to the “Stanford community” and my hope is that, in distributing hand to hand, the debate will no longer be viewed as “us” versus “them” but a friendly exchange of ideas. It has come to my attention that many students at Stanford are unaware The Review even exists. Some students have been unable to find our publication on campus, as it seems to have mysteriously vanished from its distribution points overnight. We seek to change that. The Review is an organization that I am proud to be a part of and it is my sincere hope that this paper might present an intellectual and thought provoking discussion. Whatever your preconceived notion of The Review, I would like to challenge you to read through the entire volume. I promise you will learn something, regardless of your political leanings.

As I mentioned in my previous editor’s note, the beginning of a new year is often a time for both reflection on the past and an opportunity to look to the future. This year is no exception. Just before school started, Emeritus Editor Ben Guthrie and I had the opportunity to attend an event sponsored by Lead 21 honoring George Shultz. Though Secretary Shultz spoke to many pressing issues of our time, perhaps the most powerful point in the Secretary’s speech was when he reflected on Ronald Reagan’s bold and ambitious goals as President. At a time when much of Washington saw Reagan as a maverick, he held fast to his core principles and history has looked kindly upon him for it. Even members of the Republican Party criticized Reagan, including George Bush, who famously labeled Reagan’s economic policy “voodoo economics.” As it turns out, Reagan was right. As we look forward, it might be prudent to look to the past in order to grasp what actually occurred following Reagan’s tax cuts or to consider the ways in which Reagan’s ambitious foreign policy was sharply criticized in much the same way that Bush’s policies are viewed today (see pages 4 and 5). It might be prudent to consider the experiences that have shaped our President’s character or the intentions of the framers of our great democracy (see pages 6 and 7). Or, it might just be prudent to consider whether the sort of debate that we have here, at Stanford, is in an intellectual spirit that might allow us to make prudent choices looking forward.
