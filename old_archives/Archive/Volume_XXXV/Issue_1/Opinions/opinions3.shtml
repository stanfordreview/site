Bush’s New Vision For Space Travel

by Ryan Tracey

News Editor

 

With the Space Shuttle’s recent return to flight, President Bush’s “New Vision for Space Exploration Program” is well under way. The program, announced in January 2004, outlined a series of missions that would eventually lead to human landings on both the Moon and Mars. The Space Shuttle will be retired in 2010, to be replaced by the Crew Exploration Vehicle (CEV). Two contractor teams—one lead by Lockheed Martin, and the other by Northrop Grumman and Boeing—have already submitted plans to NASA for the CEV and will test their first prototypes in 2008. Amidst all these developments, many scientists and economists are critical of the Bush space plan. However, the coherent direction, feasibility, and spirit of the program should not be discounted. President Bush’s new vision will undoubtedly inspire a new generation of scientists and challenge the limits of modern technology—reaffirming America’s great intellectual and entrepreneurial strength.

One of the most cited criticisms of Bush’s space plan involves the relative cost of sending humans instead of robots into space. Admittedly, the recent Mars rovers, both of which have achieved remarkable success, cost under $900 million total. Compare this to the $500 million it costs for each Space Shuttle flight or the anticipated $65 billion to be spent through 2020 in order to send man back to the moon under the Bush program. Historically, robotic missions cost significantly less than human space flights.

Nevertheless, the relative cost of sending humans into the heavens is justifiable. Most certainly, a significant number of engineers, physicists, and other professionals of the past generation owe their interest in science to human space missions like the Apollo program. Robots and their programmers cannot inspire a nation’s youth to pursue a scientific career nearly as effectively as the revered astronauts of NASA’s recent past. Since the inception of time, humans have been charged with a compulsion to explore the universe. Without space exploration, humans retain few avenues to further satiate this impulse. Surely, images and numerical data from a remote robotic sensor will not suffice. If for nothing else, society should support manned space initiatives for the sake of continuing a long tradition of expanding the human realm.

Assuredly, there are many other societal benefits to an ambitious human space exploration program. Sending a man back to the Moon or Mars will require an enormous amount of research and technological development. Although the same is essential for robotic space programs, the radical redirection of the goals and requirements of a space vehicle to accommodate humans will necessitate a more considerable amount of work and progress. Work and progress in space programs, as history has shown, can produce a vast amount of benefits for society. CAT scanners, MRI machines, pacemakers, Velcro, and modern communication are a few of the things that owe their origin to space exploration research. Countless lives have been saved—and made better—through the enhancement of space programs. Bush’s plan places a particular focus on researching the effects of space travel on the human body. Even though such research will be most directly applicable to designing a successful mission to Mars, information on the human body’s reaction to space travel has and could contributed more to research on aging, osteoporosis, and other pertinent health problems. Unquestionably, Bush’s space program would expand upon these results and allow humans to better understand—and enjoy—their own world.

Sadly, the United States does not give the space program nearly as much financial or moral support today as it did during the Cold War. During the Apollo Program, in 1966, NASA comprised 4.4% of the federal budget. Today, NASA funding, even after Bush’s budget increase, remains below 1% of the federal budget. The $60 billion supposedly needed for Americans to land on the moon would cost each taxpayer around $300 over the course of the next fifteen years. This is a fairly small amount when you compare it to the $557 billion the Social Security Administration will dole out for monthly benefits in 2005.

Of course, society will have to be patient to see if President Bush’s Moon landings and visits to Mars become a reality. His father also proposed a plan to get humans to Mars. However, the cost of his plan—over $450 billion in 1989 dollars—quickly turned people off and the initiative soon failed. In contrast, “The New Vision” has already achieved some success. NASA was allocated the money Bush requested for initiating the new program from Congress, although it took some negotiating. Many scientists and researchers speculate that the program will take man to the Moon, but lose momentum thereafter due to exorbitant costs. As a result, many of the great benefits and inspiration from the original plan will be lost. The American populace, government, and NASA need to develop a deep commitment to visiting Mars if the program is to succeed. Regardless, Bush’s initiative is a vital first step.
