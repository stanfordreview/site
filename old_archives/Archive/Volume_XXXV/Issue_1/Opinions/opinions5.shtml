Editor's Note

Hello and welcome to Volume XXXV of The Stanford Review! As this is the first issue of the 2005-2006 school year, I find it particularly appropriate to extend a warm welcome to the freshman class of 2009. Indeed, as I embark on this new experience as editor, I am both in the process of saying goodbye to old friends and meeting new friends. Over the past several years, I have come to realize that this pattern – the ebbs and flows of friendships with seasons – is not something to be looked upon with disdain, but instead is simply a function of life. Yet, that is not to say that my closest friends are merely a product of chance.

Last year, at the fall activities fair, Editor Emeritus Ben Guthrie approached me to write for The Review. Admittedly, I was somewhat hesitant about joining a group of outspoken conservatives. To be quite honest, my freshman year had been somewhat bittersweet. Though I enjoyed many of the amazing freshman experiences that Stanford had to offer, I also felt out of place at times, particularly in regard to my views on public policy. Any attempts to articulate arguments clearly and concisely to the masses inevitably degenerated into discussions of blowjobs and body bags. The last thing I wanted to do was to stir the pot further, especially in the run up to a heated election.

Just as I was about to leave The Review table, however, a representative from KZSU asked Ben if he wanted to go on the air to discuss the recent presidential debate. For one reason or another, nobody at The Review table wanted to take up the offer. Thus, it came with some surprise that I found myself on KZSU debating the president of the Stanford Democrats, Kai Stinchcombe a few minutes later. Yet, it was a plunge that I am glad I took. In the end, I shook hands with Kai. My brief stint of FM glory led me to realize that there is indeed a place for policy discourse on the otherwise relatively monolithic campus of Stanford University. And so began my career with The Review.

After being a staff writer and then opinions editor, I am now editor-in-chief. Though I cannot speak for the entire staff, I will say that I have found a community in The Review. Many of The Review’s previous editors, who inspired me with their openness during my freshman year, have subsequently become my friends. Thus, it was with a hint of sadness that I said farewell to Joe Fairbanks, Piotr Kosicki and Gary Raichart at our final board dinner last spring. Yet, over the past year, I have also made friends with many of the individuals that comprise this year’s staff. We are the new generation of The Review that will continue to proudly carry forth its mission.

The Review’s role on campus is no less relevant than it was in its inception in 1987. The paper was founded in order to counter a movement to eliminate the Western Culture humanities core in favor of a more “diverse” curriculum. Since then, it has evolved with different editors, shifted foci and impacted various aspects of campus life. Yet, the guiding principle remains the same; our motto is “fiat lux” – let there be light. We seek to present an alternative perspective, to shine light on aspects of our Stanford community that would otherwise remain dimly lit in the shadow of intellectual apathy. We recognize that words such as “diversity,” “freedom,” “social progress” and “culture” represent guiding principles rather than specific mandates of policy. There is room for discussion as to whether affirmative action really promotes the type of diversity that will ultimately eliminate racism or whether a selectively applied door to door distribution policy is appropriate in an environment where the “winds of freedom blow.” All too often, members of the Stanford community justify policy under the veil of vague overarching principles such as “equality” and “justice.” Yet, I submit that such individuals are not acting within the framework of intellectual discourse upon which our very school motto is predicated.

If there is one thing that my studies have taught me, it is that often times the intuitive solution is not the best solution. Our paper exists to expose the spurious reasoning of the left in a logical and intellectual manner in order to affect a more positive policy outcome that has withstood the rigors of pointed discourse. We welcome all perspectives that fall under the broad scope of “conservative thought.” You will find that our editors range from libertarian to conservative and everywhere in between. If you would like to become part of a community that welcomes intellectual challenge over apathy, contrarian thought over conformity and most importantly, believes in individual liberty over collectivism, I encourage you to join us at our first meeting and to consider writing for The Stanford Review.
