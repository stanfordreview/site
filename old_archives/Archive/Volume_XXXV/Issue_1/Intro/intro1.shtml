Why I Write for The Review

by the Staff of The Review

 

I write for the Stanford Review because my political philosophy is conservative, and I believe one of the great experiences of university life should be joining in the free exchange of ideas and opinions. The Review expresses conservative viewpoints on a very wide variety of issues. Any casual and objective reader of the major media, as well as the Stanford Daily, will be hard pressed to find our viewpoints represented. That is a shame: good newspapers by definition should be balanced in the presentation of both news and opinions. In the absence of that ideal, however, the Stanford Review fills what would otherwise be a vacuum of presentation and explanation of the conservative perspective. Writers for the Review find they have many choices about what to write, and how often. I hope the Class of 2009 takes the same opportunity I have enjoyed in becoming a contributor to the Stanford Review.

Diane Raub

 

There are really so many reasons why I write for The Review: publicity, money, great career connections, ideological solidification, etc. However, I am determined to stick with The Review because it continues to excite and challenge me outside of the classroom. This has become especially apparent through my experiences with investigative news. It’s amazingly exhilarating to put together an investigation piece that the majority will initially reject and attempt to debunk—as is Stanford student body’s reaction to most articles in The Review. If the article is controversial enough, a well-researched piece can lead to intense and passionate dialogue. Dialogue—mind you—that forces people in the community to reflect on their own beliefs. Regardless of whether they resultantly change their beliefs, I take great pride in encouraging Stanford students to reexamine their political ideologies. It is this pride that brings me back to my computer to write article after article.

Ryan Tracey

 

From the first time I ever engaged in active political discussions, I realized that I could never categorize myself as a liberal or a conservative. I hold to be true values and opinions that span the range of the political, social, and economic spectra. However, in spite of the restrictive categories people seem to be obliged to place themselves under, I believe strongly in maintaining open dialogue between those with varying viewpoints, an activity I felt to be lacking not only at Stanford, but around the world. I write for the Review to satisfy that thirst for discussion on a personal level, and with the hope that someday all people can sit down and exchange views with people in the public sphere in order to make the world more aware, and invariably better.

Navin Kadaba

 

I write for The Review because it is a chance to present viewpoints that many Stanford students may not be exposed to in a constructive manner. I have learned that there is a place for reasoned debate on our campus, and that extremist language and insults do not get us anywhere. My goal as editor is to publish material that furthers the ends of positive and rigorous debate.

Alex Medearis
