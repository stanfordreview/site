Samuel Alito, Consitutionalist

by James Hohmann

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

President Bush came through on his campaign promise to appoint well-qualified judges who will not legislate from the bench when he chose Samuel Alito to be Associate Justice on the Supreme Court. Judge Alito embodies the kind of judge America needs to have more of on its courts: sound experience in public service, fairness to all parties in a case, and a consistent conservative record of respect for precedent and the law of the land.

Promisingly, the Wall Street Journal Editorial Board wrote that, based on their analysis of his 15 years of opinions as a judge, “Alito shows every sign of being a careful constitutionalist with a deep respect for precedent.”

John C. Eastman, a law professor at Chapman University in Orange, told the Los Angeles Times that Alito’s rulings on the U.S. 3rd Circuit Court of Appeals “demonstrate a profound understanding of the limited role of the judiciary” and a respect for precedent.

It goes without saying that picking a justice for the Supreme Court is of great importance. It’s a lifetime appointment and one of the best ways a president can shape his legacy long after he leaves office. President Bush has now chosen two immensely qualified judges who as justices will help keep this country on the right track for a generation.

Perhaps the only negative to the Alito nomination is that the Supreme Court will have one less Stanford-educated jurist and one more Yalie. With Rehnquist and O’Connor gone, only Kennedy and Breyer will have attended the University (both as undergraduates). To be fair, Scalia was a visiting professor and Ginsburg was a fellow at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences before they joined the court.

In all seriousness, Judge Alito is extremely well qualified for a job on the Supreme Court. His credentials are impeccable and unassailable. He graduated at the top of his class from Princeton as an undergraduate and later Yale Law School. He clerked for the US Court of Appeals, served as assistant to the US Solicitor General during the first Reagan administration, fought the mafia and organized crime as US Attorney for New Jersey in the late 1980s, and has built a reputation for honesty and integrity as a judge on the Third US Circuit Court of Appeals since he was approved unanimously by the Senate in 1990.

The Washington Post called him “an exceptionally strong nominee.” The Rocky Mountain [CO] News said that “Alito’s resume is even more impressive than John Roberts.” The New York Post pointed out that Alito has amassed “more judicial experience than any [Supreme Court] nominee in more than 70 years. ... He has compiled an unambiguous record of respect both for the law as written and for the judiciary’s proper role.”

The Los Angeles Times on November 1 quoted several attorneys who have appeared before Alito and lost their cases but still praised his intellect and integrity. One of them, Rutgers Constitutional Law Professor Ronald K. Chen, wrote, “I disagreed with his decision. Probably, Sam and I disagree on a number of constitutional issues. That does not mean I don’t have the greatest respect for his intellect, integrity and ability. I am very pleased by the nomination.”

The Christian Science Monitor elaborates on one of Judge Alito’s most endearing traits: hard work in his commitment to discover the truth. “From his record as a Yale law student, a Federal Court Clerk, a Federal Prosecutor, and an Assistant US Solicitor General - who argued 13 cases before the High Court (winning 12), Alito is known for diving into the details of law like a dogged researcher,” the Monitor reported.

Unlike Harriet Miers, who had almost no paper trail, Judge Alito has more than 350 documented opinions he has written since joining the court. Certainly, he is anything but a stealth nominee. His respect for the law as a judge has won praise from lawyers of all political stripes. When we look closely at the long paper trail, we find a nominee who has been pro-first amendment, pro-student, pro-market, pro-second amendment, pro-states’ rights (limiting the abuse of the commerce clause), pro-individual rights, and pro-family.

Alito has demonstrated a strong free-market philosophy in his time on the court. He has strictly interpreted contracts, ruled to rein in junk lawsuits, limited out-of-control and unwarranted ‘discrimination’ cases, and defined fair burdens in shareholder plaintiff suits. The strict definition of the law has helped companies do business, and his decisions on the Supreme Court will help America’s private sector stay competitive in the changing global economy.

On religion, Alito has ruled for government neutrality with respect to religion. In this way, the judge has preserved the right to religious free expression. In 1999, he ruled in Fraternal Order of Police v. City of Newark that Muslim police officers could keep their beards without losing their jobs. Alito argued that if some exceptions were made, than Muslims must have an equal right to practice their religious beliefs. In ACLU v. Schundler, Alito allowed a holiday display that had both religious and secular symbols on the grounds that the state was not taking sides. He further established his bona fides on this issue when he supported government neutrality regarding religious expression in Child Evangelism Fellowship of New Jersey v. Stafford Township School District last year.

On freedom of speech and the rights of college students, Alito ruled (according to NPR) that a Pennsylvania law prohibiting student groups from running ads for alcohol was unconstitutional. He upheld the rights and autonomy of the Pitt News at the University of Pittsburgh.

The judge upheld both the second amendment right to bear arms and the authority of states to make their own laws when he ruled in a 1996 dissent known as Rybar for the right to possess weapons.

As the Washington Times concluded in its editorial about Alito, “The President has nominated an exceptionally qualified and experienced judge. Sam Alito is a clear, conservative choice who deserves the up-or-down vote to which court nominees are entitled.”

Let’s give him that vote.
