On the High Road (or "Conservatives can Drink and Write Articles too!")

by Bob Borek & Nick Hoy
Contributing Writers

Shot.

He’s wearing tweed, right? The pipe hanging out the right corner of the mouth, tobacco climbing toward the ceiling fan, right? White moustache dancing over his lip as he spouts whatever happens to be flowing from right ear to left at any given moment. You can question him, challenge him, jump onto his desk and start pounding your fists on the walls, and he’ll smile, take a deep draw on that pipe of his and dismiss you.

Back in high school, most of us had teachers wearing collars, always cautious of the politically correct electric fence that runs tightly around their classroom rhetoric. Discretion apparently is not included in Stanford’s $40,000 tuition. Welcome to peer pressure of ideas. Everybody says what they want and thinks what they want as long as they don’t stray too far from the rest of the pack. If they do feel the need, their spines better be made of steel because they are going to be weathered by a long line of relentless attacks.

I’m a freshman. Fresh and pure like the Canadian snow that I left behind. Fresh like you feel after you’ve chewed on a pack of Mentos. Fresh and impressionable. I don’t think I even know what to think. I swallow words like illicit alcohol; I wallow in acquired knowledge and bathe in the oasis of Stanford “learning.” Messages, adages, maxims, proverbs, life lessons, labor supply curves: the semi-permeable membrane that surrounded my brain is now fully permeable. I let everything in without discrimination (because discrimination is never, ever, ever allowed). I believe everything I hear.

Perhaps the old Stanford Indians label wasn’t as far off as we thought. Tribalism is alive and well at The Farm, wearing blue and riding on donkeys straight down the moral highroad (the moral highroad that quite naturally leads to San Francisco). The tribal elders (those wearing tweed) stand at the front of auditoriums, hold the fort at seminar tables, preach Tolstoy and algorithms. They are invariably convincing. They ooze wisdom like chocolate from a fresh-baked cookie. They are, quite literally, the leaders of the pack.

Shot.

No shame either. No humility. No enlightened thought that maybe, one day, I might be wrong. The popular belief is that if a professor speaks slowly enough and calmly enough, he will make no mistakes. Misquote a passage maybe, be ten thousandths of a point off the right answer to the differential equation, but these are trivial mistakes. They can literally hear no evil. They can speak no evil. How can they do evil, do they do anything?

Where is this palace of liberalism? How do we cultivate these enlightened ideas, see the obvious answers to out nation’s woes that the President of the United States is too much of an ass to see for himself? Not much of a vantage point is it, sitting behind that huge gaudy desk in the oval office? Power corrupts, no? What does absolute power do? Much better this fairy tale campus in the sunny rich suburbs of San Francisco. That’s where you ought to go if you want to learn about the world, find the most detached place you can. The less you do, the more you have time to think.

Shot. Touché.

“I’m like William Faulkner. Maybe Jack Kerouac.” I’m severely disenfranchised. I’m the poor little white kid, who just doesn’t quite fit in. Inconformity is the new conformity, just as pink is, I am told, the new black. Subtleties accumulate, gradually become less subtle, until I know, unequivocally, that I am a liberal. I love John Kerry. I hate tax cuts. National security? That’s a conservative buzzword.

Shot. Shot.

I got nothing. I’m still thinking, maybe if I t.....a....l....k m....o....r....e s........l........o.........w.........l........y.
I give you the new conservative battle cry, for our protests of protests and con-quests for success. Or at the very least, I try to. But I think, just maybe, I forget.

Shot. Shot. Shot. Shot.
