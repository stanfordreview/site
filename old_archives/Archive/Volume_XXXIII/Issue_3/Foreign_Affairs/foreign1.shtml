Iran, the Next Nuclear Power?

by Jake Suyderhoud
Staff Writer

 

Slowly but surely Iran has succeeded in breaking every rule in the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty and admitting to it. The only rule it still denies breaking is trying to build an atomic bomb. However, the shady and secretive actions of Iran leave many wondering if they can just take Iran’s word on this issue. Secret experimentation with plutonium and uranium, purchases of machinery from the same black-market as Libya used when experimenting with its own atomic bomb, and current work on a heavy water reactor much better suited for producing nuclear weapons than for making electricity leads one to ask the question, “What is Iran really up to?”

On November 1, the International Atomic Energy Agency called for Iran to suspend its uranium enrichment program to calm international concerns as well as build up some confidence over its “peaceful” use of nuclear energy. Mohamed El Baradei, director general of the IAEA, told the General Assembly of the United Nations that he has “asked Iran to pursue a policy of maximum transparency, so that we can bring the outstanding issues to resolution.” However, Iran, after years of lying to the IAEA, has yet to suggest that it is going to make a miraculous roundabout and do anything of the sort, recently voting to continue its uranium enrichment activities and to keep out inspectors. With the rise of hardliners in the Iranian government, the task to denuclearize Iran looks even more daunting.

If Iran continues in its activities without opening up to the international community, it may be referred to the UN Security Council. The United States has been pushing for this for a over a year, since inspectors, acting on information from an exiled opposition group, uncovered almost 20 years worth of covert nuclear research. However, Iran’s threat to drop the NPT has led some nations to tread lightly on the situation. Neglecting the situation will not make it stronger. Production of nuclear weapons in Iran not only undermines the NPT, a fragile enough document, but also may incite its neighbors to experiment going nuclear – something that would be extremely dangerous in that volatile region.

If peace-minded Russia and Germany want to avoid a conflict in Iran, they need to join the US and Britain and France and lean on Iran until real progress towards its denuclearization is made. Fear of having Iran pull out of the NPT is not enough – Iran soon will only be one more spilled secret away from being in violation of it. The international community would need to threaten real sanctions to make the clerics of Iran move on this issue. If the rest of Europe does not start placing pressure on Iran to give more answers, then another conflict will undoubtedly arise in the Middle East.
