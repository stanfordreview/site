Editor's Note
Becoming Disillusioned with The Disillusionment: Protest Hoping In The 21st Century

Johnny Openshaw

I cannot claim to be a seasoned protester. I was not at Seattle. I have never been jailed for blocking traffic or smashing plate glass windows. I have never marched down the a street holding a paper sign with the words "ban", "free", or "outlaw".

My chance to join the ranks of the disillusioned came on April 17 when there was a planned protest in San Francisco against the IMF and World Bank, a mini-Seattle so to speak. Not that I have anything against the IMF or World Bank myself, but this was an opportunity to see up close what a protest (one complete with the oxymoronic "protest permit") works. Briefly I toyed with the idea of getting a black outfit for the occasion, but settled just for wearing something dark. And practicing my "break the bank”, I took the train to SF.

Protests have played a large role in the creation and history of this nation. From the American Revolution to the protests concerning civil rights, the United States has been a country where citizens can make themselves heard. A protest represents the epitome of the grass roots movement. The Boston Tea Party presents a model of the ideal protest -- the motive (taxation without representation), the action (turning the harbor into a giant tea kettle), and the result (the American Revolution). I went to the IMF and World Bank protest hoping to find remnants of these three necessary factors.

I left the protest that evening disappointed. The motivation of protesters ranged from getting their seldom read socialist periodicals out to the masses, to persuading the gullible of the "obvious" connection between President Clinton and a soon to come intergalactic invasion. Some were there to gain support for other protests sprouting up in neighboring areas. Prominently displayed were pamphlets protesting certain political prisoners and calling for freedom of particular death row inmates. By the large, the protesters were a friendly lot. Among other invitations, I was asked to attend a "cage-in" -- where, if I so wished, I could spend some hours in a cage to express my disdain for the use of laboratory animals in scientific research. (I passed on that one.)

The problem was that few of the protesters seemed to have any solution (logical or otherwise) to the World Bank or the IMF -- problems which supposedly led them to collect in a San Francisco plaza on this Monday afternoon. I do believe that protest can bring about positive change. This has been shown time and time again. But protesting without a cause (and a couple of teenagers dressed in black parading about does not constitute a cause) or without even an inkling how the problem could be assuaged, this borders on the ludicrous. Protesting just to protest solves nothing.
