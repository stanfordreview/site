"Break The Bank" And "Defund The Fund"
 by Bob McGrew
News Editor
Photographs by Johnny Openshaw

Conservatives have often had suspicions about the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank as taxpayer-funded institutions designed to tame global markets. Libertarians like Nobel Prize-winning economist Milton Friedman have called for them to be abolished outright. On April 16th and 17th, however, the sins of the IMF and World Bank became the target of massive protests in Washington D.C. by the same activists who shut down the WTO in Seattle last fall. Activists organized counterparts to the "A16" march in cities around America, with the largest and most vibrant in San Francisco on April 17th.

Twenty-odd Stanford students left from Columbae to join the San Francisco protests, a motley crowd from SEAS, Students for Environmental Action at Stanford, SLAC, the Stanford Labor Action Coalition, and a single activist from Amnesty International.

After the protesters grabbed themselves an empty car on the Caltrain, a tall Indian named Mandeep Gill stood to speak. He had gone to the demonstrations in Seattle last fall against the WTO and, as a seasoned activist, even spent time in jail for civil disobedience. Mr. Gill confessed that he didn't know that much about the IMF or the World Bank, because, after all, he was a busy physics student. But he invited the students to tell what they knew.

At first, one student read from a rather even-handed account of the history of the twin Bretton Woods institutions, and then a rather less even-handed indictment of their current policies. The next students read flyers like "How IMF Policies Destroy Forests" and "False Profits: Who Wins, Who Loses When the IMF and World Bank Come to Town."

Of course, the protesters didn't attack the IMF and the World Bank as the nemesis of global free markets, but rather for their distinctly capitalist structural adjustment policies, which require the government to cut spending, remove trade barriers, and increase exports in order to receive funds. The brochures almost began to make the IMF look good.

Unbeknownst to the protesters, as they traveled to the rally, the protests in Washington D.C. for which this was to be solidarity were vanishing fast under the influence of a cloudburst. As it began to rain, the college students who went to D.C. to "Break the Bank!" and "De-fund the Fund" packed their bags and went home. As there was no rain in San Francisco, however, the demonstrators stuck it out through warm, sunny weather and crystal-clear skies.

The rally filled the center of Justin Herman plaza where Market ends in Embarcadero, though it was by no means packed. The people there were a chaotic mix of college students carrying signs, elderly activists handing out the Socialist Worker newspaper, reporters followed by cameramen, and black-clad anarchists beating drums.

The anarchists were everywhere. Most wore black pants, a black shirt, and some added a black jacket with a hood. They then covered their faces with bandanas so as to leave only the eyes showing. Some had "Free Mumia" emblazoned on their shirts, a reference to Mumia Abu-Jamal, a one-time black journalist on death row after his conviction for shooting a Philadelphia police officer.

Most of the protesters, however, eschewed black in favor of T-shirts with slogans. While some were merely advertising radical groups, like "Earthfirst - No Compromise in the Defense of Mother Earth," others were more sinister. "There's a sleeping cop in all of us," one T-shirt read. "He must be killed."

One person who did not look sinister was Brad Hinson, a cheerful-looking student from the College of the Redwoods in Ft. Bragg. He was colorfully dressed, in contrast to the black-clad groups, and his dyed-red hair contrasted with the black flag he was carrying half-furled.

"I'm here because I'm totally dissatisfied with the way the global economy is going right now," he said. " I'm personally an anarchist and I feel that there's a pressing need to abolish currency and all forms of authoritarianism in economic relations in order for us to get ourselves off the track of environmental destruction and human exploitation and all those other things."

Mr. Hinson had joined the Seattle protests against the WTO. In fact, he talked knowledgeably about the roots of Seattle-style tactics as pioneered by Spanish anarchist groups during the Spanish Civil War. But he opposed, as a matter of tactics, the kind of property damage and window-smashing that gave the so-called "black blocs" of anarchists a bad name in Seattle.

"It's a very controversial tactic," said Mr. Hinson. "I think it can be very effective, but it can also alienate people. I'm not one to denounce it, because plate glass is plate glass - you can melt some more sand and make more. With any construction project, you have to clear ground - you have to destroy in order to build. I'm here to push the cause of worldwide anarchist revolution."

As Mr. Hinson left, his pennant unfurled, revealing it to be pure black without design. At that moment, however, a hush fell over the plaza as the speaker from the striking Bike Messengers Union declared "a moment of silence for the bike messengers and walking messengers who are no longer with us and all your friends whose lives have been tragically cut short by this evil corporatist system."

Sometimes the rally seemed less about the IMF or the World Bank and more about getting rid of capitalism. Some signs read "Human Need, Not Corporate Greed," while others railed against multinational businesses and the greedy businessmen who run them.

Neither Dan Ruck, from the College of Marinn in San Raphael, or his friend Brianna Seth, however, seemed to be railing so much as listening.

"I think that just by being at a protest - by having your body here - it shows that your consciousness is aware of everything that's going on," he said. "There's too many problems to solve just by showing up at a place and doing a few chants, but the fact that you're here demonstrates that you're aware. That's the first step in any revolution, isn't it?"

When asked about the reasons they were against the IMF and the World Bank, it was Ms. Seth who laid out the charges.

"That they enslave and kill people who are just trying to live their life and who don't have a choice. And that they're destroying the environment."

While that might seem a tall order for two institutions that do little but give away money, the two did manage, among other things, to unite a disparate crowd into a chanting, sign-waving mass.

An island of peace, however, formed around two students sitting on the sidewalk chalking out an abstract figure. As it turned out, Chad Cordell and Leah Bass, from the Art Institute in San Francisco, were listening as well as drawing. Mr. Cordell had even been to the Seattle protests. But the two of them, on the outskirts of the demonstration, turned out to be the most moderate ones to be found.

"From what I've heard about it, [the IMF] is not the greatest thing. I'm not necessarily in favor of shutting it down, because having funds available to the world is not a bad idea; it's all about how it's used. I'm kind of for reform of it, instead of funding things like pipelines through rainforests."

From the 17th floor of the Hyatt hotel overlooking the plaza, the rally, overwhelming while in its midst, seemed but a small part of the pulsing life of San Francisco. Indeed, the hotel itself is plush, clean, comfortable, and elegant - the essence of capitalist affluence. Even as the demonstration turned itself into a giant march down Market, it could clearly be seen that the protesters who filled the street for a full city block, flanked by policemen, could not stop traffic or even the pedestrians shopping in Market's lavish stores. San Francisco's life as a hub for trade across the Pacific continued, as the protests caused little more than an annoyance for motorists trying to get on with their life.

In the windowed hallway looking onto Market stood two businessmen talking amiably, one American and one Japanese, both the kind of corporate executive against whom, as the supposed beneficiaries of the World Bank and the IMF, a large part of the protest was directed. Looking down at the stream of activists protesting greedy businessmen, the IMF, and the World Bank, the American executive told his Japanese colleague, "Hell, I'd protest against the World Bank myself!"
