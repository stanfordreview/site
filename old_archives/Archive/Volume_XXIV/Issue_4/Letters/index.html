Letters
Elian Gonzalez
Dear Editor:

I have a number of disagreements with Mr. Gibbs assertions and conclusions in this article. Mr. Gibbs discusses the rights the child would have in Cuba versus here in the USA. Mr. Gibbs says the boy would have "the right to speak freely about whatever he chooses without fear of government punishment. Really? Try speaking, for example, as an advocate of the medical use of marijuana and see how quickly you become a target of smear campaigns and harassment by the police. Our own Senator Feinstein is busy pushing for passage of a bill that would prohibit internet websites from containing instructions for growing or using marijuana. When Californians overwhelmingly passed the Medical Marijuana Initiative, the State District Attorney decided the public had been "duped" and ignored the will of the people he was elected to represent. Do they have a law against burning the flag in Cuba? We're very close to having one here - a law befitting a totalitarian government far more than a so-called democracy. The right to earn money free of government control? Ever hear of taxation? In Cuba, the boy "would only be free to do whatever the government wanted him to do". Try not registering for the draft and see what happens here.

I find it deeply troubling that Mr. Gibbs seems to accept without question the testimony of those Cuban-Americans who tell us how horrible life is there, how there is no freedom. I find this conclusion as troubling as his assumption that those would advocate the child's return to his father and his homeland have "no idea what it is like to live in an unfree society." I'm assuming Mr. Gibbs is referring to the overwhelming majority of Americans who believe that the child should go back with his father. How Mr. Gibbs comes by this omnipotent knowledge is a mystery to me -- I'd love to have absolute insight into the minds of millions of people I've never met and to whom I've never spoken. Mr. Gibbs proposes that these people try living in Cuba for some time to experience that country's deprivations firsthand, while ignoring the fact that the USA doesn't even allow its citizenry to travel from the US to Cuba.

I wonder as well why Mr. Gibbs doesn't trouble himself to question America's boogie-man demonization of Castro and Cuba when contrasted with American government support for Latin American dictatorships from Haiti to Panama to Chile -- dictatorships with human rights records that make Cuba look like Switzerland. Given this country's widely acknowledged history of lies and deception regarding our involvement in the repression, imprisonment and torture of the citizens of many Latin American countries, I wonder why Mr. Gibbs so blithely accepts what our government tells us about Cuba. Do you remember the Tonkin Gulf incident? Or don't they teach that shameful episode in American history in colleges anymore?

Mr. Gibbs says we "cannot assume that Juan Miguel Gonzalez is speaking for himself." But we CAN assume that a little six year old boy, made to deliver a videotaped message to his father, at the behest of those who would use him as a pawn in their political power struggle, is? Why should we trust the Miami relatives, who exposed this little boy to daily mob scenes on his "behalf", paraded him in front of network cameras, all to further their own obviously politically motivated message? If Mr. Gonzalez is not eager to return to Cuba with his son, why hasn't he defected? Why would he willingly return to Cuba and risk ostracism, imprisonment, brainwashing, torture?

Mr. Gibbs also insists we consider the mother's wishes, since she "had custody" of the boy at the time. Did she? The truth here is murky at best -- She took the child with her to follow her boyfriend. Whether she had "custody" in any legal sense is subject to debate. I've read that the father had actual Òcustody" of the child in Cuba. And what should we think of a mother who would take a child into a situation where's there's an exceptionally high risk of dying? The fact is that the mother is dead, and her wishes will forever remain unknown to us.

I am frankly appalled at Mr. Gibbs' claim that he "wouldn't rule out God having something to do with it." This deus ex machina seems to me to be nothing more than a last-ditch, desperate attempt to shore up a weak point of view. Why argue your point at all, if in the end it's "God's will?" Why not just blindly accept whatever happens as the work of God? And I suppose it's pointless to ask why God drowned the mother, or allowed Castro to live this long, since (I've been told) "God works in mysterious ways." I personally don't believe in a God who would force a child to watch his mother drown and then send a pod of dolphins to escort the child to the "land of the free." The founding fathers may have believed our inalienable rights were "endowed to us by the Creator," but those same rights are protected and preserved for us by the laws of our country. And the laws of this country say -- as they should -- that the child belongs with his father.

Sincerely,

Robert Fagan
