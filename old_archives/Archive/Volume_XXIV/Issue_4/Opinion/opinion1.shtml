Opinion
Who's To Say What The Law Is?
by Dr. Charles H. Kendall
Guest Writer

Three years ago in the May 19, 1997 issue of The Stanford Review, I wrote a guest commentary entitled: "Who's to Say What the Law Is?" With a mostly new student body it is time to repeat the message, hoping many of you will use the wonderful Stanford libraries to verify the information I am going to offer you, then see if your American history, political science, and law professors are teaching their subjects accurately, and let me know.

Please ask your professors, leaders of the Intercollegiate Studies Institute and the Federalist Society exactly where in the U.S. Constitution the judicial branch has ultimate authority to interpret laws, or where is authority granted for judge made, precedent-setting "case" laws? Why are legal dictionary definitions of "person," "truth," "rule of law," "perjury," "common law," and "due process of law" so different from those in standard, unabridged English-language dictionaries? Why are law students taught that there are no fixed truths? Why is God out of American law and virtually all public discourse, according to at least seven prominent American legal historians? Guest lecturing for decades about constitutional legal systems, I have ascertained a pattern of misrepresentation from numerous historians, political scientists, law school professors, judges, and lawyers about what our constitutional system is supposed to be.

Unfortunately, several of our lawyer Founding Fathers at the Philadelphia Convention in 1787 devised an unfinished Article III (judiciary article), to be finished by them two years later by the Judiciary Act of 1789. This act put back immediately, or made it possible to put back soon thereafter, every major abuse of legitimate Anglo-American common law our ancestors had just fought a war of independence over! Unconstitutional oaths for judges and marshals, reducing grand and trial juries' authority and putting both under the thumbs of officers-of-the-court; jailing for contempt; signing of "informations" in criminal cases without getting grand juries; and on and on these seditious Judiciary Act of 1789 sections go. Were you ever taught the entire Judiciary Act of 1789? I wasn't; not in high school, college, or graduate school. A retired Rochester, New York police officer persuaded me to obtain an official copy and read it. His criticisms about this largely seditious act were right on the mark, and I urge each of you to get a copy and read it through. I showed copies to National Association of Scholars friends who are active history and political science professors, neither of whom had previously read the entire act. Both exclaimed to me, "My God, Charlie, is this seditious material what's in the first years of our national government?"

After the Civil War there was constant pressure by bankers and lawyers to increase their control over the American economy and politics. In 1875 the American Bankers Association was formed in Saratoga Springs, New York. In 1878 the American Bar Association was formed in Saratoga Springs by lawyers and by many bankers as well. In 1892 the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws (in short, the Uniform Law Commissioners -- ULC) was formed in Saratoga Springs. Its stated purpose was to circumvent the Xth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. Each states' citizens pay for this clandestine group's meetings, although the meetings are closed to the public and the press. The ULC is a nearly secret arm of the American Bar Association, with very strong input from the bankers and the rest of the financial services industry. The Council of State Governments and the American Law Institute are ULC spin-offs. But the ULC handmaiden agencies doing most of the damage to our constitutional system of government, with separation of powers and overlapping checks and balances, are the 50 states' and Congress's legislative reference bureaus, giving officers-of-the-court nearly total control over all bill-drafting language. If this doesn't work, the courts have been disallowing or setting aside statute laws they do not like, as well as making precedent-setting "case" laws, which they fallaciously tell us are common law.

If you readers would like, in future articles I will describe particularly bad sections of the Judiciary Act of 1789, and the ongoing transgressions by the ULC and its minions, including our major print and broadcast media. But this is enough to get you started.
