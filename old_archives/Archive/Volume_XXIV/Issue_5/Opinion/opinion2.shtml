Opinion
eBay Meets Mayfield: A Market-Oriented Draw
Everyone can finally breathe a sigh of relief about the Draw. Now that everyone except the unassigned knows where they are going to live and the uncertainty is ended. Of course, the complaining has only barely begun. In truth, the Draw seems like an awfully poor way to allocate housing - we only use it because no one has found a better way.

The Draw has two major problems - it's unpredictable and it's unfair. In few other areas of life are we as vulnerable to the whims of a random number generator as in housing. Normally, we can plan out our lives in advance, making choices based on what we can afford in order to ensure that we get most of what we want. Thanks to the Draw, however, a student has exactly one choice to make in finding a room: preferred or unpreferred. To ameliorate the harshness of that choice, there grew up a complicated system of priorities to allow an escape for the few: for those into art or Asian studies, public policy or Slavic issues, the Draw may be less painful. But for engineers or draw groups interested in many things, the choice is stark: preferred or unpreferred.

Even more important than planning out who to live with and near, however, is planning how to pay for housing - and the differences between houses can amount to thousands of dollars. A student stuck with a bad draw number is de facto stuck with a university-run dorm - with the most expensive housing and board bill. The difference between Stern and Synergy is $2115 - and 2000 draw numbers for women. While unpredictability in location is only unfortunate, unpredictability in price can mean a great deal more - and two thousand dollars is a great deal.

The Draw can also be unfair. Consider the fabled "6000 Club." All it takes a senior to get in are draw numbers totaling over six thousand - such as getting two 1750s and a 2500 - and each year about thirty seniors join. While some groups end up with very low draw numbers every preferred year, most people would prefer the certainty of having at least some good housing to the chance of living in Bob two years of three. After all, the odds for that are about 400 to 1.

The pitfalls of the Draw, however, are not intrinsic to the process of allocating rooms. After all, in the real world, people don't draw straws or cast lots for housing - they buy it on the free market. Stanford can do the same thing by granting each student housing points, and then auctioning off rooms on Stanford's own version of eBay. People would be able to plan their lives and finances in advance and ensure that they won't have to live in Stern all three years. Most of the miserable uncertainty associated with the Draw would disappear, replaced with the work of figuring out how much a particular room is worth, given the future trade-offs of spending points now.

In the past, it would have been prohibitively complicated to allow students to bid for housing. This no doubt explains why colleges that provide housing have almost universally decided upon the draw system. But thanks to the miracles of electronic commerce, it's finally possible to allocate rooms efficiently and fairly.

In a nutshell, the plan is this: each upperclassman would get 5000 points to be distributed as he chose over his three years of guaranteed housing. He and his roommate could bid together for any kind of room in any dormitory. After all, a single in Lantana might cost more than a one-room triple in Bob depending on people's preferences. Draw groups would have an almost unlimited ability to split up or stay together as they please, depending on how much each is willing to pay for their rooms. RA's, RCC's, and other staff would continue to get housing, leaving them free to pay for better housing the other two years. Priorities could be reformulated as discounts.

Instead of leaving things up to chance, students would be able to trade off a worse room now for a better room later or vice versa. Alternatively, one could choose to live in a place like Yost all three years. Obviously, even though every student would have the freedom of choice in rooms, no one could choose to live wherever he wants all the time. But that's the way the real world works. Contrary to the assertions of some on the Left, it takes, among other things, planning and self-discipline to get ahead - unlike the Draw, real housing doesn't fall like manna from heaven.

Is a market-oriented approach too complicated for Stanford students? Certainly, it is more complicated than the Draw. But it need be little more complicated than eBay - which even Berkeley students can use. Besides, figuring out how much something is worth is one of the basic skills of American life. A market-oriented Draw would remind everyone that all real choices are between satisfaction now and satisfaction later. That trade-off is inherent in life. But, subject to that trade-off, markets allow people to choose what they most desire.

And that's a lesson worth learning.

Bob McGrew is a sophomore majoring in Computer Science. He wishes a little more of that manna from heaven had come his way.
