Opinion
What Is The Theme Of The Theme Dorms?
The big word these days is diversity - we are supposed to believe that more diversity is better for everyone. But what actually happens when we put an emphasis on diversity?

By "embracing" diversity, we have actually reduced diversity and supported separatism. We have separate dorm facilities for people of different races, separate campus offices which are supposed to be for a particular race, and many student groups which are for only one particular race. Has anyone stopped to think that this is exactly against the original point? Wasn't the idea of the civil rights movement to bring people of different races together to get along despite their racial differences? Maybe I'm just dense, but I have a hard time understanding how having separate campus groups for each race facilitates this goal. Let's look at some goals that separatists probably had in mind, and then notice that none of these goals are achieved.

Suppose the original goal was to bring people of different races together. While this may be a noble goal, the current method does not seem to work. If people of different races are separated, they obviously cannot interact since they live in different dorms, clearly negating the original goal of bringing different races together.

Suppose the original goal was to reduce racism between different ethnic and racial groups. If one's aim was to achieve this goal, it would seem that one would attempt to bring the different groups together to talk and interact. However, creating separate facilities for different groups does just the opposite: it separates the groups and reduces the chance for dialogue and interaction. This is unlikely to reduce racism. This is all, of course, assuming that it is actually possible for Stanford to eliminate any racism among nearly full-grown adults.

Suppose the original goal was to allow minority students to be around people of their own race. This goal in and of itself is not practical and Stanford should not support it. In the real world, students will not be able to be around students who are all their own race; in fact, minority students from predominantly minority communities will more than likely be in an environment whose racial composition is very different from their home. Thus, if anything Stanford should be preparing those students for this reality by immersing them in a multi-racial environment, not isolating them in a single-race environment that they will most likely never be a part of in the professional world. In addition, this approach assumes that every minority student feels most comfortable around people who are his own race. This is by no means true, since many minority students at Stanford are members of the middle class and have therefore grown up and become accustomed to being around people who are not the same race.

Suppose the original goal of this separatism was to allow each group to be "represented" on campus. This idea is illogical. The notion that one office or campus group can represent every member of a particular race is appalling, as is the notion that each different race somehow has separate and distinct interests that other students don't have. It makes the sweeping assumption that every member of a particular racial group is so identical that one office could represent them all. It assumes that all members of a particular racial or ethnic group wish to define themselves by their race, and that they have no other interesting properties or interests by which to define themselves. It assumes that Johnny, who is race X, can be represented by the X Cultural Center Office, even though Johnny may have no common interests or beliefs with the majority of people who are a part of office X. Wasn't the whole point of the civil rights movement that people NOT define themselves based on race, but on their own unique personalities and interests? The idea of separate offices and facilities for each race does not achieve this goal.

It seems then that there is really no reason to have so many separate dorms and groups for each different race, so why then do we have them? There are probably two reasons. For one, the people who support this kind of separatism are not held to any standard of accountability whatsoever. They do not have to show why and how their method of separatism has succeeded in reducing racism or bring students of different races together. Why? Any attempt to hold them accountable will probably be branded racist. This brings up the second reason for continuing separatism: political correctness. Stanford administrators and staff dare not criticize the policies of any of the "official" ethnic or racial group centers on campus, lest they be branded racists and reprimanded. They are therefore forced into silence.

Is it better to have separate facilities and offices for different races? There is absolutely no evidence to show so. In fact, if anything they hurt relations by encouraging separation and therefore reducing chances for dialogue and interaction.

By John Gibbs
