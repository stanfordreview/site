Editor's Note
Ethics and the Academy

Johnny Openshaw

Is Peter Singer on a crusade to save the orangutangs, the baboons, the apes, the mandrills and the whole host of screeching primates? If so, that should include us. At any rate, it's fitting that this issue of The Review "Ethics And The Academy" hits the stands shortly after Singer, a professor at Princeton and one of the best known (certainly the most notorious) ethicist in the world, made his appearance at Stanford.

When Princeton brought in Peter Singer, he was the guy everyone wanted to hate. Front page news in every tabloid in the country, he was depicted as "Dr. Death", the guy who wanted to smudge out the existence of the mentally ill and other "misfits". He was the one who believed that not giving money to international aid organizations was the near equivalent of traveling to a third world country and opening fire on a few of the inhabitants.

Knowing this, I found it somewhat ironic to hear Peter Singer stand in front of a crowd and declare that if we can help a person without inflicting damage to ourselves, we have a moral right to provide aid. If Peter Singer is marked, it is not with a notorious reputation, but with the astounding ability to argue multiple points of view from a moral standpoint.

This issue of The Stanford Review aims to scratch the surface of ethical issues of research universities. The coverage here is not only on Peter Singer. We explore certain experimental procedures at Stanford Medical Center and a new Stanford "think tank" which researches the ramifications of "persuasive technology".

This issue of The Review also marks the end of the term and the time to pass the torch to the new editor, Bob McGrew. I would like to publicly thank my staff for their hard work. This paper certainly would not flourish without their varied talents.

Wishing you all a wonderful summer!

Johnny Openshaw
