Slow Population Growth Strains Western Economies

by Omkar Muralidharad
Deputy Editor

The median age in India is 24; in Germany, it is 43. In India, the average woman has nearly 3 children, while in Germany, each woman only has 1.37. The Indian population is growing, while the German one is poised for decline. As these statistics show, we are in the throes of a profound demographic shift. One of the most important changes in the next decade will be the graying of Europe.

For a population to remain stable, each woman must bear around two children. This ensures both numerical and demographic stability in the long-term: there will be roughly the same number of people, and their ages will be distributed in a similar manner. In some countries, such as India, the fertility rate is well above this replacement benchmark. These countries’ populations are growing and becoming younger. In others, such as most industrialized nations, the fertility rate has dipped well below it. Their populations are aging, and will eventually shrink. Although the impact of this discrepancy will not be fully felt for half a century, it will affect life considerably in the next 10 years.

To a Malthusian pessimist like Stanford’s Paul Ehrlich, a contracting population might seem a blessing. Notwithstanding the evidence to the contrary, such as Ehrlich’s famous bet, it might actually be beneficial but for the accompanying phenomenon of aging. As a population shrinks, it becomes disproportionately old. The old, in coldly material terms, are a burden on the young. They produce little, but require much tending in the form of pensions and healthcare. Most of the time, there are sufficiently many workers to render this burden light. As a population ages, however, there are more people to take care of and fewer people to do the caring.

These effects will be felt most strongly in Europe. We are already beginning to see the struggle between old and young. For example, in Germany, cur­rent pension levels are unsustainable. The political clout of the aged, however, has prevented any reforms, such as lower benefits or later retirement. Taxes, however, cannot be raised - the young already struggle under an onerous tax burden. Deutsche Bank esti­mates that 29% of the average worker’s salary goes toward pensions. The common solutions are thus unfeasible.

The only viable solution seems to be immigration, but it comes with its own problems. Increased social tension will provoke an increasingly violent backlash, already seen in Jean-Marie Le Pen and associated anti-immigration politicians. In addition, immigration on a truly massive scale will be required to reverse the population drain. Europe, unaccustomed to such influxes, will probably be reluctant to choose this path. It will thus face a slow exit from geopolitical center stage, as younger countries surpass it.

Americans will not be so dramatically affected. Although America does face problems with Social Security, it is far less severe than the European crisis and politicians may well be able to reform the system. America’s birth rates are higher than Europe’s, and immigration is a long-standing tradition. In the 1980s, the world experienced a fall in fertility rates. We experienced a rise – according to some predictions, Americans will outnumber Europeans before 2030. Since Americans are, on average, one-third richer than Europeans, this demographic shift will radically alter the economic landscape.

In addition, America is already profiting from the rise of younger Asia. America’s diverse, open culture has enabled it to harness Asia’s cost advantages, especially in manufactur­ing. As Asia and other young countries become richer, so will America.

More important, however, will be changes in American foreign policy. As Europe spends more of its money on social programs, America will be forced to play an even greater military role. The NATO alliance will also be strained by America’s closer links with the Latin America, strengthened by the burgeoning Latino population. America will soon, if the demographic trends hold, be younger, richer, and more powerful than Europe, one of its closest geopolitical rivals. Those who think America’s power is peaking are mistaken: the American era is just beginning.
