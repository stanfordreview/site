Bulletproof Special Fees, non-SOCC Exec. Break with Tradition

by Ryan Tracey
Deputy Editor

The student body’s approval of every undergraduate Special Fee group on the ballot, along with Melanie Kannokada and Aneto Okonkwo’s victory in the ASSU Executive race, demonstrated a remarkable break with recent ASSU election history. Traditionally, the student body vetoes at least a few Special Fee groups and elects the executive candidate endorsed by the Students of Color Coalition (SOCC). Kannokada and Okonkwo failed to receive the SOCC endorsement, which was instead given to Victoria Carr and Fernando Castillo—who lost by just over 250 votes.

Only the Stanford Axe Committee and The Stanford Progressive came close to not receiving approval for Special Fee funding. If one hundred students voted “no” instead of “yes” in either case, the groups would not receive funding this coming year. This is strikingly dissimilar to last year’s results, where eight undergraduate Special Fee groups suffered defeat. MEChA, the Stanford Axe Committee, and The Stanford Progressive all received approval this year, although students rejected their budget requests in last year’s election.

A significant group of students worried that an investigation that The Review released about the Black Stu­dent Union (BSU) before the election would adversely affect the success of BSU Senate candidates and the BSU Special Fee. However, both the candidates and the fee seemed to escape untarnished. Dan Stringer, a BSU affiliate, in fact received the most votes of any Undergraduate Senate candidate. In addition, the student body elected three of the other four BSU-affiliated candidates. The BSU Special Fee received approval from over 66% of students, an increase from last year’s 62.46%.

Candidates endorsed by the Stanford College Republicans (SCR) also achieved remarkable success. Of the four candidates that SCR President Donald Stevens encouraged members to vote for in an e-mail, three were ultimately elected. SCR decided to endorse both democrats and republicans, in an effort to formulate an Undergraduate Senate that “will push for student issues next year, and leave politics to the political unions.” Compare this to the Stanford Democrats, who endorsed twelve candidates—of which, only eight were elected. Four of the nine officially SOCC-affiliated candidates and one of the two NAACP-affiliated candidates also won.

Students passed Measure A, an initiative to combine the Community and Programming General Fees into a single fee, by a large margin. Advisory Referendum 1, a gauge to assess the student body’s support of making dorms “opt-out” rather than “opt-in” to door-to-door distribution of student publications, was supported by a smaller margin. Just less than 53% of students voted in favor of Advisory Referendum 1. Advisory Referendum 2, an assessment of student support for funding nonpartisan events that are put-on by politically partisan groups, only received the support of 35% of the student body.

Interestingly, candidate support of Advisory Referendum 1—the door-to-door distribution measure—accurately predicted the success of senate candidates. In a survey done by The Review in the issue just prior to the election, six candidates solidly declared their support of Referendum 1. Five of these candidates were elected.

While Measure A will immediately go into effect this year, Advisory Referendum 1 simply informed the ASSU of the student body’s support for an “opt-out” policy. The ASSU Senate and other organizations must act of this referendum for a new policy to actually go into effect. Students will be able to receive a refund from the Special Fee groups that they do not necessarily support next quarter at refund.stanford.edu.
