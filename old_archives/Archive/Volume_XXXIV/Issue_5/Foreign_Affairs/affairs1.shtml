Amnesty Expanded as Iraqi Insurgency Loses Steam

by Andrew Wright
Senior Staff Writer

Iraq’s new President, Jalal Talabani, announced that he will expand the promise of amnesty for Iraqi insurgents to encompass more groups than were previously covered by the amnesty granted by the interim government in 2003.

After the overthrow of Saddam Hussein, the interim government offered an amnesty to all insurgents with the exception of rapists, kidnappers and murderers. Talabani is offering a wider blanket that seems to include most of the members of the formerly barred groups. ‘’There are two kinds of killing: In battle or in action, this could be covered by the amnesty,” Talabani said. “Those who are involved in killing innocent people, detonation of car bombs, killing people in mosques and in churches, these would not be covered by the amnesty.’’

Talabani did not specify whether the amnesty would apply to the insurgents who had killed American or foreign troops or Iraqi security forces. He also did not elaborate on how an amnesty program would work.

The amnesty expansion comes at a time when the insurgency movement appears to be declining in strength and possibly facing schism. The success of the elections in January and the growing presence of Iraqi soldiers and security officers have weakened support for the insurgency. Various insurgent factions have recently exchanged threats over disagreements about the use of violence, especially violence directed towards civilians. In Ramadi, some fighter groups are posting fliers denouncing “extremist” tactics. Some of the fliers promise retaliation against militants and threaten to reveal their locations and identities to the police.

The amnesty is a strategic decision designed to reintegrate insurgents in civil affairs and make them a part of the political process. Accord to Steven Casteel, the senior US consultant to Iraq’s Interior Ministry, the strategy is working. In an interview with the USA Today, Casteel said, “Groups that participated in the insurgency are now coming forward and saying they want to participate in the politics. Normally, it’s a cell leader coming forward through an intermediary.”

Talabani said amnesty is part of a program to draw Iraqi insurgents into efforts to build democracy, strengthen the economy, lessen public support for violence and reduce future attacks. ‘’With a comprehensive policy we can eradicate terror in the country within months,’’ he said.

Responding to recent protests against the U.S. military presence, Talabani said he was no rush to see foreign troops leave. He views the continued presence as a natural part of the aftermath of the war, which he considers necessary and just. According to him, ‘’The war was not the best way, but it was the only way to liberate Iraq.”
