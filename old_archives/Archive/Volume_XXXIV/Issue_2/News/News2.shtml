Physician-Assisted Suicide Scrutinized

by Hans Allhoff
Columnist

On Tuesday, February 22, the Supreme Court agreed to hear an interesting new case on physician- assisted suicide. The plaintiff, contrary to what we might expect, isn't a doctor (or death-eager citizen) claiming a state law prohibiting the practice violates his constitutional rights. The Court, in fact, has already rejected the argument that the Constitution affords terminally ill people a “right to die.”Rather, the plaintiff is the federal government— the Bush administration in this case—claiming Oregon's Death With Dignity Act allowing assisted suicide violates federal law.

The Controlled Substances Act, the government will argue, places a ban on lethal drugs unless they're used for “legitimate medical purposes.” A hastened death is not one of those purposes. Why does this make Gonzales v. Oregon an interesting case? Because even though it is about assisted suicide broadly speaking, any decision the Court reaches will be much more subtle. If the Bush administration is successful, and doctors who help terminally ill patients die are in fact violating federal law, those doctors might not be finished.

They might be able to come back and try to argue that the Controlled Substances Act is itself unconstitutional, as applied to them. The actual legal question the Court will take up is one of administrative law: Is the attorney general (before, John Ashcroft, and now, Alberto Gonzales) allowed to apply the Controlled Substances Act to drugs used to assist suicide? Or, as the Ninth Circuit held below, does the Act only speak to “drug abuse and prevention” in which case Gonzales may be out of his element?

The holding, whatever it is, is going to be narrow.Let me suggest another reason, though, why this is an interesting case. Oddly enough, it is Republicans, typically deferential to state governments, who here are asserting the federal government's right to interpret and enforce a national standard governing how controlled substances may and may not be used. It's worth asking whether this is consistent with their own political theory.

Are they for national education standards, national penal standards, or national health and safety standards? Most likely yes; what's driving Republicans here isn't a theory of federalism after all, but rather the moral view that assisted suicide is too close to killing for comfort.What are your views as to whether we ought to be allowed to end our lives when we see fit? If you're for assisted suicide on personal autonomy grounds, you're a true libertarian. If you're against it on “culture of life” grounds, you're an honorable conservative.

There is no doubt that this is a controversial issue on the “Right.” But by my reckoning, when it's our generation's time to go, physician-assisted suicide will have lost its sheen of controversy.

By then, I suspect there will be a new constituency – asserting their right to die just because they find life uninteresting – and a complementary constituency – denying anyone a right to live and consume social resources beyond a productive and functional age. (Some political and moral philosophers have already taken this latter point up.) Then, a case like Gonzales v. Oregon, seen in perspective, will seem positively tame.
