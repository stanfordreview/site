Social Security Dialogue Needs Reform Too

by Simon Berring
Executive Editor

Social security never goes out of style as a political issue, but in the weeks since President Bush’s state of the union address, the public dialogue around it has reached a crescendo.

Neither party has come up with any really new material in the last ten years, but their voices are getting louder. So, it is incumbent on us to try to figure out what in the debate makes sense before we get bullied into toeing one or another company line.

First, I have a language nitpick. Anybody who has read George Lakoff’s popular stuff knows that the vast right wing conspiracy exercises Orwellian control over political language in America, and yet nobody can mention Bush’s Social Security plan without talking about how much it costs.

Call me old-fashioned, but when somebody talks about the monetary cost of something, I assume that he is worried about having to spend more money on it. Unless you suspect Bush of wanting to increase payouts, though, there is no way he is increasing the cost of Social Security. He just wants to tweak the revenue stream, and a decrease in government revenue walks, talks, and smells like a tax cut. I don’t want to substitute one bit of rhetoric for another, but it is important to point out that anybody talking about “taxpayers footing the bill of privatization” is calling in from the Twilight Zone.Aside from language, though, there appears to be one dominant source of confusion in this debate. Social Security as-is has split personalities.

We should, at least for the purposes of discussion, treat the insurance program, the income redistribution program, and the investment program as separate entities. Worries about starving octogenarians can be directed to the first two programs, and questions about how today’s workforce and youth should plan for their retirements can focus on the latter.Equipped with this sort of framework, we can discuss private savings accounts without – in principle – having to worry about a lot of sticky political and sentimental issues.

The takehome point is that even though there are a lot of subtleties to any proposal about social security, a substantial part of the issue (and the majority of your FICA tax dollars) can be reduced to the simple matter of whether the government should run a mandatory-contribution pension program.If we could simply focus on that dialogue for a moment, America would be doing itself a favor. It is a relatively clean-cut case of Nanny-State vs. Liberty, and yields to familiar sorts of empirical questions, so we might be able to reach some sort of resolution quickly.

Then, having made sure that the bulk of Social Security (and our money) was doing what we wanted, we could turn to the complicated and likely-unresolvable questions of desert and responsibility that permeate the remainder of the issue.
