UN Fails Sudan, Genocide Continues

by Diane Raub
Deputy Editor

Here at Stanford we have recently had the opportunity to examine the catastrophic events occurring in the Darfur region of the Sudan. As last week’s visit by Sudanese ambassa dor Khidir Haroun Ahmed clearly showed, however, two very different stories about this situation are circulat ing throughout the international com munity as a whole. One side, promoted emphatically by the Bush administration, the U.S. Congress, the Parliament of the European Union, and government and NGO officials from around the world, notes with alarm the violent campaigns of arson, murder, torture, and rape carried out by the Sudanese government in collaboration with the Arab Janjaweed militias. For months it has condemned the events as a genocide now approaching Rwanda in scale.

In the spring of 2004 Jan Egeland, U.N. Under-Secretary for Humanitarian Affairs, repeatedly insisted that the violence against the Darfurians was “ethnic cleansing” (although the U.N.’s official position on the issue has changed noticeably since then). The other story, offered by Mr. Ahmed, staunchly refuses to recognize the reality of genocide, dig nifying Sudanese government actions as “counter-insurgency warfare.” Ahmed is not alone. For months, the African Union has refused to declare the tragedy a genocide, send ing a token number of monitoring personnel (as of Febuary 6, 2005, only 1400) which falls pitifully short of its planned deployment (3500 to 4000) and of the ability to do the least bit of good in protecting the victims of geno cide.

Often the best these AU “protecttion” forces can do is idly watch the atrocities occuring in front of them. African countries of the Arab League have also sided with Khar toum, deferring to the government’s “sovereignty” when faced with the question of whether to send in outside aid. Following an October 2004 sum mit, the heads of Chad, Egypt, Libya, and Nigeria issued a joint statement stressing their “rejection of all foreign intervention in this purely African question.”

This position falls in line with Khartoum’s repeated insistence that the international community has somehow twisted the truth to vilify the Sudanese government; that instead, rebel forces that have attacked military targets and police are to blame for the justifiable government crackdown. Most dismaying—but predictable—is the reaction of U.N. Secretary-Gen eral Kofi Annan, who seems muddled over which side to believe.

In June 2004 Mr. Annan asserted that he is “serious about preventing genocide”, even warning of future military inter vention in Darfur, but he has yet to back his rhetoric with any meaning ful actions. In fact, after receiving a report compiled by the International Commission of Inquiry on Darfur on January 1st, 2005, Annan declared “I am not ready to describe [Darfur] as genocide or ethnic cleansing yet.”

This U.N. report, while emphasizing the grave degree of the humanitarian crisis in Darfur, takes painstaking measures not to label the situation genocide. Unfortunately, close inspection of the report reveals that the authors meticu lously omitted the most serious crimes committed in Darfur, opting instead to use shaky logic to circumvent the awful truth of the situation.

The U.N. Commission report on Darfur explicitly states that in addition to numerous aerial assaults on villages conducted by the government military and the Janjaweed, “(h)undreds of incidents have been reported involv ing the killing of civilians, massacres, summary executions, rape and other forms of sexual violence, torture, abduction, looting of property and livestock, as well as deliberate destruc tion and torching of villages. These incidents have resulted in the massive displacement of large parts of the civil ian population within Darfur as well as to neighboring Chad.” It goes on to conclude that “(e)xcept in a few cases, these incidents are reported to have occurred without any military justification in relation to any specific activity of rebel forces.”

Additionally, after observing that “such attacks were also intended to spread terror among civilians so as to compel them to flee the villages…” and that “(i)n a majority of cases, victims of the attacks belonged to African tribes, in particular the Fur, Massalit and Zaghawa tribes” the authors ultimately conclude that the tragedies occurring in Darfur should be classified as “large scale war crimes.” All of these descriptions seem to fit perfectly under the definition of geno cide outlined in the 1948 Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide.

The authors’ refusal to classify it as such becomes even more astonishing given their further assertions in the report: “One should not rule out the possibility that in some instances single individuals including Government officials, may entertain a genocidal intent”, how ever “if any single individual…has such intent it would be for a competent court to make such a determination on a case-by-case basis”. Sudanese Vice President Ali Osman Taha has already publicly declared Khartoum’s staunch opposition to trying any Sudanese official indicted in Darfur crimes in a court outside of the Sudan. In statements like these, the report does all but confirm the Darfur atrocities as genocide.

This position seems even more astonishing given the fact that the authors worked with very incomplete evidence. U.N. officials were not allowed access to mass grave sites in Darfur until after the govern ment had declared these sites “secure.” Darfurian human rights groups have received security documents detailing the government destruction of eight mass grave sites, including the names of various officers involved.

Members of the U.N. investigative committee were never allowed into the region of Wadi Saleh, where some of the worst mas sacres have taken place. Many of the report’s claims grossly misrepresent the true nature of both the government attacks and the horrific living condi tions which millions of displaced Dar furians now must endure. The list of flaws goes on and on. (To read in more detail about the report’s numerous failings, see Eric Reeve’s “Report of International Commission of Inquiry on Darfur, February 2, 2005” Parts I and II at http://www.sudanreeves.org.)

The facts of the situation undeniably classify the events of Darfur as genocide, revealing any refusal to recognize them as such as self-interested political accommodation. If the U.N. report had declared a “genocide,” Mr. Annan would have been obligated by stipulations of the 1948 Genocide Convention to lead the U.N. into real, material steps against the violence. This would have landed him in conflict with China, which has veto power in the Security Council and significant oil interests vested in the Sudan. Unfortunately for the mil lions of Darfurians living in mortal peril, too many at the U.N. are will ing to sacrifice the truth for political convenience. It is a very old story.
