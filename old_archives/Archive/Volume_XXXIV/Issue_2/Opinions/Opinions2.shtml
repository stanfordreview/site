The Iraq War: Worth the Cost? Con

by Dara Hazeghi
Staff Writer

“A government that is big enough to give you everything you want is big enough to take it all away.”

Barry Goldwater’s words ring as true on the 2nd anniversary of the invasion of Iraq as they were when he first uttered them. Unfortunately, President George W. Bush seems unconcerned that a government big enough to bring freedom and democracy to the rest of the world is certainly big enough to curtail it at home.

Indeed, what are we to call what has happened in Iraq? Was it a war? If so, why did not the Congress declare war, as is its prerogative? Is it a nation-building exercise?

Even in the loosest interpretation, the Oct. 10, 2002 “Congressional Resolution on Iraq” that paved the way to war limited the mission to the removal of Saddam Hussein and his “weapons of mass destruction.”All of which begs the question of why we are still in Iraq.

Saddam Hussein was captured more than a year ago. The Duelfer Report, delivered in Sept. ’04, marked the end of the search for WMDs in the country. In short, we did what we claimed we went to do.Today, we’re suffering from a serious case of mission creep. Now we’re bringing ‘freedom’ and ‘democracy’ to Iraq, not to mention permanent military bases and the world’s largest American embassy. This all comes at considerable taxpayer expense.In wartime, civil liberty is usually the first casualty. There are 135,000 Americans today in Iraq, fighting and dying. These men and women enlisted to defend America.

Today, they fight in an undeclared war for President Bush’s vision of Iraqi freedom. They are conscripts in a nation-building exercise.Make no mistake about it; elections in Iraq are a welcome event. Should Iraq turn into a peaceful democratic nation, the world will undoubtedly be a better place.

But is this really a situation in which the American government has both the right and the responsibility to act?The purpose of our Constitution, and thus our federal government is stated in its preamble. In particular, “… secure the Blessings of Liberty to ourselves and our Posterity,” does not indicate any intent to authorize social engineering of foreign nations on a massive scale.On the other hand, President Bush’s Feb. 2, 2005 State of the Union address speaks of, “… eliminating the conditions that feed radicalism and ideologies of murder.” Bush is quite explicit that American intervention in Iraq is a part of this mission.This is not a small mission. Radicalism flourishes across the world, from Peru to Indonesia, and from Zimbabwe to Tajikistan.

Rooting out its causes is a huge undertaking, and not one, which a person would expect a strict-constructionist of the Constitution to take.Today, the state is larger and more powerful than it was two years ago, and conservatives are silent about it. More than $156 billion has already been spent, and American troops will remain ‘promoting democracy’ in Iraq through 2006. We are engaged in what more and more seems to be “a war to make the world safe for democracy.” Who’d have thought Woodrow Wilson would be reincarnated as a conservative Republican?
