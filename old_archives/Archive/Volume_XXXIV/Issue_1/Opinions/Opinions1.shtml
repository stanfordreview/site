Black History Month Commemoration

by Editorial Staff

Do you know why February is designated as Black History Month? In 1926 Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a high school teacher who earned his Ph.D. in history from Harvard University, was disappointed by the general absence of African-Americans in the history books. As a result he designated the second week in February as Negro History Week. Negro History Week was celebrated in black schools, churches and YMCA’s across the United States. Dr. Woodson chose the second week in February to honor the birthdays of Abraham Lincoln (February 12, 1809) and Frederick Douglass (February 14, 1818).

It was not until 1976 that Black History Month was established for the entire month of February. Besides being the month to remember African-American history, February is also the month of President’s Day – which occurs on the third Monday of the month. President’s Day has traditionally commemorated Presidents Washington and Lincoln, both of whom were born in February. In the spirit of Black History Month and in the wake of President’s Day, The Stanford Review would like to commemorate the “Great Emancipator” Abraham Lincoln. As there has not yet been an African-American President of the United States, no one has a greater claim to our accolades than Lincoln.

Unfortunately, the legacy of Abraham Lincoln has been under attack lately. Liberals try to point to Lincoln’s earlier “racist” record in an effort to disassociate him and the Republican Party from the abolitionist movement.

Detractors often use a statement Lincoln made during a debate in his 1858 Senate campaign as evidence. In response to an attack that he supported citizenship for African-Americans, Lincoln responded, “There is a physical difference between the white and black races which I believe will forever forbid the two races living together on terms of social and political equality…and I as much as any other man am in favor of having the superior position assigned to the white race.”Despite this racist rhetoric, Lincoln was a staunch opponent of slavery. In an 1855 letter to Joshua Speed he wrote, “You know I dislike slavery; and you fully admit the abstract wrong of it.”

Later in an 1859 letter to Henry Pierce he quipped, “This is a world of compensations; and he who would be no slave, must consent to have no slave.”

Not only in word but in deed, Lincoln aided the African-American cause of liberty and equality under the law. Lincoln proved to be one of the most important figures in the abolitionist movement. Lincoln’s leadership led to the Emancipation Proclamation, which not only gave black Americans freedom, but also provided the foundation for the thirteenth, fourteenth, and fifteenth amendments that led to the development of modern American liberty. These advancements suggest that Lincoln’s legacy ought to be viewed as positively today as Dr. Woodson undoubtedly viewed it eighty years ago.
