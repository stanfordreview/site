French Losing Faith in Chirac

by Jake Suyderhoud
Deputy Editor

The leader of France has long been praised by his countrymen and many in Europe for opposing the US-led war against the Saddam’s Iraqi dictatorship— an illegal French trade partner. However, with the recent success of the election in Iraq, the French people may soon find this noble stance less significant. Chirac will soon find himself being scrutinized on the issues that matter most to the French people— domestic issues.Now in his tenth year of office, the longest among all G8 leaders, Chirac is still preaching the domestic reform policy he was when he was elected.

Earlier in January Chirac declared that “France must be on the offensive. We must act with determination for strong and lasting growth.” “We must act urgently” to combat unemployment, he said. This plan is one to prepare France “for the next ten years,” Chirac suggests.

However, after ten years of these promises already, one wonders if this will be enough. Looking back to his inauguration, we see these same pledges over and over again. “I will engage all my force to restore the cohesion of France,” he promised at his inauguration in 1995.

“Employment will be my preoccupation at all times.” One hopes, even for his own sake so that he wouldn’t suffer from the anxiety, that Chirac would have made some progress in this topic of concern. However, in these last ten years, the French economy has made little progress.

The unemployment rate has fallen by half a percent and annual growth is up by .1%. These numbers aren’t anything for President Chirac or the French people to be especially proud of.

And neither are any of Chirac’s new policies innovative. He will not attack the core of his economic problems such as an over-regulated market and many of this 72-year-old’s promises are for ten to fifteen years – an ambitious lay-out for any public-office holder, especially one of his age. But the question remains, even if he has the opportunity to be in office for ten more years, will anything truly get done?

With his approval rating falling from 64% when he took office to 41% now, Chirac should understand that he needs more than anti-American rhetoric and empty promises to win an election. Sooner or later, his inaction will catch up with him and the French people will have a new president.
