Editor's Note: Founding the Stanford Review

by Benjamin Guthrie
Editor-in-Chief

Welcome to a new volume of The Stanford Review!

Each new volume brings a new editor, a new editorial board, and new ideas. Of course ideas are not formulated in a vacuum. I am indebted to my predecessors especially Piotr Kosicki, Gary Raichart, Stephen Cohen, and David Stat who helped shape my ideas about the Review.

Two years ago, Piotr Kosicki highlighted “diversity” as the theme for his volume. This theme persisted beyond the end of his volume. Gary Raichart turned campus attention to the racism of some “cultural” groups on campus. Stephen Cohen extolled the virtues of one maligned minority, the conservative woman. David Stat brought a global perspective to the Review with the introduction of a new Foreign Affairs section. I intend to continue the theme of diversity, in particular diversity of thought and opinion.The Stanford Review serves a crucial role on campus as the only newspaper with a conservative perspective.

The ASSU News Readership Program delivers three newspapers to the dining halls on campus: The New York Times, San Jose Mercury News, and The Stanford Daily. The editorial boards of each of these newspapers are unabashedly liberal. In each case effort is made to offer alternative points of view, but there is hardly parity of perspectives. The Stanford Review is not a daily a newspaper, but we will nevertheless stand valiantly as a counterweight to the liberal media establishment on campus.Starting a new volume offers an appropriate time to reflect on the origins of The Stanford Review.

At the end of January, the Review held its inaugural alumni reunion, featuring guests Richard Epstein, University of Chicago law professor; Peter Robinson, former speechwriter for President Ronald Reagan; Thomas Sowell, world-renowned economist and syndicated columnist; and Arnold Beichman, Hoover Fellow and Cold War journalist. The weekend was a great success, as the caliber of the guests listed above reveals. We had about 60 attendees. Although the reunion was not primarily a fundraiser, our alumni demonstrated phenomenal munificence, which will help sustain the Review for the future.

One of the highlights of the alumni reunion was the “Story of the Founding of The Stanford Review” dinner at Peter Thiel’s penthouse in San Francisco. After lavishly wining and dining us, Review founders Peter Thiel and Norman Book regaled us with the story of the founding of this newspaper.The Stanford Review was founded in 1987 in the context of several politically charged debates. The first was the debate over the Western Culture humanities core, which the Review supported. Ideologues championing the end to the Western Culture program chanted, “Hey, hey, ho, ho Western Culture’s got to go.” The upshot was the end of the Western Culture program and the beginning of the more multicultural Cultures, Ideas, and Values (CIV) program.

According to The Stanford Daily, “The 1988 legislation required that every track include works by ‘women, minorities and persons of color,’ study a non-European culture and address topics of race, gender and class.” The controversial CIV program was replaced by the current Introduction to Humanities program (IHUM) in 1997.

The second debate that ushered the Review into existence surrounded political correctness. In the 1980s to be politically correct was to be sensitive, aware, and forward thinking. Thiel emphasized that the Review tried to “change the way people thought about these things” and to “engage in the ideas” rather than blithely accept this orthodoxy of political correctness.Eighteen years after its founding, Thiel questions, “How much do we actually influence debates? How much do we actually change people’s minds?”

He admits it is hard to measure the influence the Review has had on the Stanford campus and the world at large. But he maintains, “I think we have made an unbelievable impact on the world over the years” just looking at the success of the Review alumni. Despite the individual success, Thiel underscored the fact that “we have a long time ahead of us.”Since its founding, The Stanford Review has provided incisive investigative reports and thoughtful commentary and has offered a lone conservative voice on the predominately liberal campus of Stanford University. I intend to preserve the tradition of The Stanford Review, engage in the debates of our time, present the nuances of the conservative perspective, promote true diversity, and always keep an open mind about new ideas.

Fiat Lux!
