Editor's Note: University Policy Stifles Free Speech

by Benjamin Guthrie
Editor-in-Chief

One of the greatest attributes of Stanford University is the plethora of voluntary student organizations that enrich the campus community. Yet Stanford’s treatment of student publi cations, including The Stanford Review, has been less than cordial. After a series of controversial articles about MEChA published by The Review in 2003, some houses began to enforce a latent Resi dential Education (ResEd) policy, which bans door to-door distribution. Stanford’s dis tribution policy is set by ResEd, and the default is a ban on door-to door distribution, but “each house, under the guid ance of their Resident Fellow [RF] or College Director may modify the stan dard policy.”

The impetus for the ban on door to-door distribution was an effort by some students to censor the content of student publications. According to an article published in The Stanford Daily, “‘There is certain sensitive content in publications that we don’t think resi dents should have to deal with in their rooms,’ said Roble resident assistant Kevin Holmes, a senior.” Not surpris ingly, Roble was one of the first dorms to crack down on the free distribution of student publications to individual rooms last year. The reasons for banning door-to-door distribution have evolved to include safety and environmen tal concerns.

West Florence Moore’s RF Patrick Young allowed his dorm staff to vote on the policy at the beginning of the school year, but residents were not consulted. Young consid ers door-to door distribution of student publications to be a “safety hazard” because there is some probability that someone might “slip” on a publication if it is placed in front of a door in a dorm hallway. Young further argues that door to-door distribution of publications imposes negative externalities on dorm residents. When students do not pick up the publications promptly after they are delivered to their doors, the hallways become cluttered. Although, it is not clear that this bothers most students.

Publications that clutter the hallways impose an additional burden on the custodians, who must clean up the leftover publications. A simple solution to this problem would be for dorms to allocate some of their funds to compensate custodians at the begin ning of the year, assum ing they believe the custodians are not already paid enough to clean up the hallways. Student publications also provide a positive e xternality by fostering community at Stanford in the spirit of residential education.

However, students are hindered by the decentral ized design of Stanford’s residences in receiving student publications. The primary alternative to door-to-door distribution is distribution to dorm lounges, but lounges serve as woe fully inadequate points of distribu tion. Students do not know when to expect new publications to arrive due to unpredictable publication patterns and long intervals between publication dates. Stanford students are very busy people, and it is not their custom to actively seek out low-visibility non daily publications. Many students are not even aware of the existence of some small publications. Unless publications are distributed directly, many students will not see them at all and will not even have a chance to decide whether they want to read the publications. Door-to-door distribution is a courtesy to students that solves these problems.

When a publication arrives at students’ doors, students who might never have thought they were interest ed in the publication or a topic within the publication may find themselves casually perusing its pages and learning a great deal. Door-to-door distribution is the best way of ensuring freedom of the press on campus, as it facilitates the shar ing of ideas and creative expression throughout the community efficiently and allows potential new readers and contributors to discover their interest in publications within the realistic con straints of daily life on campus. Chair of the ASSU Senate Admin istration and Rules Committee, Chris Nguyen, proposed an advisory refer endum to allow students to voice their opinion on the matter of door-to-door distribution.

The referendum asks if students support an opt-out policy for door-to-door distribution, in which the default policy for student publications would be to allow door-to-door distri bution, unless dorms vote to opt-out. Though not ideal, this measure would move the policy in the right direction and significantly improve the status quo. Free speech at Stanford requires a reasonable outlet for publications to reach students, and currently door-to door distribution is the only effective way of accomplishing this goal.
