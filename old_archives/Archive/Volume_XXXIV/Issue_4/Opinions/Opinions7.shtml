Stanford's General Fees System Needs Fixing

Staff Editorial

 

Since today is Tax Day, it is appropriate to reflect on the unique voluntary tax system for Stanford students – the special fees system. It is voluntary in the sense that after being required to pay into the system, students can obtain a refund each quarter for part or all the amount taken from them. While a number of improvements have been made with regard to Stanford’s fees process, there still remain a number of loopholes and procedural issues that need to be addressed. Stanford’s system can essentially be divided into two sections. Most groups that require substantial funding receive their special fees pending student approval during spring elections. In order to even be considered for the ballot, groups must present a detailed budget that illustrates all costs the group incurs and what specific costs they wish to pass on to the student body. In general, this part of the system appears to work relatively well. Funding proposals must pass at least two levels of scrutiny, and it is therefore difficult to falsify budget information.

Other groups, however, make appeals for funding directly to the ASSU, bypassing student approval. These groups are still funded by the student body, yet the money is taken out of the “general fund” rather than a specific special fees line item. About 91 groups exploit this method for funding. Unfortunately, there is no way to ask for a group-specific refund when it comes to the general fund. Thus, students are forced to either ask for a complete refund of general fees, thereby penalizing groups they support, or directly fund every group that is approved by the ASSU, thereby funding groups to which they may maintain strong objections. The result is that a number of groups that many might agree should not receive funding are able to take money from the student body while the majority of students remain oblivious.

We believe that the student body needs to reevaluate the effectiveness of the general fees system. The current system is unfair in forcing students to make a binary decision between funding a number of ideologically radical groups or to fund no groups at all. For instance, among groups receiving general fees funding is the Stanford Labor Action Coalition (SLAC). The Stanford Labor Action Coalition recently used student funds to pay for protest materials and, according to their website, is openly opposed to President Bush’s political agenda. Students that do not subscribe to SLAC’s self-professed ideology have no recourse other than to withdraw funding from the entire general fees system. It is high time that the general fees system be reformed so that students may choose to fund the Archery Club while not being simultaneously forced to fund far left-wing groups such as SLAC.
