D'Souza Captures the Conservative Spirit

by Milton Solorzano
Staff Writer

For those interested in the ideological roots of The Stanford Review, or the humor shared by conservatives on a number of current issues, Dinesh D’Souza’s book Letters to a Young Conservative is perfect. Letters is structured around a series of ‘letters’ written to a fictional Chris, a young moderate-to-right student on an elite college campus (a character with whom I share particular affinity). D’Souza responds to specific inquiries regarding conservatism and politics in general, and also details his own history and political philosophy more and more as the book continues. Filled with seriously funny anecdotes from D’Souza’s speaking experiences on liberal campuses, the book is as entertaining as it is informative, and should be a pleasure to read for those on the right.

With chapter titles such as “Fighting Political Correctness,” “How Reagan Outsmarted the Liberals,” and “Authentic vs. Bogus Multiculturalism,” D’Souza offers a recent history of liberal/conservative interactions and explains how his ideology was changed by experience. D’Souza himself claims that, “by the end of my freshman year my views were mostly liberal” (16), though soon thereafter, he became involved in a brand new publication at Dartmouth, the conservative Dartmouth Review (sound familiar?), modeled after William F. Buckley’s National Review. After working with the Review, D’Souza realized his own political opinions were largely misinformed and guided by the peer pressure of an elite liberal institution. Knowing this, D’Souza went “cardinal”—finding conservatism to be “a framework, durable and yet flexible, for understanding the world” (22).

As I read about D’Souza’s own beliefs and political interactions, I think about my own. I remember attending church as a child, and seeing my faith and my Lord insulted and blasphemed by “popular culture.” I remember when my teachers would impose politics in the classroom (a pathetic abuse of authority over a captive audience). I remember growing up in a military family, and learning a small part of the cost of freedom when my father served in Iraq. And perhaps most importantly, I remember my strange interactions with people who argued for moral relativism—the idea that nothing can be morally “right.”

This concept, and its antithesis, is at the heart of why I’m a conservative—because I believe. I believe in the fundamental worth of humans, and therefore deny governmental systems which deny individuality (e.g. communism). I believe that the right to life is sacrosanct, and cannot be abridged by convenience or threat of embarrassment. And I refuse to believe that the actions of terrorists and their allies—the same individuals who fired upon my father—are “freedom fighters” misunderstood by cultural bias and unjustly attacked by an arrogant America.

I believe that conservatism is the only means of preserving the moral and intellectual foundations of our nation and our people. While the messages of the left are often hateful toward the US and its policies, the message of conservatism is hopeful. Without this message, the moral relativism argued by the left would rot out our nation by destroying our moral foundations.
