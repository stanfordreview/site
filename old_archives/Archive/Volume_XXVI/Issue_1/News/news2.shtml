Bacon Blames NAFTA For Harming Rights
by Alex Robbins
Staff Writer

On February 27, freelance photojournalist David Bacon came to Stanford to speak about NAFTA, globalization, and the Mexican labor movement. His appearance was sponsored by the Stanford Labor Action Coalition, and during his presentation he showed students numerous slides documenting the condition of Mexican workers both in Mexico and the United States. As Mr. Bacon presented these slides, he gave a first-hand narrative about each one, depicting for the assembled students both the harsh poverty under which many Mexican workers live as well as their attempts to ameliorate their condition through labor activism and protest. He also repeatedly stressed the harm caused by the North Atlantic Free Trade Agreement, asserting that "NAFTA really harmed workers rights."

Mr. Bacon's presentation focused on workers in industry and agriculture, and on how NAFTA and the resulting influx of American employers affected them. He told students that although American companies do sometimes pay higher wages than locally based ones, the average wage for a factory worker is still only around 30 to 40 pesos a day, which is about 3 to 4 US dollars, while milk, in comparison, costs approximately 15 to 20 pesos. Mexcian workers thus have very little money remaining to provide their families with necessities. Mr. Bacon's photographs backed up these statistics visually by depicting the impoverished conditions under which many Mexican workers live. Freshman Nicole Louie called these photographs "powerful," and noted that "it's often a lot easier to be captured by an image than by words." One photograph, for example, showed the dilapidated, claustrophobia-inducing home of Francisco Lopez and his family, in which there was a gas stove dangerously close to the beds and a washing machine dumping water straight into a hole into the ground, because the Mexican government refused to build a sewer system.

Despite this poverty, however, Mr. Bacon asserted that "in a formalistic sense" Mexican workers enjoy more legal rights than their brethren in the United States; Article 123 of the Mexican Constitution explicitly states that "every person has the right to dignified and socially useful work." Other legal protections include a prohibition on strike breaking, a right to healthcare, and requirements that companies establish schools and provide daycare for their workers' children. However, Mr. Bacon stated that such rights usually exist "only on paper."

He remarked that although for the most part "government ownership [of companies] gives workers more power," although that "is not to say there are no problems with public companies." In fact, he stated that the "governing elite" of Mexico was able to bring unions into traps because unions were part of the government system they controlled, whereas in the United States "we have the advantage that we're not so closely tied to the government." Nonetheless, Mr. Bacon still expressed the opinion that, "for workers it's really bad if you get your company privatized," later clarifying in writing that by this he referred to "industrial workers up to the 1980's."

Mr. Bacon also commented on specific political policies. He faulted NAFTA for, among other things, allowing goods and money "but not people" to cross the U.S.-Mexico border, and he called the proposed Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA), championed by President Bush during his presidential campaign, "NAFTA on steroids." When answering questions, Mr. Bacon also criticized the Bush administration's proposal to issue thousands of new conditional visas that would allow Mexican citizens to remain in the United States for as long they held a job there, calling it "exploitive" because "it ties their right to stay in the U.S. to their jobs." He supported, however, a general amnesty for immigrants who had already crossed the border illegally.

More than one student agreed with Mr. Bacon's strong support for immigration. Freshman Andrew Sand, whose parents immigrated to the United States from Taiwan, commented that "if there were no immigration I wouldn't be here." Another freshman, Justin Snapp, also supported Mr. Bacon's general attitude towards immigration, expressing the belief that "immigration is a good thing. It's what this country was built upon." He disagreed with Mr. Bacon, however, with regard to actual policy, stating that "excluding people from participating in the American workforce is never a good idea." Also, despite their common support for immigration in general, Mr. Snapp differed with Mr. Bacon over President Bush's conditional work visa proposal, remarking, "I don't see how giving people the choice to accept a visa is 'exploitive'."
