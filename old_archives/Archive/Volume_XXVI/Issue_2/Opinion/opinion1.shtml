Opinion
Graffiti Not A Hate Crime
by Jeffrey Davidson
Contributing Writer

Sometime between March 14th and March 20th, some idiot decided to scribble some nonsense on some classroom walls. Had the content of the nonsense been something about cartoon characters or invisible dog fences or sociological organization theory, no one would have cared. The scribbles were not comments on any of those things; they were some asinine rants about racial minorities. Nothing on the walls had much of a coherent thought to it, and everything seemed sort of foolish and banal.

In typical Stanford fashion, however, howls of anguish erupted as soon as these acts of vandalism were discovered. The Stanford Daily published an even more typical encomium to the cult of the PC, quoting Vice Provost for Campus Relations LaDoris Cordell saying, "'it is offensive not only to the group that is targeted; it is an assault on all of us.'"

Don't get me wrong—vandalism is illegal and wrong. Racist speech is unpleasant if not illegal. But the silly comments on the walls were not "an assault on all of us." The comments at worst could be described as an assault on the targeted minorities. The rest of us are involved only as observers, and I assume that most of the rest of us can live our lives much as before.

The comments were similarly not "hate crimes," as the police department insists. Murder can be a hate crime; assault can be a hate crime; robbery can be a hate crime; even vandalism of someone's personal property can be a hate crime. This doesn't even come close. No one in particular even owns those walls, unless it's the president of the university, who is not a minority at all. It's a crime because it's vandalism. I suppose there's hatred in the messages, but it's a sort of juvenile, omnidirectional hatred, not a hatred with any kind of purposive content, certainly not a hatred with political content.

I don't think we need to worry about the inchoate musings of some high school kid who gets dumped by his Asian girlfriend and makes the leap that he (1) hates all Asians; and (2) wants to express that anger on Stanford's walls. Speech can be dangerous, but it's most dangerous when it has real meaning. Telling the world about the editor of this newspaper's drug habit (just kidding) might be harmful. Even more harmful is the kind of speech that actually speaks in a vaguely logical way against minorities. In one of my classes today, a young lady mentioned that she thought that minorities in the inner city had almost no chance in life, since they were poor and their skin was dark and because their families didn't care. Arguments like that set minorities back more than idiotic ramblings on classroom walls. Arguments like that suggest that minorities, unlike everyone else, have no control over their lives. The psychological damage of a lifetime of comments like that, I imagine, could be quite significant.

Come to think of it, there might even be reasons for minorities to welcome the recent acts of vandalism. The vandalism seems to be unifying minority communities to some extent. It also makes racists look really, really dumb. The dude can't even write straight. He also doesn't understand the use of the apostrophe. With enemies like that, minority communities can feel very, very certain that they're making some progress. It was not long ago that the racists were members of Congress, cultural icons, and the business elite.

Assistant Professor Laura Leets has a survey on the web(http://www.leets.cust.walltech.com/intolerance/index.html) asking, in part, how people would like to redress this problem of really dumb comments on the walls.
