The Last Page
US News & World Report

April 1, 2004

Dear Reader,

It has come to our attention in the past few years that many of our readers are concerned how we compile our annual ranking of American universities, colleges, and graduate programs. The growing suspicion is that we "juggle" the numbers in order to anoint a different top ranked school each year and keep our newsstand sales high. This is simply not the case.

Our reporters are trained to find the real story beneath the statistics. Each year they comb campuses across the country observing first-hand the performances of students, professors, and administrators. What they see during these visits supplies our editors with the data they use to assemble the magazine's final rankings.

Normally we keep the contents of our reporter's notebooks confidential. However, our hope is that by providing examples of how we evaluated the academic prestige of certain institutions we might clear up some misunderstandings, at the same time offering some helpful hints to those universities eager to improve their standing.

ANN ARBOUR, MI -- University of Michigan fell two spots in this year's ranking courtesy of English professor Roger Carlson's decision to leave all the grading of his fall semester papers to his graduate assistant. When the graduate assistant protested that she would not be able to mark all the papers and still have time to prepare for her upcoming oral exams, Professor Carlson advised her to "get used to academia," suggesting that it was only necessary to read "the first and last paragraph of each paper and skim the parts in between." Three-fourths of Carlson's students received some type of an A.

BOULDER, CO -- Upset that students seemed more engaged in the daily crossword puzzle than her lectures on Medieval Architecture, Professor Abby Waugh decided to substitute a crossword puzzle for her final exam. Three-fourths of Professor Waugh's students received some type of an A. The University of Colorado gave up three spots in the ranking.

ATLANTA, GA -- Moments after being informed that she would be granted full tenure, professor of Economics Gail Griffin confided to a fellow faculty member, "You know what this means. Office hours this semester will be Friday one o'clock p.m. to Friday one o' five p.m." Emory slipped one spot in the ranking.

EVANSTON, IL -- Junior Matthew Leonard recently boasted to a cafeteria table full of classmates that he has not once made it through the assigned reading for a class on contemporary short stories. Asked how he manages the weekly discussion sections without reading the material, Leonard explained, "Simple. Always refer to whatever you've read as a 'text,' always make the point that the author's tone was 'ambivalent,' and never appear that impressed at anything you've read." Northwestern lost two spots in the ranking.

STANFORD, CA -- Men's basketball team reaches the Final Four. Stanford dipped eight spots in the ranking.

Please direct any comments or questions to our editorial department. Best of luck on this year's survey.

Sincerely,

Editor, U.S. News and World Report
