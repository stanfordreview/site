For Donohue, Criticism Continues
by Clayton Brown
Contributing Writer

Eighteen months ago, Professor John Donohue of the Stanford Law School and his colleague Steve Levitt of the University of Chicago found themselves in the eye of a raging political storm. News had broke that the two researchers were attempting to link falling crime with the legalization of abortion, and this May the tireless efforts of this daring duo will finally come to fruition in the Quarterly Journal of Economics.

According to the two professors, their theory follows commonsense logic. Children who are subjected to poverty, abuse, or poor familial conditions tend to commit crime. The legalization of abortion reduced the number of children born into these unfavorable circumstances. In 1992, the first wave of children aborted in 1974 would have turned 18. The absence of these criminally inclined youths accounts for the recent drop in crime. The graph shows that violent crime, property crime, and murder all began to decline sharply in 1992, exactly eighteen years after Roe vs. Wade began to have an impact on the abortion rate.

Mr. Donohue's research first came under the scrutiny of the public in late 1999. A reporter heard him lecture at the University of Chicago and decided to write the story. The Chicago Tribune published an initial report, which inspired a flurry of articles in major newspapers and magazines across the country. Morning talk shows pounced on the issue, pro-life and pro-choice groups deployed their sentries to debate the issue, and the web became a host for a blaze of heated disputes. Mr. Levitt characterized the immediate response of the public as "knee-jerk reactions," and Mr. Donohue recounted it as "overwhelming." He recalls the phone ringing relentlessly that week. Under the oppressive siege of reporters, he decided to stop answering the phone. He once told Levitt, "call me at exactly 9PM so we can talk." Mr. Donohue expects a similar response when the paper is published in May, but hopes that the hype will draw attention to the validity of his research.

When the abortion theory made its unexpected debut in 1999, Mr. Donohue and Mr. Levitt had to deflect a host of counter arguments. Some experts argue that increased incarceration, increased police forces, and the economic expansion that began in 1992 is responsible for falling crime. One person even criticized Mr. Donohue and Mr. Levitt for "not publishing their research in a peer-reviewed journal," failing to mention that the story broke prematurely, and that a peer-reviewed article was forthcoming. Mr. Donohue believes that the published report is a "better product" and "a more complete analysis." He acknowledges that crime is influenced by a myriad of social factors, but the report controls for almost every variable, ranging from the burgeoning economy to increased beer consumption.

Mr. Donohue's research has failed to win the support of either pro-life or pro-choice groups, and instead has landed on the most uncertain terrain of the abortion battlefield—no man's land. April Holley, a spokeswoman for the National Right to Life Committee, disagrees with "the notion that it's appropriate to solve any of society's problems by killing unborn children." Other pro-life advocates of repute have labeled the study "bone chilling" and "disgraceful." In the other trench, pro-choice groups fear that the Mr. Donohue's work may misrepresent their views to the public. For instance, Ms. Holley announced that the paper "smacks of eugenics." Pro-choice groups do not want to be haunted by a ghost plank of pre-natal capital punishment.

Mr. Donohue does not believe his paper implies policy change for abortion. However, he believes that lawmakers can use his research to help reduce crime in the future. He recommends two policy avenues: "Reducing the number of unwanted births, which can be done less controversially than abortion; or trying to aid children who are born into adverse circumstances." Moreover, he believes that people should be suspicious of politicians who claim that their program is responsible for falling crime, and politicians should be skeptical of positive results.

Although Mr. Donohue's report is on the docket for publication, his research is hardly finished. His next task is to transcend national boundaries and show that abortion has had an impact on crime in other nations. Mr. Donohue smiles as he remembers the initial excitement of pouring over British abortion data, but says that it became an arduous task when he realized that the criminal and hospital districts did not correspond. Mr. Donohue confesses that he has "spent the last five months going over maps of England trying align the crime data with the abortion data." He believes that he is moving closer to results with the international data and hopes that a comparative study of several nations will yield fruitful policy options. For instance, he may discover that the nations with strong safety nets for unwanted children have substantially less crime. But even if the research fails to inspire legislation, Mr. Donohue believes that his research is very informative and a formidable challenge to many of the current theories. And, most importantly, he smiles and nods his head unequivocally when asked if he has enjoyed his research.

Mr. Donohue is willing to admit that there is no seamless algorithm for crime analysis, "The world is complicated, and statistical associations cannot always reconcile correlation and causation." In May, when the phone begins to ring relentlessly, there will certainly be more than a few critics who toss javelins at Mr. Donohue's research, but the future of crime will be the only truth teller. Mr. Donohue says that this is just "one big puzzle" that he wants to solve, so that he can "illuminate the darkness in some way."
