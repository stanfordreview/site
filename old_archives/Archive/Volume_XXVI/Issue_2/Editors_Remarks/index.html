Editor's Remarks
David Horowitz never called The Stanford Review. Instead, he called The Stanford Daily. He wanted the Daily to publish an ad that he had been circulating in other college newspapers. The ad was entitled "Ten Reasons Why Reparations For Blacks Is A Bad Idea--And Racist Too."

The Daily examined the ad, but rejected it. It joined 38 other schools in doing so. The Daily has made little mention of the ad since then, except for a brief news article on April 13, addressing how other schools have handled the issue. Though the Review was never offered the ad, we decided to publish it anyway. Our purpose is not to condemn nor praise Mr. Horowitz, nor is it to validate nor reject his arguments. But, if the ad is potent enough to incite such indignant reactions on other campuses, the Review thinks that Stanford community ought to at least view the ad.

At Brown, students raided newsstands and stole copies of The Daily Herald. At Wisconsin, students stormed the office of The Badger Daily, called the editor-in-chief a racist and demanded that she resign. At Berkeley, as at fourteen other schools, students succeeded in getting its newspaper, The Daily Californian, to apologize. The Review decided that Stanford students, at the very least, deserved a chance to react.

However, the Review has not published the ad only to raise controversy. Indeed, the issue that Mr. Horowitz has publicized is not insignificant. It deserves discussion. If not one Stanford publication were to print the ad, the entire Stanford community could go on ignoring the issue. Thus I encourage you to examine Mr. Horowitz's ten points, just as our opinion's editor, Scott Rasmussen, has done. Next week, Henry Towsner will write about how the Daily has handled the issue.

Despite the fact that the Daily did not publish the ad (perhaps in hope of preventing a riot on this campus), Stanford has not been a quiet place. Unfortunately, the most prominent issue recently has revolved around some racist graffiti that a student found on a classroom wall.

Though some students have been dismissive about the issue, some have called for a campus wide discussion. Some students believe that the administration has reacted appropriately to the incident, but other students have accused University officials and the police of withholding information that students should know. The incident has resulted in at least one Daily column and at least one Web forum. In this issue of the Review, contributing writer Jeffrey Davidson shares his thoughts on the matter.

But Stanford students have been affected by off campus politics as well. Unfortunately for everyone, the California electricity crisis is not yet old news. Alec Rawls offers his judgement as to how the state has handled the problem. Finally, two of our old editors have returned to this issue with colorful contributions. Brooke Brody-Waite has recently returned from spending a quarter in the UK, and she has brought with her an update on the upcoming British election. She shares with us an article that she orginally published online for The National Review.

Also returning after a substantial hiatus from publishing is Michael Toth. He will be graduating in June and decided that he ought to get his voice in the Review a few more times before he is off campus. Turn to the backpage for some of Mr. Toth's trademark satire.

Enjoy the issue!
