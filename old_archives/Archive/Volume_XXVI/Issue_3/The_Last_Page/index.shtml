Manners, Morals, and Dance

Manners are of more importance than laws. Manners are what vex or soothe, corrupt or purify, exalt or debase, barbarize or refine us, by a constant, steady, uniform, insensible operation, like that of the air we breathe in. ---Edmund Burke

A couple dances the tango at JAMMIX, the monthly ballroom-dancing party in Roble Gym. Their steps match across the dance floor, feet moving in intertwined patterns between the other couples on the dance floor. No words are spoken, but the two navigate the basic step along with its dozen variations flawlessly. It looks like either mind reading or a choreographed set piece, but the dance is neither. Each variation from the basic step comes as a lead from the man, followed by the woman.

The leads involve the hands, the eyes, the body, but they are not random or spontaneous. Instead, the motions and understandings of leading and following emerged over the years as tango moved from Buenos Aires to Paris to New York. Like any social convention, they were not set down except incidentally. Nor were they rigidly prescribed according to arbitrary and immutable notions. Instead, they were adopted because they worked, and worked well enough for generations of dancers to pass on what they knew to their partners and their pupils. Eventually, the methods of leading and following were codified into programs followed by ballroom dance studios, laid down in books and taught in classrooms. But the heart of dancing is not in the rigid codes, any more than it is in the sequence of footsteps the couple makes – the heart of dancing is communication according to the accumulated wisdom of what works. When that wisdom fails, improvisation makes up the difference, and sometimes it may even result in the birth of new wisdom.

Dancing is, in a sense, communicating to reach a goal while remaining courteous and considerate to your partner. Dancing is a set of manners, as little as we think of it that way. But manners, too, evolved over the centuries through individuals interacting. Table manners, the kind everyone knows, evolved as first nobles and then commoners learned to use silverware and to sit around a table drinking and eating without embarrassing themselves or their neighbors. Eventually, just as in dance, these informal experiences and norms were codified, as books by Emily Post gave some the wherewithal to enforce the rules rigidly. The code of manners that the nobles created to enable them to eat in polite company without discourtesy became a symbol of status. Knowledge of the elaborate conventions became a mark of wealth and breeding rather than a way to eat without discomforting the guests. But the point of manners is not merely to act according to some arbitrary notion of goodness, but to act in a way that allows one to get along best in society. Social conventions and traditional behavior represent the wisdom of accumulated generations for how to live together harmoniously in society. Social conventions not only allow one to avoid discourtesy, but they also confine social behavior to patterns which are easier to recognize and follow. Communication without boundaries is meaningless; that is why languages have phrases and paragraphs. Social conventions provide the structure for interaction in the same way.

But since the sixties, individuals resenting these confining social patterns have broken free of traditional conventions and of manners themselves. While not dead, chivalry is looked upon with suspicion, especially in academia. Traditional courtship no longer exists. Indeed, if anything explains the lack of dating at Stanford, it is that too many men no longer know how to ask someone on a date, or what such a date would involve. Once social and sexual rules are fluid enough, the guidelines for acceptable interaction disappear, leaving the couple to figure things out entirely on their own.

Traditional manners in many ways are dead and cannot be revived after a generation of moral apathy. And, for those manners which had lost any semblance of their original purpose and had become codified into rigid, all-confining rules: good riddance. But just as humans cannot speak coherently without a grammatical structure, it takes a social structure of manners and courtesies to allow them to interact in society. Indeed, in the nineties, manners made a comeback along with family values. But all too often, manners are still under-appreciated today. Here’s a call for a new dedication to manners and conventions. After all, manners represent accumulated wisdom, not an unchanging social fabric which, once broken, cannot be remade.

Manners oil the mechanism of society, smoothing interactions in places where conscious communication would fail. A tango cannot work when one partner has to announce his moves to the other. Society, like the tango, requires that sense of unconscious communication. Without it, society will devolve either toward choreographed authoritarianism or unscripted anarchy. Learning to interact with courtesy and manners, however, like learning to lead and to follow, allows society to dance with unconscious coordination toward the betterment of all of its members.

Bob McGrew is an Editor Emeritus.
