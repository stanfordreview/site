Lovins Praises Capitalism
by Alex Robbins
Staff Writer

Last Thursday night, "Earthweek" keynote speaker Amory Lovins expressed the hope that members of his audience would "organize the non-violent overthrow of bad engineering." Addressing a packed auditorium as part of a benefit for Palo Alto's Ohlone Elementary School District, Mr. Lovins advanced the theme of a book he recently co-authored: "Natural Capitalism: The Next Industrial Revolution." Mr. Lovins, a Harvard and Oxford educated experimental physicist, author or co-author of 27 books, MacArthur Fellow, environmental-issues speaker, and business consultant, was sponsored by a myriad of groups both within and without the Stanford community, including SEAS, whose mission statement among other things declares that "we cannot really address root causes unless we confront the racist power structures, capitalism, and corporatization that surrounds us."

The message Mr. Lovins delivered, however, was decidedly not anti-capitalist. The very name of his book and presentation implies as much—"Natural Capitalism," is written to persuade members of the business world, not attack them. Its fundamental tenet is that preserving nature and natural resources is not only better for the planet but vastly more profitable for business as well. In his response to one student's question, Mr. Lovins quipped of capitalism, "if you can think of a better system, good luck," and he stated at the beginning of his lecture that the Industrial Revolution has made society "over one hundred times more productive," which "created the middle class and everything we have around us."

For over an hour and a half, Mr. Lovins presented a mixed audience of Stanford students and environmental activists with what at times almost seemed like a sales pitch, as he enumerated case after case of the tremendous cost-savings and profits accrued due to business practices that conserve natural resources. The thrust of Mr. Lovins' argument was that the future of business lies in intelligent and conservative use of this capital. As he put it, "a true capitalist will reinvest in nature." Mr. Lovins gave example after example of instances in which companies efficiently used natural resources to save tremendous amounts of money—Southwire Corporation, for example, revamped its facilities and halved its energy use, saving 4,000 jobs.

While he discussed everything from hydrogen powered cars to efficient use of pipes, Mr. Lovins focused particularly on energy efficiency and the technology available to drastically cut down on energy costs; one of the many examples he gave was of the design of his own Rocky Mountain Institute's building in Colorado (Mr. Lovins and his wife are the CEO's), which was built to require no heating. It's sophisticated engineering not only eliminates the need for heating, but the building even houses a banana farm, and, according to Mr. Lovins, it cost less to build than a normal building with a heating system, as well as eliminating the ongoing expense of fueling a heating system. Although some students interview found the presentation's level of detail very informative, freshman Laura Ward found it "disappointing that the material was not very well tailored to the audience. It seemed more like he was giving a business presentation."

In general, Mr. Lovins forwards an extremely sanguine view whose results should appeal to both environmentalists and capitalists: he describes how the world, the United States included, is moving towards a "closed-loop system" where waste is thought of as "unsalable production." He even predicted the end of dominance of the petroleum based automobile within ten years, and its death within twenty.

Mr. Lovins, however, was not quite an advocate of economic laissez-faire; as he put it, "markets are an excellent servant, a bad master, and a worse religion." He talked of the "market failures" that hold society back from his vision of Natural Capitalism. He discussed market "externalities," that is, costs which are not fully born by the person who causes them--giving an example of this Mr. Lovins asked rhetorically, "how clean would your car emissions be if they didn't come out the back but were pumped right into the car?" According to Mr. Lovins, however, such "classic externalities"--representing the traditional environmental question of whether to favor productivity or the health of the environment--are becoming outmoded. Although self-described environmentalists and capitalists may have their doubts regarding the feasibility of the Natural Capitalist vision, freshman Lisa Ehrlich, though she herself holds a somewhat more pessimistic outlook as to the future of the environment, nonetheless described Mr. Lovins' presentation as "refreshingly optimistic and attainable."

Alex Robbins is an undeclared freshman.
