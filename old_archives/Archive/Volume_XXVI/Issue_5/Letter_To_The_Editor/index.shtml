Letter to the Editor
Dear Editor,

Recently, I was walking down the hall in my residence when I noticed that many doors had identical and seemingly handwritten notes taped onto them. Looking closer, I noticed that they were photocopied and that they were placed by SEAS (Students for Environmental Action at Stanford) in order to present the organization's opposition to the construction of California's 34th prison in Delano. As a self-described moderate, I like to read the opinions of SEAS, as they have often presented many valid arguments in favor of reasonable and worthwhile causes in the past.

One of the notes opposed the construction of the ne prison due to the high cost of incarcerating prisoners in California, especially in comparison with the state's expenditures on education. The other note began with a hard-hitting question; it asked, "Did you know that slavery is actually legal in the US?" This, of course, is in reference to the common practice of using incarcerated convicts as unpaid labor. The note mentions that "[Convicts] get no benefits or vacation, have no rights to unionize, and are severely punished for deciding not to work." I realized that the problem goes further than that; imprisoned individuals are also denied the other, more basic rights, such as free assembly, the right to bear arms, and access to almost all public places. Their privacy is limited and they are not free to come and go as they please. Clearly, their rights are being abridged.

At first, I failed to see how these two arguments were compatible; one says the state spends too much on prisons and the other eschews a money-saving (and explicitly constitutional by the 13th Amendment) practice. Clearly, not building a single prison isn't a complete solution to either, let alone both, of these problems. The only tenable solution is to close all the prisons and set all the convicts free. This is the only solution which keeps costs down and prevents the continued infringement upon the rights of California's criminals. I urge the readers of this newspaper to move beyond a penal system which doesn't work; we must fight for the abolition of all prisons in California and beyond.

Best,
Joshua Zide '02
