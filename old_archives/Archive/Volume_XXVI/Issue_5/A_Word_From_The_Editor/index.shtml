A Word From The Editor
On the front page of its May 9 issue, The Stanford Daily presented another creative interpretation of the David Horowitz controversy, by way of a news article. Rather than recount the new collection of misinformation contained therein, I encourage you to read Henry Towsner's response for an alternate interpretation of the facts.

Despite the relatively mild exchange of commentary between the Daily and the Review, David Horowitz's anti-reparations ad did not strike Stanford as loudly as it did many other college campuses. Students apparently did not feel it to be important enough an issue to integrate it into the recent 3-in-1 White Plaza protest. In fact, apart from the foothills march during autumn quarter, and the expected volume of presidential elections demonstrations in November, Stanford students have not seemed terribly angry this year. Surely this does not mean Students have run out of causes to protest. Seemingly, there are several important reasons to complain. For a few select issues, the Review might even agree.

It is suprising that more student groups do not protest tuition hikes on a regular basis. Stanford, along with its colleagues Harvard, Princeton, and Yale, have publicly admitted that universities raise tuition as a matter of course, to stay competitive. If Harvard were not the most expensive school to attend, how could it possibly be the best? In its own policies, Stanford seems very hypocritical. It has just recieved the largest ever single donation to an American University and it was the top recipient of financial gifts last year, at nearly $100 million above the second place recepient. Furthermore, administrators have been quite vocal in their ostesnible desire to use some of these funds to assist undergraduate programs. What better way to assist undergraduates than to reduce the tuition?

It is true that many students who are in dire financial need do get the necessary financial help, but there is nonetheless an entire category of students that is unable to attend Stanford. This is the group of students whose family's incomes are sufficiently high to not qualify for financial aid yet sufficiently low to not be able to bare the financial burden of actually attending Stanford. The University certainly claims to value diversity; if it lowered tuition by $3500 per quarter, it could achieve diversity with a wholly new collection of students.

Alternatively, if Stanford students don't care about this issue, they might rather take up an old issue, and actually promote it persistently. I have heard very little about development in the foothills since the autumn quarter march. Neither have I heard much from the Stanford Greens. Students created a group last fall to represent the Green Party, because the group that had started in 1992 fell apart after that year's election. This current group of Greens claimed that it would stay active after the elections, but as of yet it seems that they have suffered the same fate as the Greens of '92.

If students who lead protests ever wonder why publications like the Review often question the importance of their causes, they might find an answer in the lack of persistance that is often apparent. If an issue is not important enough for a student group to latch on to for more than a few days at a time, then how can that group expect the campus to take it, or the issue, seriously? Groups who have devoted themselves exclusively to demonstrating and protesting, such as the Radical Cheerleaders, seem to be headed in the right direction--pressure over time creates change, both in earthquake science and social movements.

If groups do not remain active for even a month after their initial formation, then they negate all the effort they put forth initially. If issues are actually important, then there is always something to say. Even the platform of the Green Party has relevance outside the context of presidential elections.

I'll admit that I won't likely take to the pulpit and start protesting myself after my tenure with this publication ends, but I will say that the Review's model of antagonism is perhaps one that other student groups could follow. We have our perennials. Every November until the grape boycott ended, the Review complained about it endlessly. Every fall since Civ ended, the Review has complained about IHUM, and every few months, we complain about the fact that Stanford's Program in Race and Ethnicity does not contain a Celtic Studies unit. Our fundamental purpose is the same as most every student groups purpose--to cause meaningful change in the thoughts and behavior of members of the Stanford community. That we want to cause a somewhat different direction of change than most other student groups does not mean that we can't all use the same strategy of approach. Professor John Manley will tell you that constant crticism is essential, and he is likely to never stop complaining about the Hoover Institution. If you must protest, please do it regularly.
