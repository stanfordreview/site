News Briefs
The Spring housing draw concluded on Sunday, May 13, the deadline for students to submit their housing preferences for the 2001-2002 academic year. Once again, much discontent surrounded the process. Many Stanford students advocate a capitalist style housing draw, in which students are allocated a number of points that they can spend over four years as they wish. This, they argue, would allow students to choose housing that represents their actual preferences more accurately than the current system, in which students receive three years of preferred housing and one year of un-preferred housing. Other complaints revolve around the online software system, Axess, through which students must submit their preferences. Invariably, the last minute rush to submit preferences by the deadline creates a bottleneck, rendering the program virtually unusable to many. CS students claim that these glitches can be easily resolved, but are still neglected. Finally, this year's draw will be more predictable than past years due to the housing crunch. Students can choose the Sharon Green luxury apartments as a housing option for the 2000-2001 academic year, despite the University's initial desire to solve the space problem differently. Also in an effort to conserve space, two of the three wings of Mirilees will be converted from doubles to triples.

30,000 visitors participated in a Powwow last week, sponsored by the Stanford American Indian Association. The Powwow is part of an ongoing effort by the student group to educate the Stanford community about Native American culture and issues. According to many student members of the group, some Native American students are reluctant to colleges and universities, for fear of leaving their own culture behind. Each year, the Powwow, which celebrated its 30th anniversary this year, thus tries to raise awareness for all members of the Stanford Community, and to simultaneously make Native American students feel more comfortable. This years theme was "Stand and Fight: Resist, Educate and Reclaim."

Debate continues over the ethics corporate sponsorships in the athletic department. Partly in response to a recent letter, signed by about 80 students and faculty, in protest of the athletic department's proposed contract with Nike, the Program in Ethics in Society sponsored a debate concerning the issue. Proponents of the contract argue that is primarily a business decision, and that the contract provides much needed equipment to athletes and relieves the athletic department of a major financial burden. Opponents of the contract have talked about the problem of giving free publicity to a company that the university should not, in their view, support. Debra Satz, head of the Program in Ethics and Society, moderated the debate. Professors Rush Rehm and David Abernethy, Provost John Etchemendy and athletics director Ted Leland participated in the debate. The athletic department is currently negotiating the contract.

Many Stanford athletic programs look towards the end of their seasons with hopes of NCAA success. The men's baseball team, led by standout hitter Chris O'Riordan, hopes to make a good showing at the college baseball world series. Both the men's and women's tennis teams, the perennial favorites in collegiate post-season play, and led by Alex Kim and K.J. Hippensteel, and Laura Granville, respectively, also hope to claim NCAA titles. The women's golf and lacrosse teams are also feeling well equipped as they finish off the year. Stanford women's golf is ranked fourth in the nation and considered a serious competitor in the NCAA tournament. The women's lacrosse team seemed no less confident as it finished off the regular season, winning the Western Women's Lacrosse League Championship.
