A Word From The Editor
My tenure at the helm of the Review has ended. I have just one disappointment--I never saw The Stanford Progressive published. When the Progressive announced itself late last quarter, the Review was very excited. It has not been since the early 90's--a period of heightened campus political activity about which the Review will forever reminisce--that there has been a liberal counterpart to the Review (then it was The Observer). To be candid, we miss the dialogue. I suppose its true that the Review always has some sort of ongoing conversation with the rest of campus, though some might call it a shouting match. But there are few instances of meaningful debate at Stanford (though many instances of meaningful one-sided argumentation, the Review included), and the Progressive was to provide the necessary element to counterbalance the Review. Maybe, if the campus is really supportive, they'll come on board in the fall. But we can only hope.

Without a genuine political adversary on campus, the Review has been left this quarter to bicker about other matters. Our point of interest recently, of course, has been The Stanford Daily. More specifically, it has been the Daily's treatment of the works of Mr. David Horowitz. The Review originally claimed that the Daily had rejected Horowitz's "Ten Reasons Why Reparations For Slavery Is A Bad Idea", but the Daily later argued that they had accepted the ad and would run it as an opinion piece. In an editorial, they criticized our decision to run the headline. In an editorial, we criticized their decision to reject the ad. In a news piece, they made accusations of misinformation. In an opinion piece, we made accusations of editorial bias. We extended an invitation to the Daily to write an open letter explaining their grievances, but they declined. A former editor of the Review wrote a letter to the Daily explaining his grievances, but they declined to publish it. Publicly, they claim Horowitz has agreed with them (he wrote them a letter saying so).

Forgive me for having told that story so many times, but it has become more elaborate with each telling--now it seems that it's over, and the Review can only disagree. The Horowitz story was important enough, however, to qualify for two of the top ten news stories of the year. The number one story of the year has to do with the cancellation of the Grape Boycott, which is the only other matter that makes me melancholy as I leave my post with the Review. For each of the two previous Novembers, I have covered the grape boycott for the Review. In the fall the boycott will be gone. Maybe the Progressive need not publish after all--what else is there to write about autumn quarter, if not grapes? The elections were last year, and Horowitz has had his day. We can only pray there are no more three-in-one protests, and Manley's American Dream is not taught until the Winter. So, all things considered, perhaps it is best that I exit right now.

But fear not! Mr. Scott Rasmussen is the editor-to-be, and he is a resourceful student of politics. He'll find something exciting to report on. He is certainly ambitious enough--I imagine his goal is nothing short of turning Stanford back into the hotbed of political activity it was when the Observer was still going to press. Mr. Rasmussen hopes to use the Review to create an invasion of controversy in 2001-2002 akin to that which we've had in 2000-2001.

For the final issue of this academic year, though, you will notice the third and final segment in a series on original sin, by Alec Rawls. It is not generally a topic that political journalists find themselves deliberating over, but Mr. Rawls has eloquently applied original sin to one of the most pressing social issues--education. In his previous essay, he addressed the relationship between original sin and socialized education, and in his current essay he talks about the role parents should play in their children's education and whether the state should be able to substantially limit that role.

In his very last appearance as a Stanford undegraduate, Michael Toth prepared a satire piece for the final page. In his trademark style, he tackles the question, "What has happened to Stanford?"

Enjoy this final issue. I will miss providing my editiorial comments very much, but please continue to read the Review, and watch for the Progressive.
