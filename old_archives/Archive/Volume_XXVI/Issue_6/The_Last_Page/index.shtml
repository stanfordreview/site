The Last Page
Stanford Rests In Peace
by Michael Toth
Editor Emeritus

Just wait a second, Uncle Leland... hold the decaf frappalatte...save the vita juice boost for later... put the veggie burger back on the grill a few more minutes... and no, I don't care for a non alcoholic glass of sparkling cider...it's important, I think I've missed something here. Was there a section in the freshman bulletin sent to my house three years ago that I forgot to peruse? Did I not get the message, or was I stuck programming my palm pilot? A memo went out, and I was busy hunting for internships, right? Or was my cell phone out of range?

Can somebody please z-write, e-mail, page, or instant message me a response to this simple question... when did college get so lame?

College students these days live a life that would have seemed completely foreign to a beer-soaked fraternity don from yesteryear. The typical pupil now begins his day shortly after dawn with a breakfast high in all the necessary vitamins and minerals, and rightfully so. Any dining hall in the year 2001 daring enough to load up its menu with bacon and cheese is liable to hear it from some concerned freshman who is worried he isn't getting enough fiber in his diet. Consequently, scoring a slab of real butter for your morning toast became as difficult as gaining admission to Harvard Medical School.

After breakfast it's off to the classroom for our budding laureates and jet-setters. But these classes aren't anything like those old yawners that promised gentlemanly C's to the poor lads who just couldn't find it in themselves to endure a single lecture's worth of Geology 101. In the third millennium the workaday assignments of your average student sounded like the groundwork for the next Manhattan Project. Nineteen year-olds busied themselves computing matrices and calculating laboratory results. Meanwhile, young humanists were no longer asked simply to read poetry, but to decode its cultural baggage and discover the existential message that lay beneath the text. Alongside each other in sparsely-lit cubicles tucked in the corners of crowded study halls, students of every background and interest tapped away into the night, building algorithms, and deconstructing literature. There was no time for beer bongs.

Dormitory rooms no longer resembled the squalid Filthadelphias that once raised many a youthful conscious to the plight of impoverished societies. When it came to adorning their walls students shied away from modern art Ð it was too disordered; likewise, religious imagery made them uneasy. Instead, it was pure Manet-Monetmania. By the year 2001 every college domicile came equipped with a bed, a desk, a dresser, and a late 19th century rendering of the English countryside. The Impressionists didn't portray loners or mavericks, rather they drew attention to leafy estates and docile children, summer cottages and lilies, respectably dressed men and women out for a Sunday stroll, people just like us, except they didn't have bananarepublic.com back then; or did they?

In the springtime of the new century, college students were fitter, happier, and more productive than they had been in years. They didn't smoke, and often didn't drink. They were quiet and polite, and when they did speak up they were aware as any corporate executive is of avoiding language that might be construed as insensitive or offensive. They scripted their weeks on electronic planners, and made time to see their acquaintances over coffee or a light dessert. It was no longer assumed that students would see one another at social gatherings. By the year 2001, double majoring, not double dating, scored you points with your peers.

Occasionally a student leader surfaced to rally his classmates around one revolutionary program or another. The mission was destined for failure. However much any university claimed it valued diversity, or having a student body that spoke its mind, a fact was a still a fact. At the end of the First Great American Century more than a few students came from identical homes with front lawns that must have been a foot deep. They shared their quarters with their parents and one sibling, two years older or younger. They saw how hard Mom and Dad worked making sure they always buckled their bike helmets and never watched any fireball movies until their homework was through. These were kids who drove nice cars, wore marked-up clothing (some even wore clothing that was so expensive it looked cheap, the hand-me-down chic look), kids who had never before shared a bathroom... and now some collegiate dreamer was supposed to convince them to ship off their golf clubs to Angola.

Not in the year 2001, not when all the things that had once captivated the youthful imagination, from ideology to art, revolution to romance, were passed over without a blink. Just look around, we're all moderates now.

Michael Toth is a graduating senior.
