Front Page
2000-2001: The Top Ten Stanford Review Stories
#1: UFW Ends Grape Boycott
Next October, The Stanford Review will have a very big problem. Every late October issue in recent memory has been devoted to the Grape Boycott. Last November, shortly after The Review covered that story for the very last time, the UFW cancelled the longstanding boycott, bringing to a halt the controversy surrounding it. Grape education programs at Stanford have now run their course, and the Review will have to bring a new issue to the pulpit. In some sense it was a victory for this publication that had opposed it for so long. But in another sense it was a sad passing of the issue which this publication had been so proud to oppose.

In memory of its opposition, The Review named Tommy Lee Woon Stanford's Person of the Year 2000-2001. The Review recognizes Woon for what he's done this year (grape education was just as prominent this year as any other) and for what he's done over the past several years. The Grape Boycott has been the most reported issue in The Review. It has been the only perennial story, and simultaneously one of the most controversial stories. President Gerhard Casper's decision to make the boycott an issue at Stanford would not have been nearly so successful without a qualified faculty member to perpetuate Casper's wish. None was ever more qualified to educate students on this issue than the Tommy Lee Woon. Student groups at Stanford have followed Woon's examples, albeit with a somewhat more biased approach, and have actively supported the boycott. Along with Woon, those Student groups created a sharp political climate to which The Stanford Review has always been able to react.

#2: The Gaieties Controversy
This year's Big Game Gaieties spawned a controversy that pitted sensitivity to minority cultures against a tradition of off-color comedy. In the performance, a brief portrayal of a Native American character prompted outrage among the Native American community on campus, ultimately resulting in the dismissal of the show's producer and the removal of the offending character from the final performance.

The first two shows, on Wednesday and Thursday night, included a scene in White Plaza where student actors portrayed six campus organizations: the Powwow, the Stanford Sheriffs, the Capital Improvement Plan, ROTC, the Harley Club, and the Viennese Ball. These characters pitched their respective causes and eventually formed a chorus line clearly reminiscent of the Village People.

The depiction of the Native American in this scene drew sharp criticism. Shortly after the Thursday performance, junior Travis Helms sent an email to several campus mailing lists in which he described the actor as a "blonde, white female," who "pantomimed 'Indian sign language,' the Sacred Pipe traditions, 'war whoops,' and pranced around the booth." He condemned the "blatant racism" implicit in this portrayal and argued that "because of their desire to have the 'Village People' in the production, they have mocked us, our traditions, our beliefs."

He also expressed concern that the degrading portrayal of Native Americans would prompt audience members to think twice about contributing to the annual Stanford Powwow.

Mr. Helms' complaint fueled outrage among Native Americans and other minority groups on campus. By the time Gaieties producer Patrick Crosetto returned to his room late Thursday night, he was already inundated with email messages from friends and new-found foes. Crosetto was eventually relieved from his position as producer. In future years, the Gaieties committee will have to okay the script with various campus student organizations.

#3: The Confederate Flag At Xanadu
Undoubtedly, it seemed like a good idea at the time.Resident assistants (RA's) traditionally prepare dorms for new residents by, among other things, marking every door with its future residents' names and by putting a sign near the entrance with the dorm's name. According to a silly and generally harmless tradition, these name tags and the dorm sign must be jazzed up in some cheesy way to present a coherent dorm decoration theme. For this year, Xanadu chose to include the confederate flag in their theme.

The Xanadu theme is based on "Dukes of Hazzard", a television show about the escapades of the Duke family of Hazzard County, in which they outwit and outdrive the clumsy, crooked county officials. Each resident's name is printed on a sheet alongside "General Lee," an orange car with a Confederate flag on its roof, and the central figure of "Dukes of Hazzard." The staff hung an orange banner with the "Xanadukes of Hazzard" outside the house. The controversy arose from the 'X' of "Xanadukes" - navy with white stars, like the cross of the Confederate flag.

After noticing the banner, ASSU Undergraduate Senator Edjah Nduom complained about it in an email sent to the Senate. Nduom's complaint helped stir up a campus wide controversy concerning the flag. Eventually, the residents of Xanadu decided to alter the sign, to make it appear less like the confederate flag.

#4: Horowitz and The Stanford Daily
The David Horowtiz fiasco hit Stanford somewhat later than most other college campuses this Spring. More than a month after Mr. Horowitz had been circulating his now infamous "Anti-Reparations" ad to colleges around the country, the Stanford Daily decided to publish Horowitz's advertisement as an opinion column. The Daily also included a response to the ad, authored by the Black Student Union. In part due to the Daily's treatment of the ad, it did not spark much controversy at Stanford. In comparison, students at Cal demanded that their daily newspaper, The Daily Californian, apologize for having printed the ad--the paper complied.

#5: Hewlett Gives 400 Million
The Hewlett Foundation has announced that it will give $400 million dollars to Stanford, the largest ever single donation to an American university. $300 million of the gift will go to the School of Humanities and Sciences. This gift is particularly welcome since, according to President Hennessy, only 12% of the current endowment is allocated to the School of Humanities and Sciences. Additionally, the gift consists of unrestricted funds, the type of charitable dollars the University values most, since it can use the resources where it deems them most necessary. The news of the Hewlett Foundation gift comes not long after reports that Stanford was the top recepient of giving for the year 2000, at $580 million, nearly $100 million ahead of Harvard, which came in second. Especially because Stanford's endowment is substanially less than several of its major competitors, including Harvard, Princeton, and Yale, the administration and Board of Trustees is ecstatic about the high giving rates.

#6: The Review and The Daily
The Stanford Daily and the Stanford Review debated the meaning of the word "reject" and the meaning of the word "advertisement" this Spring, in reference to David Horowitz's anti-reparations advertisement. During admit weekend, the Review published the headline, "The Ad The Daily Didn't Want You To See", referring to the ad. Many prospective students and parents viewed the headline and begin to question the Daily about what its intentions were. The Daily, pertrurbed by the Review's headline, thought that the Review might have defamatory intent and published an editorial saying so. The Review, perturbed by the Daily's editorial, thought that the Daily might have political intents, and published an opinion piece saying so. In the end, the Review claimed to not have intended to defame, and the Daily claimed not to have intended to be political. But the Daily came out unscathed. No one burned the paper, as happened at Brown, demanded a resignation, as happened at Wisconsin, or requested an apology, as happened at Berkeley.

#7: Campaign Contributions
Each election sees campaign collecting and spending climb to ever higher peaks, and the year 2000 was be no exception. At Stanford and nationwide an outpouring of gifts has fattened campaign bank accounts to record growth. As of October 1, the residents of the 94305 zip code had donated about $313,000 to various political causes, a figure that surpassed the same area's total giving in the 1996 presidential season by twofold and continued to swell through Election Day.

The 177 checks which comprise the total figure were written by professors, university staff, and the family members of both. Students chose to save, at least this election year. The recipients can be broken up into three main categories: candidates, parties, and Political Action Committees. Gifts totaling less than $200 are omitted since the Federal Elections Commission keeps no records on them.

The presidential candidates benefitted from dollars from members of the Stanford community.
The presidential candidates received about $58,000 in direct contributions from the residents of the Stanford zip code. Senator Bill Bradley raked in the largest share with $36,300 followed by Governor George W. Bush with $16,900 and lastly Vice-President Al Gore with $4,195. Former New York Knick Bill Bradley benefited from something of a home court advantage in the fundraising game, having served as visiting professor at Stanford in the year before the primary.

#8: Stanford and Bush
More than two years ago, George W. Bush began compiling a policy team of political and academic masterminds to advise his campaign, many of then from Stanford's own Hoover Institution. Now, while some of them are heavily involved in the current presidential administration, many others have decided to sit on the sidelines in the first Republican administration in eight years.

Condoleezza Rice was one of a handful of Stanford professors who joined the Bush Administration in 2001. John Taylor was another.
Members of the Hoover Institution first became involved in Bush's campaign more than two years ago, well before Bush had even decided that he was running. As the Review reported in a previous volume, Hoover Fellow George Schultz hosted a meeting at his house which included Hoover Fellows and Stanford professors John Taylor, Michael Boskin, Martin Anderson, and Condoleezza Rice.

Although many people from the Hoover Institute have advised George W. Bush—including some of the people closest to his campaign—surprisingly few will actually have jobs in his administration.

Perhaps the most notable person to leave Stanford for a job in the Bush administration is Condoleezza Rice, who was Stanford's provost for six years and a senior fellow at the Hoover Institution. She will be serving as Bush's national security advisor.

While Ms. Rice ultimately accepted a job as the national security advisor, many people who were closely involved with Bush's campaign do not wish to have a job in his administration. One such example is John Cogan, a renowned economist and senior fellow at the Hoover Institute and who teaches public policy at Stanford.

According to John Raisian, director of the Hoover Institute, "John Cogan was offered the position of the director of OMB (Office of Management and Budget) but declined because of family reasons." Mr. Raisian cited the fact that Mr. Cogan has six children as one of the primary reasons why he did not want to accept the job. Other members of the Stanford community to take jobs in the Bush administration included Taylor, who accepted the position of undersecretary of the treasury.

#9: The Language Merger
Students became vehement when the administration announced that it planned to merge six languages into one department. Six languages had previously existed independently, each with their own department, their own department chair and their own funding. This spring, the administration declared that it would be more efficient to make the six departments one, employ just one administrative chair and fund each of the language programs through one department.

Stanford undergraduates and graduates banned together to protest this merger, in writing, in vocal protest in White Plaza and in silent protest outside of the faculty senate meeting. Students complained about the merger because they felt that it would devalue each of the individual language programs. Students who focus on the humanities at Stanford have long complained that the university overlooks their programs and needs in favor of sciences and engineering. They have complained about inequities in funding in the past - some fear that the administration is paying less attention to some departments on all levels and that the departments are therefore in danger of withering away. Many were afraid that the proposed merger was another symptom of the university's neglect.

Another complaint had to do with the tension the merger would ostensibly create among the several departments. A graduate student who helped lead the protests noted that the merger would create five new faculty positions, though there were six departments. This left an open-ended, and very difficult question, the student warned, of who would be left out. Still other students complained that the merger denied the unique culture of each language to group them with different languages. Academic concerns, these students argued, ought always come before administrative concerns.

#10: Racist Graffiti Discovered
Early in the Spring, some Stanford students discovered some racist graffiti on campus. The graffiti, the students complained, contained language that was offensive to women, blacks, Asians, and to minority groups in general.

The students who led protests against the University's alleged mistreatment of this problem were upset were a variety of reasons. First, they were upset by the graffiti itself, that it could ever come to exist at Stanford. One student who tried to raise awareness of the problem pronounced that it was not only offensive to the targeted minorities, but rather it was an "affront to us all".

Second, students were disturbed by how the University and the Stanford police department handled the problem. The University recognized the problem and dealt with it quietly. However, according to many students, the University attempted to sweep the problem under the rug. The officials involved argued that it would not be productive to announce the incident to the entire school community and raise unnecessary fears and demonstrations. Student groups disagreed, arguing that the only way to deal with this problem was to raise campus wide awareness.

Students who were personally offended by the graffiti were also upset that some of their fellow students didn't seem to take notice of the problem. A columnist for the Daily criticized the Stanford community for not reacting severely enough, whereas a columnist for the Review criticized the student groups for reacting too severely.

The Review's own Jeffrey Davidson authored a column on the matter. In it he explained that it would be silly for the University to react too intensively because it was an isolated incident, because it represents the "inchoate musings" of one misguided member of the community, and because it doesn't pose a very immediate threat to any of us. However, many students would be inclined to disagree with Mr. Davidson.

Some disagreed so much, in fact, that they referred to the graffiti as hate crimes, thereby tacitly placing the offense in a category that does deserve, in their view, more serious attention.
