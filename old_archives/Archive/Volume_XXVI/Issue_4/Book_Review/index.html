Book Review
Glen Campbell Chronicles Hoover's Growth
by Henry Towsner
Editorial Staff

As Director of Stanford University's Hoover Institution from 1960 until 1989, W. Glenn Campbell receives much of the credit for building the Institution into what former President Ronald Reagan called the "'brightest star' in the constellation of think tanks." Perhaps then it is fitting that his book, The Competition of Ideas, reads like the archives he spent thirty years tending.

An astonishing amount of his nearly four hundred pages are spent detailing the fund raising efforts of the Institution in astonishing and often tedious detail. Mr. Campbell insists not only on mentioning what appears to be every major donation made over a thirty year period, but discussing most of them in some depth, including who made the donation, when, why, and what Mr. Campbell and his colleagues had done to persuade the donor.

Even when not writing about finances, Mr. Campbell often shows an unwillingness to state his own opinion which, while admirable in a scholar, is unfortunate in an author writing for public consumption. Instead, he turns to numbers, facts, and extensive (not to mention occasionally excessive) quotations. The numerous letters and statements from other people, usually compliments directed toward Mr. Campbell or the Hoover Institution, while interesting at first, rapidly become repetitive. Similarly, the section on his youth is surprisingly dry, giving little feeling for his life, and one odd section consists of three pages devoted primarily to a listing of awards he won in elementary school (including "I had the best baby beef steer at the Ilderton Fair . . . my partner . . . and I won the Inter-County Trophy for judging beef cattle . . . we also won a medal for judging swine").

Mr. Campbell's book often feels like it is really three different books which have been fused together. One is akin to the books about management often put out by corporate CEO's: a sort of instruction manual on how a think tank ought to be run. The second book is the story of W. Glenn Campbell, and his life before, during, and after his tenure as director of the Hoover Institution. Unfortunately, the majority of The Competition of Ideas is devoted to the third book: the financial records of the Hoover Institution.

If you are willing to sift through the tedious portions, the remainder of the book is far more interesting. The nominal subject is how the Mr. Campbell built the Hoover Institution up to its current status, and the battles he fought with the University to do it. The portions devoted to that are usually quite absorbing. Indeed, their only flaw is that Mr. Campbell did not go into more detail; too often a long segment listing one donor after another is interrupted for a short piece discussing some attack by the faculty or president of Stanford on the operations of the Institution. Because these portions seem to be an afterthought, it is hard to get a sense of why things are really going on. A typical example is when Trustee David Packard threatens to fire Mr. Campbell; since up to that time Mr. Packard had always been presented as a supporter of Mr. Campbell, it is unclear what made him change his mind.

The most interesting parts of the book, however, are those in which Mr. Campbell's style lightens up a bit and he presents pithy, often amusing comments about politics and society. Typical examples are the remark repeated on the cover of the book, "Business men also suffer from a real disadvantage in dealing with professors . . . they have full-time jobs, employees, stockholders, and directors to satisfy, while tenured professors can spend much of their time scheming." Or referring to his two terms on the University of California Board of Regeants, "My first term required no state senate confirmation ('to keep politics out of the system') . . . [for my] second term…I did have to seek state senate confirmation (also 'to keep politics out of the system')."

The Competition of Ideas is, in the end, a disappointing book, not because it is not worth reading, but because the parts that are worth reading make it clear how much more there could be. Nonetheless, its weaknesses make it hard to justify except for those who are particularly interested in the subject.

Henry Towsner's father is a Massachusetts Democrat.
