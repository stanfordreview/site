Opinion
University Not A Political Institution
by Jeff Davidson
Contributing Writer

If you look closely at a Stanford athlete at work, you will notice a little mark on each article of clothing s/he is wearing. That mark is the Nike swoosh. The reason all of our athletes wear the swoosh is that Nike pays for all of our athletes' clothing. So wait, we get thousands and thousands of dollars worth of free athletic gear, and in return, all we need is to deal with a little check mark that comes on those thousands of dollars in gear? Only among Stanford liberals could this seem like a bad deal.

Yet, in the midst of contract negotiations for the renewal of the Nike-Stanford arrangement, a furor brews. A group of students and faculty have put pressure on the administration to create an "opt out" clause, so that individual athletes who don't like Nike can get uniforms without the logo. Such a clause is entirely reasonable and the administration has promised that it will be included. Many, however, have requested that Stanford withdraw from the contract altogether, so that Stanford might be free from the stain of association with the evil Nike empire. This request is wrong for many reasons.

First of all, the university should not be in the business of making political statements. Any statement made will no doubt offend some part of the Stanford community, and there's no very good reason for Stanford to make such statements. Withdrawing from this contract, which carries no cost to Stanford, is clearly a political act. Staying in the contract doesn't constitute an endorsement of Nike's labor practices, only an endorsement of Nike's practice of giving away free stuff to universities.

Taking a check from people doesn't mean you like them. If everyone stopped accepting money from people they dislike, the United States would grind to a halt. I have a hard time seeing how taking a welfare check from the government is the same as endorsing our foreign policy toward Nigeria. I have a hard time seeing how taking clothing from Nike is the same as endorsing child labor.

But I will go further, because I don't believe that Nike is evil. A recent letter to the Daily makes the following screed against the company-- "Nike, for example, has a well-documented record of human rights abuses in the past. These include the employment of child labor, the use of toxic elements in shoe manufacture, the exploitation of Third World workers (all conveniently subcontracted), inadequate wages, sexual harassment (most of the sewers, stitchers and gluers are young women), forced overtime, physical abuse and firing union organizers." The conclusion is basically that people, and the University, ought not buy from Nike.

I sympathize with the plight of workers in the Third World. But let's remember that the alternative to working for Nike is not being a software engineer on Page Mill, driving a Porsche and taking long lunch breaks at Spago. Developing nations are poor, and foreign direct investment often provides the highest wages available to unskilled workers. If Nike leaves a country; the country becomes poorer. There are a few valid complaints above--Nike shouldn't physically abuse its workers, use toxic materials, or sexually harass young women. But complaints about low wages, child labor, overtime, and unions fundamentally ignore the economics of developing nations. Frequently, all those nations have to offer (in terms of productive factors) is inexpensive labor, which they can use to export to the developed world. Those exports bring in capital that can be used to build domestic industry, one day resulting in development and affluence. Pressuring Nike and others to change their ways robs the Third World of the best chance it has to remove itself from oppressive poverty.

Child labor, for instance, has been eliminated in the developed part of the world. But child labor in the Third World is a financial necessity for some families. The average citizen of the Third World lives in desperate poverty. The best wages tend to exist working for foreign companies in cities. Adults, tied to rural communities by land and tradition, can't really take advantage of those jobs. Children, with greater mobility, often take the jobs that their parents can't, providing for their entire families. Take away Nike, and Nike's child workers turn to unproductive jobs in the country, look for scarce work in the city, or turn to crime or prostitution. Not rosy alternatives, compared with relatively plush employment at Nike. The liberal conception of what Nike does makes no sense if we look at what would happen if Nike didn't exist.

Stanford should not send political messages. But if we do, the message should be a word of encouragement to Nike, thanking them for helping Third World countries develop so that real alternatives to dire poverty will one day exist.

Jeff Davidson, a sophomore, is majoring in political science.
