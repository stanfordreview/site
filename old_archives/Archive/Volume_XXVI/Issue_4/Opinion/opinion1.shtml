Opinion
Re-Thinking The Alcohol Policy
by Mike Fainelli
Contributing Writer

In the sumptuous warmth of the springtime evenings, the Stanford party scene reaches its annual prime. Throngs of revelers besiege the outdoor kegs and lap up the beer as fast as it will pour. Before the peak season arrives we might do well to revisit the policy which supposes to govern the drinking at these festivities, the alcohol policy.

Alas, it is the driest of subjects. However, under the influence of a stiff drink, one can appreciate a certain out-of-wack humor in official statements like "the unlawful use, distribution or possession of alcohol is prohibited on the Stanford campus." So before venturing any further into this discourse, you are advised to reach for the strong stuff and make a toast to the enlightened utopia that is Stanford University. Shall we say "Let the Winds of Freedom Blow?"

The official policy, printed verbatim in the back of every course bulletin, is a master piece of doublespeak and doublethink. Every clause can be broken down into three components: 1) what is said and meant 2) what is said and not meant and 3) what is meant but not said. The confused stance owes to the balancing act the University must sustain in the middle ground between two opposing forces--the puritanical under-21 law on the one hand and the thirst for alcohol felt by the majority of human beings (even the underage) on the other. Even while squirming between the de facto and the de jure, the university must take measures to protect itself from a potential lawsuit in case one of us drinks too much and dies. The resulting policy sets in motion an incredible legalistic charade.

For example, we have the prohibition that dorm funds cannot be used to buy alcohol. All at once it's a wink to thirsty students, a nod to the police and a veritable bum cover. Dorm funds are used to buy everything but the alcohol: the DJ, the cups, the cocktail juice and, perhaps, even the margarita mix. The stage is set for drinking on the University's tab. Then, the dorm's most enthusiastic drinkers go around with a hat mooching aggressively for beer money, perhaps skimming some off the top for services rendered. This rag-tag Mafia uses the leftover money to buy an excessive number of kegs.

At this juncture, a thoughtful policy would allow the resident assistants or resident fellows to intervene and use dorm funds to buy a sensible amount of alcohol. But as employees of the University, they could get fired for doing so. Instead, the hands-off policy pushes the control over alcohol purchases outside the reach of the University and into the hands of a freshmen keg Mafia.

Amazingly, freshmen dorms are able to register keg parties with the Office of Student Activities, which for its part, hands out a few dozen ID bracelets and orders the underage party-planners to check identification at the door of their underage dorm. In this way the liability for that dreaded catastrophe of a dead student is thrown around like a hot potato among the powers that be.

What's really scary is the possibility that the Stanford alcohol policy might one day work as advertised, and completely dry up the freshmen dorms. In this case, the demand for alcohol would merely spill over into the fraternity houses, where freshmen can get lost and drunk simultaneously and then pass out, half a campus away from home. But at least our bank account is untouchable. In the event of a lawsuit, Vice Provost for Student Affairs James Montoya can take the stand, point to the frat boys and testify with conviction that it was they not us, who paid for the beer, and they, not us, who poured it. Cover my bum, that's the important thing!

History teaches that the harder a government stuffs the cork into the bottle, the more likely it will explode off in violent and perverse ways. At Stanford the tension is building. At the onset of the academic year, the administration ratcheted up the strictness of the alcohol policy by including four class dorms under the clause prohibiting dorms from using their funds to purchase alcohol.

A greater reserve of courage would propel the University policymakers to adopt a more laissez-faire set of alcohol rules and to take a public stand against the silly under-21 law. But this is asking too much of the Stanford bureaucracy. It is still reeling from the echoes of the lawsuit that slammed the Massachusetts Institute of Technology after the drinking death of a freshman in 1997.

And, so, with the introduction of this year's stricter alcohol policy, the winds of fear and happenstance, not the winds of freedom, are blowing Stanford closer to under-21 conformity.

Mike Fainelli, a senior, will graduate in June.
