The Feminist Case Against Abortion
by Clayton Brown
Contributing Writer

Serrin Foster, president of Feminist for Life, spoke before a Stanford audience of fifty in the Oak Room of Tressider on April 25th. She was sponsored by the Stanford Students for Life, with assistance from the Christian Legal Society, the ASSU, and the Speakers Bureau.

Foster has been active in championing the needs of pregnant women for over 17 years, and since 1994 she has served as executive director of Feminists for Life of America. Foster has a distinguished career as a speaker. She debated the governor of Massachusetts at the 1996 Republican Convention, she was the featured speaker of the first joint pro-life-pro-choice convention, and she participated in a panel discussion at Harvard University at the invitation of former Senator Allan Simpson. The mission of her organization is to institute practical solutions for the social deficiencies that lead to abortions. The group is responsible for several acts of legislation before Congress, and seeks to act as the link between pro-life and feminist interest groups.

Foster began by giving the crowd an interesting perspective on the history of feminism. She lionized several pro-life women, such as Elizabeth Cady Stanton, who contributed to the feminist movement. As she described the historical roots of her organization, Foster smiled and quipped, "Women are most like God because they can give life."

Foster’s unique position as both a pro-life advocate and a feminist gives her significant power in the political realm, but she focused her talk mostly on the prevalence of abortions on college campuses. She "challenges students to question abortion and asks college and university administrators to provide resources for pregnant and parenting students." Foster noted we rarely see pregnant students on campus. The reason: "one out of every five abortions is performed on a college woman."

According to Foster, Feminist for Life "serves as a catalyst for developing resources and progressive solutions for pregnant and parenting students by asking women what they really want—because women deserve better." She urged women on campus to press the university to better accommodate pregnant women. She suggests that a more lenient academic policy or housing and financial benefits may make the experience of pregnancy easier for women. If we can make unplanned pregnancy an "unplanned joy," she argues, we can reduce the number of abortions.

Before opening the floor to questions, Foster concluded her speech with a rallying call, "It is time for women to concentrate on the right to be themselves in the workplace, at school, and at home. I hope you will join us." The audience rewarded her with an enthusiastic applause.

Clayton Brown is a freshman.
