A Word From The Editor
On May 3, The Stanford Daily published an editorial, partly in response to a headline the Review had published two weeks earlier. The Review had published, on April 18, David Horowitz's anti-reparations ad accompanied by the headline, "The Ad The Daily Didn't Want You To See". The Daily's editorial on May 3 argued that the Review's headline was factually unreliable, and that it might be designed to undermine the Daily's credibility. I hate to quibble, but I must respond.

First, the Daily suggests that the Review was operating on a "questionable factual basis". To clear up the matter, the Review received its information from two sources. The Review viewed the information on the website of David Horowitz's publication, Front Page Magazine (www.frontpagemagazine.com and the Review spoke with a source at Horowitz's office. Both sources indicated that the Daily rejected the ad. Thus, our information was both public and reliable.

However, the Daily's editorial goes further and suggests that the Review's headline would be correct only under a certain interpretation of the word "ad". At the Review, we never have any intention of redefining the English language, but we implore all campus publications to be accurate with their facts. When the Daily was offered the Horowitz ad, they chose not to publish it. Rather, they opted to publish the information contained therein in conjunction with a response from the Black Student Union.

Whether or not that in itself is a form of censorship, and therefore much different from publishing the ad on its own as a paid advertisement, is debatable. The facts, however, seem clear. The Stanford Daily rejected the advertisement, in favor of an opinion column. When the Review published its headline, the campus had heard hardly anything about the Horowitz issue, and more than a month after it had become prominent nationwide. It seems the Daily hadn't yet wanted the campus to see the ad. Never, in its April 18 issue, did the Review speculate about the future of the Daily's involvement with the issue.

But the editorial in the Daily continued with its accusations. The editorial proclaims, "we ought to deal in truth and clarity, not semantics and sensationalism." The editorial questions the timing of the headline, pointing out that it was published during Admit weekend. If the Review indeed believes in the accuracy and value of its headlines (and I assure you that it always does, its Horowitz headline included) then why shouldn't it publish them during Admit Weekend, when the population of the campus rises significantly and potential readership increases? If the Review designs its headlines to target a specific population that it thinks is susceptible to deceit, then it would be disingenuous to publish them at all. In fact, the Review's purpose is to inform, not to deceive. In issues of timing, the Review takes into account only how it might attain maximum readership.

However, the Daily also suggests that the headline was sensationalistic, as if it were not grounded in real value. If the timing of a publication's headlines should be designed to attract a wide audience, then so should the wording of its headlines. If the Daily accused the Review of publishing a headline that was direct, focused, and even biting, I wouldn't argue. If it accused the Review of designing its headline to both inform and excite, I wouldn't argue. If it accused the Review of trying to arouse its readers political instincts and take notice of a controversial issue, I wouldn't argue. That's not sensationalism.

But what I find most distressing is the Daily's accusation that the Review would want to undermine the Daily's credibility. First, the Review has no interest in undermining the credibility of The Stanford Daily--what would the Review gain? The Daily and the Review do not compete for readership. Most likely, the two newspapers appeal to altogether different bases of readers. But more to the point, the two newspapers address much different issues, and from wholly different perspectives. Surely, one would not find it redundant to read both newspapers. Undermining the Daily would not further the Review's cause.

In fact, it would likely hurt the Review. Though The Stanford Review and The Stanford Daily are not competing newspapers, they do co-exist, and each paper stands to gain from the other's critiques. The Daily has certainly made political commentary on the Review's subject matter in the past, if not on its very existence. While bickering will help no one, constructive criticism will help both papers to sharpen their focus. For the Review to destroy its relationship with the Daily by needlessly undermining the Daily's credibility would set a thoroughly useless precedent, and it would make it much more difficult for the Review and the Daily to feed off of each other. No organization, regardless of size or quality can possibly make progress in the absence of extensive and constant external examination. The Daily is indeed better off for having the Review right next door.

If our friends at the Daily were to read this issue, they would discover a compelling collection of commentary and thought. You'll notice that contributing writer Alec Rawls returns from a one issue hiatus with a piece on original sin. If you are intrigued with the subject, or only with Mr. Rawls' take on the subject, you may read more by Mr. Rawls' on moral theory and religion in upcoming issues.

You'll find a book review of an anticipated new history by former director of the Hoover Institution, Glenn Campbell. Mr. Campbell chronicles the rise of the Hoover Institution from its more obscure, earlier days through the Reagan administration and its rise to prominence. Henry Towsner assesses how well he does.

You'll find opinion pieces by more recent contributors to the Review. Mike Fainelli addresses Stanford's current alcohol policy, while Jeffrey Davidson talks about the athletic department and its relationship with Nike.

Finally, you can read about Serrin Foster, the feminist who opposes abortion, and the talk she gave at Stanford. Enjoy the issue.
