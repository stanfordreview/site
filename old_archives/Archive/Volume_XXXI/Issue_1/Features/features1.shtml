McClintock Conservatism
by Joseph Fairbanks
Senior News Editor

When the list of candidates to replace Governor Gray Davis was finalized no one predicted that such a great amount of the attention would focus on two Republicans vying for the party's backing. But as the frontrunners emerged, so did the divide in the Republican Party's support for its candidates. The party would be forced to choose between a tried and true conservative in Tom McClintock or a political, but very popular novice in Arnold Schwarzenneger.

Over the last few weeks before the Recall election, McClintock's support has steadily been rising as more Californians hear his message and approve of his performance in the debates. But even as his standing rises in the polls, so does that of his only viable Republican rival. With a surge in television and radio advertising, along with the attention that comes with the debates, McClintock has emerged as the true conservative choice, contrasting himself with Schwarzenneger on many issues. He prides himself on record, consistently voting pro-life, anti-tax, anti-big government, and pro-death penalty.

McClintock's political record is an impressive one.

He was first elected to the California legislature in 1982 at age 26one of the youngest state-wide office holders in the country and within two years became the Assembly Republican Whip, a top leadership position.

In 1984, he was named one of five outstanding young Californians by the California Jaycees, and received the civilian Medal of Merit from the Ventura County Peace Officers Association for rescuing an elderly man from a condominium fire.

During his tenure in the State Assembly he became a favorite of Republicans across the state when he authored the Mello-Condit-McClintock Tax Rebate Act, which returned $1.1 billion of tax over-collections to the taxpayers of California. Additionally, he was the author of California's current lethal injection death penalty law.

After two failed attempts to be elected to higher office, McClintock was named Director of Economic and Regulatory Affairs for the Claremont Institute's Golden State Center for Policy Studies. In 1996 he opted to return to the State Assembly, easily winning his seat and holding it for two more terms before being elected to the State Senate in 2000.

When he entered the Recall race, McClintock promised to draw upon his experience in the Assembly and the Senate to help clean up the corruption and government waste plaguing Sacramento under the leadership of Gray Davis and his Lieutenant Governor, Cruz Bustamante. He has pledged to stop the car taxâ€”a tax Governor Davis recently tripled by way of Executive Order, bypassing the State Legislature. The cost of Davis's tax increase is expected to raise the tax burden on the average family by $460 per year, according to McClintock.

"The state's problems are not a shortage of revenue. After the car tax was reduced and after the dot-com collapse, and after the state's revenues plunged, we're still taking in 28 percent more revenue than four years ago while the population and inflation combined have grown only 21 percent, yet Davis has increased spending by 40%," says McClintock, as evidence that the tax increase is unnecessary.

McClintock also wishes to void the energy contracts negotiated by Davis. Davis has been accused of costing Californians billions of dollars during the energy crisis by asking his chief negotiator to negotiate what many felt were massively overpriced energy contracts. Further controversy came when it was revealed that Davis's chief negotiator was a consultant for the energy industry, presenting a clear conflict of interest. According to his website, McClintock has pledged to "immediately sign a stipulation for the court case seeking to void those contracts, admitting a conflict of interest, allowing the court to void those contracts and save the people of California a small fortune."

McClintock has also pledged to ask legislature to pass bill similar to Arizona's worker's compensation law, slashing worker's comp costs by 2/3 and reversing the flow of jobs across the state line. If the legislature doesn't act, McClintock says, he will take the issue to a state-wide ballot.

Trumpeting these messages, McClintock quickly has risen in the polls from the low single digits to an impressive double digit standing evidence of the peoples' positive reaction to his plans for reviving California's economy. His sudden surge in support also indicates an unwillingness for some Republican loyalists to trust an untested Schwarzenneger.

As the election has draws closer, McClintock refuses to yield to the calls of his fellow Republicans to withdraw from the race, which would virtual guarantee a Republican victory. Some suggest McClintock's unwillingness to drop out is meant as a snub to the California Republican Party, which has been reluctant to come to his aid not only during the Recall, but also during his past Senatorial campaign. Its reluctance of the leadership to support him has not impaired his ability to become a political star, however. He has been described as an up-and-coming national figure and mentioned as a possible candidate to replace Senator Barbara Boxer.
