Davis' Failures Led to the Recall
by Chris Fish
Senior News Staff

Between the court challenges over the election date, the circus of candidates, and the partisan bickering, it's easy to forget the central issue of this recall: Gray Davis. California's first ever recall only comes as a response to the uniform dissatisfaction with Davis's performance. So let's take a moment to reflect on just what Davis has done to earn his place in the record books for having the number one lowest approval rating of any California governor.

Perhaps the best place to start is fundraising. After intense lobbying in early 2002, Davis signed into law a bill increasing the pay to the state's prison guard union by 34 percent. A few weeks thereafter, Davis received a generous donation along the lines of $251,000 to his campaign war-chest. Some may consider these two events unrelated, but the California electorate did not. GOP chairman Shawn Steel claims the increased pay will cost the state approximately a billion dollars in the next six years.

Another controversy that cost Davis precious approval points in the polls came about after a flap with the California Teacher's Association. In May 2002, CTA President Wayne Johnson came forward claiming that during a policy discussion meeting with the Governor a request was made that the group donate to Davis's campaign. After refusing the governor's request multiple times, Wayne was informed he may not get the support for expanded collective bargaining rights pertaining to curricula that his group was looking for.

Let us not forget the never before seen consecutive brown-outs that plagued California under Davis's governorship. After supporting an energy policy that makes it nearly impossible to build new power plants in California, Davis could do little but tell Californians the best way to stop the power outages was to not use as much electricity. Realizing this strategy was dubious at best, the Governor looked for a quick fix to a big problem. His solution: he directed energy traders to purchase energy at well-above market rates, and signed off on vastly overvalued energy contracts totaling over 42 billion dollars. Not only did these contracts give California more energy than it needed, it also gave the state the biggest budget deficit it has ever had.

Rather then come forward and admit to California that serious budgetary problems lie ahead, Davis hid the state's financial problem until after his reelection was assured. In Davis's first four years in office, revenues increased by 25 percent. Rather than saving this revenue, Davis squandered the surplus on new spending measures, and in doing so increased the state's general fund by over 40 percent. It doesn't take an expert accountant to realize this is not sound fiscal leadership. Yet when conservative opponent Bill Simon predicted a 20 billion dollar budget deficit, Davis claimed that those Republicans were just plain "wrong." Just weeks after the November election in which Davis narrowly won his reelection, the State's Legislative Analyst predicted a 21 billion dollar shortfall. A month afterward, Davis came forward admitting the state deficit might be even higher. After all the numbers were tallied, Californians were the last to learn the state was looking at something more like a 38 billion dollar deficit.

Realizing the deficit was destroying the little remaining confidence that California still held for him, Davis went about looking for a solution to the budgetary crisis he had created. But rather than admitting previous mistakes and attempting to roll back some of the bureaucracy he had added just a few years earlier, he had an even better idea: more taxes. The only problem was there were quite a few Republicans in the state legislature that weren't too keen on this idea. Rather than get boggled down in the legislature, Davis decided to just fix the problem himself. Thus in June 2003, he tripled the state Car Tax by executive order. California law allows the state controller to increase the car tax on a month to month basis only when cash is needed to reimburse local governments for lost revenue. Not only is Davis not the state controller, but the above condition necessary for a lawful increase in the car tax is not being met. The result: according the Department of Motor Vehicles the average Californian now pays the state $390 each year for the right to own a car.

Amidst the chaos and confusion that has characterized this recall, it becomes increasingly easy to forget what brought this about in the first place. Contrary to what some Democrats may claim, the California recall is not a right-wing attempt to take over the California government. It is merely an unprecedented response to a set of unprecedented mistakes and miscalculations on the behalf of our Governor.
