Professors, Community Leaders Discuss Recall
by Harrison Y. Osaki
News Events Editor

Stanford In Government (SIG), a student group that promotes political involvement on campus, held an informational panel of Stanford professors and Hoover fellows on the California Recall on Monday, September 29th. The panel, which was entitled "Recall-ifornia," was designed to inform students about the recall and to allow students to question their professors on what consequences the recall has for California and democracy in general. In general, the professors expressed a common belief that the recall is symptomatic of an overall problem with the political system, but expressed optimism for the theory behind the recall.

Five of the six advertised panelists were in attendance- David Brady of the Public Policy Program and a Hoover Fellow; Shanto Iyengar of the Department of Communication; Roger Noll of the Public Policy Program; Tom McEnery, former Mayor of San Jose for twelve years; and Quentin Kopp, a California Superior Court Judge. Morris Fiorina of the Political Science Department did not make the event. Roughly twenty to thirty people attended the panel, which was held in the Cypress Room in Tresidder.

The moderator Neil King, a Hoover Media Fellow, opened the panel by asking the professors whether they felt the recall was a "bold new democratic experiment" for California. Professor Brady began the responses with the statement that the recall was neither a "right-wing conspiracy", nor "bold", nor an "experiment"; instead, Brady said, the true results of the recall would have to be seen in hindsight, after the completion of the election. Former Mayor McEnery followed up Brady's statements with a brief explanation of the history of the recall and its roots in the Progressive Movement and Hiram Johnson in the early 1900's. McEnery characterized this recall as a "grassroots revolt," and pointed out that a great number of common people were participating in the recall election, which was unusual for California. For that reason, McEnery felt that the recall was an experiment.

Professor Noll opened his comments with the statement that individual voters do not usually waste their time on learning issues because their votes are, compared to the overall total, worthless. He felt that the central problem of California's economic situation part of the impetus behind the recall was created in part by voters, who are contradictory in that they like both tax cuts and spending money on initiatives. According to Noll, when Proposition 53 passes, 90% of the California budget will be determined by interest on debt, spending on initiatives, or federal mandates. Noll also cited the California energy crisis and worker's compensation issues as issues behind the recall, but unlike McEnery, Noll felt that the people behind the recall were "taking advantage of voters' ignorance." Professor Iyengar, who followed Noll, questioned whether Arnold Schwarzenegger could win a normal primary if he had to run for governor in a typical election.

Former Mayor McEnery, in conjunction with Judge Kopp, expressed the belief that the problems with the government in California began at the top. McEnery said that people such as the Fiduciary Committee have betrayed their constituents, and cited several examples where the Republican and Democratic parties have actively stopped initiatives using money that would be baneful for both parties. Kopp talked about his own experience in creating a bill to prevent over-collecting of money for political campaigns, and the problems he faced in doing so.

The panel then took questions from the audience. The first question asked how the recall would change the political system, to which the panel members generally said that it would not result in significant change. In general, the panel agreed that the governorship of California is a fairly weak position. Noll commented that the governor gets some control over monetary funds, but has little political power. Kopp disagreed with the rest of the panel, however, in that he believed that the governor has power through leadership, which he felt Gray Davis has not provided. Kopp pointed out several instances where he felt Gray Davis had demonstrated leadership, such as in 1999 where Davis made clear that he expected judges to work with him, and also pointed out that the governor has power over redistricting.

Noll and McEnery then had a discussion on who was responsible for the current budget crisis, with Noll expressing his belief that the current system was hopeless because the legislation, backed by special interests, has been primarily responsible for the budget crisis. McEnery did not think the system was hopeless, although it did required repair.

The topic of the conversation then turned to Arnold Schwarzenegger, as an audience member asked whether or not Schwarzenegger would have the ability to effectively govern, and whether the structural problems that Gray Davis was being blamed for were his own fault or the fault of his predecessors. The professors were generally in agreement that the success of the new governor would be to some degree out of his control.

The next question concerned the consequences of having recalls of weak governmental officials, on which the professors generally replied in concurrence that the political consulting business over-benefited from recalls and the initiative system. While mostly in agreement (to varying degrees) that the initiative system was beneficial for democracy and California in general, McEnery's statement that the "incestuous relationship" between political consultants, politicians, and special interests was responsible for many of California's problems was characteristic of the group. In particular. McEnery cited the relationship between Cruz Bustamante, Indian Casino groups, and a particular consultant that he (McEnery) had worked with.

The professors were also asked what the consequences would be on a national stage. Noll commented that California, due to low voter turnouts, was a fairly easy target for recalls and thus did not have anything necessarily to do with national politics or conspiracies.

Noll ended the session by castigating the state assembly for being out of touch with the populace. He commented that there was "bipartisan chaos" in the assembly, and that the lack of institutional memory meant that few reforms or bills helpful to California would get past career bureaucrats.
