The News in Brief
by Harrison Y. Osaki

Stanford Dining Revamps Tresidder

Stanford Dining has added new options to Tresidder Union for hungry students. After taking control of the Tresidder dining area from Bon Appetit last year, Stanford Dining has decided to bring in more outside restaurant chains in response to student dining interests. A few of the restaurants under consideration for contracts included In-N-Out Burger, Pizza Hut, and Subway. In-N-Out eventually was rejected by Stanford Dining and the Tresidder Dining Advisory Group due to In-N-Out's demands; which included a stipulation that In-N-Out must own the property on which it is located. Dining Services' investigation of vendors was conducted during the summer and under time and financial constraints. The first two restaurants will be Peet's Coffee and Subway, which will open in October. Meanwhile, other restaurants, including sushi, pizza, and possibly Indian food, will be added later. Dining Services has stated that it will flexibly change the vendors in order to best answer student requests.

Students with meal plans that include points may apply up to 50 points per quarter toward these dining options. Dining Services has also modified the CoHo's menu and dining areas, such as by eliminating the Starbucks products with Peet's Coffee and adding furniture. Dining Services has also expressed interest in improving the air conditioning and adding wireless internet access in Tresidder. As part of an agreement that Stanford University made regarding the deal, former Bon Appetit employees were given preference by Dining Services for the new Tresidder positions, and virtually all of the former Bon Appetit staff has been rehired.

Noted Hoover Fellow Passes Away

Edward Teller, a Senior Hoover Research Fellow at the Hoover Institution on Stanford campus, died on September 9, 2003. Dr. Teller's particular fields of expertise were in defense and energy policy for national and international concerns. Dr. Teller played an important role in the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos during the development of the atomic bomb and the first atomic nuclear reactor. He became a professor of physics at the University of Chicago in 1946, but eventually returned to Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory three years later. Dr. Teller was the first chairman of the Nuclear Reaction Safeguard committee. He was a strong proponent of the research surrounding the development of the hydrogen bomb and firmly believed in a strong national defense as a deterrent to the Soviet Union's military threat. He later became an advocate of the Strategic Defense Initiative (also known as Star Wars). Dr. Teller's peers occasionally criticized him, as his vigorous belief in the continual need to increase American military power conflicted with the more pacifistic beliefs of some of his colleagues. While at Los Alamos a rift developed between Dr. Teller and his fellow scientists; Dr. Teller believed that the other scientists were too hesitant to develop nuclear weaponry. The antagonistic relationship with many of his peers when the nuclear physicist Robert Oppenheimer, a prominent anti-nuclear weapon advocate, had his security clearance revoked for national security reasons; Dr. Teller was held by many people to have been responsible the event, although Dr. Teller did not make any accusations himself. Dr. Teller was also a prominent advocate of fusion power as an alternative energy source. He was 95 when he died.

Stanford Bookstore Computers Temporarily Offline

The Stanford Bookstore's computers were taken temporarily offline from September 15 to September 17. A corrupted file was the apparent cause of the disturbance, which forced the Bookstore to use backup files for their services from the 17th to the 20th. Bookstore employees were forced to write receipts out by hand. The crash inconvenienced many students who were buying books for the then-immediate beginning of the quarter, and many tourists were visiting Stanford as well. This event has happened before on a similar date, which the Bookstore also repaired.

University Nixes New Laundry Plan

A much-anticipated new flat-rate laundry policy has been rejected by Stanford University in favor of its old coin-operated Laundromats. The decision not to use the policy, in which students would be charged a flat $28 per quarter to do laundry to receive unlimited access to dorm washing machines, was based on a series of complaints and concerns that Undergraduate Housing Operations received from students at the end of Spring Quarter 2003. The ASSU proposed the system, which would have gone into effect this year, at the end of Spring Quarter 2003. The program was supposed to address rising Laundromat costs and reduce the burden on students for laundry. However, since the ASSU's proposal arrived at the end of Spring Quarter, there was not enough time for housing to address the many student concerns raised by the proposed program. A proposed letter addressed to the student body as a whole explaining the new proposal was drafted but never sent out. Furthermore, housing decided that the ASSU had not sufficiently consulted with the student body as a whole and that there was not enough time to address the student concerns. Student Housing remains open, however, to future ideas for unlimited laundry plans and will be holding more focus groups and surveys to determine the feasibility of implementing such a plan next year.

Row Houses on Probation

Three Row Houses have been put on social probation for violation of alcohol drinking rules during freshman orientation. Theta Delta Chi, 680 Lomita, and Xanadu were placed on probation for 30 days for serving alcohol during the period of time in which New Student Orientation was held, and failing to follow proper guidelines. The houses will have to participate in a program that will examine their actions, and any further violations of University guidelines will result in a year-long probation. The fraternities generally accepted responsibility for the situation and have promised to make amends, although some of them denied knowing about the unusual circumstances for New Student Orientation.

ASSU Refuses to Endorse Coaltion Against Prop. 54

The ASSU Senate entertained a bill on September 30 that would have endorsed the Stanford Coalition Against Proposition 54. The Coalition is a cooperative effort among several student groups organized for the explicit purpose of rallying students to defeat Prop. 54. The bill was written as an endorsement of a student group that was against Prop. 54, instead of an actual endorsement against 54, in an attempt to sidestep the ASSU's Constitution, which prohibits political statements about issues that do not directly affect Stanford students. Proponents of the bill suggested that the political cause of the Coalition did not matter, and that an ASSU endorsement would merely be supporting political activism. In response, the Coalition For Proposition 54, an amalgamation of various conservative groups on campus, challenged the ASSU to amend the bill to include an endorsement of both the Coalition For and the Coalition Against Proposition 54. This amendment, requiring a simple majority, was defeated 5-8, with 2 abstentions. However, the original bill endorsing only the Coalition Against Proposition 54, requiring two-thirds majority of all voting members to pass, also failed 7-4 with 4 abstentions.
