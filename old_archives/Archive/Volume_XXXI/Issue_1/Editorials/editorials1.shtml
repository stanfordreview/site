Editorials
Democracy in Action
by Sam Shapero
Senior News Staff

The election to recall and possibly replace California Governor Gray Davis is definitely this year's three-handed monkey of American politics. It's definitely a rare animal, as only the second recall election of a state governor in American history. There's no doubt that it's somewhat of a circus act as well: 135 candidates for governor, including a porn star, a sumo wrestler, Bob Dole (not the former senator), and, of course, Ah-nold "Total Recall" Schwarzenegger.

Opponents have leveled several charges against the recall election, perhaps most poignant that it has made California the laughing stock of the nation. It's unfair, however, to characterize that as solely the fault of the recall process. First of all, there have already been countless recall elections in California on a smaller level; scores of local officials and four state legislators have been successfully recalled in the last century. Additionally, it's important to recognize that California is the largest political entity within the United States. It's not surprising that so many people would want to be governor: California offers one of the most prestigious political positions in the country, and has a large pool of citizens. Besides, California was regarded as a funny place long before the attempt to recall Davis.

Some politicians see the recall as a more sinister plot. California Senator Diane Feinstein, an outspoken opponent of the recall, has called the election a "carnival" and a disaster for California. Last month, Feinstein worried that Davis, who received a majority of the vote in 2002, would be replaced by a candidate who might receive as little as 20% of the vote. She and other allies of Davis said they were afraid that the will of the electorate last year might be trampled by a small right-wing conspiracy.

The idea that the vote to recall the governor is somehow undemocratic is ridiculous. At heart, the recall brings the choice to keep the governor to the most democratic body of all a majority of the voters. The only way to make the recall a better expression of the popular will would be to force people to vote. (As a side note, interest in the recall has caused a marked increase in voter registration. About 6 million Californians have registered to vote since the 2002 election, an increase in the rolls of more than forty percent.)

One has merely to look at the beginnings of the recall provision to appreciate how democratic it really is. A special election in 1911 gave California voters the power to directly pass and repeal laws, through the initiative and referendum provisions, and to recall elected officials. All three of these powers were intended to increase popular participation in state government to combat corruption and the influence of special interests.

A far more serious complaint is that the next governor might be approved by only a small plurality of California voters. It is far easier to question the legitimacy of plurality vote then that of a majority.

A calm look at the world around us should put that vote into perspective. Plurality is a well recognized form of democracy. Most European governments, for example, operate on such a principle with the controlling party in the government often having received far less then a majority. Surely Feinstein would not consider those governments to be illegitimate. Even closer to home, elections for national office are also chosen on a plurality basis. Senators, representatives, and electors are all chosen this way. It is only because of the two-party system that one candidate regularly receives a majority.

Secondly, if the recall succeeds, then the likely next governor, Arnold Schwarzenegger, would have at least an implicit majority approval. Many voters have seen the polls, and come Election Day, most will know that Schwarzenegger is the likely replacement governor. Thus many voters will only vote "yes" on the recall if they believe that Schwarzenegger will be a better governor than. Davis.

Finally, it is highly likely that, should the recall succeed, Schwarzenegger will receive more votes than Davis did last year. In last year's election, voter turnout was abysmally low only about a third of Californians voted and Davis won a slim majority; so roughly one in six Californians voted for him. If, however, voter turnout increases in proportion to the number of registered voters, (about 40 percent) and Schwarzenegger's poll numbers stay constant (also 40%), then the new "governator" can expect to have the votes of roughly 19% of California, about two points more than Davis. While these numbers are approximations and subject to change, they at least demonstrate the large possibility that Schwarzenegger will have a larger mandate than the current governor.

As the recall and replacement are then legitimately the will of the people, then the other complaints of Feinstein and other allies of the governor reduce to partisan arguments. The claim of a right-wing conspiracy to replace Davis ceases to be of interest to most voters, considering the conservative vote is currently split. The likely winner, Schwarzenegger, while a Republican, can hardly be described as right-wing with his pro-choice, pro-gun control positions.

Do the above arguments mean that the recall is a good idea? Not necessarily. Do they mean Californians should recall a governor whenever he becomes unpopular? Definitely not. What you should understand, though, is that whatever the results on Wednesday morning whenever the dust clears, they'll represent the will of the California electorate.
