Editor's Note
Democratic Battle
by Chris Fish
Opinions Editor

Many new surprises have come forth in epic saga of the Democratic nomination in the last month. Perhaps the biggest has been Sen. John Kerry's emergence as the front-runner. Or maybe it is has been John Edward's reemergence from the ranks of the Democrat's no-way-in-hell candidates including Al Sharpton, Dennis Kucinich and recently withdrawn Carel Mosley-Brown. But the biggest losers this week have clearly been those orchestrating the far-left's take over of the Democratic Party.

While Dean's creeping rise in popularity had been slowly developing, the polls varied so widely that it was clearly still anybody's race. But when the mainstream Democratic endorsements started to roll in, the Dean machine's winning streak was beginning to look unstoppable. The past weeks' endorsements by Al Gore and Sen. Tom Harkin gave Dean the centrist credibility that his campaign seemed to be lacking. Dean's popularity continued snowballing as measured by both his place in the polls as well as his opponent's attacks.

Dean's emergence as the clear front-runner and his subsequent relegation back into the pack demonstrates the volatility and unpredictability of the Democrats in this next election year. But the ebb and flow of Dean's campaign masks the complexity of the forces working underneath the typical American's radar. For the battle between the candidates is an epic conflict of ideas fighting for the ideological front-seat of the Democratic Party.

It was not long ago when Americans complained of Democratic and Republican platforms that seemed more similar than different. But the days of seemingly homogenous political debate are ending, with the far-left putting forth their best effort yet to take over the Democratic Party.

There are many battlefields in which the conflict between the left and center has typically played out including policy objectives and fundraising techniques. But perhaps the most obvious dimension of conflict that demarcates the Dean campaign is not one of extreme policy so much as extreme personality. The unprecedented anger coupled with the sharp attacks and brutal characterizations of the Bush Administration have not been seen by any front-running Presidential candidate is quite sometime. This tone and mood stands in stark contrast to that of the Clinton-era Democrats. While the feel of Clinton Democrats could be generalized as "happiness through incremental change," Dean's tone of outrage and disgust calls for nothing short of revolution.

This outrage is best expressed through the wildly belittling and crude comments Dean uses to describe President Bush's administration. On National Public Radio, Howard Dean said the Saudi's may have warned President Bush about 9/11 before it happened and the President chose to do nothing. He scolded Bush's sex-education policy saying, "I once thought the problem was we needed the surgeon general to explain the birds and bees to the president." But perhaps it's Dr. Dean prognosis of President Bush's psychological state (featured in Rolling Stone) that is the most telling: "This president is not interested in being a good president. He's interested in some complicated psychological situation that he has with his father." These ludicrous comments are not coming from a morally indignant college student, but from a leading figure of the Democratic Party. Can you imagine Joe Lieberman making such comments?

But yet this ideological divide is not drawn across the party boundary but within it. It is important to recognize that Dean seeks to expand this chasm within the Democratic Party since he stands to benefit most from it as the only person on the extremist side with enough broad-scale appeal to possibly lead. His campaign's wild rhetoric and disregard for convention accentuates his interest in not just breaking the mold, but making it terribly obvious to everyone that he is breaking the mold.

Although expanding this ideological chasm has been an effective campaigning technique, if Dean is chosen as the Democratic contender for the President, he will effectively become the voice of the Democratic Party. Having risen to power by drawing a line between moderate Democratic stances such as those of the DLC, or as he calls it "The Republican Part of the Democratic Party," Dean will be forcing those moderates to support a candidate standing for extremism or to jump onto the Republican bandwagon. It is because of this likely party defection that most political observers see a Dean candidacy as a sure fire Bush victory. But the often unrecognized effect of a Dean candidacy is loss or not, he will undoubtedly shift the American political debate ever more to the left.

In a political environment in which a citizen's party affiliation is a long-term constant, any hope for mass defections is at best elusory. Although one's ideals, values and beliefs largely shape his choice of a political party, one's political party often changes his ideals as well. Because of the reflexive nature of this relationship, an extremist Democratic Party, will undoubtedly lead the mean political perceptive of the population more to the left. This long-term consequence means that even if Dean never gets a chance to turn his policy into practice in the oval office, if nominated, he might transform the landscape of what America considers realizable policy in a permanent way.

Ultimately, the defining result of this Presidential primary may not be a Democratic Presidential candidate so much as a Democratic Party platform. If Dean has his way, we may end up with a one-party democracy.
