Editor's Note
Leave-takings
by Gary J. Raichart
Editor-in-Chief

The beginning of this article will sound much like an Oscar acceptance speech, though I promise it will be well worth it for you to read to the end. Rather than accepting an award, however, the following piece commemorates my last issue as editor-in-chief of the Review.

First of all, I would like to thank my staff for putting in the hard work and extra effort needed to put together the paper every week, and even more so for putting up with me. Setting aside the time to work on a paper is hard enough, let alone having to deal with the stigma of working for the evil conspiracy known as The Stanford Review.

Secondly, I would like to thank Dr. Arnold Beichman for organizing our weekly "Pizza & Politics" luncheons with Hoover Fellows. These weekly meetings have been an invaluable facet of my education at Stanford. This rare opportunity gives members of the Review staff the opportunity to discuss issues of vast import with some of the most influential people and the greatest minds in the world.

Thirdly, I'd like to thank my predecessors who have laid the framework for the sustainability of the Review so that we might fight the good fight for decades to come. I would also like to thank all of our alumni, subscribers, and donors who allow us to continue to publish through their generosity.

Next, there are two staff members who warrant special consideration this volume. First, I'd like to thank Ben Guthrie, who is our incoming Business Manager, for orchestrating the on campus distribution of the Review in light of Residential Education's banning of door-to-door distribution in all dorms that did not explicitly allow it, which made his job much more difficult than anticipated. Also, I'd like to thank Christine Boehm who had to put up with personal attacks (including a rather humorous emailed voodoo curse) in response to an editorial that she knew nothing about until it was published.

Finally, I would like to thank my family and friends for providing me with the support and confidence needed to get through life and my pastors for leading me in my Christian faith.

And now the Oscar music starts to play that signifies that it's time for the star to wrap it up. But let me not be the editor that goes gently into that good night. Nay! The fat lady has not yet sung.

At the beginning of this volume, I announced that the theme of this volume was "Selling the Farm," in reference to whether Stanford remained true to its roots and how Stanford differs in actuality from the way it presents itself to the world.

Through the investigation of the Office for Religious Life (ORL), we found that Stanford had strayed far from the standards established for religious life on campus by the Stanford family. The ORL now promotes the acceptance of all religions and beliefs, promoting relativism to its fullest extreme. Except for the issue of gay marriage, of course. They support that with alacrity.

Moreover, we also have been witness to a betrayal of the principles of liberal education, which Stanford proclaims to hold so dear. Yet, as a result of the complaints of a few people who were offended at the content of one of our editorials, Residential Education began enforcing the ban on door-to-door distribution for the first time in years. When there's a policy with which the Daily, the Progressive, and the Review all disagree with the administration, that's a pretty good sign that it might be time to change that policy.

Why did Stanford start believing that its students need to be protected from potentially offensive ideas? In 1990, Stanford implemented a speech code which served to stifle speech on campus due to fears of insulting fellow students. How could such a code have been set in place at an institution like Stanford? Isn't Stanford supposed to stand for the free exchange of ideas? Surprising as it may be to those residing within the Stanford bubble, a lot of people have a lot of ideas that are highly offensive and even insulting to some people. The best way to counter such ideas is not to pretend they aren't there by not talking about them. Rather, the best way to counter ideas that offend you is to realize those ideas exist and speak out against them. Thanks to the efforts of Review alumni among others, the speech code was thankfully struck down by the courts.

Yet, Stanford doesn't seem to have learned its lesson, still choosing to restrict speech through preventing door-to-door distribution. While other reasons, from the extra clean up it requires for the janitorial staff to "it kills trees", are often cited, the real motivation behind the ban is the protections of students from material which might offend them.

I contend, however, that it isn't such a bad thing to be offended. Rather, when something really offends me, I am much more prone to respond and get fired up about my beliefs and ideology than if someone makes some PC appeal to humanity (which really just makes me nauseous).

Well, the Oscar audience is now chucking rotten apples at me, and I can hear the first few notes of the fat lady's song, so I guess it's time to sign off. I leave you now in the very capable hands of Stephen Cohen, who will be the next editor-in-chief. I can only hope he does a better job than I.

May God bless Stanford and God bless America.

Sincerely,

Gary J. Raichart
