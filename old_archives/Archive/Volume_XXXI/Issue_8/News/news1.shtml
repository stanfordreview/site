Representative Eshoo Criticizes Bush, Arafat
by Sam Shapero
News Staff Writer

United States Congresswoman Anna Eshoo visited campus last Monday, during which she attacked President Bush's foreign policy and voiced support for the state of Israel. Mrs. Eshoo, a Democrat and an avowed liberal, is Stanford's representative to the United States House of Representatives. She spoke about America's policy in the Middle East in Tresidder Student Union, at an event sponsored by the Stanford Democrats, Hillel, and the Stanford Israel Alliance.

Mrs. Eshoo criticized what she called the President's "policy of preemption and unilateralism," in the war against terrorism. "I think the Bush doctrine is the antithesis of who and what we are," she said.

She specifically attacked the decision to go to war in Iraq. Although a member of the House Committee on Selective Intelligence, Mrs. Eshoo said that she never received enough evidence from the administration that Iraq was building weapons of mass destruction to warrant going to war.

She did admit, however, that the war had brought some benefit, including democratization of Iraq, and the fact that "we've scared the hell out of the people in the region."

Mentioning only two of America's coalition partners, Mrs. Eshoo criticized President Bush for not inviting enough countries to help us in Iraq. "[America] cannot go it alone, we cannot do it alone," she said, regarding the war on terror. "We have to be the leaders, but we need followers."

Instead of focusing on Iraq, she said America should have attempted to solve the Israeli-Palestinian problem to reduce the threat of terrorism. This conflict "cannot be off the list. It cannot be on the backburner."

"We are the unparalleled, indispensable power in that part of the world. Unless and until we [solve the crisis], the terrorism that dominates our news will continue to rise, the aggravation it represents will continue."

Mrs. Eshoo answered questions from the audience after her relatively short speech. She repeated her belief in the importance of the resolution of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. "As long as that conflict continues, there are some who will use it as an excuse for terrorism."

She also came out strongly supportive of the State of Israel. "Israel is essentially a nation under siege," she said.

At this point, an unidentified man from the audience interrupted Mrs. Eshoo, and tried to argue with her, accusing her of being blind to the plight of the Palestinians. After the crowd shouted him down, Mrs. Eshoo angrily responded to the man that she was not blind to such a situation.

"The notion that America closes her eyes to what Israel is doing is untrue; the ongoing policy is against settlements," she said. She argued, however, that it made a lot of sense to stand with Israel in the conflict.

"I don't think America is unbiased when dealing with democracy. The basis of the relationship [with Israel] is democracy. Name another democracy in the region -- there isn't one."

Mrs. Eshoo admitted that she did not know how to solve the conflict, but was "absolutely" certain there was a way to do it. She cited the Camp David accords between Egypt and Israel as an accord that has never been broken. She insisted that much more effort would have to come from the Palestinian people to replace their leadership. Peace would not come until there was "a viable leader on the Palestinian side."

She called Palestinian Prime Minister Ahmed Qureia, "a rubber stamp for [Palestinian president Yassar] Arafat," whom she attacked even more vigorously.

Arafat "is not defensible," Mrs. Eshoo said. "He speaks out of both corners of his mouth. He can't be negotiated with." In a later interview she commented, "you can't find anyone in the world who stands with him."

Mrs. Eshoo also discussed possible roles for the United States in relation to Iran. She argued that America should support the younger, moderate forces in Iran. "We do know there is within Iran some seventy percent of the country who can't tell you about the Shah."

At the same time, however, Americans should not be surprised that Iran is trying to develop nuclear weapons. "Well guess what?" asked Mrs. Eshoo. "Any country that wants to be a major player on the world stage, that's the requirements."

She cautioned against hawkish policy, however. "I think what we are coming to ... is that people are now saying 'if they don't behave, I guess we'll have to invade,' But that is not the case ... we cannot be trigger-happy." America needs to show her power, she said, "and her goodness as well."

Reaction to the talk was mixed, but a contingent of Stanford Democrats provided a supportive audience. Marie Jonas, a freshman and member of the Stanford Democrats said that she was really impressed, but was reluctant to support the congresswoman's take on Israel, saying only that "it's hard for someone to have a strong position on the issue."

Several members of the Stanford College Republicans (SCR) also attended Mrs. Eshoo's talk. A few had the opportunity to talk with her before the lecture began, including SCR Vice President Bob Sensenbrenner, but he declined comment to the Review.
