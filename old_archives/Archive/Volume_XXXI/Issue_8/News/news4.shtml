The News in Brief
by Shawn M. Sims
News Staff Writer

Stanford Professors Host Talk Show on Philosophy Issues

On January 13, Stanford professors Ken Taylor and John Perry aired the first episode in the regular series of their innovative "Philosophy Talk" radio program. Listeners of San Francisco's KALW, 91.7 FM, heard a heated discussion between the two California philosophy professors and their colleague from the U.S. Naval Academy, George Lucas. The talk covered the ethical implications of the doctrine of preemption in Bush's foreign policy. All three academics questioned the use of force for constabulatory purposes. Last June, Lucas held a summer institute for post-secondary school faculty at the Naval Academy entitled "War and Morality: Rethinking the Just War Tradition for the 21st Century."

Taylor currently chairs the Department of Philosophy and recently published Reference and the Rational Mind, exploring the nature of concepts, names, and normativity. Perry is now the Henry Waldgrave Stuart Professor of Philosophy and formerly served as the Chair of the Department of Philosophy and the Director of the Center for Study of Language and Information.

The duo conceived the idea for Philosophy Talk over coffee while discussing the disregard for valid philosophical pondering by the "talking heads" on Fox and MSNBC. Their pilot, "Is Lying Always Bad?" aired August 30, 2003, and covered plagiarism in journalism, presidents lying and even Huckleberry Finn as he relates to Kant. Future topics of the show include: identification with race and affirmative action, immortality, globalization, marriage and monogamy, drug legalization, and the insanity plea. Supported by Stanford's Provost Office, Humanities Center, and Office of the VP of Public Affairs, as well as the Hoover Institute and KALW, Philosophy Talk has attracted enough funding to run at least through February. It can be heard Tuesdays at noon or as live streaming audio at www.sfusd.k12.ca.us/programs/kalw/index.html.

National Nanotech Initiative Boosts Stanford's Resources

On December 3, 2003, President Bush signed the 21st Century Nanotechnology Research and Development Act, after the House and Senate passed legislation to provide the funding. This allowed the Board of the National Science Foundation to create the National Nanotechnology Infrastructure Network, which opened for business this month. It is an improvement upon the ten-year old Nanofabrication Users Network, increasing the participating institutions from five to the following thirteen: Cornell, Stanford, Georgia Tech, Harvard, Howard, North Carolina State, Pennsylvania State, UC Santa Barbara, and the Universities of Michigan, Minnesota, New Mexico, Texas at Austin, and Washington.

Cornell's Sandip Tiwari heads the Network and is enthusiastic about sharing the school's resources, which exceed those of most institutions. The NSF has allocated $14 million a year for a proposed five years. Technology at the level of 1 to 100 nanometers is extremely expensive, requiring lengthy decontamination procedures and highly specialized equipment. Most research thus far has been goal- rather than discovery-driven and dependent on funding with an agenda. The initiative encourages Internet contact between researchers and hosts other web-based resources for the public and K-12 teachers. Undergraduate students can gain summer research experience based on application of principles to real life. Implications of nanotechnology include: materials design, manufacturing, detection of chemical or biological weapons, photonics and electromagnetism studies, health care, robotics, and energy conservation, among others

Proposed Solution to Stanford/Palo Alto Conflict of Interest

On Jan. 8th, the California State Senate decided to act immediately on Senate Bill 1086, which would add non-profit entities such as Stanford University to the list of exemptions from Section 1090. This code currently prohibits elected officials from negotiating contracts with corporations for which they work unless they have been employed more than three years or the corporation is nonprofit. Since Stanford is technically a "nonprofit trust with corporate powers," the election of Stanford's Vice Provost of just over two years, LaDoris Cordell (D), to the Palo Alto city council on November 4 jeopardized relations between the city and the school. Some of the pressing contracts for winter quarter include hired police help for events and land leases for soccer fields.

Many senators, lawyers from Stanford, and the Palo Alto mayor Beecham believe that Bill 1086 is consistent with existing law for nonprofits and should pass before March. Cordell, a 1974 Stanford Law graduate, did not discover Section 1090 until after her election. Palo Alto citizens favor her, particularly over incumbent Nancy Lytle, known for inciting controversy among council members. Some individual city occupants have proposed that Cordell's campaign neglected research of guidelines and in effect "disenfranchised" voters.

Unhappy with the way elections are often funded, Cordell spent only $3,000 on grassroots efforts including fliers, lawn signs, local coffee talks, and a website entitled "Another Way." Her accomplishments include opening the first law office in East Palo Alto, becoming the first African-American female judge in Northern California, lifelong minority activism, and increasing minority enrollment at Stanford. She presides over the Affirmative Action policy and the Office of Campus Relations.

University Budget Surplus Larger than Expected

The most recent report from the Board of Trustees at Stanford shows a $46 million budget surplus for the 2003 fiscal year, a contrast to the $17 million deficit from last year. Market investments took a sharp upturn in the last quarter of the year, accounting for much of the favorable returns. Until June 2003, experts had predicted a deficit for the year. Roughly two-thirds of the increase, however, is contained in restricted funds, so University financial advisors recommend caution. Both endowed professorship funds and department gift funds are basically already spent.

The surplus is also due largely to a salary and hiring freeze implemented by Provost Etchemendy for the year, a last minute effort to avoid 100-300 layoffs. Department heads were encouraged to spend wisely, but the hold on raises allowed academic expense reduction that was less than the projected 10 percent. Fiscal year 2005 will still involve tight spending in academics, and Etchemendy believes that department heads are cognizant of the financial problems that still remain. Both he and President Hennessy do foresee faculty raises in the near future, especially with health care costs rising faster than inflation. The hiring freeze will remain.

If not for the gifts from Stanford donors, the surplus would be nonexistent. The $10 million not held in closed funds will likely be used for one time expenses such as revamping of computer systems.
