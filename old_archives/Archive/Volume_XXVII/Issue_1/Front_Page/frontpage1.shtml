Front Page
Stanford Community Responds to Terrorist Attacks
by Alex Robbins
News Editor

Since September 11, the Stanford community has responded to the terrorist acts with outrage, compassion, and solidarity.

Click to view enlarged picture
Stanford students working and living in Washington, D.C. and New York City experienced firsthand the day's events as they unfolded, and many throughout the world had loved ones and friends in the two cities on September 11.

September 11 has had a wide-ranging influence even those in the Stanford community who were not directly affected by the attacks. Six hours after the initial attacks the Stanford community held a noon vigil at Memorial Church. Dean of Religious Life Reverend Scott McLennan led the service, and President John Hennessy and Vice Provost for Campus Relations LaDoris Cordell also spoke. Stanford News Service reports that at least 1,000 people were in attendance.

Mr. Hennessy released an official statement offering sympathy and prayers for the victims and expressing "grief and horror over these brutal attacks. " He reassured the Stanford community that the University is taking all necessary security precautions and that his senior staff "will continue to meet on a regular basis in the coming days to assess the situation and develop appropriate responses as necessary."

Most of those at Stanford responded as appropriately as they could: within hours, the blood banks at the Stanford Medical Center were being filled to capacity. Stanford University Medical Center throughout Sept. 11 day received over 400 units of blood, which it shipped to Sacramento Medical Foundation Blood Center. Five hundred units of blood from that center are being shipped to New York City on the ground via fire trucks. The Stanford Blood Center is booked through the end of September, but donors are urged to return in early October. "The nature of these tragedies means that a very large amount of blood will be needed," commented Stanford University Medical Center spokesperson Ruth Anne Richter. "It's important that more blood is supplied on a continual basis."

Students also reacted by discussing the events with one another. By evening, students were actively writing about the day's events with their dorm mates and classmates over the campus's majordomo email lists. Locals in New York and Washington, D.C. reported on their whereabouts and safety, and students from around the country offered their support to everyone affected by the tragedies. One frosh dorm list from 1999-2000 that had been dormant throughout the summer had a total of 6 messages within twelve hours.

Religion was a common component of many people's reactions to the attacks. On Sept. 11, approximately thirty students held an evangelical prayer session in front of Memorial Church at 10:15 PM, although the religious beliefs of those in attendance ranged from Catholicism to Islam. On the following Friday thousands of Stanford students, faculty, and staff attended "A Gathering of the Stanford Community" in the Main Quad from 12:00 to 12:30 PM, to pray and to remember lost loved ones as part of America's National Day of Prayer and Remembrance. The memorial service itself embodied the diversity of the Stanford community: Muslims, Jews, Catholics, and Protestants, and Hindus all prayed in their own traditions for the victims of Tuesday's attacks. The Reverend Scotty McLennan, Stanford's Dean for Religious Life, invited those in attendance to call out the names of lost loved ones. At first haltingly, then rapidly, names of the lost rose up from the crowd. "It's all right even if the names tumble out of you and come at the same time on top of each other," said the Rev. McLennan, "for we lift up all; we lift up each and every person's name in prayer."

The attacks also prompted far less benign responses. On Tuesday itself unknown persons committed numerous (though fictitious) copycat threats: a bomb threat directed at Hoover Institution Library prompted the evacuation of Lou Henry Hoover Building, the Hoover Memorial Building, Hoover Tower, Meyer Library and Green Library. In the days following the attacks, according to Stanford President John Hennessy, "a number of hate-filled e-mails have been sent to the Islamic Society and the Muslim Student Awareness Network. . . . we are not certain where these e-mails originated, but we are taking them very seriously. The incidents have been reported to the police and computer security and will be thoroughly investigated." On Thursday, Sept. 13, a bomb threat prompted Stanford Hospital and the Lucile Packard Children's Hospital to partially evacuate, although as on Tuesday the buildings were searched and no bomb was found. "It's unthinkable that someone could do this at this time," commented detective Lori Kratzer of the Palo Alto Police Department. "If we find out who made this call, we will seek criminal prosecution."

Since September 11 many have turned to political involvement in dealing with the attacks. On September 11, at 8:00 PM, Students Against Military Madness held a vigil for peace in White Plaza that drew over a hundred community members. Most of the assembled were there to express condolences and articulate fears: Kay Ritchey, a local resident, expressed fear for her children since "the world was so evil, or some people in the world can be so evil." Arthur Keller, another resident, commented on some of the political causes and consequences of the attacks, pointing out in reference to the Taliban that "in the 80's we trained a bunch of terrorists," and commenting, "I see a very real possibility that some people in our government will use this as an opportunity to restrict our civil liberties."

For the most part, however, Stanford students and community members--like Americans and their friends across the world--have rallied around rather than turned against American society. On the afternoon of October 5, students of all religious backgrounds--some of whom were explicitly encouraged to attend by their own respective religious leaders--gathered to hear a short sermon dealing with the Islamic view of such terrorist acts. The sermon was entitled "How the Terrorists Tried to Hijack Islam," and reiterated the Muslim community's condemnation of September 11's attacks.

In addition to expressions of religious solidarity since the attacks, there recently have been more expressions of patriotism than ever witnessed by most people here: large shopping centers in the area around Stanford remained almost continuously sold-out of American flags for weeks, clearing whatever shipments arrived in a matter of hours. There has been an unprecedented out-flowing of support, especially in money and in blood, to the victims of the attacks. And particularly, there has been an overwhelming reaffirmation of the value of a pluralistic and free society. Speaking to the thousands assembled outside Memorial Church on the National Day of Prayer and Remembrance, Stanford President Hennessy, cited Stanford's motto, "the wind of freedom blows," as a value to follow in days ahead.
