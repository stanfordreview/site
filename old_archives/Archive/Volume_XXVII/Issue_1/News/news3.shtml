U.S. News Ranks Stanford #5
by Bry Martin
Staff Writer

Click to view enlarged graph
Stanford University crept into the top 5 of the annual US News and World Report rankings of undergraduate programs at National Universities for the first time since 1998, according to a release in the September 17th "Best Colleges" issue. In an otherwise torpid year wherein none of the top eight universities were displaced or dropped in ranking, Stanford entered a three-way tie with Massachusetts Institute of Technology and the University of Pennsylvania for the fifth position.

Dean of Admissions and Financial Aid Robin Mamlet responded to the news with caution, stressing that the rankings should not replace closer scrutiny on the part of prospective students toward a given university: "[The rankings] are limited when one uses them as a substitute for really good careful research on whether or not the fit is right at that particular place for any given student." Nevertheless, Dean Mamlet noted that the US News ranking provides many who lack the attention of a college counselor a creditable launching pad for further research: "For each time it gets a student to say, "There's a name," add it to their list, and take the next step with that particular institution, and look at whether that is a good place for them, I think that's a service."

The US News rankings started out in 1983 as a biennial survey asking Deans, Presidents, and Chancellors at universities nationwide to rank the top schools in their respective fields. From the relatively simple scope of its first format, the US News formula has branched out into a far more ambitious, comprehensive, and at times, controversial rubric for measuring academic excellence. Graduation rates, class sizes, the percentage of alumni who give back to the university, freshman retention rates, and the average expenditure per student all figure in varying proportions to the final ranking. The fact that the formula far more approaches an art than a science has at times attracted controversy. The September 19, 1999 issue of the Washington Post reported that a constantly changing editorial methodology, not the universities themselves, was sometimes the critical determinant in the fluctuations of a college's placement from year to year.

Stanford Sophomore Dinyar Patel highlighted this point: "The Top Seven Universities remain the same by and large, and the differences in ranking within these seven really reflects the whims of US News Editors more than anything else." Patel noted that in the year that California Institute of Technology was threatened with a withdrawal of grants in 1999, US News changed the formula to heavily weigh the category of expenditures per student, in which CIT far outspent any other school. CIT went to number one and several other engineering schools bounded up the charts, only to return to their normal positions a year later when US News again changed their formula in a manner that tempered the results of the year before. Rather than trusting overall ranking, Patel said that he looks carefully at the more specific components of the rankings, "I look at specific parts. You can't compare MIT and Princeton. They're apples and oranges. I look at components like the student-per-faculty ratios and alumni giving."

Yet even these quantitative areas may be framed in a way that does not represent the full picture. Dean Mamlet pointed out that Stanford University affords a symbiotic relationship between its graduate and undergraduate programs, allowing professional school professors to teach undergrads in courses. Under the current framing of the US News rankings, however, these professors do not count in the school's overall student/faculty ratio, thus skewing the numbers to Stanford's disadvantage. Despite this caveat, Mamlet believes that the frequent changes in formula—including the regard given to expenditures-per-student—has yielded over time a better and more reflective ranking than in the past: "They have taken a number of steps over the last ten years to make it a better reflection of some things than it once was and I think some of those things are looking, for example, at the dollars spent per undergraduate."

Stanford's performance in 2001 was the spitting image of years past. Ranked first in academic reputation according to a survey of nationwide administrators, Stanford has held the top spot in this category since 1996. Student selectivity dropped to sixth place from the previously held 5th despite a more harrowing 13% admittance rate down further from last year's 15%.

The rest of the scores displayed similar slight rises and falls that balanced out to a marginally more competitive showing: Alumni Giving rose from 16th to 11th place, the Graduation slid to 7th from 6th place, Faculty Resources lunged from 19th to 15th place, Financial Resources rank stood still at 9th, and the midrange of incoming SAT scores gained almost imperceptibly.

Stanford's performance may give credence to Dean Mamlet's admonition that while the rankings may fluctuate, the reality of a University remains relatively constant: "I don't believe the difference between six or five has any significance and I don't think Stanford changes from year to year, but I do think that a new placement does sell magazines."

Stanford's highest position in the US News rankings was first place, which it held in 1983, 1985 and 1987, the first three years US News and World Report published the rankings. Stanford's lowest position was sixth place, which it has held multiple times, the latest last year. In a separate "Best Values" section weighing the value of the education against the cost of the education, Stanford University rated fourth amongst national universities.
