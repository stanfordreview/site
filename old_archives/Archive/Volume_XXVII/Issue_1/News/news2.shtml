Week In Review
Peace Groups Protest Coming War
Members of the Green Party, Middle East Children's Alliance, several socialist organizations, and other sympathizers assembled September 20th at San Francisco's Golden Gate Lutheran Church. According to the organizers, a primary goal of the gathering was to "oppose President Bush's war of racist scapegoating," which will "no doubt lead to a loss of even more innocent life." Other groups have followed suit around the nation, congregating in "Town Hall Meetings" to form their own coalitions. Similarly, pro-peace organizations at Stanford University have prepared plans to travel to San Francisco and protest at 4 o' clock P.M., the day at which any bombing is reported. These students have been meeting to construct methods of educating their community about non-violent resolutions to the conflict, including the use of the World Court, although how the World Court would enforce its rulings was left unclear.

Clark Suspends $60 Million Stanford Gift
Jim Clark, the founder of Netscape, recently decided to suspend $60 million of his $150 million donation to Stanford's "Bio-X Initiative." He wrote an op-ed piece in the New York Times entitled, "Squandering Our Technological Future," in reference to President Bush's decision to limit federally-funded stem cell research to pre-existing lines of stem cells. Clark asserts that, without federal funding, stem cell research in the US will be crippled to the point that it will not progress, and other countries will emerge as the new technological leaders of the world.

Stanford Expands Health Insurance Options
Stanford employees will have a new health insurance option in 2002. CIGNA will administer the new plan, called the Stanford Employee Preferred Provider Organization. This new option, which will be the most expensive health insurance plan offered, will allow employees to go to any doctor or hospital, including Stanford hospitals and affiliates.

Stanford Welcomes New Vice Provost for Student Affairs
Gene Awakuni, vice president of student affairs at Columbia University, will replace James Montoya as the Stanford Vice Provost for Student Affairs beginning in January 2002. After receiving bachelors and masters degrees at the University of Hawaii at Manoa and a masters and doctorate at Harvard, Awakuni worked at UC-Irvine, UC-Santa Barbara, and California State Polytechnical University, where he demonstrated an ability to balance conflicting campus groups and concern for student issues. At Columbia, Awakuni co-authored Resistance to Multiculturalism: Issues and Interventions, and taught psychology and multiculturalism courses. Regarding his new position, Awakuni said, "I'll get to work with the best and brightest students from all over the world. Stanford is an extraordinary place."

Stanford Alumnus Nominated to the California Supreme Court
Stanford alumnus, federal judge Carlos Moreno, was nominated by California Gov. Gray Davis to the California Supreme Court. If confirmed he will be the first Latino in 12 years and the only Democrat out of the seven justices on the court. Moreno received rave reviews from multiple judicial evaluation groups, and was previously raised by a 96-0 vote in the Senate to the federal bench. Moreno, whose "memories of Stanford are very fond," is expected to be approved in the same landslide fashion by the California legislature and sworn in on Oct. 17 in San Francisco for a 12-year term.
