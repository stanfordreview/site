Opinion
Is Christianity a Socialist Religion?
by Alex Robbins
News Editor

A close of mine recently referred to Christianity as a "socialist religion." As I recall, some famous theological professor also once claimed that "any serious Christian must be a socialist." Even Tony Blair, now the U.S. Ambassador-at-Large for the war effort, on September 22 of last year referred to "individuals who saw a connection between the values of Christ and the values of socialism and who chose to work out these connections in the rough and tumble of party politics."

At the same time many from the more secular end of things based their leftist political philosophies on moral systems very reminiscent of the dictates of Christianity (although many other religions besides Christianity--most other religions, so far as I know--strenuously advocate selfless caring for and charity towards others, I am only really knowledgeable regarding Christianity and so will restrict my comments accordingly). I suspect that most Stanford students lean to the left because they harbor a general sentiment that society does have a certain obligation to its poor and downtrodden, and that caring for and helping others is a good thing. It is a sentiment with which I wholeheartedly agree, and--Objectivist complaints about collectivist, selfless philosophies aside--one which I will not attempt to defend here.

I'm going to begin from the assumption that we all agree with the overall premise that generosity and caring are desirable things: that human beings do have some sort of definite, concrete duty towards each other during their time in this world.

So here, then, is the question: why isn't everyone who agrees with this sentiment a socialist?

One response is hypocrisy. Most of us, myself definitely included, have trouble fulfilling what we believe we ought to do. If you don't ever feel this way I suspect you're setting the bar too low--like adherents to philosophies which glorify whatever it is the individual happens to want to do at the moment. Perhaps, while most of us agree that we should help others, only the honest and truly compassionate go off to become socialists.

Perhaps. However, I know of quite a few instances of hypocrisy the other way--people who are great advocates of charity in general (read: when it comes to other peoples' giving) but tend to become a bit cheaper in their own actions, both financial and interpersonal.

So let's grant that there are people who hide behind Republican values to justify their selfishness, but also plenty who think they're kind and good people just because they're Democrats. We still need to find some sort of actual reason that Christianity and selfless moral systems in general don't necessitate the most extreme forms of socialism—it certainly seems to follow from a religious system that enjoins us to give up one of our two coats to the man who has none.

The crux of the issue lies in a subject very apropos today: how freedom interacts with morality. In The Brothers Karamazov, Dostoevsky depicts a scene in which a Grand Inquisitor in Spain from the 16th Century confronts Jesus. In his monologue, the Inquisitor tells Jesus that the fatal flaw in the Gospel was human freedom. He protests, powerfully and convincingly, the absurdity of giving humans a glimpse and taste of salvation and then allowing them the freedom to never attain it.

Dostoevsky's Inquisitor espouses one of two very fundamental and very distinct brands of morality. He picks the social morality rather than the individual one, the one that dictates not only what you must force yourself to do, but what you must force others to do.

The difference between the worldly moral dictates of Christianity and socialism is akin to the difference between "ought to" and "must." It is a concept that Americans especially should be entirely familiar with. Are we not, after all, a country founded on tolerance and diversity, on the notion that people ought to be free to think and generally do as they will even when we disagree with their choices? Our constitution defends the rights of Nazi and Communist groups to protest, the right of people to be mean to each other, the right of individuals to follow a religion that a large chunk of the population believes will land them in eternal damnation.

The fact that we as a society allow certain types of behavior does not mean that we condone it. To the contrary, we could hardly have a tolerant society if allowing things required supporting them; we would destroy ideological diversity and replace it with a government-enforced, coerced set of normative values.

Christianity, in fact, demands such tolerance. Whatever its past abuses, a fundamental tenet of Christianity is separation of Church and State: of paying God and Caesar separately, so as to not confuse what one as an individual should do with what one, as a member of society, should force others to do. Did Jesus ever have anyone arrested for doing ill, or advocate temporal punishment for disobeying Scripture? Quite the contrary: he separated religion from the state. He dispensed with the religious police. Most moderate socialist and leftist philosophies I've encountered have a good number of agreements with Christianity. They too, in principle at least, tout the importance of the individual, in human freedom, and in tolerance.

This being true I fail to see how one can justify using the coercive power of the government to enforce what the majority of the population sees as a morally desirable end. Whatever we can agree is the appropriate role of the government--from merely defending citizens from immediate harm to dealing with market failures and externalities--I submit to you that forcing other people to act in a morally desirable way is not one of them. Were that true the religious right would have a field day.

So, to all the socialists out there: the government is not justified in taking money from some people and giving it to others merely because you think those first people aren't spending it the way they should. By the same token, tearing down a pacifist sign is not justified merely because someone believes that its message is dangerous and idiotic. In this society we have accepted one primary guiding moral principle: individual liberty. If one cannot accept the right of his neighbor to do things that displease him, one cannot be a good American, since one cannot enter that social contract or in good conscience swear to uphold the Constitution of the United States. Once someone passes that hurdle, however, one can be a good and loyal member of society regardless of how that person worships, thinks, or conducts personal affairs. Those other things are the coinage paid to oneself and to God, not to political society.

Our political system is a limited one, one designed to allow people to pursue both true happiness and salvation (a synonymous concept for many of us). It is not a way to enforce all forms of "good" behavior (as though the behavior would be good if it were forced) so much as it is a way to protect people from certain types of harmful behavior.

So yes, society does have an obligation to its poor. And to the Objectivists: yes, Christianity does call for sacrifice, like Communism and socialism and a host of other philosophies. We all have a duty, pecuniary and personal, to other human beings, and we will all be the less for it if we neglect that duty. But if we truly believe in the values we preach--if we truly value both freedom and compassion--then we will lead by example, not fiat.

Alex Robbins is a sophomore who basically agrees with all the stuff he wrote above but still greatly appreciates his Stafford loans.
