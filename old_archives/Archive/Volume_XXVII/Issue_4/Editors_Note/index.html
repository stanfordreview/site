Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Scott Rasmussen
Three weeks ago, the Undergraduate Senate of the Associated Students of Stanford University (ASSU) passed its first political resolution since being constitutionally banned from doing so after a controversial statement against the Gulf War. The Stanford Review published a petition in the last issue which stated, in part, "While many Stanford students do support affirmative action, many do not. For the Senate to claim to speak for all students without even considering the dissenting opinions within its constituency is dishonest and disrespectful."

The Senators didn't listen. They were at it again on November 27, this time passing two additional political resolutions. One called for 50% of Stanford's energy to be solar power by 2020, as Mark Zavislak reports. The other called for President John Hennessy and Vice President of General Services Lou Saksen to halt a planned subcontracting of workers at the Stanford Medical Center.

For the sake of argument, let's set aside the merits of the resolutions. The ASSU should not be spending its time and Stanford students' money (watch for a coming article on ASSU salaries) on political posturing. From a constitutional perspective, the ASSU is restricted from commenting on matters unrelated to student life. From a democratic perspective, the ASSU shouldn't make blanket statements on behalf of the entire student body, particularly since Senators didn't campaign on these issues. From a pragmatic perspective, the ASSU could more efficiently add value for the student body by doing its job--delivering on its campaign promises to improve student services, and maybe even updating its year-old website, too--and by letting administrators do theirs.

An end may be worthy, but that doesn't justify any and all means of achieving it. This principle is relevant not only for the ASSU, but for student demonstrators acting on behalf of one of the same causes. As Matt Barrett reports, five students were arrested on November 29 after forcing entry into the Stanford Medical Center, verbally harassing the staff, and refusing to leave. The students demanded to speak to Mr. Saksen--immediately--notwithstanding the fact that Mr. Saksen had already met with representatives from the same organizations earlier in the week.

The Stanford Daily put the matter best in a November 30 editorial: "Students involved with these protests have stated repeatedly that the University has refused to listen to their grievances. However, meetings were held, and it is important to point out that it is possible to listen to views and yet disagree with them. When student protesters resort to such radical measures in order to make their voices heard, they damage overall student credibility with the administration. Protest is an essential right of all members of a community, but it is one that should not be abused."

The Stanford Review attempted to give these students an opportunity to have their voices heard without resorting to radical measures. As Brian Ball recounts, the Stanford Community for Peace and Justice apparently preferred to make headlines through breaking the law.

Be that as it may, the staff writers at the Stanford Review are making their voices heard on a subject that has received increased attention since September 11: religion. Many in this nation have turned to religion as they search for answers and meaning in a tragedy so difficult to understand, and Stanford is no exception. Stanford's Deans of Religious Life were recurring figures on the Stanford Daily's editorial pages in the weeks after September 11, and Joe Lonsdale examines their words to students. Ryan Wisnesky points out that the Deans have not been without their say on another subject: gender-neutral pronouns in reference to God.

Alex Robbins and Alec Rawls tackle tricky subjects for many people: how to reconcile religion's calls on individuals for charity and tolerance with a state's need to provide liberty and justice. Topsy St. Matthew-Daniel reports on another attempt to find meaning in a post-911 world, the first international walk for capitalism.

Lastly, I would be remiss not to mention that a dear friend of the Stanford Review and the longtime director of the Hoover Institution, W. Glenn Campbell, passed away on November 24. He will be missed by the many people whose lives he touched, including our staff. We reprint an obituary written by Hoover fellow Arnold Beichman which first appeared in the December 1 Washington Times.
