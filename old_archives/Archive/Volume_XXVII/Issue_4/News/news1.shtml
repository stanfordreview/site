Religious Leaders Promote Use of Gender-Neutral Pronouns for God
by Ryan Wisnesky
Staff Writer

For the last several years, religious services at Stanford have been changing public prayer and hymns to reflect a non-gendered God. The practice, which reflects a broad liberal movement across many religions, began when new Deans for Religious Life came to Stanford several years ago.

The use of gender-neutral pronouns for God is nothing new. Beginning in the 1970's, many liberal churches in both Christianity and Judaism became aware of the inadequacies inherent in describing God as male. According to Rabbi Patricia Karlin-Neumann, a Dean for Religious Life, "changes in prayer books reflect the philosophy of the people. The people say Ômy understanding of who I am must be reflected in the hymns.'"

To this end, liberal churches, of which Stanford is on the cutting edge, began changing common prayers and hymnbooks to reflect either a non-gendered God or referred to God as female in addition to the traditional male. A common Jewish prayer, for instance, that states: "Our Father our King" may be changed into "Our Parent our Ruler", or even "Our Mother our Queen." Some prayer books go even further, actually changing ancient scripture in order to create a different translation.

Of course, these changes are alien to many students. One student, who did not wish to be identified, said: "I personally do not think of God as either male or female. But I take great offense to the fact that some people are changing thousands of years old scripture simply because they do not agree with it."

Both Judaism and Christianity run into serious issue when creating new prayers. In Christianity, the scripture explicitly refers to both Jesus and God as male, and ancient Hebrew is a gendered language in which God is most often referred to as male. However, there are various theological arguments about this, ranging from textual, such as the fact that ancient Hebrew does on occasion refer to God as female, to moralistic, such as the father-son dualism being easier to digest in a male dominated society.

The goal of the movement, however, is not so much to transform God into a female, as it is to allow each person the freedom to decide for him or herself, instead of relying on scripture. However, as freshman Kevin Blue pointed out, "Why am I supposed to trust scripture about everything except the gender of God?"

For Rabbi Karlin-Neumann, this is a non-issue. She cites Maimonides, a 12th century Jewish scholar, who said, "We can only say what God isn't." The use of gendered pronouns in scripture is then a simple approximation to ultimate truth. This philosophy fits very well with the rest of the liberal religious movement, in which people are encouraged to interpret scripture in many cases where traditional churches read scripture literally.

The four Deans of Religious Life support these new changes, but have slightly differing viewpoints. The Reverend Maurice-Charles, another Dean for Religious Life, says that his personal preference "is not so much to use Ôgender neutral' language for God as to use an array of language for God that includes the feminine while not excluding more traditional language." He adds that "(a) language referring to the people of God should always and everywhere include the entire community (no "mankind" for example) and (b) we all recognize that masculine language for God in no way defines God as a man or masculine." His sentiment reflects that of the Memorial Church Clergy as a whole, although there are debates about whether to discard gendered language completely. However, in fitting with the diversity of Stanford as a whole, any group that uses the Memorial Church is free to refer to God in any way that it pleases.

Not everyone, however, thinks the use of non-gendered pronouns for God is necessarily a bad idea. Dana Schmidt, a freshman, said, "If you have a continual paternal view of God it can lead to assumptions about a being that can't be articulated in one word. I don't think you need to change prayers, but you shouldn't make it an absolute." Kenny Hamilton, a freshman, said "I would accept the use of Ômy parent' over Ômy father' to prevent any chauvinistic misconceptions."

On the other side, however, some students have very strong feelings. George Crowley, another freshman, explains: "I understand in Judeo-Christian Religions that God is male so I think this is political correctness at the expense of accuracy."

Regardless of how one feels about this issue, one thing is clear -- Stanford will maintain its long tradition of being at the forefront of liberal thought, in both social and spiritual areas. Because most students do not attend public services at the Church, this is not a pressing matter for most. However, because of Stanford's reputation as a leader, the actions of the Memorial Church will undoubtedly influence churches across the world for some time to come.
