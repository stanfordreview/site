Californians to Walk for Capitalism
by Topsy St. Matthew-Daniel
Staff Writer

If you see a group of people carrying signs demanding more freedom and less government on December 2nd at noon, do not be surprised. These are the participants in the first ever global Walk for Capitalism. The event will take place in every major city around the world, including countries like Russia, China and Germany. Silicon Valley participants will congregate at the Tech Museum of innovation and walk to the City hall, 2 miles away.

The event will consist of short speeches, highlighting the virtues of capitalism and free trade, and contrasting it to alternative options. The 'Defender of Capitalism Award' will also be given to a local living person who has provided the best defense of laissez-faire capitalism from an ethical perspective. After the walk, participants will meet for food and drinks at a nearby restaurant. The big question is: why a walk for capitalism, and why now? What does it have to do with students? The Review set out to explore these questions.

The first reaction you might get from some people is: "Must there be a walk for everything?" Why would we want to walk for capitalism? According to the WalkForCapitalism organization, you should walk to "Proudly uphold: Free trade, globalization, liberty, profit, free speech, technology, universal individual rights, intellectual and material prosperity, private property, rule of law, innovation, creativity, human dignity and happiness."

The event is emphasized as a "positive day of promotion, discussion and festivities - NOT a counter protest." Capitalism day, or as it is more formally known, International Freedom and Prosperity Day, is the celebration of the principles and possibilities of capitalism. Organizers claim the objective of this event is to uphold the virtues of capitalism, and show why it is not only the best economic choice, but also the most 'moral' choice for humanity.

According to Silicon Valley organizer, Logan Darrow, capitalism has been looked upon as something we are ashamed of - almost sorry for. "People agree with the outcome of Capitalism, but they have this incorrect notion that somehow it is wrong or it's immoral - that we should feel guilty about it."

Mr. Darrow argues that the ideals of capitalism were never sufficiently and efficiently grounded in the minds of people. "For example the ideal of democracy has a strong moral foundation and therefore people support it. Capitalism's moral foundation was never identified, we are trying to build that foundation." Essentially, the organizers believe that if people get a grasp of the true meaning and implications of capitalism, they would be willing to support it just as democracy has been supported and not just view it as a selfish philosophy. To this end, the objective of the walk is to send a clear message about the underlying moral and ethical virtues of Capitalism—the most important being the concept of mutually voluntary trade, as opposed to by force.

Why is this debate coming up now? It has been going on forever. According to the organizers, capitalism has been under attack--especially in light of recent events, with the September 11th tragedy. Mr. Doros, WalkForCapitalism Founder, feels the destruction of the World Trade Center was a direct attack on the ideals of Capitalism. "It was no less than a *deliberate* strike upon: Life, Liberty, Property, and the Pursuit of Happiness. These are the Founding Principles of the United States of America - and they are universal."

According to Mr. Darrow, "the terrorist attacked a symbol of Capitalism; so we need to defend Capitalism at an intellectual level if we want people to stop attacking it."

But this has not been the only attack. Organizers claim capitalism has been under attack on every front of American society—from the government to the corporations, and the classroom to the movies. So the timing of the event is critical in terms of educating people, and more importantly in moving forward to effect change. A lot of government decisions are expected regarding tax policies and regulation in light of the current economic downturn.

Organizers feel our leaders views on capitalism will affect what solution they provide to the current recession. "If they think the solution is to raise taxes and increase regulations, that's going to hurt the economy. If they realize capitalism is both morally and economically better, their solution will be lowering taxes and lowering regulation and increasing freedom."

What is the role of Stanford students in all this? Student involvement is the most important thing. Mr. Darrow points out that American history has shown that students have been primary agents of change: "Changing the intellectual atmosphere in the university is the most important goal. Universities are mind factories. Students coming to university from high school are like raw steel - and the professors are like giant steel forges. And right now the forges are almost universally post modern - which means anti-reason, anti capitalism, and anti-American."
