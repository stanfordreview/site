The Week in Review
by Alex Pagon
Contributing Writer

Battle over Assisted Suicide
Attorney General John Ashcroft is challenging Oregon’s assisted suicide law (the Death with Dignity Act) by asserting that the law interferes with federal prescription drug regulations. Ashcroft also announced that any doctor who used the assisted-suicide law would lose their license to prescribe federally regulated drugs. The state of Oregon has filed a legal challenge to this directive. In past rulings the U.S. Supreme Court determined that the Constitution doesn’t provide a constitutional right to assisted suicide, but the individual states have the right to choose whether or not to allow assisted suicide. Hearings before U.S. District Judge Robert Jones will began on Tuesday, November 27.

Stanford Wins Big Game
The Cardinal retained the Axe for the seventh consecutive year with a 35-28 victory over Cal. Sophomore quarterback Chris Lewis led Stanford with four touchdown passes as the Cardinal won an uncharacteristically sloppy game. The Bears ended their Pac-10 season without a win, and Stanford finished tied for second with Washington and Washington State. Barring an unexpected turn in events, the Cardinal is slated to make a trip to the Seattle Bowl, which will be played on December 27 at Safeco Field.

Taliban in Retreat
In the past three weeks, the Northern Alliance and other opposition forces in Afghanistan achieved sweeping victories in the war in Afghanistan. The opposition to the Taliban controls most of the country, including the capital, Kabul, since the Northern Alliance ignored the wish voiced by the United States and several other nations that Alliance forces not move into the vacated capital of Kabul. This unexpected march into the city left the United Nations scrambling to assemble an effort to ensure the creation of a broad-based government in the vacuum left by the retreating Taliban. Nonetheless, the liberated territory was the scene of much celebration. Afghanis threw off the repressive remnants of Taliban rule as women cast aside their burkas, men shaved their beards, and music returned to Afghanistan.

Scientists Clone Human Embryo
Medical researchers at Advanced Cell Technology in Massachusetts cloned a human embryo by removing the nucleus—which contains the cell’s DNA—of a human egg cell and replacing it with the nucleus of a human body cell. This egg cell then began to develop in an embryonic state. The researchers maintained the goal of the experiment was to create a source of stem cells to treat disease, not to create a human being. Researchers hailed these developments as exciting progress in the field of biomedical research and pointed to the possible treatments that could result from these findings. On the other hand, Republicans and Democratic lawmakers voiced caution and skepticism and hinted at a legislative attempt to ban the cloning of human embryos.

Res Ed Fails to Develop, Enforce Distribution Policy
The Office of Residential Education held a November 13 meeting with Resident Fellows and leaders of the Stanford Chaparral, the Stanford Ad Agency, the Stanford Review, and the ASSU Publications Board. Each group discussed how it was impacted by Res Ed’s ban on undergraduate door-to-door distribution. Despite the meeting’s purported intent of revising the distribution policy, three weeks later Res Ed has still not articulated a decision. In the absence of a credible policy, members of the Chaparral distributed their publication door-to-door on November 18. The Review will follow suit for this issue.
