Solar Energy Now Group Works to Change Stanford Energy Policy
by Mark Zavislak
Staff Writer

California alone produces more carbon dioxide per year than Germany. Approximately 85% of America's current energy needs are met with the conventional nonrenewable resources of petroleum, natural gas, and coal. Each of these sources emits carbon dioxide upon combustion. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has published reports that predict an increase in average global temperature anywhere from 1.4 to 5.8 oC. This explains much of what is driving the students involved with Solar Energy Now.

Solar Energy Now is a student-run organization that takes leadership from Kyoto Now, a movement dedicated to a U.S. ratification of the Kyoto Accords. On November 22, they set up a table in White Plaza near the Stanford Labor Action Coalition and the Redwood Action Team at Stanford (RATS). Solar Energy Now is closely affiliated with RATS, especially on the question of oil in Alaska. The Bush Administration has recently been considering opening up oil drilling in a small portion of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge (ANWR). Solar Energy Now is strictly opposed to the proposed drilling. Emily Goodwin, a leading member of Solar Energy Now, explained that they were "Trying to encourage citizen action and taking our democracy into our own hands by writing a letter to our Senator Feinstein."

At the table, members of Solar Action Now tried to convince students to sign a petition to Senator Feinstein. Part of the petition reads, "I urge you to resist all attempts to enact short-sighted energy legislation that would open the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge to drilling and perpetuate our dependence on oil." It continues, "Instead, we can help achieve America's energy independence by promoting energy efficiency, renewable energy sources, and alternative fuels -- the quickest, cheapest and cleanest path toward long-term energy security."

At the table, they were also passing out literature from Greenpeace concerning possibilities for California to be powered completely by renewable energy sources. The brochure entitled "California is Powerhouse" explained how a combination of solar, wind, geothermal, biomass, bio-gas, and conservation/efficiency would satisfy the current energy requirements. It also outlined the "Californian 2020 Vision" that "50% of electricity in California is generated from renewable energy sources by the year 2020."

Such a switch of energy production, however, is not without its costs. If "the total theoretical energy capacity" in the state were realized, they could produce only 74% of its current electrical needs. An additional 35% of conservation and efficiency completes the requirements with 9% to spare. But since California is constantly growing and consuming more energy, renewable energy sources may not be able to keep up. Additionally there is a cost consideration. A weighted average of all the associated production costs averages out at 7 cents per kilowatt-hour (kWhr). Energy today is considered competitive at about 3 cents kWhr. California would have to bear more than a doubling of all electric energy sources. Homeowners and renters would see their electric utility bills soar.

The Greenpeace brochure and the petition notably lacked mention of nuclear energy. Nuclear power sources currently produce 8% of all U.S. energy consumption according to a 1998 Energy Information Administration study, approximately equivalent to the current renewable energy production. Recent issues with power supply across the country have prompted the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to approve many plants' plans for an increase in energy output, totaling a 20% increase in capacity. The Nuclear Energy Institute reports that the nuclear industry has announced its own "Vision 2020," a plan to "add 50,000 megawatts of new electric capacity to the electricity grid, as well as increase by 10 percent existing capacity via power uprates and improved efficiencies by 2020." Nuclear energy production costs for 1998 averaged out at 2.13 cents per kWhr.

Amanda Roll Pickering, another representative for Solar Energy Now, explained that the primary issues that the group had with nuclear energy focused on waste disposal and general dangers with production. It takes hundreds of years for nuclear waste to return to normal radioactivity; in the mean time it must be stored in a secure facility. She noted the lack of a uniform nuclear disposal site in the U.S., and that currently all waste is being stored at the power generation site itself. Nuclear energy is not without its share of disasters: Chernobyl and Three Mile Island are the most famous. Chernobyl, the worst nuclear disaster ever, was based upon a flawed reactor which killed thirty-one people directly. At Three Mile Island, while a reactor was destroyed, the core remained intact as radioactive gasses were vented, and there is no accepted evidence that this ever harmed the public. Thankfully, the worst disaster in Western nuclear energy generation essentially did not harm a single person.

But setting aside nuclear considerations, Solar Energy Now is at least pressing the University to switch much of their electricity production from natural gas to solar and other renewable energy resources. It remains to be seen how far Stanford will take any solar energy initiatives.
