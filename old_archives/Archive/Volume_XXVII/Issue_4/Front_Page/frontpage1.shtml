Front Page
Five Students Arrested in Labor Dispute
by Matt Barrett
Senior Staff Writer

On Thursday, November 29, five students were arrested at the Stanford Hospital while protesting the hospital's subcontracting policy. According to an e-mail circulated by the Stanford Labor Action Coalition (SLAC) "8 students confronted (VP of General Services) Lou Saksen in his office, demanding that the (hospital)" refrain from subcontracting out 10 housekeeping positions."

After refusing to leave Saksen's office and refusing to cooperate with hospital security, the students were physically removed from the premises. Monica Henestroza, co-coordinator of SLAC, put her arm in the doorway to prevent the door from being closed and was hurt. While the students were being arrested, another student videotaped the scene and two others reported to a mass of student protesters outside the hospital who had marched there after convening in front of Memorial Auditorium.

Mr. Saksen recently announced that he would sign an agreement as soon as possible to subcontract out the positions. The hospital will purportedly save at least $60,000 a year by doing so. SLAC claims that the hospital's actions "exploits workers and denies them the right to organize." The ASSU senate voted on Tuesday, November 27 to endorse the SLAC campaign. The resolution cites the subcontracting procedure as unfair because the temporary workers who would be hired as a result are "historically paid lower wages, are not provided with health benefits, are treated with less respect, and cannot necessarily unionize."

Furthermore, SLAC insists that the amount the hospital would save is insignificant. According to student Owen Cen Li, a SLAC representative, "[The subcontracting agreement] will represent less than 1% of budget cuts, but will affect workers 100%." The temporary workers would be paid $8 per hour, $4 per hour less than the workers the hospital would hire. SLAC considers the wage rate unacceptable. The SLAC e-mail argues, "Saksen has shown no regard for the fact that poverty wages will be paid. . . .He believes that $8/hr is a living wage in Palo Alto."

Many students appear to take the subcontracting policy personally. At an ASSU Senate meeting on November 27, Ms. Henestroza remarked, "99% of the housekeepers are workers of color. Because there are students of color who feel that they are connected to these people, we are deeply invested in the issue." Ximena Martinez, an ASSU senator, noted, "[A lot of students]. . . .view the workers as their relatives."

Students expressed outrage at the arrests. Their cries of protest after the students were arrested ranged from indignant -- "You pushed them! You shoved them! You bruised them! This is not what an institution of higher learning does!" -- to the crude -- "some f**ked up s**t is happening here today."

One student proclaimed, "Students do not need to be arrested in order to bring attention to an exploitative, unjust, and discriminatory practice." Orlando Lara, claiming to represent only himself, wrote a letter to President Hennessy immediately following the arrests announcing, "I hold the Stanford Administration responsible for this and demand that measures be taken. . . ."

However, not all students agreed with the protesters. Michael Kives considered the arrests justifiable. "I fully support the arrest of the various students, because they had invaded Saksen's office. They were asked to leave, and were given several chances to exit on their own volition. Instead, they created a problem in an effort to garner attention. I have no respect for their tactics."

Jeffrey Davidson agrees with Mr. Saksen and the hospital's decision to subcontract. "Subcontracting is a necessary procedure for the hospital to continue to operate according to the high medical standards that the Stanford community expects."

The protestors who had convened at the hospital marched to President Hennessy's office, building 10 of the Main Quad, after the arrests. They arrived at his door, banging pots and pans, waving banners, and demanding "Hennessy's itinerary and his cell phone number."

According to Ms. Henestroza, "The [president] has control over the VP of the Medical Center, (and). . . .the VP approves the budget." Therefore, she, and SLAC, hold Mr. Hennessy accountable for the subcontracting procedure and insisted that he do everything in his power to change the system. Ms. Henestroza also observed, "[Former President Gerhard] Casper interceded in the past."

Upon arriving at the president's office at 1:00pm, several students from the large crowd tried to force their way inside. One student battled a security guard in trying to pull the door open. Eventually, three of the students were admitted inside to discuss the issue, despite the fact that Hennessy was not actually there. Students from several organizations, including MEChA and the Radical Cheerleaders, joined representatives of SLAC in the Quad. Throughout the afternoon, the assembled students protested both the arrests and the subcontracting process. They yelled, "Hey! Ho! Let our students go!" and "Hey! Ho! Subcontracting has got to go!"

Initially, the mob attracted the attention of many passers by. The protesters later dispersed throughout the Quad and went into individual buildings announcing the news of the arrests, imploring others to join in the protests. By 3:00 the mass had thinned out, but the banners were still visible and the remaining students were still vocal. One of them said that she would remain in front of Mr. Hennessy's office until SLAC's demands were met.
