Front Page
Stanford Clergy Discuss Terrorism, War
by Joe Lonsdale
Staff Writer

Following the September 11th attacks, members of the Stanford community turned to religion for comfort and mourning. In the weeks that followed, students turned their attention toward contemplation and discussion of the issues surrounding the tragedy. Religious leaders responded with their opinions on what we need to understand and focus on in this difficult situation. Their comments address both spiritual and practical concerns regarding our response to the terrorists.

Rabbi Patricia Karlin-Neumann focused on the emotions surrounding the tragedy. "Our family has widened. We feel an unexpected, perhaps unprecedented kinship with all Americans...the word 'community' has been transformed to include all of the people in the countless stories we have heard and seen and read, stories of victims and survivors, stories of tragic loss and miraculous rescue," she said in a High Holiday sermon. Rabbi Karlin-Neumann reminded us to allow bright light to shine into the darkness of the tragic day, by focusing on positives: "The terrorists could hijack an airliner, but they could not commandeer the humanity of its doomed victims."

Meanwhile, the debate regarding our appropriate response has found its way into religious leader's focus. Scotty McLennan, Dean of Religious Life, wrote a controversial essay, "What Would Jesus Do? Three Position on Christian War." He discussed Crusades, the idea of the "just" war, and the philosophy of complete pacifism.

Implicitly criticizing a short-lived attitude of President Bush, he noted: "President George W. Bush used the word Ôcrusade' to describe America's planned response in the early days following Sept. 11, but quickly dropped the term. . . . [A crusade] has traditionally involved applying an absolutist good-versus-evil distinction not just to enemy military forces, but to whole peoples."

Reverend McLennan admitted that the terrorists' actions clearly did not meet the criteria for just war--they attacked civilians, he noted--and went on to discuss non-violent alternatives to a violent response. He cited peaceful resolution as the logical conclusion of American values of liberty, equality, and democracy: "What can we do now as individuals to avoid the last resort of full-scale war? Certainly we need to let our voices be heard politically, from direct contact with our legislators and national leaders to public rallies and demonstrations which express our will. We should work with local media to be sure that peaceful alternatives to violence are being covered and that the language of warfare is not being used inappropriately. We need to find creative ways to unite with those in America from whom we might feel most alienated, or who might feel most alienated from us, including our Muslim brothers and sisters. We need to support each other, face our fears and work together to uphold the great American values of liberty, equality and democracy, and the unique American treasures of openness, pluralism and hope."

When questioned about talk of non-violence in a religious context, students had mixed feelings. One enlightening response came from sophomore Abe Gupta: "It isn't their [the religious leaders] chief objective to discuss what the United States is supposed to do in Afghanistan. They're here to help us spiritually, to help us come to grips with the situation and to look at it from a healthy perspective, without any of the hatred or frustrations that naturally arise in such a tense situation. The reason they focus so much on the option of non-violence is not because they necessarily disagree with the President. It's because all of us need to be willing to look inside ourselves, willing to seriously consider our alternatives, to make sure we're acting for logical, spiritually sound reasons, instead of feeding on self-destroying revenge."

Rabbi Patricia Karlin-Neumann also focused on nonviolence in her discussions, urging America to stand up to its moral values, instead of stooping to the level of the terrorists. She referred to a famous quote from the Hebrew script, "Ethics of Our Fathers", which reads, "In a place where there are no human beings, strive to be human." She commented, "We have firmly declared to the world that our values, our cultural foundations, our humanity cannot be uprooted by hatred, by violence, by fear. We pray that those values will also be realized in the days, months and years to come in our response to the terrorists. If our response to hatred is to be filled with more hate than they have, will we be the human beings our tradition demands us to be?"

While the majority of campus religious leaders urge us to consider a spiritually uplifting, understanding, and just non-violent approach, President Bush continues to proactively pursue a calculated course of military action in Afghanistan. None of the religious leaders have explicitly approved of the peace rallies or our country's actions in their published writings. Instead, they have focused on the benefits of the power to resist hatred, and the ability to understand.

Reverend McLennan spoke for the consensus of their responses when he insisted that the perpetrators be brought to justice, but found it useless to focus on this point. "It isn't helpful to say that it was the work of madmen or cowards. These carefully calculating people, willing to commit suicide for their cause, were neither. Why would they hate America so much that they would want to do us such grievous harm? Gandhi used to say that if only we could learn to put ourselves in the shoes of our opponents, 75 percent of the world's problems would disappear."

Shortly after the attacks, Reverend Maurice Charles looked at the situation from the terrorists' point of view, and gave a powerful account of a cause of their zealotry, and how the horror of their "religious" actions fit in with our own strong beliefs: "Charismatic leaders prey on those who feel afraid, alone and powerless. Their targets include those who have experienced a recent trauma, members of a downtrodden community, those without hope or whose lives have lost their meaning and - dare I say it - those who feel besieged by powerful governments or foreign lands. What can be more empowering to the powerless than to be called a member of a holy section on a divine mission to do God's will? Charismatic religious leaders discourage independent thinking and quash the voices of dissent, all in the name of faithfulness and loyalty to God."
