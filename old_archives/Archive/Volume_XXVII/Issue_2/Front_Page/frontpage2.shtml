Front Page
Numbers Point to Uneven Faculty Allocations
by by David Regele and Topsy St. Matthew-Daniel
Senior Staff Writers

Click for
full-size image
Resource allocation tends to be a touchy subject in most departments. What department has the highest student to faculty ratio? What department has the least student to faculty ratio? What is the ratio between classes offered and the number of faculty? Who needs more faculty allocation? How are these decisions usually made? The Review set out to answer these questions by wading through a mass of course catalogs and enrollment statistics, and the results lead to more new questions than they do answers.

In this study, the Review looked at nine representative departments: Computer Science, Classics, Economics, English, Linguistics, History, Physics, Political Science, and Psychology. To get the information needed, the Review gathered data on the number of faculty in each department (faculty including all Emeriti Professors, Associate Professors, Assistant Professors and Lecturers), the number of undergraduate majors, and the number of students enrolled in each department's classes. The results shown are for spring of 2001.

The numbers indicate that some departments have a relatively low number of faculty in comparison to their counterparts. For example, the Computer Science department has a ratio of 1 professor to 5.9 majors. The Economics department also has a high rate of faculty/student ratio, with 6.15 majors for each professor. The Classics department and Linguistics department have the lowest faculty/major ratios, 1 professor to 1.35 majors, and 1 professor to about 1.35 majors, respectively.

The departments with the greatest number of faculty don't necessarily have the most majors or enrolled students. English, Political Science, and History have the greatest number of faculty -- 77, 58, and 58, respectively. The departments with the highest number of majors are Economics (332) and Computer Science (301).

On the other end, the departments with the least number of faculty also have the least number of undergraduate majors. Classics and Linguistics have 27 and 18 majors, and 20 and 21 professors, respectively.

The makeup of the faculty in each department is quite different. The English department has the highest number of lecturers (22), making up almost 30% of its entire faculty. However, most departments have a similar percentage of Emeriti Professors, with an average of 22%.

The results are robust and consistent across two other metrics: graduate students per professor, and undergraduate enrollment in spring quarter classes per professor. Computer science has the highest graduate student to professor ratio: 8.96 to 1. Classics has the lowest: 1.2 to 1. Computer science also has the highest enrollment per professor ratio: 37.57 to 1. Linguistics has the lowest: 6.33 to 1.

Why is this the case? The Provost was unavailable for comment in time for this article. However, some of the departments have explanations.

Professor Gavin Wright, chair of the economics department, commented about the faculty allocation in his department. According to him, "the economics department has long felt allocation of faculty resources is inadequate to cover student demand for economic courses. The internal and external reviews that were completed in the Spring of 2000 confirmed that our faculty size is well below that of economics departments at peer institutions, and that economics faculty teaching Ôproductivity' is among the highest at Stanford."

However, help seems to be on the way. According to Mr. Wright, plans from the Humanities and Sciences Dean's office are in progress, albeit slowly, to expand the department in the next few years.

The political science department does not seem to be in a strain compared to its counterparts (1 professor for every 2.74 majors), but the numbers do not tell the whole story. According to Thomas Taylor, the undergraduate administrator in the Political Science department, the department needs more faculty allocation. "This fall we are offering 30 courses and seminars. Nine of our faculty are on leave all year and several more are on leave for the fall quarter only."

The low rate of faculty to students becomes even more critical when faculty take sabbaticals or retire. Mr. Taylor commented, "Recently two prestigious faculty members retired and we have not replaced them. I feel we are relying too heavily on visitors to help us teach our classes."

According to Mr. Wright, the economics department is also going through the same pains, with an "unusual number of faculty on leave" this year.

Overall, the data demonstrates that faculty resource equity is an area the University must look into if it serious about ensuring high quality education for all of its students, as well as a conducive teaching environment for the faculty.
