Front Page
Students Take Sides on the War
by Joe Fairbanks, Keun Lee, and Joe Lonsdale
Contributing Writers

"Bombings? Who bombed Afghanistan?" asked Stanford sophomore Joe Shermetero to his dorm email list. His question was surprising but not atypical; Stanford students' responses to the war against terrorism are as diverse as the student body that they come from. Some support the war, some don't, and some are indifferent. Some make this known in White Plaza rallies, some discuss it over email, and some even display it on their doors.

The most visible campus reaction to the war so far was an October 16 peace rally sponsored by the Stanford Community for Peace and Justice (SCPJ). The rally featured a variety of speakers and performances, including a Stanford student of a Kuwaiti descent, Talisman, the Islamic Society of Stanford University and Stanford Capoeira. Free food was provided by the group Food Not Bombs. Freshman and SCPJ member Anna Mumford commented, "I think it's great that so many people are here in support of peace and tolerance."

Dissenting voices attended the rally as well. Lara Mataac, a second-year law student, held a Star Spangled Banner and a sign saying, "We Must Defend Ourselves--God Bless America." "I am here to represent what I think is a larger body of the students who support the war. I just think that the military action is a more reasonable solution."

Lamia Afifi, a fellow at the Stanford Medical Center, summed up the event: "It's important that everyone's voice gets heard. That's what I think this is all about."

Other student organizations have also become involved in the debate. Just hours after President Bush announced attacks on the Taliban regime had begun, the Stanford Objectivist Club sprung into action by setting up a table in White Plaza with information regarding the war and a petition supporting the war that students could sign.

Stanford Objectivist President Jason Rheins said that his group was trying to show the President that he is supported in this war. "We are going to war in self-defense and to reinforce our moral obligation against oppressive terrorist organizations like the Taliban dictatorship and the al-Quaeda network." When asked about the possible deaths of civilians as a result of our bombings, Mr. Rheins said, "the blood of the civilians and the refugees is on the hands of the Taliban."

The Objectivist table was the center of several debates between war supporters and opponents. The general consensus, however, was that most people supported the war. Several people, including Stanford Alumni and tourists, thanked Mr. Rheins and member Christopher Fish for operating the table. One such person was a Pakistani man. "I wanted to thank them for doing the right thing and supporting this war against oppression and tyranny."

The SCPJ also staffed a table in White Plaza every day. Member Peter Stair, sitting at the Stanford Community for Peace and Justice table, explained his feelings concerning the war. "I'm just here to find out what the hell is going on. Not specifically about the war, but about the terrorist attacks in general."
