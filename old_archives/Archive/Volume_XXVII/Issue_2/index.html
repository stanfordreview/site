Students Take Sides on the War
"Bombings? Who bombed Afghanistan?" asked Stanford sophomore Joe Shermetero to his dorm email list. His question was surprising but not atypical; Stanford students' responses to the war against terrorism are as diverse as the student body that they come from. Some support the war, some don't, and some are indifferent. Some make this known in White Plaza rallies, some discuss it over email, and some even display it on their doors. ....Full story in Front Page.....by Joe Fairbanks, Keun Lee, and Joe Lonsdale

Numbers Point to Uneven Faculty Allocations
Click for
full-size image
Resource allocation tends to be a touchy subject in most departments. What department has the highest student to faculty ratio? What department has the least student to faculty ratio? What is the ratio between classes offered and the number of faculty? Who needs more faculty allocation? How are these decisions usually made? The Review set out to answer these questions by wading through a mass of course catalogs and enrollment statistics, and the results lead to more new questions than they do answers. ....Full story in Front Page.....by David Regele and Topsy St. Matthew-Daniel

A Word From The Editor
There are many parts to a Stanford education. Some are not too serious--like the mud tug-of-war shown here. Incidentally, the Review staff somewhat embarrassingly lost to a team of computer science section leaders. Other parts of a Stanford education are serious: meeting life-long friends, and identifying future goals and preparing to achieve them. Still other parts of a Stanford education are in-between on the serious scale. Many are not very serious in the long run, although they may appear to be when students are going through them; the housing draw, for example. But I want to focus on something that is often overlooked: academics, the part of our education that should be treated more seriously than it currently is. ....Full story in A Word From The Editor

Grade Inflation in America's Top Schools
It's exam time again. Which brings this thought: when it comes to assessing how challenging universities are, the task is easy, right? Harvard is harder than Fordham, Stanford is harder than NYU. Wrong. An honest student will tell you the truth. Once you make it to a top-tier school, whether you work hard or not, your academic record is likely to be filled with gold stars. ....Full story in Opinion.....by Eve Piscina

Freedom Forum Examines IHUM
As a Stanford freshman, Rachel Scarlett-Trotter felt her required Introduction to the Humanities class included few of the great works of literature and philosophy. Today, Ms. Scarlett-Trotter, Joshua Hawley, and Grey Baker, all seniors in history, are heading The Freedom Forum, a new student organization which has formed a commission to examine the Introduction to the Humanities (IHUM) curriculum and make suggestions for reform. ....Full story in Campus Focus.....by Josh Hawley
