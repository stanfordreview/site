Stanford Students Meet the First Female Prime Minister of Pakistan
by Justin Diener
Staff Writer

In a speech to Students and Faculty of Stanford University, sponsored by the ASSU Speakers' Bureau, former Prime Minister of Pakistan Benazir Bhutto laid out many of the issues that she dealt with in her administration. Many of these issues now must be dealt with in the United States' campaign against Terrorism in Afghanistan and around the world.

Her speech was formulated as a letter to students in which attempted to educate others about the current issue of terrorists, her accomplishments as Prime Minister, and the difficulties that the current and future administrations will have with Pakistan and in dealing with Afghanistan.

"It is important for the American Leaders and people to understand the difference between the religion of Islam and those who would use its name to spread terror. The two are very different: one is religion, the other is politics. Those who exploit the name of Islam are hypocrites they are criminals they are not clerics. Their goals are to establish theocracies of ignorance for their own manipulation. In the end they will surely be defeated. Just as America defeated communism the terrorists will be destroyed by basic human nature."

Ms. Bhutto explained that fundamentalists such as the Taliban function by controlling the access of their citizens to information and economic opportunities that would help them to see the evils of the Taliban regime. While Ms. Bhutto attempted to explain how the Taliban and other such groups functioned, she was quick to remind her audience that the United States had a role in their creation.

"During Soviet occupation of Afghanistan my country to became the breeding ground for religious manipulation. Thousands of idles were recruited, raised, and armed to fight the Soviets in the name of religion. They were taught to kill communist non-believers in schools funded by the anti-Soviet coalition. My country was left alone when world attention walked out the door to harvest the legacy of the war fought by a brave people for purist reasons."

While a portion of her talk dealt with the current war against terrorism, she also promoted several solutions to current problems facing countries such as Pakistan and Afghanistan. Ms. Bhutto, while remaining an ardent proponent of generally socialist causes, promoted democracy, freedom of information, and justice as the long-term answer to the current conflict.

"Let us never give in let us never, never, never, let fear stand in the way of justice, but that is exactly what they want. The greatest fear of the terrorists in my mind is the spread of information, social equality, and democracy. These three principles choke off the oxygen of terrorism."

Ms. Bhutto understands the need for continuing economic development throughout her country and the other countries in the nation. She proudly cited a statistic revealing her country's success: "Pakistan was described as one of the ten emerging capital markets and attracted more than 20 billion dollars in private sector foreign investment in the power generation sector alone. It helped create jobs for unemployed youth and it also helped create 50,000 white-collar jobs here in America."

In a speech designed to help students understand the current conflict in Central Asia, questions quickly arose regarding the Former Prime Minster's actions as the leader of Pakistan. In the question and answer period following the speech, allegations of corruption were quickly raised. These issues raised by the students included concerns about well known complains that several government—including the Swiss—have had with her personal finances and her current conviction in absentia in Pakistan.

While these questions posed to the Ms. Bhutto were rather hostile in their very nature, she dealt with them so that she was able to win over much of the audience with her responses. Excluding a small group of students who disagreed with her interpretation of the history surrounding her terms as Prime Minister, her speech was well received.

Faisal Rashid, a Stanford sophomore of Pakistani descent, characterized her speech, "Her whole speech centered on why a democratic Pakistan will lead to a more just Pakistani Nation, which is a supposed pillar of American Foreign Policy. She was clearly equating Pakistani and American goals."
