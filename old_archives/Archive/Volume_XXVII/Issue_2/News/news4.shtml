Stanford Economists win Nobel Prize
On October 10, 2001, the Stanford community became two Nobel Laureates richer. Professors Emeriti Michael Spence and Joseph Stiglitz of the Graduate School of Business became the sixteenth and seventeenth living Stanford Laureates, jointly receiving the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics for their three decades of work in the field of information economics. The two will share the one million dollar award with Professor George Akerlof of the University of California-Berkeley. Mr. Spence remains active with the Graduate School of Business, while Mr. Stiglitz has taken a position with Columbia University.

--- Bimal Patel

ROTC Programs Gain National Attention
In light of the recent terrorist attacks, ROTC has come under closer scrutiny at college campuses nation-wide. Since September 11 the ROTC debate has reemerged at Harvard, with a number of prominent alumni petitioning to have the program reinstated on campus. Harvard, like Stanford and a number of other private universities, discontinued its on-campus ROTC program originally in reaction to the Vietnam War and more recently in protest of the U.S. Military's discrimination against homosexuals. Both universities, however, currently allow students to cross-enroll in the program at neighboring schools. While many people see support for the military as the moral responsibility of any college administration, others advocate far more than the current restrictions on ROTC and have even petitioned the university to deny admission to students with ROTC scholarships.

---Dominic Heil

ASSU Sponsors Leadership Training
On October 14th the ASSU held a leadership conference targeted at training students with leadership skills how to work for community service and social change. Workshops included "Goals, Strategy, and Tactics," presented by Lolita Roibal, and "A Power Map of the University," headed by the ASSU leadership itself. Matt Brewer, ASSU President, touted this year's conference as especially important since "there are so many people who have started and wish to respond to the events of September 11th."

---Mark Zavislak

Stanford Duo Advances to TopCoder Final Round
Daniel Wright and Jon McAlister, two Stanford University juniors majoring in computer science, have advanced to the final round of the 2001 TopCoder Invitational tournament to be held Nov. 2-3 in Connecticut. Mr. Wright and Mr. McAlister, currently in first and second place, respectively, will compete for a $250,000 purse, with the winner receiving $100,000. At TopCoder's previous competition in June, the TopCoder Collegiate Challenge, Mr. Wright finished tied for ninth, and Mr. McAlister won second place. Contestants' scores are based on two criteria: velocity, the ability to write good quality code with speed and accuracy, and intensity, the ability to use a certain coding language effectively under pressure. TopCoder's competitions have attracted more than 4,300 software developers since its introduction in February.

--- Sheena Chestnut

Ethicists to Discuss Genetic Engineering
Two controversial philosopher/ethicists, Peter Singer of the Princeton University Center for Human Values and Peter Sloterdijk of Germany's Staatliche Hochschule für Gestaltung, are coming to Stanford to debate the ethics of genetic engineering and in particular the political and social consequences of decoding the human genome. Mr. Sloterdijk supports eugenics and a Neitzschean ‘human breeding' program. Mr. Singer does not object to the new possibility of choosing a child's genetic makeup, although he does object to the implications this would have for class in America. Their discussion, titled "Genetic Shopping?" will take place on October 25th in the Stanford Law School building, room 290 at 7:00 P.M.

--- Katie Mattson Kollitz
