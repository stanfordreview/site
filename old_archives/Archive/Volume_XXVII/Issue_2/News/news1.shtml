Stanford Scholars Host Energy Conference
by David Myszewski
Senior Staff Writer

California politicians have had considerable difficulty in solving the state's energy problems, so the Hoover Institution and the Stanford Institute for Economic Policy Research (SIEPR) co-sponsored a two-day conference on California's electricity problem with some of the brightest thinkers in the nation.

Ideas about the flaws in California's energy system and its possible solutions were presented at several discussion sessions and panel presentations during the two-day conference, which featured several prominent speakers, including former California governor Pete Wilson, former Secretary of State George Shultz, economist Milton Friedman, Secretary of Energy Spencer Abraham, Jim Sweeney, and several other Hoover fellows and prominent university professors.

Many of the speakers emphasized the immediate importance of the energy infrastructure in California and the rest of the nation.

The conference's keynote speaker, Secretary of Energy Spencer Abraham, outlined three key issues for the Unites States' future energy plans: greater energy diversity both in suppliers from whom energy is obtained and the different kinds of energy the United States uses, increased production and efficiency within America, and a modern energy infrastructure.

Implicit in that infrastructure is the need for transmission lines and pipelines that allow energy to be quickly and efficiently moved from one location to another.

"I need hardly remind the people in this room that we can have all the oil in the world, we can have the capacity to generate mega megawatts, but if we can't move the power from where it's produced or refined to the homes, businesses, and gas stations that need it, the energy does us little good."

Mr. Abraham noted that this summer, Chicago lost the use of just one refinery, causing a 34-cent increase in the price per gallon of gas.

"When even a small part of our infrastructure goes down, we feel the effects."

The conference also featured panel discussion with Jim Sweeney, Milton Friedman, and George Schultz, a panel that was moderated by John Raisian, director of the Hoover Institution.

Jim Sweeney
In that discussion, Mr. Sweeney, Stanford Management Science and Engineering professor and Senior Fellow at SIEPR, examined some of the political problems in creating new infrastructure elements.

"We need some infrastructure development. We really need more natural gas pipelines, and we really don't need the PUC [California Public Utilities Commission] voting against almost every infrastructure proposal."

Mr. Abraham suggested that alternative sources of fuel are increasing in importance. "We will also need to increase the role of renewables--and we will--by revamping research and development and increasing the movement of mature technologies--like solar, wind, and geothermal energy--to the market. We will also concentrate more resources on promising technologies that represent the next wave, such as distributed energy systems, fuel cells, hydrogen-generated energy, and fusion."

Despite every effort the country can make to meet the demand that the United States' high tech society will have in the near future, larger strides will need to be made in energy.

"We are simply not going to meet what is expected to be about a 30 percent rise in energy consumption in this country over the next 20 years without new production and without greater gains in efficiency. . . .Energy efficiency, conservation, and renewables, however, will never do the job alone."

Mr. Abraham pointed out that Mexico and Canada are two countries with untapped sources of energy that the United States should pursue. In fact, Mr. Abraham's first foreign visit was to Mexico, where he spoke with energy officials from several different countries.

"For too long, Americans have thought that our only important international energy relationship was with the Persian Gulf. Certainly, that region is important. But our Administration is looking beyond the Persian Gulf. We are expanding our relationships with other energy producers, and building new partnerships to strengthen our overall energy security."

Besides basic energy infrastructure, diversity of sources of energy is important so that the country does not become too dependent upon any single region or source.

Said Mr. Abraham, "We need an assortment of fuels and a multiplicity of sources. Dependence on one type of fuel, such as natural gas, leaves us vulnerable to price spikes, while excessive dependence on one supplier, or one group of suppliers, leaves us vulnerable to reductions or cutoffs."

Mr. Sweeny argued that one of the most important aspects of solving the problem is to stop making political decisions and start making decisions that solve the problem.

"We need to find ways to reduce dependency on political decision-making, because we see where that's brought us."

George Schultz, former Secretary of State and current Hoover fellow, agreed that the state's limited capacity to make good judgments about energy policy stemmed from simply having too much money that politicians thought they had to spend.

"There is only one reason the state bought high and sold low: it had the money. It's a moneypot just sitting there, and it invites use. The lesson I learned from this was: be sure any government entity operates without a surplus."

Milton Friedman
Renowned economist Milton Friedman pointed to prices as a key factor in the energy shortage.

"Economists don't know much. But we do know how to do one thing: create a shortage. If you want to create a shortage, hold the prices down."

Despite the problems that California faces, Mr. Schultz said that the original plan could have worked--in fact, it did for a couple of years--but the fatal flaw was that politicians failed to adapt to changing circumstances. When people realized that the current plan was flawed, they didn't alter the plan to remedy the situation. He conveyed this idea by an experience of his in the Eisenhower administration, where government offices were planning for several potentially difficult situations, called "alert planning."

"Eisenhower said the plan was essentially worthless. The important thing was the process of planning."

In order for a good plan to work, he argued, one must not only have a good plan, but one must be able and willing to alter the plan as it is needed, and that process of planning is what aids an individual in adapting the plan to changing circumstances.
