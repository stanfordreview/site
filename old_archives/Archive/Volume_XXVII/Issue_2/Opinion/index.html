Opinion
Grade Inflation in America's Top Schools
by Eve Piscina
Guest Writer

It's exam time again. Which brings this thought: when it comes to assessing how challenging universities are, the task is easy, right? Harvard is harder than Fordham, Stanford is harder than NYU. Wrong. An honest student will tell you the truth. Once you make it to a top-tier school, whether you work hard or not, your academic record is likely to be filled with gold stars.

Why? Well, reputation.

When I was choosing a graduate school, its reputation guided my decision. I knew that Stanford, my choice in the end, had a glowing reputation: first-rate professors, low attrition rates, and graduates with stellar GPAs and boast-worthy job placement. This was the school for me. In these respects, I have not been disappointed. Dropping the Stanford name always brings a great response. The scholars are world-renowned. GPAs are high, and it is rare that graduates aren't met with enviable career opportunities.

But there is a catch. In order for Stanford to continue to recruit top students, it must, like other schools in its league, boost the grades of academically so-so students, skewing the image of the student body into one that seems hard to one-up. Let's face it, some students expect to coast once they reach a school like Stanford, and sacrificing academic standards is a price most universities on this level are willing to pay to stay on top.

"But it's up to the individual professors," you perhaps protest. "Professors at first-rate schools give high grades to first-rate students because they have clearly earned them." It's a nice thought. But professors, like other "professionals" in competitive America, are looking to propel their careers forward. First there's tenure; then there's the peace of mind of being left alone. Students don't complain about 'A's; 'B+'s keep lazy students at bay. This leaves time for research. Professors whose teaching is associated with poor marks, in contrast, aren't going to look good in the eyes of either students or administrators, which can lead to esteem problems, loss of courses, or even bad committee work. These are the main reasons why professors knowingly -- and eventually unknowingly -- inflate grades.

Here's an example. Last term I enrolled in a course taught by a world famous political philosopher. I expected it would be difficult (the requirements included reading considerable portions of works by mindbenders such as Hegel and Heidegger). I figured it would demand considerable time and attention on my part, but I was excited by the prospect of working with this scholar and being intellectually challenged. Throughout the term, I was fascinated by the lectures and studied hard for our first exam. After taking it, I realized that I still had not grasped some of the material, and I thought that the exam grade would surely reflect that. Later that week the professor returned the exams to the class of sixty students. He declared, to my surprise, that the top half of the exams were "made A's and the bottom half were made B+'s." Not much motivation to try to grasp the materials.

Cynics might say that it's up to graduate students to motivate themselves, and that grades really don't matter. But this kind of practice actively discourages academic rigor. It is especially ironic in a class on philosophers devoted to rigorous distinctions. But this is not an isolated incident.

Of course things should be different. As an undergraduate, I was a student in a well-respected second-tier school that knew grades should serve as an honest assessment of a student's progress. There I was used to having to work hard for a good grade. Which meant, among other things, I had a good sense of what I had learned and what I hadn't.

Here at Stanford, I usually work equally as hard because knowledge and understanding continue to be important to me. But there are too many of my classmates trying a little less to understand, feeling a little less the need to challenge themselves, and believing a little more that less work will win them the same high praise as before.

Yes, it does occur to me: why shouldn't I, when my fingers are tired of turning the pages, go to bed a little earlier, skim some chapters, and remind myself in the back of my mind that a little less work won't make a difference?

Grade inflation helps keep the top schools in top shape. It guarantees that students will be grabbed up by corporations, as well as by other institutes of higher learning. Perhaps employers will be disappointed with some of their proteges, young men and women of awesome potential who have grown to believe that rewards come without hard work.

Woody Allen observed that success in life required only showing up. This appears most true, sadly, at places like Stanford -- where so much potential seems to evaporate in the abundant sun. Sure, go ahead and blame the sunshine. But from what I hear, the cold New England winters don't make Harvard any harder.
