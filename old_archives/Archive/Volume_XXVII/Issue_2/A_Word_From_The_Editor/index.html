A Word From The Editor
There are many parts to a Stanford education. Some are not too serious--like the mud tug-of-war shown here. Incidentally, the Review staff somewhat embarrassingly lost to a team of computer science section leaders. Other parts of a Stanford education are serious: meeting life-long friends, and identifying future goals and preparing to achieve them. Still other parts of a Stanford education are in-between on the serious scale. Many are not very serious in the long run, although they may appear to be when students are going through them; the housing draw, for example. But I want to focus on something that is often overlooked: academics, the part of our education that should be treated more seriously than it currently is.

Classes are the defining characteristic of the Stanford experience, but there is little public dialogue about which ones are offered, or what is taught. The majority of Stanford classes are taught by talented professors who teach material of high value, as Stanford's #1 ranking in academic reputation by U.S. News and World Report for six years running demonstrates. Nonetheless, disparities in faculty resources across departments and gaps in the core curriculum diminish the quality of a Stanford education and demand our attention. David Regele and Topsy St. Matthew-Daniel reach a noteworthy conclusion after digging through Stanford bulletins, analyzing enrollment records, and interviewing students and faculty: there is a tremendous disparity between the level of faculty resources devoted to students in different academic departments. Classics and Linguistics students on average have more support than do their counterparts in History and Political Science, who in turn are much better off than the least-supported students, those in Economics and Computer Science. The results are robust and consistent over various metrics of majors per professors, students per professors, and class size, and they deserve close attention if Stanford believes--as it should--that students deserve an equitable education.

Once students are in the classes, what do they learn? Nels Hansen, Dave Kim, Andrew Wright, and Ming Zhu set out to answer this question for the one common denominator of Stanford students: the frosh Introduction to Humanities requirement (IHUM). They discovered that there are substantial differences between the IHUM options: in the professors, in the reading lists, in the topics, and perhaps most importantly to the students, in the exams.

In response, Stanford students are speaking out to the Administration and the press, asking for a more rigorous education. Senior Josh Hawley responded to the lack of traditional Western Civilization material in IHUM by creating the Freedom Forum, a group committed to core curriculum reform. Graduate student Eve Piscina published an article with the Foundation for Academic Standards & Tradition criticizing the grade inflation that is currently rampant at Stanford and at its peer institutions.

Stanford, of course, also offers an enormous range of educational opportunities outside the classroom, and current events have only expanded students' options to involve themselves in the world around them. Joe Fairbanks, Keun Lee, and Joe Lonsdale profile how students reacted to the war in Afghanistan by staging demonstrations, by reaching out, and by discussing politics in their dorms and over email. Former Prime Minister of Pakistan Benazir Bhutto continued the dialogue with an October 18 Memorial Auditorium speech supporting the U.S. military strikes, as Justin Diener reports.

Terrorism has not eliminated the other problems facing our society, however, and Stanford's scholars are committed to giving them the careful attention and study that they deserve. The Hoover Institution and the Stanford Institute for Economic Policy Research held a joint conference on October 18 and 19th to address California's electricity crisis. Dave Myszewski gives you an inside look into the debate of the conference's experts, among them U.S. Secretary of Energy Spencer Abraham, former California governor Pete Wilson, and Nobel laureate Stanford economist and Hoover senior research fellow Milton Friedman.

Current events are offering Stanford students a vibrant education outside of the classroom, but it shouldn't take a tragedy to produce intellectual stimulation and rigorous debate on campus. The University can go a long way toward improving the quality of its students' education by reevaluating both its core curriculum and the way in which it allocates faculty resources across departments.
