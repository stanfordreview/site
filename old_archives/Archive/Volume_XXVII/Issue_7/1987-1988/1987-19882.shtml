There is More to Hoover than Controversy: Hoover Offers Many Benefits to Stanford
by Jennifer Bryson
Volume III, Issue 1: September 1988

(Click for larger image)
In the course of recent years, members of the faculty and administration have criticized a range of institutions at Stanford for being too "conservative" or "reactionary."

As a result, Stanford scrapped plans for the Reagan Presidential library in 1986, and earlier this year the Faculty Culture program beginning with the class of '93. But perhaps no part of the University has come under as relentless an attack as the Hoover Institution.

Some members of the Stanford community have demanded that the Institution be brought under "normal academic governance" (under which the University could hire and fire Hoover Fellows at will), while others hope for nothing less than the severance of all ties between the University and the think tank.

As spelled out at its founding in 1919, the fundamental purpose of the Hoover Institution is to impart knowledge and understanding and to further peace and freedom by its scholarship, research, and vast library holdings. At the time, Hoover donated $50,000 for the purchase of source documents dealing with World War I and the Bolshevik Revolution. By 1922 the acquisitions have grown to the size of a full library, and in 1957 the present name, The Hoover Institution on War, Revolution and Peace, was adopted.

A wide range of resident and visiting scholars have made Hoover a leading center for research and policy development in domestic and international affairs. the Institution's 120 researchers include renowned scholars such as Angelo Codevilla, Milton Friedman, Sidney Hook, Seymour Martin Lipset, Alexander Solzhenitsyn, Thomas Sowell, and Edward Teller; six of the Hoover Fellows are Nobel Laureates. Hoover Fellows and staff members have served with distinction in the administrations of both major political parties and advised Presidents and other elected officials on a range of social, economic, and foreign policy issues.

In addition to a body of first-rate thinkers, the Institution now has one of the world's largest private libraries. The initial "special collection" donated by Hoover quickly grew to encompass important social, political and economic developments in the 20th century. The library now includes 1.6 million volumes, 3,860 current newspaper and journal subscriptions, and over 40 million documents in the 4,000 special collections.

These vast resources emphasize the development of democratic institutions, international affairs, peace negotiations and movements, political ideologies (especially communism, Nazism, fascism, nationalism, and colonialism), political upheavals and revolutions, changes in the status of women, underground resistance movements, governments-in-exile, and military history.

The faculty members who oppose Stanford's ties to Hoover, led by professors Manley and Rebholz, have accused the Institution of partisanship. However, some recent research by Hoover Fellow George Marotta suggests that the charges can go both ways. Marotta found that, among Hoover Fellows, 34% are registered Democratic, 54% Republican, and 14% independent. On the other hand, the Stanford faculty is 80% Democratic, 10% Republican, and 10% independent. From his survey, Marotta concluded that the Hoover Fellows are not too different from the U.S. public in political orientation, although they may appear conservative relative to the Stanford faculty. (According to a 1987 Gallup Poll, the general U.S. public is 39% Democratic, 32% Republican, and 29% independent.)

Unfortunately, the great mutual benefits of cooperation between Hoover and the University are often forgotten. Distinguished faculty are attracted through joint appointments; research is supported through fellowships and grants to Stanford faculty; conferences are cosponsored; extensive library and archival facilities are shared; and many Hoover supporters become financial contributors to Stanford, and vice versa.

Last but not least, Stanford students also derive significant benefits from the presence of the Hoover Institution. Students have access to the wealth of resources in Hoover's library, work as research assistants to the scholars, and interact with Hoover Fellows who take part in campus speaking engagements.

All told, there is much more to Hoover than some of the critics might suggest. But do not just take our word for it: check it out for yourself.
