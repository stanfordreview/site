Ehrlich Warns of Population Crisis
by Mark Zavislak
Staff Writer

On Wednesday, November 14, Paul Ehrlich, the Bing Professor of Population Studies, spoke at the Memorial Church as part of the "What Matters to Me and Why" series of speakers. The Stanford Report billed the series as an opportunity "to encourage reflection within the Stanford community on matters of personal values, beliefs and motivations in order to better understand the lives and inspirations of those who shape the university." Previous speakers include Scott Sagan, who spoke about the need for improved international security. Mr. Ehrlich is perhaps most famous for his book The Population Bomb. In it he expounded on the need for awareness of an upcoming disaster in the world. In the prologue he wrote, "The battle to feed all of humanity is over. In the 1970s and 1980s hundreds of millions of people will starve to death in spite of any crash programs embarked upon now. At this late date nothing can prevent a substantial increase in the world death rate." Needless to say, the crash programs were not needed and the world developed without any such disaster.

But what remained important to Mr. Ehrlich was alerting the general populace to the dangers of overpopulation. Although those predictions did not ensue, they could have, in his mind, and that was the problem. In his talk on Wednesday, he emphasized the importance of "how we treat each other" and "how we treat the planet." In reference to the latter, he remarked at how little he felt President Bush knows about environmental science, a symptom that pervades society.

A central problem for Mr. Ehrlich is getting his values out there. He ventured that there is essentially "no university audience that disagrees" with the importance of population limits and the environment, but that there has been a "total failure to communicate [it] well to the general public." In passing, he attributed that failure to major efforts to suppress discussion and the "propaganda machinery" of our government.

Of course, there were many dissenters to Mr. Ehrlich's predictions of world progress. Julian Simon, who earned a Ph.D. in business economics, begged to differ. When Mr. Simon heard the dire predictions Ehrlich was making in his books and on television, he knew that they went against all the facts he had already studied. Wired magazine reported him as saying, "It absolutely drove me out of my skull. Here was a guy reaching a vast audience, leading this juggernaut of environmentalist hysteria, and I felt utterly helpless. What could I do? Go talk to five people?" This eventually led Mr. Simon to publish an article in Science refuting many of the myths that people desiring population control believed.

This article soon led to a bet between Mr. Ehrlich and Mr. Simon over the change in prices of raw materials over a decade. In 1980, Mr. Ehrlich picked five metals that he thought were indicative of an increase in scarcity of natural resources, and wagered $1,000. Ten years later, all five decreased in price adjusted to inflation, some substantially so. Mr. Ehrlich mailed Mr. Simon a check for $576.07.

Later in the talk, Mr. Ehrlich touched on human behavioral results of having such an expansive world culture. His concern is that human culture, our vast body of cultural knowledge, is evolving so fast that any given individual has only a small understanding of the global significance of events and technologies. As short as a century ago individuals were near full possessors of their entire culture, but modern life has drastically reduced that. More importantly, Mr. Ehrlich explained that our ability to "do" evolves much faster than our ability to "understand." That is, people often use technology without full understanding of all of its consequences.

Mr. Ehrlich moved on to talk about firearms, claiming that more and more arguments "are solved by firearms," and suggested with a bit of humor that the solution was not to ban firearms but "ban all ammunition," which got a laugh out of the audience. Additionally, he believed that the only reason that firearms are even allowed nowadays is a "misreading of the 2nd Amendment."

Towards the end of the talk, Mr. Ehrlich repeated much of the rhetoric he had been famous for. Previous "behaviors made us extremely vulnerable." We are "largely responsible" for the destruction of the tropics. Today there are "more people living in poverty" than "people were alive when I was born." "Maybe using the world's oil" is immoral of us.

Almost four years ago, Mike Toth argued in the Review that "The Stanford biologist has yet to acknowledge any fault in his career of failed predictions, and frankly, The Review is not holding its breath." Mr. Ehrlich seems to have held any such acknowledgement of wrong predictions for another speech. Indeed, he still claims that we are the "most overpopulated people in the world."
