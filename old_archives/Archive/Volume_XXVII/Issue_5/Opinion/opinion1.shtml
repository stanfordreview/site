Opinion
The Stanford Review Goes Postal
by David Myszewski
News Writer

For students at Stanford--no matter how much they want to avoid it--the post office is a necessity. Students dread the long lines and like to poke fun at the facility, but the post office may be better than students think.

During August--well before the terrorist attacks and the anthrax scares slowed down the efficiency of the post offices--the Review engaged in a multi-state experiment.

Members of the Review staff sent 3 letters to both the Palo Alto and the Stanford post offices from the following locations: Aurora, Colorado; Brookline, Massachusetts; Washington, D.C.; and Norcross, GA. In addition, members of the staff located in Palo Alto and Stanford sent 3 letters each to these four locations across the nation. There were a total of 48 letters sent--12 to Stanford, 12 to Palo Alto, and 12 from Stanford, and 12 from Palo Alto.

The goal of the study was to see whether there was any consistent, measurable difference in efficiency between the Palo Alto and the Stanford post offices. The Review found several anomalies in the delivery of the mail, pointing to one general conclusion: the postal system is inconsistent. Only on rare occasions did the Review's letters from Palo Alto and Stanford--which left on the same day--arrive at their destinations on the same day. Neither post office, however, was consistently slower than the other.

Stanford students who send multiple letters to the same destination often share a common experience--two letters sent at exactly the same time on the same day, going to the same destination, may arrive at their destination on different days. The Review had the very same experience. Of the sixteen groups of three letters that were sent, eight of them had at least one anomalous letter--a letter that arrived one or more days later (or sometimes even earlier) than the others. This result was not at all a surprise to Ray Chun, the Manager of Customer Services for the Stanford retail branch of the post office system. "It's quite common. There is a lot of machinery involved in sorting the mail, and it is possible that two pieces of mail can get split up in the mail sorting machinery."

If the machinery directs the mail in two different directions, as Mr. Chun suggests, two pieces of mail going to the same location may go to that location differently. The two pieces of mail could travel to their destination by a different truck or a different plane, for example. Since two pieces of mail may take different routes to their destinations or leave a given city at different times, it is fairly easy for two pieces of mail sent on the same day to arrive at their destination on different days. The post office is moderately consistent in getting letters to its customers in a fixed amount of time. Most of the letters--35 of the 48 total--arrived at their destination in three days or less, regardless of the locations from which the letters were sent. All of the locations from which we sent mail were close to big cities, which has an impact on this figure. (Norcross, GA, is close to Atlanta, Brookline, MA, is close to Boston, and Aurora, CO, is close to Denver.)

The fundamental problem facing the postal system, though, is sheer volume. Not only must complex machines must sift through millions of pieces of mail per day, determining who the letters go to, they must also determine which route they will take to their ultimate destination, and properly organize each of them so that customers may receive their mail in an expedient manner.

Without machines, it would be impossible to efficiently sort through every piece of mail that goes through the system. Humans cannot sort mail at anywhere near the rate at which some machines sort it--nearly 40,000 pieces of mail per hour, or about 11 per second.

The sorting process is further complicated by the fact that mail can come in all different sizes. Despite this modest complication, however, there used to be almost no difference between the length of time that it took for letters to arrive at their destination versus the time it took packages, according to Mr. Chun. The anthrax threat, however, has changed all of that.

"That situation has increased the security of mail. We have to do more additional handling and screening in order to get mail delivered. We need to protect our customers as well as our employees."

The Stanford post office has undergone some changes in the last two years, but according to Mr. Chun they haven't really made a difference in the speed with which the mail reaches its customers. The new facility has made almost no difference. "It's really the same. The new office was built specifically more for customer service."

The new post office makes it easier for customers to find what they need and to be served by the postal employees, but there were very few changes made to the infrastructure within the post office. The layout makes it easy for customers to access many services for which they would have previously been required to wait in long lines.

After the situation two years ago, when some workers of the post office were caught throwing away first class mail, there weren't really many staff changes made, according to Mr. Chun. "That made people more aware that things were going on--we just need to follow procedures more.. .People leave, look for other jobs; it's pretty much the normal way of life."
