Vice Provost of Student Affairs Awakuni Speaks About Diversity and Tolerance
by Andrew Wright
Staff Writer

Wright: Describe your experiences at Irvine, Santa Barbara, Cal Poly and Columbia. What were your biggest successes? What did you learn?

At UC Irvine, I started as a staff counseling psychologist and eventually became the director of the Counseling Center and Special Assistant to the Vice Chancellor for Student Affairs. My job title at UC Santa Barbara was Assistant Vice Chancellor for Student Academic Services. In that capacity, I oversaw the budget for the division and had responsibility for ten departments ranging from Educational Opportunity Programs to Admissions and Outreach. As Vice President for Student Affairs at Cal Poly, I oversaw 24 departments and 350 staff. My position at Columbia, Vice President for Student Services, encompassed all the business services, including the Bursar's Office, Housing and Dining, and other Student Affairs areas like Health and Related Services, Financial Aid, and the Registrar's Office.

What did I learn? I learned the importance of listening closely to what students, faculty, and others have to say about how we can better meet their needs. I also learned the importance of establishing strong, trusting, ongoing relationships with students that may become especially important when major issues arise and conflicts occur between the administration and the student community. If there is a foundation of trust in those relationships, there is a much greater likelihood that honest, respectful discussions can occur regarding difficult issues.

Wright: What are your goals as VP of Student Affairs at Stanford?

Since I have only been at Stanford for a very short time, I plan to do a thorough assessment of the division before I articulate specific goals for student affairs. Of course, future goals will include a continuing commitment to providing excellent service and strong co-curricular programs and initiatives.

Wright: What are your thoughts on the way Stanford has treated diversity-related issues in the past? How do you intend to address diversity at Stanford?

I have been here for only a few days, so I am still very much in the learning process with regard to Stanford's history on many issues. I share Stanford's very strong commitment to diversity broadly defined: age, sex, sexual orientation, veterans status, physical and mental ability, national origin and ancestry, as well as race and gender.

Wright: How would you define an act of intolerance?

The University has established an Acts of Intolerance Protocol for Faculty, Staff, and Students. This protocol defines an act of intolerance as, "conduct that adversely targets an individual or group on the basis of sex, race, color, disability, religion, sexual orientation, and/or national/ethnic origin."

Wright: How should those who commit acts of intolerance be dealt with by the University?

The Acts of Intolerance Protocol provides a framework for the university community to use when an act of intolerance is committed. The protocol recommends that any person who is a victim of an act of intolerance or who witnesses an act of intolerance report the act to the Vice Provost for Campus Relations LaDoris Cordell or to me, the Vice Provost for Student Affairs. If the act contains a threat of physical harm or violence, it should be reported immediately to the campus police. If the act of intolerance is in the form of graffiti, the police department will be responsible for removing it and for conducting an investigation. If the act of intolerance is communicated via email, the original message should be forwarded to LaDoris Cordell or to me.

The Acts of Intolerance Protocol is not intended to impose consequences on individuals who commit such acts. The University will rely on existing laws and organizations to take action where warranted against individuals who are suspected of committing illegal acts and/or of violating the University's Fundamental Standard.

Wright: Is there a public record of all acts of intolerance? Can the Review make a copy of it?

The Dean of Students office has recorded a list of reported incidents from the past two years. Tommy Lee Woon, Assistant Dean of Students, has been maintaining this record and can provide information to The Review. Information relating to incidents preceding this period has been collected, but it is not currently accessible, although it will be in the future.

Wright: Did the students recently arrested at the Stanford Hospital act "out of line," as the Stanford Daily said? What should the response of the Stanford administration be to events like this?

The students involved in the recent demonstrations are clearly serious about their views on the subcontracting issue. While I have been thoroughly briefed on this situation, my knowledge is based on second and third hand information and, as a result, I feel it would be inappropriate to comment further.
