A Word From The Editor
Editor's Note
I have a confession to make: the editors of the Stanford Progressive are actually nice people. The Review staff congratulated the editors of Stanford's new left-wing political magazine by taking them out to dinner after their inaugural issue hit the campus last week. Much to my surprise, the evening ended without the usual name-calling, placard bearing, and slogan shouting emblematic of politics at Stanford a la White Plaza. Instead, we had thoughtful debates about a wide variety of issues affecting our campus and our world: from ASSU resolutions to U.S. foreign policy, and from Stanford war protestors to the Constitution. And since what doesn't kill you only makes you stronger, the Review emerged with better ideas, stronger argumentation, and firmer resolve to prove that they are wrong.

I'll take a potshot in the interests of being a good neighbor and getting our rivalry off to a good start, but quite frankly there's little to complain about in the Progressive's support for the war in Afghanistan, critique of campus protestors, and dislike for the Stanford Daily. At any rate, I take issue with Michael Zakaras' conclusion, "Like any other war, this one needs to be watched closely. But unlike other wars, this one deserves our support." Which "other" wars is he talking about? The Revolutionary War that delivered democracy to the world? The War of 1812 that responded to aggression? The Civil War that delivered legitimacy to our democracy? World War I and II that defeated totalitarianism? I know the Spanish American War, the Korean War, the Vietnam War, and the Gulf War aren't the most popular in some circles, but they defended American interests and promoted democratic ideals. Mr. Zakaras could have tried to make a case against them if he chose to, but instead he makes the same type of far-reaching assumption as the anti-American and anti-Western protestors from whom he attempts to distance himself in his editorial: that he speaks for the good of humanity and needs no other justification.

I'll take off the gloves now and wish the Progressive a warm welcome to Stanford. I hope and expect that we'll hear a lot from them. And of course they'll hear from us.

As usual, this issue takes a critical look at what is happening at Stanford: both in the big-name speakers and in the behind-the-scenes services that students depend upon. Students certainly had the opportunity to meet the famous at the close of fall quarter, with an impressive speaker lineup that included Former Secretary of the Interior Bruce Babbit, Assistant Secretary of the Interior Lynn Scarlett, Stanford Professor of Population Studies Paul Erhlich, and White House independent counsel Kenneth Starr. We are fortunate to have speakers of this caliber come to campus, and I encourage you to take advantage of this unique part of the Stanford education.

The Review also examines lower-profile but no less important issues at Stanford. Kenny McCaffrey and Katie Kollitz investigate the finances of the ASSU and its companion Stanford Student Enterprises (SSE). The upshot: high-ranking ASSU officials make quite a lot of money--the President, Vice-President, and their Cabinet share a total of $20,500--and this money is provided by SSE, which refuses to go into any detail on how its operations earn it. Dave Myszewski conducts an innovative study of the Stanford and Palo Alto post offices by mailing letters to and from them and Review staffers around the country. He concludes that the Stanford post office isn't as bad as students complain it is, but that the U.S. Postal Service isn't entirely consistent, either.

In Opinion, Andrew Wright interviews Vice Provost of Student Affairs Gene Awakuni, recently arrived at Stanford from Columbia. Mr. Awakuni explains his perspective on the Acts of Intolerance Protocol, initiated to protect students from hurtful conduct but which some fear may become another speech code. Alex Robbins devises a different way to deal with extremists on the political fringe: recognizing them for what they are and removing them from the Democratic and Republican political mainstream.
