Front Page
Interior Officials Speak on Environment
by Justin Diener
Senior Staff Writer

Whether mere coincidence or a planned coordination, Stanford got to hear from both Bruce Babbitt, former Secretary of the Interior, and Lynn Scarlett, current Assistant Secretary of the Interior for Policy, Management, and Budget, on what role environmental policy should take in the future.

Mr. Babbitt spoke in Kresge Auditorium as the third lecturer in the Stanford Law School's Robert Minge Brown Lecture series on Environmental Issues. The title of his speech was, "An Environmental Agenda for the 21st Century" in which he used his experience as Secretary of the Interior to identify improvements to be made with regard to human impacts upon the environment. Ms. Scarlett was invited to speak by Stanford in Government in order to provide the Bush Administration's perspective on environmental issues to the student body.

Mr. Babbitt, in order to address several of the issues that face how Americans interact with the environment, chose to look at the challenges in the twenty-first century through the lenses of urban sprawl and how it was dealt with during his time in office. He provided a case study of development in the Los Angeles Basin of how controlling urban sprawl can be achieved.

"One day a fellow from the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service came in and said the California Gnat Catcher is going to be listed on the Endangered Species List. It lives in scrub land. And I asked why are you troubling me? And then the bad news came it lives in Coastal Sage scrub, which is in a Mediterranean style ecosystem that happens to comprise approximately 80% of the remaining undeveloped land between Los Angeles and San Diego."

Understanding the problem with the listing he restated for the audience, "The problem with a moratorium on development between Los Angeles and San Diego is that this isn't public land, it is private land and it is owned by some fearsome people."

Mr. Babbitt went onto describe how the listing of the Gnat Catcher under the ESA forced a change in land planning upon the communities of Los Angeles and San Diego. This created a whole new challenge; "We had to persuade people that there is more at stake than the view from their balcony." Babbitt then went onto describe how land use planning had changed in Ventura County.

Mr. Babbitt explained how the people took the power of the planning commission away from the local officials. Through a countywide referendum voters voluntarily limited the growth of their communities to their current boundaries to preserve the remaining land for other uses. He saw this widespread act of conservation as a result of people standing up for their beliefs and gave the following reason as a justification, "People support the Endangered Species Act (and conservation measures) because it embodies values which run deep which relate to ethical and religious values." Mr. Babbitt ended by responding to several questions regarding his role and experiences as Secretary of the Interior.

Ms. Scarlett began her speech by discussing how the 9-11 attacks changed and reinforced the approach that the Bush Administration through the Department of the Interior is taking to address important environmental concerns which face the nation. She advocated what she termed as the 4 C's approach to solving environmental problems, which are Conservation through Cooperation, Consultation and Communication. She attempted to distance herself and the administration from the policies of imposing heavy regulatory penalties on individuals for being involved through property ownership in environmental issues.

She spent much of her time describing how interested parties while they may share different opinions can find common ground to achieve environmentally friendly goals. In the case of a watershed in Wyoming she described how individuals landowners have decreased erosion into streams, and made their land more wildlife friendly by employing new wildlife friendly fences. Ms. Scarlett believes that the way to improve the overall quality of the environment is through cooperative efforts by interested parties to develop positive solutions. On the issue of punishment through regulatory rules she believes that they should be employed as a motivational tool rather than as the means to address environmental problems.

This approach, she termed as the New Environmentalism that she and the administration hope to implement throughout their tenure in office. During questions she was asked repeatedly about the drilling in the Alaska National Wildlife Reserve, which she explained that she supported.

Sid Gospe, a sophomore who attended the talk by Babbitt, commented, "I particularly found the stories about the land use planning in the Los Angeles area interesting." In regards to the way in which private landowners cooperated with the federal government Mr. Gospe said, "I thought the cooperation between the Irvine Land Company and the government was positive, however, I am afraid that this cooperation is an anomaly and not the rule for the country." He added that he believes future cases will lead to litigation.

Nicole Wright, an undergraduate who attended the Lynn Scarlett's speech gave her impressions as well, "I think she projected a very friendly and inclusive tone, she refused to play into the confrontational manner of some of the students present. She was very poised and articulate. However, I don't think that she answered some of the questions regarding financial incentives in a head on manner; rather, she answered them circularly. She frequently resorted to a good cop/bad cop kind of rhetoric where she said my agency wasn't responsible for that detrimental environmental practice for which some of the audience were brow-beating her."
