ASSU Funded by Endowment, Enterprise
Ever wonder what happened to the $70 fee that ASSU charged you at the beginning of the quarter? Most students believe it goes toward supporting the ASSU itself, but this is not the case. Rather, it funds a number of student groups annually selected in spring quarter special-fee elections. Stanford Student Enterprises, a collection of student-run businesses, funds the ASSU itself. "If you can grasp this subtlety and understand that no student money supports the ASSU," says Ross Davisson, ASSU Financial Manager and President of SSE, "you will know something that not many students do." He explains, "The ASSU is self-funded." ....Full story in Front Page.....by Kevin McCaffrey and Katie Kollitz

A Word From The Editor
I have a confession to make: the editors of the Stanford Progressive are actually nice people. The Review staff congratulated the editors of Stanford's new left-wing political magazine by taking them out to dinner after their inaugural issue hit the campus last week. Much to my surprise, the evening ended without the usual name-calling, placard bearing, and slogan shouting emblematic of politics at Stanford a la White Plaza. Instead, we had thoughtful debates about a wide variety of issues affecting our campus and our world: from ASSU resolutions to U.S. foreign policy, and from Stanford war protestors to the Constitution. And since what doesn't kill you only makes you stronger, the Review emerged with better ideas, stronger argumentation, and firmer resolve to prove that they are wrong. ....Full story in A Word From The Editor

Ken Starr Visits Stanford
Kenneth Starr, former Independent Counsel, discussed law and his career as a featured speaker at the Stanford Law School on Nov. 12, sponsored by the Stanford Federalist Society. Taking questions from undergraduate as well as graduate law students, Mr. Starr discussed a range of topics ranging from school vouchers to Whitewater investigation. Dubbing the Monica Lewinsky debacle as "the recent unpleasantness," Mr. Starr provided frank insights to how he managed the Whitewater investigation as an Independent Counsel. Asked about the best and worst advice he received throughout the investigation, Mr. Starr said," the most important advice was to keep my portion of the investigation narrow and focused." ....Full story in News.....by Keun Lee

Ehrlich Warns of Population Crisis
On Wednesday, November 14, Paul Ehrlich, the Bing Professor of Population Studies, spoke at the Memorial Church as part of the "What Matters to Me and Why" series of speakers. The Stanford Report billed the series as an opportunity "to encourage reflection within the Stanford community on matters of personal values, beliefs and motivations in order to better understand the lives and inspirations of those who shape the university." Previous speakers include Scott Sagan, who spoke about the need for improved international security. Mr. Ehrlich is perhaps most famous for his book The Population Bomb. In it he expounded on the need for awareness of an upcoming disaster in the world. In the prologue he wrote, "The battle to feed all of humanity is over. In the 1970s and 1980s hundreds of millions of people will starve to death in spite of any crash programs embarked upon now. At this late date nothing can prevent a substantial increase in the world death rate." Needless to say, the crash programs were not needed and the world developed without any such disaster. ....Full story in News.....by Mark Zavislak

Vice Provost of Student Affairs Awakuni Speaks About Diversity and Tolerance
Wright: Describe your experiences at Irvine, Santa Barbara, Cal Poly and Columbia. What were your biggest successes? What did you learn?

At UC Irvine, I started as a staff counseling psychologist and eventually became the director of the Counseling Center and Special Assistant to the Vice Chancellor for Student Affairs. My job title at UC Santa Barbara was Assistant Vice Chancellor for Student Academic Services. In that capacity, I oversaw the budget for the division and had responsibility for ten departments ranging from Educational Opportunity Programs to Admissions and Outreach. As Vice President for Student Affairs at Cal Poly, I oversaw 24 departments and 350 staff. My position at Columbia, Vice President for Student Services, encompassed all the business services, including the Bursar's Office, Housing and Dining, and other Student Affairs areas like Health and Related Services, Financial Aid, and the Registrar's Office. ....Full story in The Last Page.....by Andrew Wright
