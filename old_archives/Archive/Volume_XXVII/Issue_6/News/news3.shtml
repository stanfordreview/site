Faculty Senate Committee Removes Latin American Studies as Major
by Sam Shapero
Staff Writer

James Fox was selected the new Director of the Center of Latin American Studies in December
Stanford's Faculty Senate Committee on Undergraduate Studies met on November 16th and removed the Interdisciplinary major in Latin American studies. The measure keeps intact the Center for Latin American Studies, which runs the Interdisciplinary Program, but the Center may no longer offer baccalaureate or master's degrees in that major. All other programs the Center runs, including classes and the honors programs, will remain unaffected, and the students already enrolled in the major will be able to complete their degree.

The decision to not renew the major was simultaneously resolved by the Senate's Undergraduate and Graduate Studies Committees. The two committees originally discussed the possible nonrenewal of the Latin American studies major at its weekly meeting on November 9th. As an Interdisciplinary program, the major was subject to periodic review and renewal by the faculty. Following a recommendation from the Deans of the School of the Humanities and Sciences, both committees agreed to abandon the major.

Religious Studies Professor Hester Gelber chairs the Undergraduate Studies Committee. "The primary concern was the fact that the level of faculty support for the major was not sufficient for us to feel it could go forward," she said.

According to the committee, the Latin American studies program had been unable to find enough professors to advise the sixteen undergraduate and thirteen graduate students that wish to take the major. Currently there are only three professors affiliated with the program whose main focus is Latin American studies, although more than a dozen professors in other fields are involved with the Center. The program had in fact been on probation for most of 2001. The absence of a director for the department was a major reason that the Faculty Senate waited until November to remove the major.

The Deans of the Humanities and Sciences suggested the cut in November as part of a larger plan to refocus the purpose of the Center for Latin American Studies. Bob Gregg, associate dean, said that the decision to revoke the center's degree-granting status was made "without prejudice," meaning that the School of Humanities and Sciences has problems with the program but not the area of Latin American Studies. "Our hope is that the program can be restructured and strengthened," he said. The following month the Deans appointed James Fox as new director for the center, to fill the post left vacant when the previous director Terry Karl left on sabbatical in June.

Mr. Fox is an Associate Professor in Anthropology at Stanford, and has had a long time interest in Latin American and Indian studies. He expressed some concern over the university's dismantling of the Latin American studies major. "It's discouraging to take over a program that's been wounded, but I'm committed and devoted to Latin American studies," said Mr. Fox. "Without a director, the program was floundering."

Mr. Fox has not been the only one to voice complaints over the termination of the Latin American studies major. Several faculty have complained that they were not warned about the decision, including Alice A. Miano, Coordinator of Stanford's Spanish Language Program.

"As yet there has been no concerted effort by the University Administration to inform the faculty or the student body of this decision, despite the significant effect it will have on the entire Stanford community," Ms. Miano wrote in an email to students and faculty.

"Instead of improving the [Latin American studies] program, the University is discarding decades of hard work and award-winning academic excellence without extensively consulting the faculty, alumni and, above all, students. The top-down and secretive nature of this decision to terminate LAS sets an ominous precedent for administrative action to eliminate other Area Studies and interdisciplinary programs."

Many students have protested the University's decision as well. Bernabé García, a graduate student in Latin American studies, questioned the observation that there was insufficient faculty support for the program, saying he was "intrigued as to how the program could be seen as weak."

"LAS was the only program that allowed me to pursue my interests in Latin America while taking top courses in several departments," he said. "I haven't had a problem engaging with the faculty at all. Professors like John Rick and Jim Fox were ready to drop everything to see me and are still encouraging me to stop by their offices."

Some students expressed concern that the non-renewal of Latin American studies may pose a threat to other Interdisciplinary majors. One editorial in the Stanford Daily posed this question. "All students who care about area studies can now only wonder if another interdepartmental program will be next: African and African-American studies? Asian American Studies?"

Stanford undergraduate Alan De La Moras has already begun to organize against the University's decision to drop the major. He said, "I am taking steps to oppose and (God willing) reverse the University's decision, and I plan to gather information and support for a concerted student-organized opposition."

El Centro Chicano, Stanford's Latino community center hosted a Town Hall meeting on January 24th to address the University's decision. El Centro has also formed a committee to investigate the processes the University used to drop the major.

Both the Committee on Undergraduate Studies and the deans of Humanities and Science have expressed their openness to student input.

Ms. Gelber explained that the issues are not closed. "The decision is not a comment on the value of LAS, and if people wanted to come forward with a proposal for a new program the committee would be open to hearing them," she said. "We need a new proposal on how to reformulate the honors program so it can stand alone without a major."

Ms. Gelber added that students may still individually design a major with a focus in Latin America. She debated the claim that the committee's decision was a threat to other majors. "When they are reviewed, all IDPs are reviewed for the same criteria," she said. "The decision has no implications whatsoever for other non-departmental programs."

While the administration and students continue to discuss the renewal of the major, Mr. Fox has assumed the directorship of the Center for Latin American studies. Mr. Fox said he hoped to revitalize the center by asking faculty to sit on oversight boards. "I want to think of ways to increase the number of faculty who are committed to Latin American studies."
