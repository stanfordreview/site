Vigil Unites Pro-Life Students
by Andrew Wright
Staff Writer

Wednesday, January 23, 2002 marked the 29th anniversary of the U.S. Supreme Court's controversial decision in Roe v. Wade. In the middle of White Plaza, the Stanford Students For Life organization gathered for an evening candlelight vigil to observe historical date that granted women the right to have an abortion. The night was cold and the group stood tightly together in a circle, their hand cupped around the warming flames. The few who paused to watch did not linger for long, but quickly headed for a heated building. The ring consisted of not only SSFL members, but of many other students who were concerned about the issue of abortion. Noel D'Angelo opened the night by remarking that the vigil was to be a night of remembrance for the victims of abortion, born and unborn. He proceeded to read excerpts from the Roe v. Wade decision, along with abortion statistics. Some people were amazed to hear that 4,000 abortions are performed everyday, totaling 1.5 million annually since the procedure's legal inception in 1973.

Arguments against pregnancy termination by Mother Teresa and Mary McAleese were read aloud in the circle as eyes fell to the ground. The importance in which the issue was held by the group was evident on every face. A testimony presented by Gianna Jensen at a U.S. Senate committee hearing was also read.

Jensen survived a botched abortion in 1979 when she was supposed to be aborted by saline solution (where the womb and usually the fetus is injected with a salt-acid solution), but the abortionist messed up, so instead her mother went into labor and gave birth to her. The nurses called for an ambulance and she survived the ordeal, but as diagnosed with cerebral palsy. She was placed in foster parentage, and was formally adopted by her foster mother's daughter as a teenager. It took her many years to learn how to walk. Her testimony was mainly about how she did not consider herself a "botched abortion," as she was often referred to as a child, and how glad she was to be alive.

Stanford Students For Life has been active now for nearly a decade. Their main purpose, according to President Eva St. Clair, is to fight for the preservation of life until a natural death. They work to accomplish this goal through spreading awareness of abortion facts and support group for questioning mothers-to-be. "We have a variety of events ranging from political events, volunteer working with women who are going through crisis pregnancies, and we're getting involved with Project Rachel, which helps women deal with post abortion syndrome," St. Clair explained. "One of our main goals is to work to provide women with an alternative to abortion. Part of living in a pro-choice society is that there should be options available."

Among those who disagreed with Students for Life was freshman Anne Friedman, who said, "Roe v. Wade restored some fundamental rights and the anniversary should be celebrated."

Educating the public about the risks of abortions is very important to the group. Booklets and pamphlets are used along with word of mouth in the group's quest to inform fellow students about some of the lesser known aspects of abortion. In addition to the physical complications associated with the procedure, they point out the possibility of harmful psychological and sociological effects. According to St. Clair, "[Abortion] hurts families too because many times a family will pressure a women into having an abortion and that creates a lot of tension within the family."

Members of SSFL have personally spent time speaking with pregnant women who were questioning their options. Consequently, many have uncommon social perspectives on the issue. A graduate student from the School of Business, Andre Vanier, offered a glimpse into his reasoning for being pro-life. "One thing we need to do is step back and look at the broader implications of abortion." He believes that although many argue that abortion is a social and personal benefit to women, it actually destroys their social support system. "Imagine if abortion was not an option and a woman found herself in the situation of an unplanned pregnancy. Family and friends would be likelier to support her in ways both emotional and material because the inexpensive and fast option of abortion would no longer cloud the decision.

Additionally, we would likely see far more societal support to help women facing these difficult circumstances." But that is not the case, and instead, we see friends and families being torn and divided over the issue, leaving far too many women alone in a time when they need someone the most. The unhappy consequence in an insidious cloud cast over social relations and has a coarsening effect on our culture.

Vanier went on to explain the rationale behind the actions of Stanford Students For Life. "SSFL stands for the proposition that the best way to advance the pro life movement is through thoughtful and persistent advocacy of all pro life issues. We believe in consistently standing up and defending the vulnerable, whether it be the unborn, sick, or elderly. In the long run, that will be the only way we will build a better society. We believe that through reason and rational arguments, we can persuade others to examine the evidence and decide what type of society they want to live in."

The vigil ended with one minute of silence preceding "God Bless America."
