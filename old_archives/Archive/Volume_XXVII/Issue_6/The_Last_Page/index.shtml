The Last Page
Week in Review
by Harrison Osaki
Contributing Writer

Stanford Top-Ranked University for Community Service
According to the Washington Monthly 22.3 percent of Stanford students who earn money from the Federal Work-Study Program do so through community service. The Federal Work-Study Program, which was created by the government in 1964 to provide aid to lower-income students, provides partial funding for working students' wages. The Haas Center for Public Service, with the support of the Stanford administration, has played a significant role in encouraging students participating in the program to work in community service.

Claims of Stanford retaliation found without merit by court
A Santa Clara County Superior Court judge in January rejected Barbara Zylbert's claim that Stanford had retaliated against her. In 1991 Zylbert made a sexual harassment claim against a faculty member of the School of Medicine; Zylbert claimed that in return for her providing information for the investigation into the claim that the University would protect her from retaliation; her now-rejected suit alleged that the University breached this contract and retaliated against her. Zylbert has a related, still-pending suit against the Santa Clara Valley Medical Center which charges the Center with retaliation for the sexual harassment claim and complaints she made while in residence at the center.

Non-Profit organization calls Stanford Ôunaffordable'
A recently released study by the non-profit Lumina Foundation claims Stanford, among other colleges, is "unaffordable" for low or median-level income students. The report also labeled Stanford as "inadmissible", with admissibility dependent on whether or not the university accepts students within the 25th to 27th percentile. Stanford and independent officials responded by claiming that the report only examines average financial aid grants, and that Stanford financial aid is most favorable specifically for low-income students. Stanford officials also added that the report might be correct with regards to other, less well-endowed colleges. Students' reactions to the report were mixed in agreement and disagreement with the cost of Stanford.

National Academy of Sciences: Reproductive Cloning Unsafe
A panel of the National Academy of Sciences headed by a Stanford faculty member recently released a report recommending the banning of human reproductive cloning, but left open the possibility of cloning embryos for medical purposes. The panel cited animal reproductive cloning experiments, in which the implanted cloned embryos are often born deformed or die in the womb, threatening the life of the mother. The panel, however, recommended that medical research would benefit from other cloning experiments. Recommendations by scientists to completely ban a specific type of medical experiment, such as this, are extremely rare.

Experimental Quantum computer breaks new ground
A team of Stanford University graduate students and IBM researchers has reported the first successful usage of Shor's Algorithm, a method theorized in 1994 that uses a quantum computer to find factors of numbers. A quantum computer uses the quantum properties of atoms and is significantly faster than a standard computer in computing certain calculations, including factoring. Using a billion-billion (1018 ) specially-created molecules the quantum computer in this experiment successfully found the factors "3" and "5" of 15 -- according to one researcher, the most complex quantum calculation performed to date. Factoring large numbers is most significantly used to protect data by cryptographers due to conventional computers' difficulty in efficiently performing the needed calculations.
