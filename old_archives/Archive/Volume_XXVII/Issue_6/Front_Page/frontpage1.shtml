Front Page
Panelists Discuss Race, Events of 1968
by Henry Townser
Senior Staff Writer

In 1968, following the assassination of Martin Luther King, Jr., Stanford canceled classes and called a campus-wide convocation. The Black Student Union (BSU) held a rally beforehand and entered Memorial Auditorium while then-Provost Richard Lyman was speaking. The group then marched onto the stage and took the microphone from him. BSU member Frank Omowale Satterwhite then read a list of demands. Eight people connected with the event were invited back to campus to take part in a panel as part of Martin Luther King Week, a two week series of events commemorating Martin Luther King, Jr.

The eight panelists included five members of the 1968 BSU: Charles Countee, Warren Hayman, Delores Mack (the only female panelist), Mr. Satterwhite, and Keni Washington. Professor Emeritus James Gibbs, Jr.; Robert Hoover, an East Palo Alto activist and mentor to many of the BSU students; and the sole white member of the panel, Willard Wyman, who was Associate Dean of Students in 1968. During both the initial statements from the panelists and the subsequent question and answer period, the discussion centered on the events of 1968 and their immediate effects, as well as how those events reflect on today.

If there was one theme the panelists dwelled on, it was that things have not changed all that much since 1968. Mr. Hoover stated that "Racism hasn't gone anywhere" and "Racism is more subtle, but it's still there," while Mr. Satterwhite encouraged activists to use the black reparations movement as a foundation, stating that "In the '60's there was a black power movement which we could... get inspiration from. In this decade, that movement is going to be the reparations movement... Stanford students need to call the University to task for how it benefited from slavery."

Besides this and other assorted advice for the current generation of student activists, several members of the panel, especially Mr. Hoover, encouraged more black students to move back to poor black neighborhoods after graduating, stating that "Integration destroyed black communities," and that black college graduates needed to serve as community leaders, something several of the panelists had done. Ms. Mack added that "You have to earn a living, you may have to work for white corporations. But don't let that be an excuse." Other panelists, in particular Mr. Washington, expressed concern with some of the national political developments of the last three decades. According to Mr. Washington, he would not have believed in 1968 that "we would still be bombing small countries in 2002." He claimed that American culture encourages males to be violent, and suggested that President Bush, whom he described as being "of diminished intelligence," was unable to understand the subtlety of non-violent solutions.

Students at the event were eager for the panelist's advice on activism, asking them what issues should be the focus of modern activists. Mr. Washington complained that "the government in this country sets the priorities," and expressed concern that environmentalism was not among them. Students also requested tactical advice. Mr. Satterwhite noted that when he was on the BSU's demands committee, it had come up with between thirty and forty demands which, on Mr. Countee's suggestion, were reorganized into only ten, a rule of thumb which Mr. Satterwhite has used in his subsequent career as a community activist.

At the end of the evening, the BSU awarded all the panelists, as well as other members of the 1968 BSU who were in the audience, with medals.
