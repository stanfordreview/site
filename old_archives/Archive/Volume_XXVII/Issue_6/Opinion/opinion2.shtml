Opinion
Orange County: Failure in Film
by Joe Lonsdale
Staff Writer

Orange County is a coming of age comedy that begins with a bout of laughter, but is ripped apart like paper machete facing off against a cannonball when it attempts to find meaning and sentiment beneath its shallow exterior. Shaun Brumder (Colin Hanks) is a bright and active but otherwise typical high school student who whimsically tours his PG-13 Southern California hometown, which is filled with sexual references, crude language, and a principal who licks his lips at the prospect of having pop-diva Britney Spears give their graduation speech. Shaun cares only about surfing and "getting laid". According to the movie's official website:

"That idyllic existence changes irrevocably one day. A friend's freak surfing accident causes Shaun to reassess things, and as he ponders, he discovers a treasure. Buried in the familiar sand, almost as if it is waiting for Shaun, is a copy of Marcus Skinner's novel Straight Jacket. He reads it ... and re-reads it, obsessively. Epiphany strikes. His destiny is not on a surfboard. He has a new passion. Shaun Brumder wants to be a writer."

After somehow finding the Stanford professor's book (almost as if it is waiting for Shaun!) in the sand and reading it "fifty-two times," Shaun becomes obsessed with writing and wants to go to Stanford. Unfortunately, his counselor sends in the wrong transcript, and he is rejected. Meanwhile, a ditzy classmate is accepted because her grandfather is on Stanford's "Board." One thing leads to another, and the classmate's grandfather visits Shaun's home, only to be completely revolted by his dysfunctional family. "Stanford is for the best and the brightest," the stern old man lectures the aspiring writer. "As long as I live, you'll never go to Stanford."

In desperation, Shaun, with his brother and girlfriend, leaves on a late-night escapade to Stanford. Our beautiful campus is played by an unimpressive UCLA, which is seen only at night. The Dean of Admissions, who is mistakenly played by a man, is apathetic and only becomes helpful when he accidentally consumes Shaun's brother's Ecstasy pills from an Excedrin bottle.

Eventually, Shaun finds that girls at Stanford (played by gorgeous models) can be just as brainless as those at his hometown, and after a brief Forrester-like encounter with his idol Professor Skinner, he returns home. The movie ends with requisite sentimentality and a coming together of family that fools only the completely unaware into thinking there was actual meaning behind the sequence of events to which they were just subjected.

Even the film's web-site (http://www.orangecountymovie.com/) demonstrates the incompatibility of Orange County's messages. The site contains the SAT Vocabulary shown above: idyllic, epiphany, and irrevocably; yet its front page shows Shaun with orange slices over his eyes, and his drugged-out brother, played by comedian Jack Black, with an orange in his mouth. Perhaps the producers do not realize that this antic is only slightly amusing, assuming, that is, that you're tired and highly intoxicated.

The movie appears to the dull-witted as "smarter than average" because it attempts to merge the simple sentiment of appreciating those who love you and the "deep" pursuit of becoming a writer with the intellectual void of today's teen movies. Mixing party scenes and drug antics with these (lame) messages creates struggling currents within the film; at times, you feel like you are watching little snippets of several detached movies, half of which are boring. In the end, the film not only fails in its attempt to demonstrate a three-digit IQ, but also loses some of its humor and all trace of being hip thanks to its vain attempt at intellectual and emotional import.

My suggestion for Saturday night: watch Lord of the Rings a second time.
