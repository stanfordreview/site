Panelists Discuss Race, Events of 1968
In 1968, following the assassination of Martin Luther King, Jr., Stanford canceled classes and called a campus-wide convocation. The Black Student Union (BSU) held a rally beforehand and entered Memorial Auditorium while then-Provost Richard Lyman was speaking. The group then marched onto the stage and took the microphone from him. BSU member Frank Omowale Satterwhite then read a list of demands. Eight people connected with the event were invited back to campus to take part in a panel as part of Martin Luther King Week, a two week series of events commemorating Martin Luther King, Jr. ....Full story in Front Page.....by Henry Townser

A Word From The Editor
It seems like the world is divided into two camps these days: those campaigning against injustice and those seeking to justify themselves. More often than not, people have to do both. In a world where the meaning of "is" is open to debate, the concept of justice is too often used as simply another type of ammunition--a political type--between enemies. We're just, they're unjust. Terrorism and civil liberties, the Palestinian-Israeli conflict, and Enron's collapse, to name only the most visible current examples, pit the just against the unjust, or so the popular press tells us. And the spokesmen for any given movement are only too happy to reinforce the point. ....Full story in Editor's Note.....by Scott Rasmussen

George Schultz Speaks at Hoover
On January 16, members of the Stanford community had the opportunity to hear from former Secretary of State George P. Schultz. He gave a speech entitled "A More Accountable World," and answered related questions at the Stauffer Auditorium at the Hoover Institution. Mr. Schultz served under President Reagan as the sixtieth U.S. Secretary of State, and at the end of his term, January 19, 1989, was awarded the Medal of Freedom, the highest possible civilian honor. In recent years he co-wrote Economic Policy, Beyond the Headlines (1998), and published his best-selling memoir Turmoil and Triumph: My Years as Secretary of State (1993). ....Full story in News.....by Mark Zavislak

Vigil Unites Pro-Life Students
Wednesday, January 23, 2002 marked the 29th anniversary of the U.S. Supreme Court's controversial decision in Roe v. Wade. In the middle of White Plaza, the Stanford Students For Life organization gathered for an evening candlelight vigil to observe historical date that granted women the right to have an abortion. The night was cold and the group stood tightly together in a circle, their hand cupped around the warming flames. The few who paused to watch did not linger for long, but quickly headed for a heated building. ....Full story in News.....by Andrew Wright

Faculty Senate Committee Removes Latin American Studies as Major
Stanford's Faculty Senate Committee on Undergraduate Studies met on November 16th and removed the Interdisciplinary major in Latin American studies. The measure keeps intact the Center for Latin American Studies, which runs the Interdisciplinary Program, but the Center may no longer offer baccalaureate or master's degrees in that major. All other programs the Center runs, including classes and the honors programs, will remain unaffected, and the students already enrolled in the major will be able to complete their degree. ....Full story in News.....by Sam Shapero

Mineta is Repeating the Internment Mistake
Searching for terrorists trying to board airplanes is like looking for the proverbial needle in a haystack. Luckily, we know which hundredth of the haystack to look in. The Al Queda terrorist network that we are at war with is composed virtually entirely of young men of Arab, Persian, Pakistani or other Muslim descent. Through a combination of racial, national, ethnic, age and sex profiling, we can focus heightened security on the tiny subgroup of passengers that any terrorists are virtually certain to fall into. Knowing where to look greatly enhances our capacity to interdict terrorism, yet airport security personnel have been ordered not to focus their search where they know the terrorists are. Why? Because Department of Transportation Secretary Norman Mineta and his family were sent to an internment camp along with 110,000 others from the Japanese immigrant community during WWII. He thinks that gives him special insight into the issue today. In fact, he is repeating the mistake that was made in WWII. ....Full story in Opinion.....by Alec Rawls
