Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
It seems like the world is divided into two camps these days: those campaigning against injustice and those seeking to justify themselves. More often than not, people have to do both. In a world where the meaning of "is" is open to debate, the concept of justice is too often used as simply another type of ammunition--a political type--between enemies. We're just, they're unjust. Terrorism and civil liberties, the Palestinian-Israeli conflict, and Enron's collapse, to name only the most visible current examples, pit the just against the unjust, or so the popular press tells us. And the spokesmen for any given movement are only too happy to reinforce the point. What is justice, then? And how do we distinguish the just from the unjust? It's an age-old question and I probably won't be able to do it justice myself, but it's one well worth considering. If we as a society are serious about ending the conflicts of our day rather than exacerbating them, a clear notion of justice is an irreplaceable requirement.

The most helpful definition of justice that I have encountered is that of Lucilius A. Emery. In Concerning Justice, he writes, "Justice is the equilibrium between the full freedom of the individual and the restrictions thereon necessary for the safety of the society . . . Restraint with necessary limits only, the necessity of which can be seen, arouses no resentment; on the contrary, it satisfies the individual, favors harmonious cooperation, profits society and increases the happiness of its members, through the appreciation of that necessity" (1914, p. 57).

In principle, justice is the intersection of individual rights and societal welfare. In practice, of course, knowing the "necessary limits" in a specific case is as difficult as defining justice itself, so the American legal tradition has tended to rely on precedent to sort out the details. That doesn't mean, however, that we can simply recuse ourselves from taking a moral stance on the great questions of our day simply because we find them too difficult. Rather, I believe that it is possible even in this postmodern age to say something of value about justice. When the exact equilibrium of rights is in question, we should err on the side of the individual at the expense of society for the following reasons:

1. The individual exists before society. Society only exists for the benefit of the individual. Whether through an implicit or explicit social contract, man joins society in order to maximize the enjoyment of his natural rights of life, liberty, and property. When society fails to protect these rights, Locke reminds us that it is man's duty to revolt and create a new, more just society.

2. A culture of individual rights is consistent with democracy. Democracy is dependent upon the active participation of each of its citizens in political life. Subordinating the rights of any citizen to the prerogatives of the state risks disenfranchising the minority at the expense of the majority. Countless examples throughout history tragically illustrate this. The United States is great precisely because it harnesses the energies of each of its individuals through an egalitarian political order.

3. Individual rights are the driving forces behind societal progress. Individuals performed the great feats of history, not governments. Governments that recognize this fact give individuals the freedom to identify and pursue their own aims, and to better the lives of their fellow men in the process. Governments that don't recognize this fact mistakenly assume that change can be dictated and in turn are swept away by the winds of change themselves.
