The Week in Review
Stanford Hospital Pilots BioTerrorism Tech
A new medical technology being tested at Stanford Hospital will be able to cut down on reaction time significantly in responding to and containing infectious diseases. The system, called BASIICS (Biothreat Active Surveillance Integrated Information and Communication System), maintains remote real time monitoring of patients to detect patterns of symptoms that might foreshadow an outbreak of disease. As some early symptoms are simple, such as fever and rash, they might never be reported. The system records answers regarding the patient's symptoms and automatically sends them to a central hub for aggregate analysis. Developed by Mountain View-based Health Hero Network, BASIICS is a revolutionary response to the threat of BioTerrorism.

Lab Restrictions Proposed for Pathogens
Sen Diane Feinstein (D-Calif) introduced a measure to place new restrictions on the research of pathogens and toxins in academic laboratories, citing recent FBI subpoenas to universities researching anthrax and the speculation that academic laboratories might have been the source of some strains of anthrax used in domestic bioterrorism. The measure would include tighter certification requirements and background checks for all students working with pathogens. Although many researchers applaud the proposal and call for tighter lab security, some believe the measure would hamper anthrax research at a time when it is most desperately needed.

ASSU Senate Votes to Support Affirmative Action
In a 13-0-2 vote last Tuesday, the ASSU Undergraduate Senate voted to endorse affirmative action and progressive hiring policies on the part of the University. The vote drew heated debate as it set a new precedent for action by the Senate and also because the Senate had refrained from making political endorsements after the student body amended its Constitution to limit its powers following a resolution against the Gulf War in 1991. The vote was called to reaffirm the Advocacy Committee endorsement of an SOCC teach-in on affirmative action.

Stanford 1st Among all University Fundraising
With over $580 billion, Stanford University was ranked 8th overall by the Chronicle of Philanthropy when compared with fund-raising organizations across the country. Thanks in part to the $1 billion Campaign for Undergraduate Education, a large portion of which is allocated to Undergraduate Financial Aid, Stanford University came out on top as the most successful fundraising college/university. Harvard University was one spot lower, at 9th. Perhaps not coincidently, Stanford's tuition is also expected to remain lower than its east-coast counterparts.

Stanford One Step Closer to the 2012 Olympics
The Bay Area was selected as one of the four finalists for the American Olympic bid for the 2012 Summer Games. The other members of the final four include Washington D.C., Houston, and New York City. Besides boosting the already top-notch economic, technological and environmental resources of the Bay Area, a successful bid would also bring tremendous publicity to the University itself in the international arena. The Bay Area bid would call for an extensive expansion and/or renovation of Stanford's facilities, including a overhaul of the stadium to accommodate the opening and closing ceremonies.

Anthrax Scare on Campus
The Office of Risk Management was evacuated twice on October 22 and October 30 due to the appearance of two separate letters containing unidentified white powders. Both evacuations were initiated within minutes without any difficulties. The police, fire, environmental health and safety groups were all called to ensure an orderly and effective response. After a thorough investigation, the FBI pronounced both suspicious letters to be non-hazardous. The building was then reopened to the public. Due to these two scares, Stanford is currently working with local officials to develop a new method for mail delivery. The exact source of the two letters is still under investigation.

Stanford Juniors Take Programming Prize
Two Stanford juniors, Jon McAlister and Daniel Wright, finished first and second, respectively, in the Nov 2 and 3 TopCoder Invitational, taking half the prize pool of $250,000. The money is the least of their rewards, however, as the TopCoder tournament is open not just to college students but also to professional programmers, meaning that on top of their prized money is the prestige of being among the top programmers in the country. TopCoder, Inc., holds tournaments to identify and attract exceptional programmers, such as McAlister and Wright, who have been TopCoder members since April, and have already won over $30,000 combined in previous competitions.

Men's Basketball Begins Season with a Win
13th Ranked Stanford Men's Basketball returned to action this past Tuesday defeating Athletes in Action in an exhibition 100-81. Highly touted recruits Josh Childress and Chris Hernandez made their debuts for Stanford. Childress scored 14 points and Hernandez went 3-3 from the three point line. Casey Jacobsen led the Cardinal with 23 points on 8-13 shooting while also dishing out 6 assists and Tony Giovacchini made his first start at point guard handing out five assists while committing no turnovers.

Football's Fiesta Bowl Hopes Fall Short
Despite 2 amazing victories over #5 Oregon and #4 UCLA, Stanford Football's most recent loss to #10 Washington appears to have eliminated almost any chance of the Cardinal going to the Tostitos Fiesta Bowl as hoped. With each Pac-10 Team having two conference games remaining, the most likely scenario is for the Cardinal to be traveling to El Paso for the Sun Bowl on December 31st, but the Holiday Bowl, Seattle Bowl, and Las Vegas Bowls are also options. Stanford's final conference game is Big Game versus Cal on Nov. 17 at home at 12:30.

Collins Twins debut in NBA
The famous Collins Twins who led Stanford to three Consecutive Pac-10 Basketball Titles have made their way to the NBA. Jason Collins, who was drafted 18th in the first round by the Houston Rockets and then traded to the New Jersey Nets grabbed a rebound in eight minutes in his debut versus Boston on October 31, 2001. In his second game he scored 4 points, grabbed 5 boards, and handed out 2 assists in 20 minutes of playing time versus the Detroit Pistons. Older brother Jarron, who was drafted by the Utah Jazz with the 53rd pick in the second round, began the season on the injured list and has yet to play in a regular season game.
