ASSU Sparks Affirmative Action Debate
by Andrew Wright
Staff Writer

Stanford is a place of spirited debates. However, even in the most contentious of circles, there exist issues that tend to be avoided, especially those involving race and ethnicity, and even more specifically, affirmative action.

Until recently, sensitive subjects like affirmative action were frequently skirted for the sake of peace, but that all changed last week when the ASSU Undergraduate Senate made a controversial decision to endorse affirmative action in college admissions.

Immediately, campus curiosity was aroused, and some questions regarding Stanford's admission policies began to sound through the dorms. As discussion spread, and with it, rumors, the Review set out to uncover the facts about the Office of Admissions and affirmative action.

Dean of Admissions Robin Mamlet was ready to respond. "Let me state for the record that Stanford does not discriminate in the admission process on the basis of either race or gender."

She continued, saying that the University has "no quotas of any kind," referring to the famous U.S. Supreme Court's Bakke vs. University of California decision, which she called an "opinion." In the controversial 1978 ruling, Justice Lewis E. Powell, Jr. declared racial quotas unconstitutional, but said that universities may use race as one of many factors to determine admission. The wording of the decision is commonly considered to leave room for interpretation, and most universities continue to find a way to achieve what Ms. Mamlet calls "educational pluralism."

The Dean of Admissions went on to say that there are several factors considered when the committee examines applications. "We look for a confluence of ability, preparation, and engagement. . . .admission to Stanford is not something that can be reduced to scores and grade point averages." Ms. Mamlet did not shy away when asked if the demographics of the student body would be different if the application did not have a box for the applicant's race. "Race and ethnicity is one of the factors we note when making our decisions."

An Assistant Director of Admissions also admitted that there were certain "target groups" to whom the committee would give extra consideration for acceptance. She was, however, unwilling to specify the groups. Another question dealt with the issue of gender. According to College Board statistics, American high school females have markedly better grades than males, and SAT scores that are approximately equal. Given these facts, students are wondering why the admissions committee projected an equal number of males and females to enroll.

Ms. Mamlet explained, "The real question here is not about national statistics, but that it is a question of whether female applicants to Stanford have better statistics than male applicants." Although she said that she does not believe they do, she also admitted, "We have yet to run an analysis on this."

The issue of native or home language is a delicate one. In an email that is on the record, Ms. Mamlet wrote, "The fact that a language other than English might be spoken at home is neither a Ôpro' nor a Ôcon': but it *is* an important part of the picture."

Comments from the student body are no less ambiguous. The students want to talk about it, but are afraid of stepping on fingers and setting off an argument. Most of them would only speak to the Review off the record. Mithulan Jegapragasan, a freshman from Sri Lanka, had this to say: "It's complex. I can see how some people might think that an applicant who is relatively under-qualified might be admitted, but something that you have to remember, and that is that the admissions committee is trying to sum up an applicant by way of a few pieces of paper, and that is hard to do, no matter who you are evaluating."

Another student, who wished to be identified as an anonymous African-American, seemed to appreciate Stanford's policies, however vague they might be. "I think fifty percent of what you learn in college is from the classroom, and fifty percent is from the things you experience, and that's why diversity is important."

Similar cautious answers are being heard in the CoHo and in the Dining Halls. Everyone knows the admissions committee implements affirmative action, but few students, if any, know how. The answers from the Administration are vague and ambiguous, which only leads to more discussion on the Farm. "Yes, ethnicity matters," "but there is no quota." "No, gender doesn't matter," "but Stanford is magically 50/50 male/female." "Language?" "It's neither good nor bad but it *is* important." Though their opinions differ, their question is the same: What is the official policy of Stanford's Office of Admissions, and why don't we know it?
