Stanford Undergraduate Student Body Now Majority Minority
by Joe Lonsdale
Staff Writer

Click for large
graph
Minorities are the majority for the first time in the University's history of 111 entering classes, as the non-Caucasian population of Stanford's entering freshman now composes over 50% of the class. Currently, Caucasians make up 48.3% of the University's student population, closely mirroring the diversity present in California. The Golden State boasts a population that is 47.3% white, with significant ethnic representation of Asians, Hispanics, African-Americans, Arabs, and Native Americans.

"Accounting for Stanford's historic policies, the diversity here is not surprising," says Mathematics major Andrew Aymeloglu. Like many Stanford students, sophomore Mr. Aymeloglu is aware of his school's proud history. The Stanfords decided from the start that this would be a forward-looking institution, and created a coeducational environment. Not only did women attend Stanford at a time when they were atypical at major educational institutions, but 60 years before desegregation in the south, Stanford's first class included a black male. Ernest Houston Johnson entered the University in 1891, and received his BS in Economics four years later. He has recently been nominated a member of the Black Student Union Hall of Fame.

The admissions office is proud of its success in attracting minority students. "We are looking for a diverse group of intellectually curious students," states the admissions homepage. Stanford has fought hard to increase its diversity over the past fifty years. Students formed African-American activist groups in the 1960's to improve the lives of blacks on campus, and to encourage people of color to come to Stanford. Starting in the early 1980's, the Asian-American population has rapidly increased to over one-fourth of the student population. Vibrant Hispanic and Middle Eastern communities flourish at Stanford, with multitudes of ethnic parties and community events.

Other schools have similar admissions rhetoric, but less substantial results. Princeton University has a minority population of barely over 30%, with Hispanic and black minorities making up only 10% of the student population. While UC Berkeley has more Asian than Caucasian students, African-Americans make up a mere 4% of their supposedly "diverse" population. Caltech, with its science and engineering focus, fairs even worse than Berkeley, while the Air Force Academy remains almost completely white. Only MIT's efforts match those of Stanford, in terms of diversity at top schools.

"I believe that excellence knows no race," says Brian Collins, a Stanford sophomore. "Since no race then is more likely to have people deserving to attend Stanford, then in a just world Stanford's demographics should be about the same as California's."

In practice this is not the case at other top Universities. The Hoover Digest published an article that lends credence to the fairness of admissions offices of schools like Berkeley and Caltech, who are unable to attract significant populations from certain minority groups. Mr. Sowell explains in his 1998 book, At the University of California, the Sky Has not Fallen, "The real issue has not been "qualified" versus "unqualified." The issue has been the systematic mismatching of minority students with the particular campuses where they have been admitted. Once racial double standards of admission were ended, it was virtually inevitable that minority students would redistribute themselves among institutions. But the black and Hispanic students who no longer went to Berkeley did not disappear into thin air or fail to go to college at all."

Why does Stanford attract so many more minority students than other top campuses?

Sophomore Hui-Soo believes that Stanford attracts a large amount of California's diverse population—a Los Angeles native, she saw no reason to flee the beautiful coast. "California has the nation's most diverse population, so it's no wonder it's reflected in our [demographics]," says Hui-Soo. Junior Neeraj Chandra believes that our diversity is due in part to a "snowball" effect: "We already are the most diverse campus, so naturally, that attracts more minorities—it's a self-propagating process."

"The liberal atmosphere and large African-American community definitely attracted me [to Stanford]," stated Junior Oman Odigie.

While California voters have struck down affirmative action and forced top UC schools to accept only qualified minority students, Stanford has kept its high standards while continuing to attract more diverse classes throughout the 1990's. "Our policy of outreach to the minority populations has succeeded in attracting a diverse undergraduate student body," noted an admissions staff member, but the office was unavailable for comments.

Quarterly dinner
in Toyon Hall
Stanford's diversity has measurable impacts on student life. "Just looking around my dorm, it's obvious we appreciate the diversity," says Sophomore David Chiu. "Take our special dinner, for example." Toyon's quarterly "Special Dinner" had three differently themed events, celebrating Indian, Scottish, and American culture. "If you look at the board down the hall, there are events from over eight different communities being advertised." Others agree that diversity "enriches" their lives here, but some students have reservations about all the ethnic activity.

"While I appreciate the perspectives diversity brings to Stanford, I don't like how several ethnic groups' activities are self-segregating," says sophomore Aymeloglu. "The massive Asian big-sib little-sib events have only a few token whites, for example." Big-sib, little-sib programs at Stanford, the largest of which is run by the Asian American Student Association, involve matching up incoming freshman with helpful upper-classmen, to socialize and familiarize the new students with the campus.

"The diversity is part of what makes Stanford Stanford", says student Victoria Schwartz. "You see so many people of different backgrounds that when you look at someone of a different ethnicity here, you don't think of them as different from you. At the same time, I think we need to be more careful to take advantage of the opportunity the diversity presents us with, by limiting how much we segregate ourselves with our own [ethnic] groups' events."
