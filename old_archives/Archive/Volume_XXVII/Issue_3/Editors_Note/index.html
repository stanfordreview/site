Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Scott Rasmussen:
Editor-in-Chief
You may ask yourself whether this picture of a bathroom belongs in the Stanford Review. A better question is whether the Review belongs exclusively in bathrooms.

Serra House Residential Fellow Ross Shachter prevented freshman Review staffer Nels Hansen from distributing the first issue of the Review on October 11, despite the house's posted notice granting permission to distribute Stanford-affiliated materials door-to-door. Freshman Review staffer Sam Shapero, meanwhile, talked to Serra's residents and discovered that they indeed had received a number of other materials under their doors, including fellow publications the Stanford Chaparral, the Unofficial Guide to Stanford, and Guide to the Good Life at Stanford. Residential Education Director Jane Camarillo responded that evening with an email informing residential staff that door-to-door distribution was impermissible, that the Review had violated this "policy," and that in the event of encountering unauthorized persons in their houses, the residential staff should call the police.

The Review had several concerns with this chain of events. In the first place, Ms. Camarillo neglected to inform us of the email; if not for an RA who forwarded the message to the Review, a freshmen distributor could have violated this "policy" and unknowingly triggered a call to the Palo Alto Police Department. Second, I use the word "policy" in quotes because the ban on door-to-door distribution did not exist in writing until Ms. Camarillo's email. There is no mention of door-to-door distribution of materials in the 2001 Residence Staff Manual; instead, it prescribes that houses develop non-discriminatory distribution policies locally. As was evidenced in Serra, there is no guarantee that the houses' written policies will be followed. Third, even if communicated broadly and enforced equitably, a ban on distribution is anathema to free speech and the free press.

This brings us to the bathroom. The reason that the Review has historically distributed door-to-door is that other methods simply reach fewer students. Papers in the Review's bins around campus are routinely stolen. Papers distributed to lounges are removed by the cleaning staff. By distributing door-to-door, the Review ensures that at least each student has a choice: Do I read it, or do I throw it away? But now that this choice has been taken from them, students will only find the Review in that most traditional of reading places: the bathroom. Our business team decided that, in the absence of any creditable alternatives, bathrooms are the most efficient campus distribution vehicle.

The troubling part of this is that the Review is not the only thing on campus which has been relegated to the bathrooms of free speech. President Hennessy signed an October 18 "Act of Intolerance Protocol" that, as David Regele reports, has drawn comparisons with Stanford's speech code, which was ultimately struck down by Santa Clara County Superior Court in 1995. The protocol carefully avoids stating consequences for committing broadly-defined "acts of intolerance" in order to skirt its predecessor's legal difficulties, effectively giving the Administration carte blanche to stifle conduct it views as "adverse" and "unfair."

Let me be clear. I condemn, and the Review condemns, racism in all its forms. It is regretful that the current war has exacerbated conflicts between student groups. But we likewise condemn those who would circumscribe free speech. As Alex Robbins reminds us, at times of crisis America must stay close to her ideals, and liberty is foremost among them.

The ideal of representative constitutional government also defines our nation. The Associated Students of Stanford University (ASSU) Senate flouted its constitutional authority and the plurality of its constituents' views by presuming to speak on behalf of the student body in a November 6 resolution supporting affirmative action in admissions and the hiring of faculty. ASSU Senators did not campaign on the basis of their political views, the ASSU Constitution forbids the Senate to speak on political issues, and the University isn't thinking of changing its progressive admissions or hiring policies, anyway. I encourage you to sign a copy of the petition printed in this issue to let the ASSU know that you disapprove of its action, whatever you may think about affirmative action itself.

While diversity of ideals and of speech is assailed on our campus, how fares the diversity of our student body? The Review has assembled a special news report examining campus diversity: in admissions, in academics, in rallies, in student group funding, and even in demographic groups you wouldn't have thought of, like Stanford's Texas Club. Our staff also weighs in on the implications of diversity in the world outside Campus Drive, including in the difficult questions of racial reparations, airport security, and the income gap. You may agree with what we have to say and you may disagree. So speak up. Don't let "bathroom censorship" silence open dialogue at Stanford.
