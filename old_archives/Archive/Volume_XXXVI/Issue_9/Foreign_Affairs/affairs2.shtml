Immigration from a European Perspective

by Simmy Martin

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

As the immigration debate sweeps the United States, it is helpful to examine other countries’ immigration policies and their subsequent economic effects. The E.U. produced about the same GDP as the U.S. in 2005, although it has double the population. And while the E.U. is not satisfied with its low productivity and economic stagnation, the European Council hopes that implementation of the Lisbon Strategy will jumpstart the economy.

Immigration can be either a problem or a blessing for various countries in terms of production. The E.U. will eventually have one unified policy of allowing E.U. workers to migrate freely within the community workforce. In 2004 the E.U. admitted 10 new members to the original 15, all of which are poorer than the former. Just as some U.S. citizens worry about immigrants lowering wages and taking jobs, the richer E.U.-15 worry about increasing unemployment and lower pay. So far these negative effects have not materialized.

Currently there are restrictions placed on newer E.U. members preventing those citizens from migrating to the original E.U.-15 for work. Specifically, when the E.U. accepted its new members in 2004, it put into place a seven-year intermediate hold on free movement from eight of the newer members (including the former Easter Bloc countries) to the older member states. Originally only Ireland, the UK, and Sweden chose to forego any constraints against newer member migration.

Ireland has fully benefited from immigration, but has used an interesting method to do so. While they have no geographical immigration restrictions, Ireland now only grants work permits for specific sectors. Clerical and administrative positions as well as general laborers and builders are not jobs eligible for a work permit.

Previously a larger exporter than importer of labor, the Irish unemployment rate was estimated to be around 4.3% in 2005, according to the CIA World Factbook. Second only to Luxembourg and Spain, Ireland has had a continuously high influx of immigration. The economy is presently booming and it appears that the selective process of naming certain sectors eligible for work permits is beneficial. Real GDP grew at an estimated 4.7% in 2005—the largest of all E.U. growth rates, according to Factbook.

Germany, on the other hand, has had a consistently low immigration rate in the past decade. Starting in 2005, however, Germany adopted a plan similar to Ireland’s, called the German Immigration Act, under which it became easier for workers of certain professions to gain permanent residence and permission to work. Highly qualified scientists, engineers, and technologists are particularly sought after.

Despite its renowned powerful technology sector, Germany is now struggling in growth, reaching only 0.9% in 2005. Germany posted an astonishing 11.6% unemployment rate in 2005, and is also attempting to deal with an ever-aging population, which minimizes the available workforce.

In an effort to combat the decreasing labor force, Germany has accepted the European Court’s van der Elst ruling. This ruling pertains to companies within countries of the European Economic Area (EEA) and their right to provide services throughout the EEA without obtaining additional work visas. A few EEA countries do not acknowledge this ruling, and maintain their own protectionist policies, but Germany can use this policy to its advantage. Considering the low immigration rate to Germany, the van der Elst ruling promotes immigration and economic development.

The Schengen visa is another kind of visa that is only usable within the Schengen area (Germany, Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Iceland, Italy, Luxembourg, The Netherlands, Norway, Portugal, Spain, and Sweden). Workers with these visas are allowed to travel freely among the other Schengen member states for business purposes. Although it is limited to only 90 days per six month period in visiting Schengen areas, the Schengen visa still encourages migration and investment within those countries.
Although Germany’s economic outlook is not stellar, and its debt is above the E.U.’s 3% deficit limit, these new economic and migration policies just may spur a turn-around in the German economic forecast.

Meanwhile, France’s government has been a hindrance to its economy by controlling large corporations such as Air France and Renault. Despite the fact that this prevents freedom throughout the economy, France wants to continue its pursuit of social equality through taxes and governmental spending.

Another impediment to a booming economy in France is the implementation of the 35-hour week. This ends up costing the government money in unemployment benefits when employers cannot afford to keep as many employees at higher wages. Like Germany, France also has a budget deficit greater than the allotted 3% of GDP.

With an unemployment rate of 10% (est. 2005), France has traditionally been an immigration destination for people from Sub-Saharan Africa and Eastern Europe. The proportion of foreigners in the total population was about 6% in 2005. Suburban immigrant communities—where many different cultures intermix—have been at the center of violence.

In the 1970s France closed its borders to immigrants who came for work and only allowed family members of current residents to enter. The recession in the 1980s caused a net loss of immigrants, but family-linked immigration still accounted for 75% of foreign migrants in 2002. Around 20% of people migrated to France in order to find work in 2002. Interestingly enough, refugee-immigration to France accounted for 10% of total immigration in 2005.

Controversies surrounding certain ethnic groups have also been featured in the news in recent years. When the French government banned the wearing of headscarves in schools, the world was astir. And now, with France considering tightening its immigration laws, protests have erupted in Paris. On May 11, thousands of immigrants marched in hopes of halting a bill that would make it harder for immigrants with lower education and skill levels to work in France. Countries such as Spain and Italy have a selective process for immigration, which basically sets quotas for certain sectors. France would be adopting a similar approach if the bill were ratified. Some opponents of the bill claim that it would simply lead to more illegal aliens, rather than the intended better-qualified workers.

Analyzing the positive effects of Ireland’s immigration policies, it appears that at least some form of restriction in France should be enforced. While it is hard to predict exactly what the ramifications such constraints would have, the integration of workers who immigrate solely to work would probably not exacerbate the integration troubles of the ethnic groups already present.
