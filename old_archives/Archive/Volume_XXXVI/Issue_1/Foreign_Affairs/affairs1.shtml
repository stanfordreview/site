Free Speech: Handle With Care

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Danish embassies lie in ashes. Scandinavian flags have been burned the world over. Arab countries are boycotting Danish goods. Many commentators are speaking of a clash of civilizations between the West and Islam. All of this, precipitated by a bunch of cartoons.

On September 30, 2005, Denmark’s largest circulation news-daily printed 12 caricatures of the Prophet Mohammed. The paper, Jyllands-Posten, had announced that it would publish whatever drawing of Mohammed it received after the publisher of a children’s book on religions announced that it couldn’t find an illustrator willing to draw Mohammed for the book (for fear of reprisal). The 12 caricatures run the gamut from very critical of Mohammed to very critical of the contest. The most controversial of the cartoons depicts Mohammed wearing a turban that looks like a bomb with a lit fuse that is about to explode and bears the Islamic creed. Other possibly offensive cartoons include a drawing of Mohammed bearing a knife and standing in front of two burka-clad women and a drawing of Mohammed standing on a cloud greating dead suicide bombers with the exclamation “Stop, Stop! We have run out of virgins!” At the same time, several of the cartoons lampoon the contest itself or make fun of Jyllands-Posten’s editor.

A month after they were published, ambassadors from ten Islamic countries complained to the Danish prime minister and asked for an apology. The issue then laid dormant for a while until in January a Norwegian paper reprinted the cartoons. Saudi Arabia recalled its ambassador to Denmark while Libya closed its embassy in Copenhagen. At the end of January, Gunmen raided the EU’s offices in Gaza and asked for an apology. The following week saw a string of attacks on Danish embassies in Damascus, Beirut, Iran and Jakarta, a consumer boycott of Danish goods, and nine left dead from violent demonstrations.

The rallying cry against the cartoons in the Middle East has purportedly been that they violate the Quran’s rules against visually depicting Mohammed. Yet blasphemy alone does not seem to be what first incited European Muslims to protest the cartoons. Omar Shakir, president of the Muslim Student Awareness Network, explained that the “issue is the way in which Mohammed is depicted. You wouldn’t have seen this outcry if you had had just a normal depiction. The circumstances that brought about this reaction were just insensitive – bigotry and racism.” These circumstances of which Shakir speaks are the perception Muslims have of Europeans’ attitudes toward Islam and Muslims. John Bowen, a professor at Washington University and a leading authority on Islam in Indonesia, elaborated, “the cartoons are perceived in the broader context of a history of Western of sleights and attacks on Islam. The most immediate parallel is Salman Rushdie and the fatwa by Khomeini. It is also against the background of the issue of headscarves in France and mistreatment of Muslims in Europe.” Since so much of the reaction seems to hinge on perceptions of Europe, and because of the difference in the scope and violence of the reactions in Europe versus reactions in predominantly Muslim countries, I suggest we consider the two as distinct entities.

Consider the Middle East and Indonesia first. A number of factors seem to have conspired to greatly elevate the possibility of violence. To begin, it’s not clear that Arabs even saw the same set of cartoons that Europeans did. Remember that the cartoons were published in September, but that these protests have only now erupted. A number of clerics from Denmark traveled to the Middle East in late 2005 trying to provoke a reaction to the cartoons. In addition to the twelve cartoons actually printed by Jyllands-Posten, they circulated three other fabricated cartoons. These three cartoons are far more insulting and obscene than the actual cartoons. One of the three shows a dog sodomizing a praying Muslim.

Thrown into the tinderbox of Middle Eastern politics, these cartoons caught fire. Most Arab governments present an intensely anti-Western and anti-Israeli face to their citizens. No one doubts that Iranian President Ahmedinejad (who has called for Israel to be “wiped off the map”) and the al Saud family, which remains in power only by upstaging Osama bin Ladin’s anti-Americanism, will grab any opportunity to win rhetorical points and run with them. David Giovacchini, a Stanford expert on Middle Eastern film, explained, “Iran is using this to make political hay. It’s really the same thing with the Wahhabis – the Saudi Arabians have been spreading their rather violent vision across the world, using their oil wealth to fund education programs in the Philippines, Indonesia, wherever they’ll have them.” In short, no one doubts that Middle Eastern politics are dysfunctional and fail to live up to the standard of peaceful modern political discourse. As striking as images of Syrians or Iranians burning the embassies of poor little Denmark may be, such actions are so depressingly commonplace that it’s hard to claim that the Arab reaction marks anything particularly profound or earth shattering.

More surprising are the peaceful dimensions this protest has taken. Do not take the recourse to violence against “blasphemous” Denmark to represent the tendencies of the general population in Muslim countries. Consider the burning of the Danish mission in Beirut. Yes, thousands protested, but only a few hundred actually participated in the burning of the embassy or the subsequent vandalism in the Christian quarter. And both of these figures pale in comparison to the half-million Lebanese who protested against Syrian occupation last year. Indeed, some of the political action taken against Denmark bears the hallmark of peaceful capitalist protest. Arab consumers organized a boycott of Danish goods that has already impacted many companies including the Danish dairy-giant Arla. Regardless of whether your sympathies lie with Denmark, seeing many Arab consumers choosing to punish Denmark with market power instead of violent demonstration is a heartening improvement.

Not even the reactions of Arab governments have been all that ground shaking. Much has been made of the recalling of several ambassadors from Denmark as a sign of diplomatic fallout. But Uffe Ellemann-Jensen, Denmark’s foreign minister for ten years and a former president of the European Liberal party, told The Review that the situation was nothing unusual: “only one [country withdrew its ambassador]–-Iran–-and that shall probably be seen in the context of the forthcoming debate on Iran’s nuclear program in the UN security council, where Denmark is currently a member, and one called home [its ambassador] for consultations – Saudi Arabia, nothing alarming in that – so I would probably not ring the alarm bells.” Mr. Ellemann-Jensen’s comments reflect a realistic assessment of the situation--that Arab governments acted in the course of pursuing their (admittedly deplorable) diplomatic and political ends, not a radical agenda.

If the Israel-Palestine conflict and the level of brutality in Iraq have taught us anything, it is that the conflict is rarely one-sided. No doubt, there are fundamentalists and extremists in the Middle East who “hate our freedoms,” but then there are Americans who maintain that Islam is a faith of violence. Clearly, they fail to grasp the irony of history. Four hundred years ago, copies of Scheherazade’s 1001 Nights burned in pyres in Paris and London because of their sensuousness and eroticism while they were freely read in Cairo and Baghdad. Today, the situation is reversed. There is no clash of civilizations to be found in today’s Middle Eastern politics, but simply politics as usual.

To see whether this is a clash of civilizations, we must look to the frontlines of this conflict, to Western Europe. What is true of the Muslim Middle East isn’t necessarily true of European Muslims. They have, in many cases, been in Europe for two or three generations, and yet they do not see themselves receiving the opportunities open to their white compatriots. These populations begin to feel marginalized, and to become quite sensitive to racism or unfairness. In the riots in France in November of 2005, we saw Muslim immigrants in Europe acting qua immigrants, not qua Muslims. So when they see cartoons that suggest their faith is a terrorist faith or an oppressive faith, European Muslims are likely to feel offended. We can forgive them for asking European governments to restrict such expression when we bear in mind that non-Muslim left-wingers are just as eager to restrict speech in the name of avoiding offense. The first shot of a culture war hardly seems the right way to characterize a reaction less severe than that of the left wing of your own culture.

A number of Muslim students on campus voiced their own reactions to the cartoons. Tanya Haj-Hassan, a Jordanian senior and member of the Coalition for Justice in the Middle East, opined, “While freedom of expression is an important value to uphold, it carries great responsibility—mainly basic morality and mutual respect for one’s fellow human beings. The publication and re-circulation of the cartoons is a deliberate provocation of the Muslim community.” But Hassan went on to condemn the violent response as “unfortunate and un-Islamic” and unrepresentative of the true nature of Islam. Omar Shakir concurred, taking issue with the newspapers that chose to reprint the cartoons: “These [cartoons] shouldn’t have been published–-even if one attempts to clarify the problem- they have no place being reprinted anywhere. From anti-Semitism to racial intolerance, there are certain things you just don’t say because they are blatantly offensive.”

I must respectfully beg to differ with Shakir and Haj-Hassan. It’s easy to say in retrospect that the cartoons were racist or xenophobic, but the line between the level of blasphemy and callousness toward religion that we have come to expect of the West and deliberate insult of a specific ethnic group is impossible to draw. Of course, this hasn’t stopped Western Europe from trying. John Bowen is right to point out that “From a European perspective (as opposed to an American perspective) [the matter of free speech] doesn’t look so clear cut. This looks like the difference between Northern Europe, which has a vituperative press and is unusually tolerant of hate speech, and a much more socially oriented set of rules in Germany or France, where you don’t have the right to use racial epithets.” Several of the people I spoke to pointed out the hypocrisy of a Europe that rallies behind the free speech right to insult Muslims but also sends you to prison for denying the Holocaust. If anything, the liberal answer is that perhaps Europe is acting hypocritically, but that the right response consists not in applying Jewish anti-defamation laws to Muslims, but rather in rolling back Europe’s present most egregious infringements against free speech.

Suppose we are to take truly seriously Voltaire’s words “I may detest what you have to say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it.” Will we then have a world full of racial epithets, anti-Semitism and racism? No, because we must take the first part of Voltaire’s proclamation as seriously as the second–-the right to hate what is said. The free marketplace of ideas dictates that anyone offended by the cartoons not only can, but should, call them xenophobic and racist, not only can, but should, call for a consumer boycott, not only can but should retort, as Tanya Haj-Hassan does, that “this is an opportunity for Muslims to demonstrate to the world the true nature of a religion whose name signifies peace and submission to God, and whose Prophet followed a tradition of tolerance, mercy, and forgiveness.” This means that we have every obligation to prosecute and jail those who kill or threaten figures like Theo van Gogh or Pim Fortuyn, but that we do not need to condemn protests against the Jyllands-Posten cartoons as illiberal, reactionary, impositions of faith or opening shots in a clash of civilizations. In the International Herald Tribune, Tariq Ramadan, a commentator on Europe and the Muslim world, quite eloquently expresses the tacit compromise that Europe must reach: “Muslims have to understand that there is free speech in Europe, and that is that… [But Europeans need to understand that] sensitive issues must be addressed with wisdom and prudence, not provocation… Do I go around insulting people just because I’m free to do it? No. It’s called civic responsibility.”

I wish that this sense of civic responsibility were more present in debates on free speech. Advocates of free speech all too often become absolutists and believe that when speech comes under fire, we must not only uphold the freedom to that speech, but the content of that speech itself. But if we truly want to show free speech’s greatest opponents that their fears are ungrounded, we must do so by showing free speech will not result in a boundless stream of obscenity and insult. It is the mark of an advanced society to both allow free expression and to know how to use express itself with restraint. Let us conclude with Ellemann-Jenen’s words: “As my old father used to say: Freedom of expression is a right to say what you think – but it is not an obligation to do so.”
