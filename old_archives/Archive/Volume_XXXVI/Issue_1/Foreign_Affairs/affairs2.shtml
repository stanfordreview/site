Hamas at a Crossroads

by Tucker Herbert

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On January 25th, in a triumphant display of patriotism, 77% of eligible Palestinians swarmed to the polls to cast their votes for the parliamentary elections. They rallied, they voted, they anticipated the result, and they elected Hamas. Democracy is on the march! This is a step towards breaking the shackles of authoritarianism that bind the Middle East. But what is democracy? When a populace elects, through free and fair elections, a group that is internationally regarded as a terrorist organization, we are forced to reconsider our definition of democracy and the maxim that democracy is always fitting and proper.

Some fear that a Hamas victory means that they will have more power, more money, and more impetus to wreak general havoc against Israel. While this is a reasonable assumption, there is another possibility. As a governing party Hamas could moderate and become more pragmatic in their approach to dealing with Israel. Or they could simply continue as planned and pursue the destruction of Israel, as is demanded in their charter. Thus Hamas is at a crossroads.

The landslide victory came as a surprise to the world, even to the political leaders of Hamas. In the West this was made clear by the awkward responses that European and American leaders offered. I can only imagine the colorful expletives that were uttered in Israel. As for Hamas, I do not think that they had even prepared for the possibility of having to form their own government, complete with ministers of finance, transportation, energy, and education.

Hamas owes its victory to a combination of its own prowess and Fatah’s inept management. They campaigned on domestic issues such as: corruption, lawlessness, and unemployment--all matters where Fatah has failed. The election was a success for democracy, or at least, as much as could be expected in the Middle East. Fatah had been in control since the days of Arafat, and carries the baggage and corruption of that era. While Fatah led a lackluster campaign, Hamas had excellent PR: they led their campaign with actions rather than words--beyond blowing up Israelis, Hamas gave food to the hungry, rebuilt homes, and developed a sense of community in Gaza. But right now, their legitimacy in the minds of the Palestinian people rests primarily on how much they hate Israel. As long as this is the case, it will be very difficult to find peace. If Hamas can bring economic success and stability to Palestine, then they will have another source of legitimacy, amd will be able to soften their stance against Israel while maintaining support from the Palestinian people.

Given that economic growth in the Palestinian Territories will help to moderate the actions of its inhabitants, the US and the EU should continue to give the Palestinian Authority economic aid. History has demonstrated that economic development supports a democratic system and builds more tolerant, peaceful societies. Desperate situations breed the sort of suicide bombers and extremists of which the Palestinian Territories are in no short supply. If Israel is committed to peace with its neighbors and the economic development of Palestine, then they should not hinder its progress. This will mean allowing Palestinian authorities to regain control over their seaports and airports, but this cannot be expected until Israel can be reasonably assured that these ports will not be used for the importation of armaments.
Some international leaders have demanded that Hamas must recognize Israel as a precursor to future aid being sent to the Palestinian Authority. This may be too much to expect at the current stage. Hamas should only be required to renounce violence against Israel at this moment--anything more is unlikely and anything less and would rightly leave the West to fear that their economic aid would fund the purchase of weapons to be used against Israel. For a governing Hamas to openly declare war on Israel would swiftly bankrupt the Palestinian Authority. In 2005, the EU gave $600 million in aid and the US gave $400 million. Interestingly, Palestine receives the vast majority of its aid from the infidel West rather than from its oil-rich Muslim brothers in the region.

Now that there is an elected body on the Palestinian side, Israel can conduct negotiations and secure a treaty that will be internationally bound by democratic legitimacy. Granted, obtaining the treaty will be difficult. If you can sign a peace treaty with Hamas, then you can just about solve any of the world’s problems. Nobel prizes would be in order.

But the alternative would indeed be a nightmare. What could develop into a full-scale war between the West and the Muslim-world would be a disaster. The idea of extremists with nuclear weapons is peculiarly troubling. In a situation where there are not two poles, unlike the Cold War, mutually assured destruction cannot be the rule of the game, as the enemy is not a defined nation state. And for the likes of al-Qaeda, martyrdom is institutionalized. Life means nothing when there is eternal glory to be achieved.

There is a bright side to this dismal possibility. If Hamas decides to continue its war against Israel, as the governing party of the Palestinian Territories, they will now be declaring an official war. The Israeli government could use more force against Hamas than they had when it was just an insurgent group. The non-Muslim international community would cut off aid and lose any shred of tolerance that they may have had for Hamas, and there could be a replay of the 1967 War—outcome yet to be determined.

Militant Islam has a limited history, and one should not fatalistically expect that it will be around for centuries. There must be a way to solve the conflict, although it will require a great deal of international effort, attention, and understanding. The conflict in Northern Ireland between the Protestants and Catholics went on for decades, replete with bus bombings and other acts of terrorism. That situation has since calmed down.

The negative global reaction to the Hamas victory is neither baseless nor unjustifiable. The terrorists-turned-governors do not have much going for them at this point. Yet a window of opportunity still exists. At least the Palestinian Territories do not face the resource curse that plagues most other Middle Eastern states. Occasionally, great triumphs of human nature arise from dark conditions. Many great musicians, poets, artists, and thinkers were shaped by their difficult times (personally, my favorite philosophers are Russian). There could be a breakthrough. To conclude, I think that the Hamas victory will lead to a moderation of Hamas and a new legitimacy of the Palestinian Authority over the Palestinian people, leading to long-term peace. I remain skeptical that I am right, but by Jove I hope that I am.
