Cameron Up, Blair Down

by Stuart Baimel

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The May 4 British local council elections brought what had been expected by many: a crushing defeat for Prime Minister Tony Blair’s Labour Party and success, at long last, for David Cameron’s Conservatives. According to the BBC, had this been a general election, the Tories would have won 40% compared to the Liberal Democrats’ 27% and Labour’s 26%.

Something has clearly gone wrong for Tony Blair since winning a third term and a majority just a year ago, although even in 2005 his margin of victory was reduced considerably from the 1997 and 2001 Labour landslides. It seems like much longer than one year considering that Blair’s popularity, somewhere around 26%, is even lower than his comrade-in arms President George W. Bush’s, who has been in a holding pattern for months around the mid-to-low thirties. This led The Economist to proclaim the pair the “Axis of Feeble,” and tellingly, this is where Blair’s troubles start.

It appears that the British people are punishing Blair for a foreign policy that has mostly consisted of two things: an association with a deeply disliked President, and an even more unpopular war. But Blair has pressed on with his support and involvement in the Iraq War, and combined with a series of damaging domestic scandals (his Deputy Prime Minister had an affair, the Home Office released criminals that should have been deported), almost everyone thought that his party was headed for a defeat in the local elections. One must remember one thing about the local elections, however: this is not Parliament. The people are willing to deal the ruling party a slap on the wrist but still support them at the next Parliamentary election.

But this defeat for Labour is more than that, because it is also about what David Cameron has done for the Conservatives in just five months of leadership. He’s taken the party to a point where it has a serious chance to win the 2009 or 2010 election, which would be a first after three straight election defeats. “The natural party of government” has returned, and Cameron has done it in a way that makes him appear eerily similar to Tony Blair circa 1996.

Declaring “I am the heir to Blair” and that he is “building on what Labour got right,” he echoes what Blair famously said in 1997, conceding that the 18-year Tory reign “didn’t do everything wrong” before crushing them at the polls by 11 points. Cameron, then, is taking the party much closer to the center. The Conservative slogan for the local elections? “Vote Blue, Go Green.” The Conservatives even outpoll Labour on environmental issues, a central issue of Cameron’s tenure. He’s taken the party close enough to the center that Conservative policy proposals seem palatable again. Previous Conservative leaders, namely Iain Duncan Smith, didn’t understand that tax cuts and Euroskepticism were not the voters’ top priorities.

And what of Britain’s third party, the Liberal Democrats under Menzies Campbell, which was recently doing well enough in the polls to reach the point where it could win significant seats in Parliament (29% or so)? The resurgence of the Conservatives, who were left for dead, has meant that voters abandoning Labour voted in huge numbers for Cameron, not for Campbell, as was the practice in the 2005 Parliamentary elections.

What does this all mean for the future of British politics? First, the next parliamentary election does not appear to be a certain Labour victory as it was a year ago. Tony Blair’s right-hand man Gordon Brown has been waiting for a decade to succeed Blair and is growing increasingly impatient. Second, Blair, like Thatcher, will not have the luxury of a dignified, leisurely departure. More than 50 Labour MPs want him out now, and the party essentially forced him to agree to a shortened tenure, with resignation to come probably between his tenth anniversary in office in May and the party conference in October. Blair’s first eight years were successful and prosperous, but the deep unpopularity of the war in Iraq has caught up with him. A cabinet reshuffle in order to sweep the dismal election results from the headlines was tepid and earned Blair even more criticism, showing just how little political capital he has left after nearly a decade in office. The party and the public stomached a war, new fees for higher education, free-market reforms of the National Health Service, and cozying up to the most hated President in decades, but it seems that it has all caught up with him.
