A Liberal’s Challenge

by Stuart Baimel

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

My name is Stuart Baimel, and I am one of the newest contributors to The Stanford Review. What might stand out about me is that I am more liberal than the rest of the staff. I would have voted for John Kerry if I could, I oppose capital punishment and opposed the Iraq War from the beginning. I am a moderate however, and find viewpoints from the other side of things that I agree with.

However, I am not interested in talking about my views on the subject, but a larger, more important one: the state of political discourse, and why I am writing for The Review.
In a word, the state of political discourse in this country is insipid. With pundits, demagogues and bloggers all around us, we are dragging ourselves into the mud that we pretend to scorn. Political debate has reached a level where it is not really debate but instead simply yelling at one another, with great amounts of hypocrisy on both sides.

That is why I am writing for The Review: because I believe in raising the level of political discourse at Stanford and ensuring that unlike much of the political atmosphere here, that an actual debate goes on. We all know that the liberal consensus dominates Stanford, and The Review is regularly ridiculed as “racist.” Does that add to our political debate at all?

Let me present an outside-of-the-bubble (and inside the Beltway) example: Ann Coulter terms liberalism both a “disease” and “treason.” Michael Moore creates a film with gross factual errors and narration methods similar to Leni Riefenstahl. No matter our own political biases, it is clear that neither are adding in any way to our national political debate. Instead, both figures on the right like Coulter and figures on the left like Moore are detracting from our political debate. Just because their books have citations does not mean that there’s real scholarship and informed opinion behind it.

So my offer, to anyone wanting to take it, is to raise the level of political discourse in this country by abandoning these figures. We can keep people like George Will, William Buckley, Paul Krugman (when he sticks to economics), and Seymour Hirsch around, because they do not throw around words like “disease” with any regularity. Liberals can’t simultaneously call George Bush “stupid” and also portray him as the mastermind of an evil neoconservative cabal. Conservatives can’t condemn the proposed impeachment of Bush for wiretapping and still defend the Clinton impeachment ordeal. We can do better, and we know we can.

We can throw out the nominations of far-right-wing judges like Alito, but also the party-lines confirmation votes. We can fairly solve the problem of gerrymandering without leaving it to politicians who clearly have a vested interest in securing their own political future.

There is a certain value to party and ideological identification, certainly, and having a coherent set of principles. But there is a strong, clear difference between believing in a coherent set of ideological principles and ideological opposition to the other side. What I want more out of both this campus and this country is impassioned, conciliatory debate, unlike the 2004 presidential debates, which featured excessive amounts of political grandstanding and direct criticism.

Who’s with me?
