The Reason and the Passion

by Tucker Herbert

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

As the “First Job Contract” (CPE) bill neared the final stages of approval by the French government, the country was rocked by protests. Currently there are only two types of job contracts that most employers can use, one with a maximum six month term for temporary workers, and the other, which allows employees to take their employer to court if they are ever fired or laid off. CPE would allow for a business to hire workers under age 26, and then let them go within two years of when they were first hired, without explanation. The public response was massive, vocal, and at times violent. Students and labor union employees all across France took to the streets demanding that this law be blocked, as it eventually was.

This rebellion is not wholly unjustified--with economic condit-ions as precarious as they are in France, it is not surprising that the idea of legally bound job security is popular. Even the most staunch supporters of the free market admit that not everyone benefits in the short term from economic reforms toward greater liberalization. However, the protests in France reflect a broad global phenomenon of young people questioning the effects of globalization and free market economic policies.

Economic theory suggests that when it is difficult to fire an employee (as it is in France), employers are considerably more reluctant to hire employees in the first place. France’s 22% youth unemployment rate attests to this fact. France has an impressive number of globally competitive companies, but due to legal constraints, these businesses often base themselves outside of France. Now the protesters would argue that to solve this problem of evasion by companies, every country should have labor laws akin to France’s. But as France cannot control the labor laws in Eastern Europe, let alone China, French citizens are finding jobs rather hard to come by.

This situation puts the French government in a particularly difficult position as the population will protest both mediocre economic performance and the reforms that could fix it. The government is partially responsible for not presenting the law correctly. The law was drafted by un-elected Prime Minister Dominique de Villepin, who was appointed last year in the aftermath of another crisis. Previous to the CPE, his biggest claim to fame in France was his anti-American poetry. No joke.

It is unlikely that the French economy will make any sort of breakthrough before the 2007 presidential election. The themes of France’s position in the global community and the preservation of the French social model are sure to feature prominently in the campaign. The elections will provide French citizens an opportunity to display their readiness for meaningful reforms. However, one must keep in mind that any dramatic shift in leadership will most likely be to the left of the current center-right (by French standards) government.

Many have made comparisons to the long decline of Britain during the 60’s and 70’s, as labor unions drove the country to a halt on a regular basis. When the British had finally had enough of stagnation and backwardness, they elected Margaret Thatcher as their prime minister, and she proceeded to heartlessly drag Britain to development. Alas, for France, there is no Margret Thatcher on the horizon. Furthermore, the current trends suggest that the factors contributing to France’s ills will only intensify as legal restrictions allow French firms to become less competitive and its workforce more un-hirable.

If France’s economy continues to wallow miserably, at least other countries may learn from its failure and accept the difficult reforms that are necessary for long term growth. However, history casts doubt on the veracity of this trend. Leftist movements in one country have exhibited a tendency to spawn similar demands/ideologies in neighboring countries, despite the failure of such economic mismanagement. Take, for example, Latin America: Chile has implemented a neo-liberal right-wing economic strategy for the past three decades and transformed itself from a very poor country to, by many measurements, the most developed country in Latin America by encouraging foreign investment, embracing the free market, and investing in infrastructure. Venezuela, in contrast has seen increasing poverty rates, unemployment over 20%, years of negative growth rates, and deteriorating infrastructure since the election of socialist president Hugo Chavez, all despite tremendous oil revenues. And yet, the wave of political support in other Latin American countries is moving closer to the Venezuelan than the Chilean model.

The popularity of the Venezuelan model explains the term used for the Venezuelan president: Hugo Chavez is often described as a Populist. Populism is a form of political technique that appeals to passion and outrage rather than rational thought or science. Such leaders have claimed that the political right is controlled by rich corporations which plot to exploit people for their cheap labor in a kind of neo-colonialism; they claim free market policies will work to keep poor people in their place. If only they (the French protesters included) could understand that history has shown that this demonized right-wing economic policy has actually had a better track record for moving the poor out of poverty. If only they knew the level of upward mobility that is enjoyed in the United States under a free market system. Asia has figured it out. One can only wonder when old Europe and Latin America will catch on.
