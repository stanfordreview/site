Let the Walls Come Tumbling Down

by Daniel Slate

Opinions Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

There is a most malign myth in this world, and it is of particular alarm how rapidly it is spreading throughout the country and abroad. Carried by the flows of globalization, that most essential force that pumps the lifeblood through the world economy, this foul fiction metastasizes like the most malignant cancer. What is this false notion? That to be a successful human being, one must specialize, specialize, specialize, and that the best time to begin going about this allegedly beneficent narrowing is the four or so years of an undergraduate education. Better even to start earlier, perhaps in high school, middle school. Swallowing this myth in full, we would set ourselves preparing for careers while still in the womb, and sacrifice the higher development of our minds before emerging into the world.

This myth ought to be recognized for the tale that it is, and adherence to it promptly labeled a disease. It is an illness of prudence, to be sure, but an illness all the same, one born out of fears and insecurities, standing upon the strong foundations of Maslow’s familiar hierarchy, except uprooted from the present and imagined into the future. Concerns for feeding and sheltering oneself are satisfied while one is a student through the money one pays to procure housing and dining. But, the prudent and ever forward-looking student retorts. There will come a day when one is not in school, and must find both work and a place to live, eat, and sleep. The favorite slogan of the careerists is that one must pay the bills. But for what reason must one pay—simply to cover the costs of heating, electricity, gas? What use are the fruits of human civilization and technical progress if they cannot be employed beyond cooking and preserving food, providing a comfortable environment for our bodies, and building for us roofs over our heads? We would do as well in a shelter in the woods with flint and steel.

No, there is something more to education than occupation. But there continues to flow through colleges and universities, Stanford not excluded, a continuous stream of students naming themselves “pre-law,” “pre-med,” all of them dedicating themselves to the future as they sacrifice the present on the altar of “pre-occupation.” For it is preoccupation, above all else, and a misdirected one at that, that is at the root of the disease plaguing education. Hordes of students scurrying about, forever shaded by the shadow of fear of their future financial security and social status, find less and less time for the fueling of their minds as they stoke the fires of competition.

This is by no means inevitable. The student who seeks to provide for himself a solid liberal education and a fertile garden in which his mind may grow and flourish will find in the course bulletin everything he needs. He may enroll in the structured liberal education program or find courses across the classics, history, and philosophy departments in which he can engage himself in thought and contemplation while gaining an understanding and appreciation for human civilization and its achievements. Such a search, born out of perceived responsibility to oneself and one’s mind, is perfectly consistent with the most important teaching Stanford imparts through its culture, classes, and resources: one has enormous and rich opportunities everywhere one looks, all the time; it is merely up to the student to rise up, go forth, and claim his education for himself. General education requirements should not be seen as irritating obstacles along a path ending in a particular piece of paper accompanied by a tasseled cap, but as opportunities to explore the best the variegated knowledge traditions have to offer. And they should be more than just a sampling, serving more properly as a doorway into higher humanist learning. Those who would scoff at such a philosophy often believe it unwise, if not outright irresponsible, to spend one’s time and money on allegedly less practical courses. They would do well to remember that scant few of those who graduate from Stanford starve or go homeless. Almost all, in fact, do quite well. The key difference is the richness of graduates’ minds.

Those who would close themselves off from new knowledge, who would secret themselves away in a room unlucky enough to be painted but one color, these deny the relevance of the verdant and vibrant world that stands inches away, waiting, a world willfully blocked off by the cell’s hermetic seal. There are some of these unhappy prisoners who manage to pluck up the courage to contest the importance of anything beyond their chosen focus. But closer inspection reveals such nerve to be mere bluster, a sham, a blind woven of wool carefully selected to let through only the wearer’s favorite light. For it is light to which he is accustomed, and while its gentle radiance still proffers warmth, its hands no longer hold the promise of challenge.

A specialized world is a filtered world, and the lumps of gold left in the sifter leaves poor what fine earth pours through. Those who climb the educational ziggurat to brand and credential themselves, stamping their foreheads and branding their cheeks with a treacherous “employable,” these self-satisfied and confident men and women condemn themselves forever to an impoverished existence. One must practice some trade, they will hurl back in their defense, in order to make a living, and in order to be successful in a trade, one must specialize. And so they trade away the riches of their soul. Nothing but the utmost opprobrium should they receive in return.

What is needed is not a will to power, but a will to wisdom. Let students who would claim the birthright owed their minds let loose great blasts against the walls built up by specialization. Seven blasts from the ram’s horn of an education both broad and deep ought to undo the walls’ cement and mortar, and cure the mythical disease that blights the promise of schooling. Let the walls come tumbling down, so that students may enter the promised land of higher education ready to eat and drink the milk of knowledge and the honey of wisdom in all their forms.
