Conduct Board Censors Review in Secret Hearing

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Stanford University’s Organization Conduct Board (OCB) held a hearing on Friday, March 10 to determine the culpability and appropriate sanctions for The Stanford Review’s January 21 violation of the door-to-door distribution policy.

The OCB makes a recommendation to Interim Dean of Students Chris Griffith on appropriate administrative actions that should be imposed on the student group if the group is found to be in violation of University policy. Griffith, however, will ultimately decide on the repercussions for The Review’s actions.

In keeping with OCB protocol, an independent five-person panel evaluated the evidence against and in defense of The Review’s actions. Three undergraduate students, one graduate student, one administrator, and a director from the Vaden Health Center served on the board.

During the hearing, The Review was allowed ten-minute opening and closing statements. The remainder of the nearly four hour hearing was comprised of the interviewing of a variety of individuals involved in the event. Director of Residential Education (ResEd) Jane Camarillo, Resident Fellows (RFs) Roger Printup and Ross Schachter, and two Review staff members were all questioned in OCB interviews. Resident Assistant (RA) David Louk was also supposed to take part, but he did not show-up for the hearing.

Most of the questions in the interviews came from the independent board. Although The Review was permitted to question the witnesses, the OCB required The Review to write-out inquiries and submit them to the chair of the board. At one point during the hearing, the board asked everyone to leave the room for more than a half hour in order to deliberate about whether The Review’s proposed questions were acceptable. After a majority vote, the board decided to censor all of The Review’s remaining questions for a particular witness, declaring them to be irrelevant to the case or repetitive.

One of the questions the board rejected would have asked Camarillo about the inconsistency in the enforcement of the policy. The question drew from wording Camarillo used in an e-mail that asked other administrators for “any assistance that you could offer as it regards discussions with your student leadership to help inform students rather than let the Review traumatize students with their messages.” The leadership of The Review believed that Camarillo’s view that The Review “traumatize[s] students,” might suggest that ResEd was targeting The Review in particular. The Board, however, rejected the question. Later in the hearing, one member of the independent panel suggested that the statement, perhaps, was “out of context.”

The censorship of questioning greatly inhibited The Review’s efforts to defend its action. Witnesses presented several inaccuracies that The Review was not able to counter until its closing statement, which did not afford sufficient time to respond. Nevertheless, Emeritus Editor Alex Medearis caught Jane Camarillo in a blatant false statement. She claimed that ResEd had updated in October of 2005 its website, which was created in 2003 to communicate each dorm’s distribution policy to the broader community. Medearis presented web archived transparencies of the website in 2004-2005 and 2005-2006 demonstrating that ResEd had in fact not updated the policy to reflect changes in dorm policies that had occurred. Medearis suggested that this failure to update the policy reflected a disconnect between students and ResEd and the lack of care that ResEd has for its own policy.

The ignorance of administrators and others to the current policy is a problem that has plagued The Review. During his Parents’ Weekend Q&A, President Hennessy told parents that he believed residents could vote to allow or disallow distribution from within their dorms. Voting, in fact, is only allowed if the appropriate RF agrees to it. Roger Printup, during his interview, recognized the inaccuracy in Hennessy’s statement: “I heard that comment and was surprised to hear it.” Printup does not allow residents to vote on the door-to-door distribution policy. Instead, like many RFs, Printup has the dorm staff decide the policy for the dorm.
Camarillo, Printup, and Shachter all maintained that the enforcement of the door-to-door distribution policy should not be based on content. Shachter, however, admitted that the distribution policy was first raised as an issue as a result of the 1994 incident in which Casa Zapata Resident Fellow José Burciaga kicked the Review distributors out of the dorm and threw away the issues they intended to distribute because he objected to the content of The Review.

Camarillo and Printup recognized that it is hard to enforce the regulation against businesses that distribute within the dorms. Generally, Camarillo sends a letter to commercial offenders. She stated during the hearing that she can’t, however, do much beyond a letter: “the challenge is the consequences. I don’t have the weight of the university to say that we won’t do business with you.” In consequence, the University is punishing students—but not outside businesses—for violating the distribution policy. Some RFs, such as Printup, have even given-up on regulating outside violations of the door-to-door policy: “I haven’t [reported outside businesses that violated the policy] this year, because I understand the difficulty of getting business entities to conform to the kind of behavior I’d like them to have. I know that these efforts have been made before.” Printup was one of the RFs to report The Review’s violation. Printup did acknowledge, however, that commercial enterprises have fewer violations this year, in his opinion—which he appreciates.

The leadership of The Review was disappointed with the apparent partiality of some of the board members. During the end of The Review’s closing statement, one member of the panel proceeded to defend one of the previous witnesses. A heated debate between the individual and The Review ensued, only to later be calmed by the chair. Some members of the board and Associate Morris Graves, who oversaw the hearing, refused on several occasions to entertain a discussion on the constitutionality of the University policy. Although The Review thought that the constitutionality of the policy was directly relevant to the motivation behind violating the door-to-door policy, Graves said that “the University currently feels that it’s on solid ground.” Thus, he believed, the constitutionality was not a relevant issue for the board to discuss.

The Review also took issue with the secrecy of the meeting. The leadership of The Review requested several times, in advance of the hearing, that the hearing be open to the public to ensure the fairness of the decision. Graves denied the request. The Review even cited the Student Judicial Charter of 1997, which states that “students accused of misconduct” have the right “to be given the opportunity to choose an open hearing” in its last request. Graves, however, argued that OCB protocol does not allow for an open hearing. He also stated, in an e-mail to The Review’s leadership that this reality did not conflict with the Judicial Charter because “the OCB is not a judicial hearing…the OCB is an advisory community/peer review.” The University can sidestep the charter with the OCB by functioning in an “advisory” role, though the Dean almost always confirms the board’s decision. The Stanford Review has the option to appeal to the Dean of Student Affairs any sanctions that might be imposed.
