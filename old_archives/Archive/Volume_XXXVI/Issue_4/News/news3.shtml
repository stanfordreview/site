Hoover's Hidden Treasures

by Carl J. Kelm

News Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Hoover Tower, never far from sight, has become an emblem of Stanford University and a memorial to our nation’s 31st president. Yet, its greatest value is not in its symbolism, but rather what it holds inside. Upon returning from relief efforts in war-torn Europe after World War I, Herbert Hoover decided to form the Hoover Institution on War, Revolution, and Peace, so that the lessons of time would not be forgotten. The knowledge that has been amassed there since its founding in 1919 is extraordinary.

Recently, Hoover Fellow Bert Patenaude decided to chronicle a fraction of what the archives hold, culminating in his new book, A Wealth of Ideas: Revelations from the Hoover Institution Archives. Correspondingly, hundreds of documents and artifacts have been organized into a new exhibit at Herbert Hoover Memorial Exhibit Pavilion, next to Hoover Tower. This amazing exhibition of some of the rarest historical items in the world is truly something to be seen. Both Patenaude and the event developer and designer, Kyra Bowling, were kind enough to be interviewed for this story.

The exposition, consistent with the Hoover Institution’s historical focus on the twentieth century, details some of the greatest conflicts, ideas, and triumphs of modern times. When asked how it reflected on the world during this era, Bowling replied, “The twentieth century was tumultuous and the exhibit reflects that, but it is also tempered with many of the century’s stories of peace and freedom of thought as well.” Indeed, there is a wide variety of materials divided into several sections, detailing events as diverse as the Bolshevik Revolution and Alfred Nobel’s decision to start the Nobel Prize. Moreover, the type of sources used is also rather varied. In order to tell the story of the not-so-distant past, the exhibit does not rely just on old letters or documents, but makes full use of political posters, watercolors, photographs, and even a piece of the Berlin Wall. There are amazing finds in each of these areas.

The Hoover Archives contain a great deal of correspondence. The thoughts and sentiments of such historic figures as Reagan, Friedman, Nobel, Pasternak, Thatcher, and Hayek are found in their own handwriting. One letter of particular interest is from Leon Trotsky in which he predicts his imminent assassination—a prophecy that was fulfilled soon after.

The exhibit also showcases a sampling of the political posters that were used throughout the century. Many of these are from the major wars, but some more recent ones are from the Soviet Union and Red China. The Solidarity movement in Poland even designed a poster meant to resemble the American movie High Noon in order to encourage people to vote for the first time. There are over 33,000 posters catalogued in the archives, and more than double that un-catalogued.

Other documents likewise impress any visitor. One area particularly focuses on the tyrannies and revolutions that took place during the twentieth century. Valuable artifacts from such places as China, the Soviet Union, and Nazi Germany are all to be found in the display. One of the most amazing Chinese items is a first edition of Mao’s Little Red Book, which detailed Mao’s political philosophy and helped enhance his cult status within communist China. The exhibit also boasts American Gen. Joseph Stillwell’s personal diary. During World War II, it was Stillwell’s job to help convince Nationalist Chinese leader Chiang Kai-Shek to continue to fight the Japanese. But perhaps the most priceless pieces of Chinese history are the sketches from Mao’s famous Long March during 1934-35. These drawings found at the Hoover Archives are the only known depictions of the Long March.

Nor is Russia neglected in the exhibit. Included in the exposition is a book of Tsarist police records with mug shots, including one of an obscure young dissident named Leon Trotsky. Also on display is a draft of the Tsar’s abdication letter, as well as the first issue of the Soviet state newspaper, Pravda. One of the more striking collections is the personal photo album of Stalin vacationing in the Caucuses.

Yet, Nazi Germany may have the most impressive archival materials of all. This is largely due to the actions of American Col. William R. Phillip, a World War II intelligence officer who was involved in planning the Normandy invasion and overseeing the trials at Nuremburg. Some of the things he donated to the Hoover Institute are incredible finds. Not only is there a first edition of Mein Kampf, but Hoover also holds the personal diary of Joseph Goebbels, one of the most important figures in the Nazi state who also died in Hitler’s bunker (the original items have not been put on display for security reasons). In addition, on display is the x-ray of Hitler’s head taken after the failed attempt on his life in 1944. Another interesting find is the Gestapo Arrest List—the names and positions of people the Gestapo was planning to arrest as soon as ground troops landed on British soil. In the exhibit, the book is turned to the page where Winston Churchill is listed as a key target.

But the exposition consists of more than just documents, photographs, and books. Rather, both beauty and tragedy are expressed by the artistic items on show. Included in the exhibition is a handwritten copy of the poem, “To Be Famous is Unbecoming,” by Boris Pasternak. The duality of the Russian Revolution is brought to life by the paintings of Russian artist Ivan Vladimirov. One of his works present depicts nobles burning their furniture to survive the bitter winter; while another shows starving people eating a horse in the streets as a gleeful Bolshevik rally goes on across the bridge. But perhaps the most haunting images belong to Thomas Sgovio. Sgovio, an American whose family moved to the USSR in the 1930s, ended up serving years in the gulag, followed by internal exile. Sgovio’s powerful drawings of the forced labor camps and of a man in solitary confinement effectively make him Solzhenitsyn with a brush.

As difficult a decision it is, Patenaude and Bowling were both able to choose what they felt were the three most important objects in the exhibition. They agreed on two things: the extremely rare Long March sketches and the personal photo album of Nazi Foreign Minister Ribbentrop from his signing of the Nazi-Soviet Pact. But whereas Patenaude pointed to the Gestapo Arrest List as a third object, Bowling was more impressed by “the rare film excerpts in the exhibit that include footage of the atomic bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki as well as 1937 footage of Leon Trotsky, Diego Rivera, and Frida Kahlo in Mexico where Trotsky was in political exile. “When it came to interesting people,” Bowling stated, “There are dark historical figures covered in the exhibit that I find compelling such as Stalin, Goebbels, and Mao, but at the end of the day, I find myself more happily engrossed in the materials of Nobel Prize winners Boris Pasternak, Andrei Sakharov, and Milton Friedman.” When asked to name a little-known person covered in the exhibit who made a great impact, both agreed it was Louis Raemaekers. According to Patenaude, the Dutch cartoonist “was one of the most influential people on the planet during World War I,” and was crucial in rallying American support for joining the war effort.

The exhibit is not only resigned to captive nations and battlefields, but covers Stanford’s own history as well. On display are the blueprints for Hoover Tower, as well as pictures of it during construction. But the entire display collectively tells a story of our school. Patenaude stated, “The very fact that all these historical documents and artifacts are located at Stanford University is, you could say, the ultimate revelation of this exhibit.” Bowling agreed, adding, “I think the Stanford-related names and stories found throughout the exhibit reflect the maverick spirit that is synonymous with this campus. Figures such as David Starr Jordan, Lev Litoshenko, Bert Wolf, Milton Friedman, and, of course, Herbert Hoover himself, embody what I see everywhere here at Stanford: inspirational individuality.”

The exhibition, open for free to students and to the public, is an excellent place for anyone to visit. Whether one is looking to kill some time between classes or enjoy a lazy Saturday, “The exhibit presents a wonderful opportunity to lose oneself in history for an hour or an afternoon,” according to Bowling. Patenaude likewise suggests, “[Students] should come and see the historical treasures that are housed just minutes away from their dorm rooms…There is always something happening at the Hoover Archives…that can enlighten all students, whatever their fields of study.”

The exposition serves as a witness to thoughts and actions, men and movements, war and peace. We should consider ourselves privileged that so many sides of modern history are on display on Stanford’s campus. Indeed, when asked what we should take away from the exhibit, Patenaude answered that it was that “Ideas have consequences. They move the world—even seemingly outlandish ideas like those presented by Hitler in Mein Kampf—which is, I suppose, the ultimate example of what can happen if you don’t take ideas seriously.” As the first heirs to the legacy of the twentieth century, it is our generation’s responsibility that these ideas and our past are not forgotten.

Patenaude’s new book, A Wealth of Ideas, is published by Stanford University Press and is available at the Stanford Bookstore, as well as at online sites such as Amazon.com. The exhibit is open Tuesday through Saturday, 11AM to 4PM. It will remain open until May 6.
