PWR Politics: Is The Program Politicized?

by Boris Hanin

Deputy Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

All first year Stanford students are required to take a course in the Program in Writing and Rhetoric, PWR I. Many students only know vaguely why they have to take this course, but that does not stop the complaints. So what exactly are these PWR I classes that are so dreaded by the freshman masses?

Dr. Andrea Lunsford, Director of the Program in Writing and Rhetoric and Professor of English, expressed her vision for the purpose of PWR in an exclusive interview with The Stanford Review. She suggested that PWR is not simply designed to improve students’ writing, but rather, she said, “Our mission is to give Stanford students the opportunity to undertake a serious piece of research and to learn how to deploy that research in a pretty sophisticated argument.” Thus, Dr. Lunsford emphasized that PWR is not meant to be a course on learning to produce good writing by teaching theories of writing, but is a class on learning to produce good research-based arguments.

Students write their research papers on the specific theme of their PWR I class. The program typically offers about 35 classes per quarter, dealing with everything from children’s media to the Supreme Court. Lunsford explained the multitude of different PWR subjects offered, saying, “Research in the field of rhetoric and writing shows that students write better…if they are writing on issues that engage them…thinking that if you are in a class where the theme is of great interest to you, we know that gives you a better chance of producing really good writing.”

Students have criticized PWR for failing to teach them writing fundamentals. Dr. Lunsford addressed the apparent lack of explicit instruction on grammar, word choice, sentence construction, and context-specific rhetorical devices in PWR I classes. She asserted, “You were taught grammar all the way through school…and there is something mind-boggling about sitting and having somebody talk for ten minutes about semi-colons…but I’m surprised that classes are not dealing with rhetorical figures and strategies.”

PWR has also been criticized for appearing to divorce the mechanics of writing from the content. This element of PWR contrasts sharply with the Writing in the Major (WIM) requirement. WIM is able to integrate content with writing because the professors within a department construct courses in which they themselves are experts, and students are coached as much on the content as the mechanics of their writing.

PWR courses are still organized around specific themes. Just as students have a lot of flexibility in choosing a PWR class, PWR professors are given great freedom in constructing their courses. When asked if there is some kind of process that a professor has to go through to get a topic certified for a prospective PWR I course, Dr. Lunsford replied, “Only an informal one.” She described the approval procedure as a kind of peer-review process.

She also stated that a similar peer-review process exists for approving PWR I readings. Some students have accused PWR courses of having very politicized readings. In Professor Kevin DiPirro’s “Dramatic Rhetoric” course offered two years ago, where “pretty much the entire course revolved around race,” said one student in the class, “early in the course we read Anna Devere Smith’s Fires in the Mirror, which is about the Crown Heights race riots in NYC in 1991.” While political topics may be interesting topics for some students to write about, some students believe that professors are taking advantage of their positions to disseminate politicized messages without offering an adequate counter-balance.

Professor Paul Bator’s PWR I course entitled “Supreme Rhetoric: Rhetoric of the Supreme Court” assigned his class to read excerpts from Cass Sunstein’s book Radicals in Robes: Why Right-Wing Courts are Wrong for America. This book was accompanied by a technical writing guide but no alternative text was provided to challenge the thesis of this politicized text. A PWR II class offered this quarter with an environmental theme read and discussed articles such as “Retalking Environmental Discourses from a Feminist Perspective: The Radical Potential of Ecofeminism” and “Looking for Common Ground.” One of the commentators in “Looking for Common Ground” stated he was “trying in various ways to help industrial civilization find its own dharma nature, and become an egalitarian, more tribal society that respects people and respects the Earth once again.”

It can be difficult for students who strongly disagree with a politicized text to effectively counter the arguments presented in the PWR class, since the author has had the advantage of years of scholarship and thought to prepare his or her argument. Dr. Lunsford did not have a quick solution to this problem, and she stated that she had not received any specific complaints of politicization from students.

Even if the professor does not provide an alternative view, Dr. Lunsford believes that it is still important to come up with and discuss possible alternative perspectives. She did not, however, comment on whether these alternative perspectives ought to be assigned as readings or if it is sufficient to rely on the integrity of the professors to bring up the perspectives in class.

Dr. Lunsford stressed the importance of a marketplace of ideas saying, “For a university to be a true university it needs to entertain all ideas … [and] part of what it means to mature intellectually is to grapple with very difficult perspectives and ideas.” Both within the PWR classroom and outside the classroom, Dr. Lunsford seemed to believe in the importance of intellectual diversity. On the subject of door-to-door distribution of student publications in the dorms, Dr. Lunsford admitted that she hadn’t been closely following the debate, but nevertheless maintained, “I thought, ‘What’s wrong with it? If I don’t want to read it, I don’t have to.’ I think the more [ideas] the merrier.”

PWR is not the only writing-focused students have to take at Stanford. The Introduction to the Humanities (IHUM) program is required for all freshmen unless they choose to take Structured Liberal Education (SLE), which is a residentially-based alternative to IHUM. Dr. Lunsford commented on the connection between PWR and IHUM saying, “IHUM is basically a course in critical reading [and] PWR is a course in writing.” She elaborated, “What they (IHUM) are teaching is a close reading of a text. We’re teaching a much broader form of argument.”

The distinctions between PWR and IHUM were not always so clear. Dr. Lunsford noted that “when I got here six years ago there was no articulation between PWR and IHUM ... but I have worked very hard with Dr. Robinson, the director of IHUM, to articulate the differences between the two programs.” In fact, she said, “When I got here, there wasn’t a writing center at Stanford and now we did five thousand tutorials last year alone…and the majority of them have been for IHUM…so we feel like we are pretty well coordinated.” Dr. Lunsford also pointed out that “twelve to fifteen percent of all Writing Center visitors last year were graduate students,” and she spoke about the possible further integration of PWR and IHUM, saying she would be glad to discuss developing explicitly complementary courses in PWR and IHUM, for example.

Another little-known component of some PWR courses is the Community Writing Project. Dr. Lunsford clarified: “If you go on the PWR website…some of [the courses] are identified as CWP. If you sign up for one of those courses you are attached to a non-profit agency of some kind . Let’s say we have a class that focuses on documentary film and the CPW section of that is students working in groups and they try to make a short film for the non-profit agency. So it’s a public service component of PWR.” Students have built and improved the entire Palo Alto Fire Department website over the last few years, for example, and students have worked with AIDS groups in the Bay Area. The Program in Writing and Rhetoric is a work in progress, and there is still room for improvement.
