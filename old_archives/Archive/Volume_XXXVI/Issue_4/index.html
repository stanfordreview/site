Hoover's Hidden Treasures

Hoover Tower, never far from sight, has become an emblem of Stanford University and a memorial to our nation’s 31st president. Yet, its greatest value is not in its symbolism, but rather what it holds inside. Upon returning from relief efforts in war-torn Europe after World War I, Herbert Hoover decided to form the Hoover Institution on War, Revolution, and Peace, so that the lessons of time would not be forgotten.

Editor's Note: How to Criticize The Review

I’ve been excited about Issue 4 ever since I began planning-out my volume in February. I realized that this issue would be the last published before the end of the quarter, and would likely contain many pieces that our writers wanted published before Spring Break.

Rekindling the Professional Argument

Since becoming Business Manager, my personal focus within The Review has shifted from producing content toward brainstorming ways of improving other aspects of our organization. Recently I gave a talk to our staff about professionalism, both in the journalistic sense and in the argumentative sense.

The Price of the Indian Mascot

There’s a great Seinfeld episode where Jerry’s dentist, Tim Whatley, converts to Judaism and starts telling Jewish jokes. Of course, Jerry complains to a priest, who asks logically, “and this offends you as a Jewish person?” Jerry’s answer: “No, it offends me as a comedian!” I share this example to ask a simple question: how does one acquire the rights to enjoy the benefits of a particular identity?

Anatomy of a Revolution:
A brief history of the Stanford Indian

The current controversy over the Stanford Indian began at the most recent Big Game (and no, it didn’t have anything to do with losing one more time). A number of people wore T-shirts with a bulbous-nosed caricature of the Stanford Indian that served as this schools mascot from the 1930s through the 70s.

The Tree of Liberty:
Yenny Wahid and the
Battle for Democracy in Indonesia

For Yenny Wahid, taking on the family business is no small task. Following in her father’s footsteps, Ms. Wahid has become one of the most prominent proponents of peaceful, democratic Islam in Indonesia. Ms. Wahid’s story properly begins with her father, Abdurrahman Wahid, a respected Islamic scholar and peace activist who took office as President from 1999-2001 as Indonesia was rebounding after 32 years of authoritarianism under Suharto’s regime.

Conduct Board Censors Review in Secret Hearing

The Right Approach to Environmentalism

The American Right is generally considered supportive of free market principles. But the Left, for this very reason, often accuses the Right of being anti-environment. However, it is quite possible to formulate an environmentalist vision consistent with market liberalism, particularly through inventive uses of property rights.

Smoke Signals

The ASSU elections are nearing. Right after Spring Break, students will be asked to elect a new Undergraduate Senate, President and Vice President. Students will also approve or disapprove of financial appropriations to many student groups. Although campaigns for the candidates are just starting, Smoke Signals already has plenty of anecdotes to explore.

"Why Can't We All Just Get Along?"

Nobody believes we should bring back the Stanford Indian. For all of the controversy that has and continues to surround the mascot, I believe dropping it was the right thing to do. If a symbol or mascot is extremely painful to a group within the university, then there seems to be no reason to continue to pain them by officially endorsing this mascot as a symbol of the university.

Epilogue: The elephant in the room: freedom of speech

I tried to adopt a very conciliatory tone in my editorial on the mascot issue. I feel that the question is best solved through debate, mutual understanding and tolerance. I am willing to listen to and discuss arguments that nobody should depict caricatures of Native Americans. You may even convince me that people who do are outmoded and insensitive.

Book Review: Bad Company

We all jump into the middle of conversations. There’s that sudden orientation process as you try to figure out what was just discussed, what is currently being discussed, and what is left on the agenda to discuss. Sometimes this process can take awhile, but generally after a minute or so you’re comfortable enough to join the discussion on the current topic.
