Rekindling the Professional Argument

by Navin Kadaba

Business Manager

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Since becoming Business Manager, my personal focus within The Review has shifted from producing content toward brainstorming ways of improving other aspects of our organization. Recently I gave a talk to our staff about professionalism, both in the journalistic sense and in the argumentative sense. After giving the talk, I realized that the art of the argument is not what it used to be. Elegant discourse between individuals has degraded into vicious ad hominem attacks that end-up convincing people of nothing, except that maybe the person giving the argument is not worth listening to. Unfortunately, it is often the case that people accept these fallacious arguments as valid simply because they are in agreement over the topic in question. However, these tenuously established accommodations are removed the moment a disagreement is reached. As a member of an organization that generally disagrees with the campus mainstream, I have seen much of this hypocrisy in action. As such, I’m taking a break from my managerial duties to explore the world of the professional argument and hopefully take a step towards eradicating the unintelligent argument.

What does it take to make an effective and professional argument? This exploration, believe it or not, begins in a PWR class. This quarter in my PWR2 class, I had to read selections from the book “Everything’s an Argument.” According to Andrea Lunsford, the co-author of the book and current director of the Program in Writing and Rhetoric, nearly every form of expression can be linked to a stance; as the title suggests, “everything’s an argument.” What interested me most was the chapter about fallacies of argument. Upon reading this chapter, I noticed that nearly every aspect of argument that the authors depicted to be an argumentative fallacy was a tactic employed by many argumentative people on this campus, particularly against The Review. Specifically, I am referring to hasty generalizations, bandwagon appeals, and most importantly, ad hominem attacks.

One form of unprofessional and ineffective argument utilized by many people on campus is the hasty generalization that The Review consists of a cult of bitter right-wingers bent on personally offending everyone on campus. This unsupported assumption is rooted in the degradation of the word conservative to mean “right wing nut-job.” Unfortunately, most of the people who buy into this theory have not done their research on our staff. The Stanford Review in fact consists of members who span the spectrum of ideas. Libertarians and traditionalists alike come together to discuss conservative ideas. Our staff is quite diverse, often disagreeing internally on a whole host of issues. The rich discussion that occurs within our staff is a microcosm for what we set out to do on the campus as a whole. The Review exists to foster dialogue on a campus that tends to oppress this discourse that provides a richer experience for everyone.

The hasty generalization that The Review is “trash” often leads to the bandwagon appeal: since The Review is the “enemy,” everyone who doesn’t think so should face ostracism from the general community. A common bandwagon appeal in the media is the Bush administration’s “either you are with us, or you are one of them” policy on terrorism. At Stanford, the policy is “either you hate The Review, or you are racist/evil/offensive” (take your pick). Unfortunately, the suppression of conservative thought in this manner is rampant on a campus that eagerly defends the rights of nearly all other special interest groups with a minority opinion. The hypocrisy is mind-boggling. Whenever I hear about a campus-sponsored event of some sort that is designed to “break down stereotypes,” or demonstrate that “everyone should be entitled to their opinions and practices,” I can’t help but wonder how they justify the oppression of conservative ideas. Between students throwing out or burning large stacks of Reviews and ResEd staff members preventing our right to door-to-door distribution, it seems that the campus has no problem discriminating against us personally, in the most unprofessional manner.

By far the most unprofessional argument is the ad hominem attack. I can’t even count the number of times I have heard “Oh the Review is garbage,” or “They are all a bunch of (insert your favorite expletive here).” That is all well and good when casually conversing amongst friends, but these comments are often transplanted into the context of a serious argument. Do people honestly think they are getting a valid point across by phrasing their argument as such? I truly hope they know that unprofessional arguments are ineffective at conveying an intelligent point.

Just recently, The Review has come under fire, once again, due to a piece we published about the Stanford Indian. Personally, I think that the Indian mascot is a relic of the past. I still get confused about the name Indian, being a South Asian variety myself. Regardless, one cannot simply erase history, nor can historical themes be taken out of context. Nobody on The Review is suggesting that the Stanford Indian should be upheld for the purpose of perpetuating stereotypes about Native Americans. But we do feel that as a part of Stanford’s rich history, it should be explored and discussed as much as any other Stanford tradition. One particular Daily columnist believes it to be a blatant act of racism. As a minority, I have been subject to racism in the past, and defined it as the usage of skin color/ethnicity as a basis of judgment, and I do not see how exploring Stanford’s history makes any judgments in this manner.

I have been with The Review for over a year now, and I have yet to hear a well thought out and well presented argument against The Review’s existence. The myriad of complaints we receive normally fall into two categories: disagreement with content or personal attacks against the staff. Disagreement is no reason to stifle discourse; if anything it should be a driving force for such debate. The only valid issue to date is the existence of unprofessional conduct on the part of our staff in the distant past. That problem was very real, and the main subject of my talk to the staff. It is always important to respect the audience during intelligent discourse. However, a dialogue only exists in the presence of two parties, and both are entitled to the respect they deserve. Anyone who seeks out large stacks of Reviews and deposits them in the recycling bin only lowers the credibility of his or her opinion. People should welcome opposing viewpoints as a challenge to defend their own. In the world, we will always encounter those who disagree with us, and we need to be prepared to understand those opposing viewpoints and provide a counterargument. Having our campus suppress dissent to make everyone happy will only be harmful in the long run. In the end, training students to have a professional attitude toward disagreement will make for a richer experience for all of us at Stanford, leading to richer experiences for the rest of our lives.
