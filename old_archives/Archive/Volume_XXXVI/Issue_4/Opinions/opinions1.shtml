The Right Approach to Environmentalism

by Daniel Slate

Opinoins Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

The American Right is generally considered supportive of free market principles. But the Left, for this very reason, often accuses the Right of being anti-environment. However, it is quite possible to formulate an environmentalist vision consistent with market liberalism, particularly through inventive uses of property rights. Another path, explored by many in industry, proclaims that it is good business to operate with at least some concern for conservation, for businesses can increase their profits by so doing. Moreover, in recent years, as religious conservatives have gained power among policymakers on the right, environmentalists within their ranks have pushed for greater concern for what they view as a responsibility to serve as responsible stewards over God’s creation. From three disparate strands of the conservative tapestry emerge three arguments for conservation, one philosophical, one pragmatic, and one spiritual. While some on the Left would like to believe that the moral values of the Right exclude concern for the environment, such belief does not tally with reality.

First, there is the spiritual approach. Environmentalism need not, and perhaps should not, be merely pragmatic. Indeed, religious conservatives have found scriptural support for conservation. In Genesis 1:26-28, mankind is given dominion over all the earth and its life. But the gift seems more stewardship than license, for at Genesis 2:15, man is placed in Eden not only to work it but to watch over it as well. Leviticus 25:1-12, with its focus on the Sabbatical Year, implies the land should not be worked until all its resources and productive capacity are exhausted. Isaiah 5:8-10 speaks of the judgment that will be visited upon those who overuse and use unwisely the land. From a religious standpoint, there is a clear responsibility to be concerned about conservation and, at least to some degree, holding the earth in trust for future generations.

Those on the Left can often be found bemoaning the Religious Right’s power over the Republican Party. But if religious conservatives’ influence leads to wiser environmental policy and a more conservation-oriented mindset in the U.S., perhaps such critics will finally fall silent on an issue for which they previously thought only they had the solution.

Second, there is the philosophical approach. The free market approach to environmentalism proceeds from the classical liberal view of human nature: it is unreasonable and unrealistic to pursue any policy or vision that ignores human self-interest. If environmentalism is to be at all effective, it must convince people they have a stake in such a movement. As such, environmental efforts should ultimately be entirely privatized. The market does not exist devoid of values, for all individual agents operating within it have ideas, values, and principles upon which they choose among options, make decisions, and act. If some of those individuals within the market value conservation, they should purchase the property rights of those parts of nature they wish to see conserved. Behind each blade of grass, each shrub, and each tree could stand a steward who is willing and legally able, thanks to the private property system, to protect it. Such an approach not only allows those most concerned for conservation to be responsible for its realization, but it also avoids coercive regulation, leaving instead only liberty.

Two objections are usually mounted to the free market environmental vision. First, it is said that corporations are ruthless and will exploit the environment as such, and that globalization is a force that inherently causes ecological disruption. Recognizing this, the State is to be favored in protecting the environment.

The problem is that this thesis is wrong. Corporations are not bent on destroying the environment, and globalization has led to great environmental protection across the world.
So, what is the true nature of the allegedly soulless corporations? The self-interest observed by classical liberalism is nowhere more apparent than among America’s business leaders. And this is as it should be. One can get a glimpse here of the third approach to environmentalism: the pragmatic. Milton Friedman wrote in 1970 that “there is one and only one social responsibility of business—to use its resources and engage in activities designed to increase its profits so long as it stays within the rules of the game, which is to say, engages in open and free competition without deception or fraud.” It is entirely possible for businesses to increase their profits by eschewing environmentally damaging methods in favor of ventures more in line with conservation. Actions that generate good will towards the corporation are essentially actions that serve the corporation’s self-interest. There is no question that certain agents within the market value environmentally friendly practices and this value can color their decision-making and purchases. Other times, environmental practices can also cut costs. Profit maximization, while fundamentally a private concern, often serves a second role as a means to what is seen as “social responsibility.” So the soulless corporation has a soul after all, arising from self-interest.

As for globalization, the fact is that with greater prosperity comes greater environmental protection, usually in the form of regulations. While market liberalism is opposed to regulations, it is clear from the evidence that liberal market reforms across the world have led to greater development, and with more money in their coffers developing states, once dependent on the harvesting of their resources for economic survival, can now look at their environments as non-economic resources worth preserving. In his book In Defense of Global Capitalism, Johan Norberg points out that corporations seeking profit in countries enacting environmental protections tend to conform to such regulations in order to expand their bottom line. Respecting environmental concerns and doing business is more profitable than not doing business at all. Norberg notes that expenditures on environmental compliance are proportionately small for most companies, and that, in seeking above all profits and good business environments, globalization can in fact lead to an environmental race to the top as opposed to the oft-proclaimed “race to the bottom.”

Returning to the classical liberal approach, those mounting the anti-market attack would also do well to remember the oh-so-tender care with which socialist governments treated their environments. The Soviet Bloc was adept at causing wanton ecological destruction and producing wastelands—for the good of all, of course. Turning to socialist tools to solve environmental problems is a tried and true road to disaster, and the market liberalism approach easily stands superior to it. Capitalism, it turns out, is much more environmentally friendly than its socialist opposite.

Market liberalism also has an answer for one of the greatest environmental concerns: pollution. Within the system of individual property rights that supports capitalism is a ready solution. Pollution is by definition a violation of others rights, understood as either a trespass against another’s person or property. Widespread pollution is largely a result of the State favoring certain polluters, allowing them much larger trespasses than should otherwise occur. Free market environmentalism’s task is to reaffirm and reestablish each citizen’s right to be free of trespass, and pollution will be reduced in this mission to defend and restore the rights of each individual. For individuals do not want their property rights infringed, and, with a stake in preventing damage to themselves or their property, will demand their rights be protected.

The second objection to the market liberalism approach usually condemns it as unrealistic or unfeasible. Only the State, it is claimed, has the resources and ability to protect large areas of the Earth. This objection ignores reality. The free market environmental program is already underway. The Harvard sociobiologist E. O. Wilson pointed to NGOs such as the Nature Conservancy and Conservation International as key to solving ecological crises and the global decline in biodiversity in his book The Future of Life. Wilson recognized that governments are in command of the resources necessary to solve long-term environmental problems, but he also noted that they have shown themselves fundamentally untrustworthy, unwilling to execute their promises to do so. While governments might be too weak of will to do what is necessary to preserve the biodiversity essential for our survival as a species, NGOs possess this will, and, with resources, can do substantial good.

Another NGO, the World Wildlife Fund, no longer focuses singularly on charismatic animals such as tigers and pandas, but aims to protect all of the world’s threatened biodiversity. The Fund forms partnerships with locals. Preserves that might otherwise stop individuals from gathering food can be made into economic assets, with locals serving as wildlife experts and guides. The result is ecotourism, a flourishing blend of market principles and environmentalism.

Another approach is private reserve acquisition. Since the 1980s NGOs have been pursuing creative debt-for-nature trades. Direct purchases of land are also common. Developing nations often auction off their forest resources to logging companies in order to raise funds for national projects. However, logging companies operate on thin profit margins, and if enough money can be raised NGOs can often outbid the loggers on a per-acre basis. The Nature Conservancy and Conservation International follow this model, buying up at-risk ecosystems and setting themselves up as stewards, often blending their approach with ecotourism. Indeed, the Nature Conservancy has purchased over its history over 11.5 million acres of environmentally important land. It protects an area larger in aggregate than Switzerland.

Conservation International, to cite just one example of its work across the world, negotiated a lease of 200,000 acres of Guyana’s national rainforest in which it runs the southeastern portion of the forest as a nature preserve. Guyana benefits from the profits it reaps, which are equal to, if not in excess of, what it would gain from a timber lease that would also result in the thinning of its forests. It may one day even gain from the trading of carbon sequestration credits; under the Kyoto Protocol, developing states can be paid merely for preserving their forests. Groups such as Conservation International, which is also pursuing similar ventures in Bolivia, Brazil, Peru, Cambodia, Indonesia, and Madagascar, help states that would otherwise sell off their resources to loggers keep their forests for future gain. Self-interest aligns with self-interest, and the world is better for it.

The market is inherently democratic. Individuals only enter into agreements and transactions voluntarily. Groups such as the Nature Conservancy, the World Wildlife Fund, and Conservation International allow people to channel their resources towards desired outcomes, in this case environmental conservation. Through use of transferable property rights, such agents of international civil society have found means to protect the environment in ways consistent with market liberalism. While governments such as that of the United States have shown themselves capable of managing large state-run parks and nature preserves, agents in civil society have shown themselves equally, if not more, competent at running such reserves. Of late, such agents are certainly more willing than states to create new reserves.

In a capitalistic society, wealth and profit stand as virtues. But there need not necessarily always be tension between conservation and profit. The market does not exist independently of values, for individuals operating within it have preferences based on ideas and principles. Keeping the earth in good condition can be viewed as a long-term capital investment, and as a trust for the future. The strands of religious, philosophical, and pragmatic conservatism unite to provide a diverse, wise, and feasible approach to environmentalism.
