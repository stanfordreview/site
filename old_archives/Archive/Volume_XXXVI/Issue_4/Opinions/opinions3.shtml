Editor's Note: How to Criticize The Review

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

I’ve been excited about Issue 4 ever since I began planning-out my volume in February. I realized that this issue would be the last published before the end of the quarter, and would likely contain many pieces that our writers wanted published before Spring Break. We also slated a feature on the Stanford Indian to appear in Issue 4. I never anticipated, however, the vast number of quality pieces our staff submitted for the last deadline.

Although a piece on the politicization of Stanford’s writing program headlines the issue, the feature on the Stanford Indian should also be of particular interest to readers. Features Editor Luukas Ilves spent a great deal of time in the University Archives, exploring the history of our former mascot. A thorough historical survey, an opinions piece, a humorous sidebar, and an epilogue were all compiled by Ilves for the feature. An article from a Native American student and a letter from one of our alumni were also included. I really think we demonstrated the truth behind the statement Editor Emeritus Ben Guthrie made in a recent letter published in The Daily: “The Review is arguably the only forum on campus where you could have an honest debate about the mascot; where multiple alternative views are presented and debated.”
The Review often recognizes the importance of presenting opposing views. Several times in our past, articles representing both sides have been printed in the same issue. Although individual writers can be criticized for taking a particular stance, the paper, because of the diverse opinions it presents, cannot. Publishing both sides improves the integrity, educational value, and quality of our paper.

Unfortunately, in the past, people have often ignored our efforts to give representation of both sides of an issue, and simply criticize us for printing an unpopular view. I know this occurred with an article I wrote last year on the Black Student Union (BSU). Almost all of those who disagreed with the article, which argued that several student groups (including the BSU) were exploiting the funding system, failed to recognize that The Review printed an article from the Co-chair of the BSU in the same edition. Readers could gain an unbiased perspective on the issue by reading both articles. Yet, students attacked The Review as a whole. The editorial board of The Stanford Daily even authored a piece entitled “Conservatives Deserve a Better Paper than The Review” in response.

Critics of The Review often also undervalue facts. I know I was particularly disturbed by the comment that “The Review’s writers must work harder to base their articles on logic and substantiated facts”—a claim made in the same Daily editorial. I spent more time researching for the BSU article than any other piece I have written for The Review. I interviewed numerous individuals, went through hundred of documents, and analyzed a great amount of data. The evidence in the piece was factual, not fabricated. Never, in the dozens of responses I received to the article, did someone detail a specific fact they disagreed with. Instead, critics made unspecific un-backed and simply untrue claims similar to that in The Daily. Only members of The Review staff appreciated the research and factual representation in my article.

I’m writing about this in this issue because I want to encourage readers and critics to not treat the Indian feature as they did my BSU article. Individuals who disagree with particular facts or their representations should respond to those specific facts. Criticisms directed at The Review as a whole or un-backed claims that the article is not researched well will be given little respect from my staff. In fact, we will probably just find them humorous. As detailed earlier, I know that Ilves spent a significant amount of time compiling data for the article. I also know that I allotted nearly a whole page for a response to the issue by a Native American student. Attacks without factual backing largely fall into the category that our Business Manager, Navin Kadaba, condemns in his article this issue.

That said, I definitely want to receive letters in response to the feature. The feature is meant to entice thoughts from the student body on a very interesting and provocative issue. I will certainly print letters that demonstrate integrity, and look forward to reading them. If you disagree with something that is said, please consider submitting one. Following the response to Review content is one of the most exciting parts of my job!

Fiat Lux!

Ryan Tracey
Editor-in-Chief
