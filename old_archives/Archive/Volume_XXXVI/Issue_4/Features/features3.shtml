“Why Can’t We All Just Get Along?”

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

Nobody believes we should bring back the Stanford Indian. For all of the controversy that has and continues to surround the mascot, I believe dropping it was the right thing to do. If a symbol or mascot is extremely painful to a group within the university, then there seems to be no reason to continue to pain them by officially endorsing this mascot as a symbol of the university. Ultimately, the claims of students and professors who are current members of the Stanford community take precedence over the wishes of alumni or outsiders in shaping the present direction of the institution.

But we should also note how much of a controversy dropping the Indian truly was. The historical record clearly shows that most members of the Stanford community and many Native Americans within and without the community did not view the Indian as a racist icon. For many, the Indian stood and continues to stand for virtue, courage and athleticism. While one may take issue with such valorization, calling it “outmoded,” “insensitive” or “unenlightened,” calling such beliefs racist is an egregious attack.

The Oxford English Dictionary tells us that a racist “believes in the superiority of a particular race, leading to prejudice and antagonism towards people of other races.” It is demeaning, insulting and polarizing to assume that racial supremacy is the motive behind Stanford Indian nostalgia. The accusation of racism is particularly dangerous in the contemporary political context when leveled by a member of a minority group against someone else. If your name appears in print next to an accusation of racism, there is little you can do to rationally and coolly rebut the charge. And in an age of Google, that charge is instantly available to everyone for eternity. In many ways, the charge of racism today is like the charge of anti-revolutionary activity in Paris in 1790, Fascist belief in the 1930s or Communism in the McCarthy era: absolute, inescapable, and all-consuming.

Unfortunately, this debate has descended to just that—accusations of racism. SAIO’s very first statement against the T-shirts included the word, and Adam Bad Wound has reiterated this charge against the Review. These accusations have had a definite chilling affect. I spoke to Kunal Gullapalli, President of Sigma Chi—the fraternity that printed Stanford Indian T-shirts. He was initially ready to comment on the matter, but later wrote back explaining that because of the level of controversy surrounding the matter he no longer felt comfortable commenting. When I solicited comments from members of SAIO, I received a long response asking me not to write an article on the history of this conflict because the simple act of talking about the past would be hurtful the Stanford Native American community. I even weighed the possibility of not affixing my name to this article and instead employing a pseudonym. Eventually, I decided that would be an absurd decision. I have faith in Stanford students. I know that Stanford students will transcend petty name-calling and instead address the substance of my comments.

Indeed, if there is only one conclusion you should draw from my historical sketch of the controversy, it is how very absurd accusations of both racism and politically correct overreach are. The record clearly shows that those who support the Indian have done so out of noble reasons. The story of Timm Williams shows that there have been victims—well-meaning people who passionately cared about Native American issues who ended up on the wrong side of history. But the record also shows that supporters of the Indian have acted provocatively and at times bellicosely. When Stanford Native Americans have spoken stridently against the Indian, they have done so with good reason.

Our campus has unresolved issues when it comes to the Indian. One part of the student body and community feels attached to an old symbol of our university. Another part of the community feels it needs to lobby the administration for more stringent restriction on the use of this same symbol. We will not settle these differences through back-and-forth flame wars in the Daily and the Review. Nor will an administrative fiat from above that may or may not find an effective solution suffice. Unless we want to be dealing with the same hurtful disagreement another thirty years from now, I suggest we do what rational adults do: we sit down together and talk about what is and isn’t offensive; about how we can minimize offense while protecting free expression; about what our community and fundamental standards should be.

I look forward to a fruitful, non-confrontational dialogue.

I would like to thank Kimball Bighorse for his contribution. His willingness to share his opinion represents the cooperative ideal to which we should aspire.

I would welcome any comments or letters on the Stanford Indian. Please email them to our editor.
