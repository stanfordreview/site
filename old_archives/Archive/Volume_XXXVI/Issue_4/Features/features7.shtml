Book Review: Bad Company

by Jack Greer

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

We all jump into the middle of conversations. There’s that sudden orientation process as you try to figure out what was just discussed, what is currently being discussed, and what is left on the agenda to discuss. Sometimes this process can take awhile, but generally after a minute or so you’re comfortable enough to join the discussion on the current topic. There’s still that nagging feeling, though, that you don’t quite know what’s going on. That’s exactly how I felt reading this book.

My dad introduced me to Jack Higgins with The Eagle Has Landed, followed shortly thereafter by The Eagle Has Flown. These are outstanding historical fiction thrillers. Higgins creates rich characters, sets the scene perfectly, and weaves it all together with masterly control of historical fact. Bad Company seems like it might have some rich characters, but I really don’t know because they’re not developed much in the novel. This is the umpteenth book in a series featuring a British secret agent named Sean Dillon. Any serial character, of course, has to have his band of merry men—in this case, consisting of some kind of British general, a British major, a computer whiz (but not a nerdy one), and a couple of gangsters — who follow him from one book to the next. Perhaps I need to read all ten thousand of these books before I get to know the characters?

But with every drawback, there’s the potential for a payoff. In this case, it’s the fact that the book is short (big font, wide-spacing) and readable (no complicated sentences, no big words, no page-long paragraphs about how much Jane hates and loves John). Perfect for busy college students.

True to form, Higgins tells a fascinating tale of fiction grounded in historical fact. In the final days of the Third Reich, German military officers are scrambling to do everything in their power to surrender to the Western Allies, fearing Soviet torture. Amidst the chaos, which Higgins effectively conveys, several planes take-off from the city streets of Berlin, including one carrying a German nobleman-turned-soldier who has been entrusted with Hitler’s secret diary—and access codes to unlimited Swiss funds. Baron Max von Berger builds himself a corporate empire after the war, reconnecting with his old troops and allowing these old soldiers to live decent lives. I found myself sympathizing with the Baron, an honorable man who never joined the Nazis, as one by one his old friends die off.

The diary records a secret meeting between Adolf and the father of the current U.S. president, and its details could be politically damaging. Refusing to stand for blackmail, Sean Dillon and his merry band find themselves in a war against the Baron. The story takes us from the fall of Berlin to the Bedouin of the Arabian Peninsula, from the Baron’s castle to London and Nantucket. The message of the book appears to be: “There are men of a rough persuasion who are willing to take care of the kinds of situations that ordinary people can’t. They’re called soldiers.” This reminds me of the famous George Orwell quote: “We sleep safe in our beds because rough men stand ready in the night to visit violence on those who would do us harm.” These soldiers end up doing lots of unnecessary killing, though, and it sometimes seems this ultra-secret military unit Dillon is part of more often resembles the Sopranos than the SAS: Got a problem? Kill someone.

But it’s all fun. Give yourself some mindless escapism.

 

Higgins, Jack. Bad Company. New York: G. P. Putnam, 2003. 287 pp.
