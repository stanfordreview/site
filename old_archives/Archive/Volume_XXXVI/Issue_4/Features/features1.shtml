The Price of of the Indian Mascot

by Kimball Bighorse

Guest Contributor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

There’s a great Seinfeld episode where Jerry’s dentist, Tim Whatley, converts to Judaism and starts telling Jewish jokes. Of course, Jerry complains to a priest, who asks logically, “and this offends you as a Jewish person?” Jerry’s answer: “No, it offends me as a comedian!” I share this example to ask a simple question: how does one acquire the rights to enjoy the benefits of a particular identity? And also to show that every lesson in life can be learned from watching Seinfeld, but that’s a different essay.

Each of us will leave this institution with a degree in hand, a member of an exclusive set of people with the shared experience of having satisfied the University’s graduation requirements. And we will rightfully cherish this membership. But let’s imagine that some person somewhere (gasp!) falsifies their resume and claims that they are a Stanford graduate, having not met the University’s requirements for graduation. Would we as true Stanford graduates be peeved? Miffed? Indifferent? Let’s say it’s not just a few scattered Radio Shack executives, but perhaps a large number of people in the work force, who gain from the masquerade to the extent that being an actual Stanford graduate no longer sets you apart to potential employers or people you meet at the pub. Despondent and disheartened, you begin to devalue your education, wondering why you bothered jumping through all those hoops and incurring all that expense to get a real Stanford degree when you’d end up with a boss who has 4 more years’ experience than you since he started working with Stanford on his resume while you were wasting your time actually going to Stanford! The error of these people is this: they claim the benefits of being identified as Stanford graduates, but without the associated costs.

Let’s take the analogy another step further. Let’s say that somehow in the near future, Stanford graduates are systematically targeted for elimination by the government. Just to be clear, not only is it legal to kill Stanford graduates, but the scalp from your corpse fetches a price according to your major in shops at the Stanford Shopping Center and Union Square. Admitting you went to Stanford is enough to get you shot; you even start saying you went to Cal to save your skin. Say this goes on for several years, long enough for you to have kids that are also Stanford graduates. They’re not allowed to sing “All Right Now” in their schools and are cruelly punished if they do.

Then your kids have kids that are Stanford graduates, but society figures things out somewhat by then and the extermination code is off the books by then. You tell your grandkids how much it means to you to be a Stanford graduate, and how special it is for them to be Stanford graduates too. Now you send them to a school that now has romantic notions of being a Stanford graduate, and think it’s pretty cool. Some over-achieving, enthusiastic student heard that Stanford graduates were brainy scientific types, so they make a sketch of someone in a lab coat with a bulbous forehead or something, and now everyone thinks it’s really cool to be the “Stanford Graduates;” someone does pseudo-scientific experiments at halftime of football games and pretends to graduate from Stanford, all to the school fight song. Everyone cheers and feels great about pretending to be brainy and scientific-like.

But strangely, your grandkids don’t quite feel good about being Stanford graduates anymore. They don’t want to be associated with Stanford at all. They feel different from their peers, and not in the way you did when you graduated. They don’t want to be the nerdy lab technician with the bulbous forehead on their friends’ T-shirts, and while you don’t vote in online polls against these representations, you certainly didn’t know any Stanford graduates like that. The American Psychological Association even does some research and finds that the phenomenon exists for young Stanford graduates everywhere.

As silly as this analogy seems, and as limited as it might be, here’s just a bit of what the American Psychological Association actually says about Native American mascots in a resolution based in part on research performed by Stephanie Fryberg, PhD, who is of the Tulalip Tribe as well as a two-fold Stanford graduate:

WHEREAS the continued use of American Indian mascots, symbols, images, and personalities by school systems appears to have a negative impact on the self-esteem of American Indian children …

THEREFORE BE IT RESOLVED that the American Psychological Association supports and recommends the immediate retirement of American Indian mascots, symbols, images, and personalities by schools, colleges, universities, athletic teams, and organizations.

The value of this exercise is at least to partially address the following question: what does it mean to be an “Indian?” Hopefully by now it’s clear that it means far more than simply going to a particular school or wearing a T-shirt, or calling yourself one for X number of years. In this case, it means sharing the experience in its entirety, both the costs and benefits associated with being Native American. Anything less dilutes and misrepresents the true meaning at best, and is brutally dishonest at worst.

It can be argued that mascots are not meant to usurp identity, and I concede that that’s true in most cases. However, these are exactly the cases in which the mascots are either not human or are extinct (think bears and Spartans, respectively—I’ll get to combative Irishmen in a minute). To make a mascot of a person is to dehumanize them, to categorize them with animals (remember “cockroaches” in Hotel Rwanda?). Further, Native people are most assuredly not extinct, despite the systematic, concerted effort by the government to the contrary. For this to be true has cost incalculable suffering, not to mention culture, property, language, and even life itself. To claim any benefit, as one sees them, of being “Indian” without paying the price makes a literal mockery of the real thing.

Can you imagine a tech start-up calling itself Googol in a lucrative overseas market? How about George Costanza claiming to be a marine biologist or an architect? Knowingly or not, this is exactly what one does when wearing a Stanford Indian T-shirt. If you want to call your company Googol, you’ll have to see what Larry and Sergey have to say. If you want to call yourself a marine biologist, you’ll have to experience what one experiences to become such. And if you want to call yourself a Fighting Irish, you know who to ask. But if you want to be an “Indian”, you may not find yourself willing or able to pay the price. But you can tell Indian jokes if you do.

Bighorse is a Senior in Symbolic Systems at Stanford. He is tribally affiliated with the Haudenosaunee/Dine.
