Expropriation in Vogue?

by Tucker Herbert

Staff Writer

 

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

What was once a common economic phenomenon in Latin America may now be reemerging from hibernation. On May 1st, Bolivian President Evo Morales sent the army to occupy foreign holdings and announced the nationalization of petroleum resources. He avowed that “tomorrow it will be the mines, the forest resources, and the land,” over which his government will seize control.

The logic is that foreign corporations signed contracts that exploit Bolivia and deprive its people of a fair share of the economic surplus of the natural resources. By nationalizing these industries, the government can renegotiate the contracts on more favorable terms, and force non-complying parties to leave the country. The extra cash can be used to develop social and health programs and education for the poor. In reality, the contracts between foreign companies and the Bolivian government are typical of any such contracts anywhere in the world. The violation of these contracts will deter future foreign investment across all industries in Bolivia. And the cash windfall from the renegotiated contracts pales in comparison to the annual foreign aid that Bolivia receives; Spain has already threatened to cut its economic assistance.

So the logic seems fuzzy at best. But if Morales did not announce the nationalization, indigenous groups declared that they would force him out of power, as they have done to many recent Bolivian presidents. His approval rating fell 12 percentage points in the month prior to the nationalization—a fact that was attributed to his inaction on this front. This is not the first time (by far) that Bolivia has nationalized an industry of a large natural resource. Tin, land, and gas—which were later re-privatized—have all been under government control at one time or another. Every time the government has promised to increase Bolivia’s development and decrease its inequality by seizing assets from rich foreigners and redistributing the wealth to poor Bolivians. But the fact remains that Bolivia is the poorest country in South America, and has maintained stark economic divisions between the indigenous peoples of the altiplano and the wealthier European decedants in the south east.

Instead of learning how to adapt to the system, many Latin American politicians would rather blame America for their country’s economic woes. However, Bolivia’s nationalization has almost no economic impact on the United States, as U.S. companies have only minimally invested in Bolivia. Most significantly, this move will drive a wedge between Leftists in Latin America. Brazil, led by moderate socialist Luis Inacio Lula da Silva, stands to lose significant economic and strategic assets from Bolivia’s expropriation of mostly Brazilian and Spanish owned gas fields. Morales recently signed a trade agreement with Cuba and Venezuela that aims to undermine the U.S. proposed Free Trade Area of the Americas. But by damaging its relationship with Brazil (and Bolivia’s relations with Chile, Peru, and Argentina are not much better), Bolivia could very well push its neighbors back to the center. If that happens, Cuba, Venezuela, and Bolivia will be abandoned to their own backwardness.

Latin American countries have tried on a number of occasions to turn to the European Union or China to reduce their reliance on U.S. export markets. While it is economically prudent to diversify one’s trading partners, many do this in an attempt to replace the U.S. completely as a trading partner. These countries have found that the EU is even more restrictive on agricultural imports than is the U.S. and that China’s intentions can be even more “imperialistic” than those that the U.S. is frequently accused of having. Nor can a program of south-south trade alone solve the problem of Latin America’s economic development. Poor countries simply do not have the demand to absorb all of each other’s goods, and such a system would be deprived of the technological boon that comes with foreign direct investment (FDI) from developed nations.

Morales complains that there are too few Bolivians employed in the management and scientific positions of the companies operating to extract natural resources from Bolivia. But if Repsol, the main Spanish company invested in Bolivia, could hire Bolivians to fill these positions rather than relocate Spanish citizens to Bolivia and pay Spanish wages, more Bolivians could receive employment. The problem is that there are too few Bolivians that are adequately trained and educated to do this work. Once nationalization is implemented, there is a distinct possibility of a collapse in Bolivian gas production due to technical mismanagement of these operations.

There is little chance that this nationalization will put Bolivia on the path to economic development. Instead of breeding instability and unsettling its neighbors, Bolivia should be taking a more cooperative role that would encourage foreign development economic assistance and FDI. Bolivia desperately needs physical infrastructure and improvements in its education system to become more developed. But by isolating itself, Bolivia will perpetuate its own depravation of the advanced technological methods that are essential to making that infrastructure and education worth while. Evo Morales declared on May 1st an end to “the pillage of our natural resources by foreign companies;” but Bolivia needs to get its house in order first—to educate its own population—if it hopes to rise from its subordinate position.

If a country truly desires to be economically bulimic, perhaps it is in America’s best interest to leave well enough alone. The hard-line policy has succeeded with Cuba. Venezuela’s Hugo Chavez feeds off of American criticism. Together, Cuba, Venezuela and Bolivia form a rather unique alliance in the world. This coalition will never envelop all of Latin America as Chavez’s Bolivarista dream would have it. Ecuador could potentially follow their lead, but even this is unlikely at the moment. Latin America may have swayed to the left in recent years, but the rest of the continent knows that they have too much at stake to drive out foreign investors. And that knowledge should serve as a moderating force for years to come.
