Kannokada and Okonkwo Reflect on Experiences

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

An interview was conducted with this year’s ASSU President Melanie Kannokada (left) and Vice President Aneto Okonkwo (right) through e-mail. Their responses are enumerated in this article.

What accomplishments in the ASSU are you most proud of?

- Club Sports – we secured $75K/yr for 3 years from the President’s Office to help the club sports group becoming financially sound while they develop a long term fundraising plan.

-7 Days of Late night food service.

-Institutionalization (transforming the organization) – there were a lot of inefficiencies within the organization when we first came in, on top of a lack of effective transition procedures and a method of transferring institutional knowledge to the newly elected. We created a formal transition process for the executive branch, as well as methods to document all of our meeting notes and best practices in an online format.

- VSO Collaboration Initiative – we held a well attended student leader summit and launched a collaboration fund that allows student groups to gain needed funding if they decide to launch their program or event with another student group.

- One on one interaction with students – This is the best part of our day – Through ASSU office hours or just in passing, we can connect with students and within 2-5 minutes help them navigate the University in order to solve a problem they’ve been puzzled about for weeks or months.

- Extending the hours of Green Library on Friday evenings.

- Our Graduate Student Council secured 40K from the Provost to fund an annual Thanksgiving Dinner. They hosted the first ever graduate student formal, and they have made great strides with faculty and graduate diversity advocacy.

What would you have liked to have done that you were not able to do?

- Mausoleum party, although it wasn’t even on our agenda for the year. Mausoleum hasn’t happened because no one has assumed leadership of the event for the past few years. During fall quarter we saw this as an opportunity, but it was too late in the game and by that time there were too many factors out of our control. Still, we were able to form a solid foundation with various stakeholders in the University and for next year’s leadership so this event can happen.

- Better communication with the student body. We actually accomplished quite a lot in many areas of campus; some accomplishments more visible that others, but we didn’t have a concrete method of sharing those with the student body. This is an area in which next year’s slate could improve.

- Working with the CDC to improve services and job offerings for non-professional grad students.

- Sustainability campaign in dorms.

If you were to recommend reforms for the ASSU, what would you recommend?

- Have a crisis management plan to form a cohesive organizational response when unexpected issues arise, such as the Commencement situation.

- Hire a marketing guru – executives and cabinet members don’t have the time to do marketing on top of all their other work. The ASSU needs to hire a marketing guru to think outside the box and connect with the average student.

- Student group funding – better communication with student groups about the current processes in place.

- Nominations Commission – we have hundreds of student representatives that voice their opinions in University committees affecting all areas of the student experience. While the online feedback form we created this year is a step in the right direction, additional efforts need to be focused to connect this feedback with the internal ASSU.

- Developing a unified ASSU yearly vision amongst the three branches and communicating that passionately from the beginning.

- Conduct a campus wide survey to find out what issues are most important to pursue in the upcoming years from the student government level. Often times ASSU officials run on platform without truly knowing the demand of their reforms.

What are the greatest challenges and opportunities Elizabeth Heng and Lauren Graham will face in taking over from you?

Opportunities: We’ve tried to build a strong foundation by cultivating positive relationships with the administration and transforming the ASSU as an organization to be more effective. We’ve essentially done this so our successors and ASSU officials down the road can be even more successful than their predecessors. We’re passing along all of our best practices and institutional knowledge to Lauren and Elizabeth, fine tuning their platforms, and developing a strategy for the year. They should be in good shape to accomplish their goals.

Challenges: Unifying the organization under a coherent vision, prioritizing projects, and funding – the general fee measure did not pass (it needed a 2/3 vote)– meaning there will still be a very limited pool of money to be allocated to the ever increasing amount of student groups.

You failed to secure the SOCC endorsement last year, but surprised many by going on to win the elections. Do you have any thoughts on the controversy around the SOCC this year?

It’s important for groups to support their own people, and SOCC was very successful in this endeavor. Essentially, we must keep in mind that endorsements don’t vote - people do - and the student body voted democratically to elect our new officials. Moving forward, we encourage those concerned by the situation to proactively get involved in ASSU leadership for the years to come.

Finally, what are you going to be doing after graduating? Should we look for
you in the political arena in a few years?

Aneto: Definitely not, I have no interest in politics. You will see me working with technology and startups in the future.

Melanie: Potentially, but not in the near future. In the short term I see my agent of change being in the arena of entrepreneurship and innovation.
