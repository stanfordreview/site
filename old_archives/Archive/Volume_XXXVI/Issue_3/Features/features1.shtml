An Interview With Larry Diamond

by Luukas Ilves

Features Editor

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

I recently sat down with Professor Larry Diamond, a Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution, for an interview. Professor Diamond served as a senior advisor to the Coalition Provisional Authority in Iraq in 2004. He is now a co-plaintiff with the ACLU in its lawsuit against NSA wiretapping efforts. The Stanford Review recently published a piece by Alec Rawls criticizing Professor Diamond’s involvement in this lawsuit (“Diamond not just in the rough, but out of bounds”, February 6, 2006). The Review believes in presenting a wide range of opinion, and I only felt it appropriate to extend to Professor Diamond the right of response.

Luukas Ilves: Do you have any comments on the article by Alec Rawls that the Review published?

Larry Diamond: I don’t think it’s productive or necessary for the debate to descend into a kind of tendentious gratuitous series of personal attacks that this article manifested. I don’t think it’s productive or appropriate for people who share common goals of waging an effective war on terror and broadly defending civil liberties, but who have different conceptions of where the balance lies between those two objectives, which can sometimes be in tension.

LI: I notice that this lawsuit has eleven co-plaintiffs, including the ACLU, Greenpeace, Christopher Hitchens and you. What brings so diverse a group of people that might not always be inclined to cooperation to work together?

LD: The only thing that unites all of the 11 plaintiffs in the ACLU lawsuit is a set of concerns about this particular program. One set of concerns is of a philosophical and national nature, one set is of a more personal nature.

On the level of philosophical concern, as a matter of principle and in terms of the defense of our democracy in the United States, the concerns are largely as they were stated in the lawsuit: that NSA wiretapping goes down a slippery slope toward an expansion of presidential power with no institutional check, that the rule of law should require some kind of judicial supervision and process of intercepting messages that American citizens are sending and receiving. If the law is deficient – and it is entirely logical to say that a law written in 1978, before we had email and cell phones, is outdated and needs modification. But I wasn’t aware that our democracy had descended to the point that it was OK for the President and the Executive to modify the law unilaterally on the basis of their own judgment and action. If the President can say “I need to do anything to protect the nation,” that allows him to do anything he wants to so long as he does so in the name of national security. I don’t think that’s an adequate standard, and I think what united the plaintiffs in the lawsuit is a philosophical and political belief that it’s not adequate.

The second level that unites the eleven plaintiffs is that each, in their own quite different ways, has reason to believe that some of their communications have been intercepted by this program--not that they have direct evidence but as a matter of deduction, weighing the nature of this program with its description; and each of the 11 have reason to believe that the quality of their work has been impaired by this quite reasonable belief that these interceptions have been going on.

LI: How has this concern affected your work in particular?

LD: The concern a political scientist would have is dealing with people in the Middle East. They might feel that if they’re sharing sensitive information, to put it baldly, they don’t want it sitting in a file at the NSA. It is possible that people would feel inhibited from communicating in the future--with myself, with other political scientists, with human rights activists, because of the fear that messages they send to these individuals and organizations in the U.S. might be intercepted. Even if they feel that “yes, the U.S. is more or less in the same philosophical corner,” their level of trust in the United States might be less than total.

There is a second concern which may be of interest to Review readers because they might wind up in this position at some point: students, Stanford students in particular, increasingly travel abroad to do research, including the Middle East. I had a student last year who went to Egypt and was conducting political interviews and research about the democracy movement and upcoming parliamentary elections. We were communicating back and forth about the interviews, for which he had promised the interviewees confidentiality. He was emailing back his professors, seeking advice, consulting about what he had learned. If we now learn that because he was doing this from an Arab country that is at the center of this intercepting effort, some of his emails might have been intercepted by this program. What does that mean about the promises of confidentiality he gave to his interviewees, about our ethical obligations and my ability to communicate with student researchers in the field in the future?

LI: What is the constitutional and legal basis of your lawsuit?

LD: The legal basis is that this program of warrant-less surveillance violates the Foreign Intelligence Security Act of 1978. The Constitutional argument is that it violates the 1st Amendment guarantees of free speech and the 4th Amendment guarantee against unreasonable search and seizure.

LI: What are the prospects for finding a legislative solution to this issue?

LD: There need to be legislative options. The answer is not for the Executive branch to simply declare what is necessary and what is legal and have the other two branches of government accept it.

We could get a longer time period of authorization for search warrants: instead of having 72 hours the administration could have a week to prepare to seek a warrant, or there could be a warrant issued targeting anybody within a certain radius of a particular individual. We’d have to think about whether the wider legislative authorization would be consistent with the 1st and 4th amendments to the constitution.

Some of my principled concern about the constraint of Presidential power and the defense of constitutionalism and the rule of law would be addressed by the pursuit of a legislative remedy rather than unilateral Executive action.

LI: What would be liable to happen if we didn’t act now?

LD: We already have processes underway that are beginning to yield some correction. It’s not just lawsuits but the investigations, hearing, initiations of Congressional action. My feeling is that power tends to want to expand and agents who hold positions of power tend not to want to be checked, monitored, and restrained. If we just said “fine, whatever’s necessary for a war on terror,” there would not be a natural limit to where the abridgment of our liberties would end. I’m not saying that this would be the demise of freedom in the U.S., but there would be an incremental creep toward greater expansion of Presidential, Executive and Intelligence agency power and, over time, very subtle diminution of our freedoms. I use the analogy of the frog boiling in water--if the temperature rises very gradually, the frog doesn’t jump out of the boiling pot and eventually maybe years or decades later the frog could die.

LI: What can Stanford students do about this issue?

LD: First of all be informed-- follow it legally. Constitutionally, write about it, debate about it on campus. Understand that we are in a period of national threat. But at the same time: in similar periods of threat to democracies, civil liberties have been vulnerable, and there’s a need for a civil, informed debate on where to find the balance. And if you’re motivated to become involved--write members of congress and advocacy articles, support efforts to correct processes so we find a better balance.

LI: The previous questions have all just been a formality. Now for the important question: we’re sitting here on the twelfth floor of the Hoover tower. Whom did you kill to get this office?

LD: Is this going to be published? The answer is dumb luck. This office was used as an administrative office by the Hoover Institution when the Hoover Administration was located on the 10th thru 12th floors of the tower. When the administration moved closer to the Hoover fellows, the offices in this complex gradually became vacant. My office was just being used by a visiting scholar. I came up here to see the visiting scholar and realized he’d be leaving soon. When I asked the director if there were any plans for the office and I volunteered myself, he graciously said “first come, first serve.”

LI: Do you feel like an evil overlord?

LD: I spent four years of my life at your age living in Wilbur hall, so it does give me some satisfaction to look down upon it all.
