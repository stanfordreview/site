Editor's Note: Imposed Silence

by Ryan Tracey

Editor-in-Chief

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On Thursday, April 27, members of the lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender community (LGBT) participated in a national day of silence to commemorate the silence of those too afraid to speak about their sexual orientation. This self-imposed silence, if implemented correctly, can be very effective. Mohandas Gandhi, certainly one of the most prolific protesters of all time, recognized the power of silence. Accordingly, he set aside one day each week of silence. Silence as a means of protest is honest, pure, and noble. Unfortunately, many liberals have recently begun to extend this principle to imposing silence on others. The protestors blocking the ingress to the Hoover Institution during the President’s visit on April 21 were attempting to silence the President and Hoover fellows—several of which are members of the Stanford faculty. As Gandhi exclaimed, “Silence of the sewn-up lips is no silence.” Imposed silence does not foster dialectic, nor does it evoke the benevolent characteristics of the voluntary silence practiced by LGBT and Gandhi. The protestors’ disrespect for the rights of the President and the Hoover Institution to have private discussions was completely disrespectful and disingenuous—a complete embarrassment for this University.

By scheduling a visit to the Hoover Institution during his California trip, President Bush paid Stanford University a great honor. His gesture showed that he respected members of the Stanford community and wanted their advice. Indeed, if his motorcade had not been blocked by protestors, he might also have paid due notice to the concerns of Stanford students. Yet, because several individuals decided to hinder the President’s plans, all of the sign-making, organizing, and chanting was in vain. The intended audience of the protest—the President—was not even given the opportunity to witness the event. He heard none of the opposition to the war, observed no criticisms of his domestic policy, and saw no support for action in Darfur. Instead, he was most likely frustrated with the immaturity of University students—students that were more concerned with making a publicity stunt than articulating their particular causes.

Sadly, the political intolerance of Stanford students will prevent conservative leaders from visiting our campus for quite some time. Bush’s decision to visit Stanford in the first place was somewhat of a surprise. San Francisco is the only city of the nation’s 25 largest that the President hasn’t visited, probably largely due to the incessant liberalism that characterizes the city and manifested itself in the April 21 protest at Stanford. Liberals don’t want to hear the counterpoint to their political beliefs. They don’t want to acknowledge that there is another side. They expect the government to protect their rights to protest and speak, but refuse to acknowledge the rights of others. This policy is completely opposed to progressive and democratic values. If left untouched, such beliefs could lead this nation to ruin. Luckily, most Americans realize this. Surely, the majority of Americans were disgusted by the April 21 protest.

The Stanford Daily Editorial Board’s gives Stanford students way too much credit for “burst[ing] the Bubble and meaningfully engag[ing] with the world outside Campus Loop” through the Presidential protest. The Editorial Board recognized the protest as proof that Stanford students care about the world and that they aren’t completely “self-absorbed.” The protest, however, proved the exact opposite. The students that blocked the motorcade route showed no concern for others—they merely wanted publicity. They wanted to be heroes, in a crusade against their own country’s leader. Blocking motorcades isn’t going to impeach Bush. It isn’t going to influence domestic policy. It will, however, get the participants on the news.

The actions of the protestors on April 21 were sickening, for that day I was embarrassed to be a Stanford student. I regret that I have to devote so much of this paper to covering the event. I sincerely believe that these individuals need to be chastised, and that others need to learn the error in their actions. If you’re going to protest, there is a select set of principles that can be followed to ensure success. Many completely ignored these principles on April 21.

Sincerely,

Ryan Tracey
Editor-in-Chief
