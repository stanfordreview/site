John Edwards’ Plan for America

by Tucker Herbert

Staff Writer

 

Forward Article to a Friend

Print Article

 

On Wednesday April 26th, John Edwards spoke before a Stanford audience on his views on how America should change. During his speech, the former senator and vice-presidential candidate claimed that “poverty is the great moral issue of our day.” Edwards currently directs an anti-poverty organization that seeks to create a government system with more mechanisms to assist 37 million poor Americans.

He went on to outline a series of policy suggestions that would bankrupt America and destroy our tradition of freedom through massive government intervention in the economy. I couldn’t help by marvel at the irony that I was listening to this speech in the same Kresge Auditorium where I began my studies in economics at Stanford.

John Edwards is a polished politician and an extraordinary speaker. It immediately became apparent how his heart-warming anecdotes and charismatic southern charm appealed to millions of voters in the past election. He backed up his arguments with logic and evidence. Unfortunately, many of the facts he cites are simply not so. John Edwards is a classic Democrat, one who believes that a tax-and-spend fiscal plan coupled with wide-scale social planning can solve America’s social and economic woes.

He began by addressing the devastation of hurricane Katrina—an example of mismanagement that has been one of the biggest embarrassments to the Bush administration. He first asserted that Katrina showed us “that clustering poor people together is a bad idea. It’s unhealthy, it’s not good for America, and it’s bad for democracy.” To counter this issue he proposed a program of housing vouchers that would give poor families government funds to move into wealthier neighborhoods. Ignoring for the moment the amazing financial burden this would entail—how would this even solve the problem? The hurricane still would have hit New Orleans and poor families would still face many of the same challenges to advancement, just in a different setting. The biggest hindrance that poor families living in poor neighborhoods face is a mediocre public school system—but the majority of Democrats have rejected the Republican-proposed school vouchers program that can directly address this problem.

Your correspondent had the opportunity to ask John Edwards the following: “You mentioned that you have been campaigning for an increase in the minimum wage. What would you consider to be the optimal minimum wage, and how would you respond to economists who argue that a raise in the minimum wage would increase the unemployment rate?” To this he responded that he believes $7.50/hour, although still not enough to live on in America, would be a good start from where we are now. He went on to argue that the economy performs better in states with a higher minimum wage because these people are less dependent on the State and contribute more to the tax base. He stated directly that the evidence contradicts economic theory. Does he really think that Stanford students are stupid enough to believe this?

Edwards proposed a program meant to tackle the asset debt problem plaguing low income Americans. Such a plan would involve setting up special savings accounts where the government would match the savings of poor working families. The government would encourage the poor to save their money by putting a dollar in their bank account for every dollar they save. What Edwards did not address was where this dollar would come from—an even bigger challenge considering the already giant size of the federal deficit. The sheer infeasibility of such a plan is almost laughable. He issued solid support for a national government healthcare system, and then rounded out his socialist agenda with the proclamation that all workers in America should be able to become union members. He described it as a right and a freedom to join a union. Should this freedom of choice not also extend to workers who do not want to be in a union? Under current laws, public teachers are required to pay union dues whether or not they desire to be union members.

While perhaps well-meaning, none of these policies would eliminate poverty in America, and the cost of implementation would cripple the economy. John Edwards neglected to address the three major economic issues that our country faces: the pending Social Security crisis, massive government debt, and a growing trade deficit. These problems require real leadership and not the populist-socialist propaganda that Edwards offered. Democrats at Stanford should demand better of their leaders. One should never blindly follow a political ideology; such behavior can only lead to that ideology’s demise, as a lack of questioning slowly erodes its own foundation. Such an epidemic is the central problem of the Democratic Party today. Polls indicate a lack of American support for President Bush--but this does not directly translate into support for the Democrats. Kerry and Edwards, after all, campaigned in the last election on a “not-Bush” platform with unsuccessful results. Although the feebleness of this tactic became readily apparent on election day, it appears the lesson behind it went unlearned.
