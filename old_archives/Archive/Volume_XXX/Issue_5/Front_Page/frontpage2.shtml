Front Page
SCR Urge Special Fee Refunds
by Charles Hallford
News Staff

The Stanford College Republicans (SCR) have undertaken an advertising campaign to convince students supportive of the war on Iraq to request Special Fee refunds from student groups that actively took anti-war stances.

SCR put up fliers all around campus and e-mailed multiple dorm e-mail lists, announcing a list of student organizations receiving Special Fees that "actively campaigned against liberating the Iraqi people." SCR fliers and e-mails encouraged students to request refunds for the Special Fee contributions allotted to these groups. The deadlines for these requests was Friday, April 18. The Stanford College Republicans recommended withdrawal of funds from the following organizations: The Stanford Daily, MEChA, Asian American Student Association (AASA), Stanford American Indian Organization (SAIO), Stanford NAACP, Black Student Union (BSU), Muslim Students Awareness Network (MSAN), and Students for Environmental Action at Stanford (SEAS).

SCR Co-President Joe Fairbanks agreed to an exclusive interview with the Review in which he defends the campaign and explains why SCR decided to campaign against these groups' Special Fees.

Stanford Review: Could you describe the extent to which this campaign has been carried out, including estimating how many dorms have been fliered?

Joe Fairbanks: We have contacted major news organizations, informing them as to the reasons we are pursuing the campaign in an effort to make this a nationwide phenomenon. At Stanford, we fliered all major dorm complexes as well as public student spaces. Additionally, we have sent letters to the Daily from members of the various organizations we are targeting, asking them to halt the political statements that have no bearing whatsoever on their founding cause and certainly to do not speak for all their members.

SR: How successful do you, as co-president of the Stanford College Republicans, expect the campaign to be?

JF: I expect the campaign to be moderately successful. We did not begin it as soon as we could have, so that damaged our possible optimal effect. More importantly, the ASSU has no way to gauge the amount of students withdrawing their fees for this specific purpose, so keeping track of our success will be difficult. The point of this, however, is to spread the discussion around campus whether it is right for ethnic groups to use the student body's money as a resource for making political statements that not only do not reflect the views of all their membership, but also does not advance the causes of their founding purpose, which is, in most cases, to form a cultural, not political, base and home for their members.

SR: Could you describe the responses SCR has received as a result of this campaign?

JF: We have received many positive responses from people who agree that these organizations should not be conducting themselves as they are with student money. So far, we have only received one negative response from one of our own College Republican members who questioned the need for the campaign and was concerned about the financial impact on overall student life. We responded that there will, obviously, be no significant negative change in the quality of student life as a result of our campaign.

SR: Critics say that your fliers overlook the positive aspects of those groups targeted. How do you respond to such criticism?

JF: We're not questioning the positive aspects of the groups, so their arguments are irrelevant. We are simply pointing out the negative aspects of the group, which no one else seems to have the courage or will to do. If no one points out these negative aspects, the groups will never be able to improve.

SR: Do you think that the presence of many ethnic-themed groups in the targeted groups is a coincidence? Why or why not?

JF: No, I don't. Usually, the ethnic-themed groups at Stanford are run by extreme pseudo-leftists who have only conformed with what is popular on a college campus and want to make political statements that will make them liked on this campus, rather than advancing the true causes of their organization.

SR: How did the idea for this campaign come about?

JF: One of our members suggested it through an e-mail. He believed we could make this a bigger issue campus-wide and hopefully get a debate started, which has seemed to work so far.

SR: Is there anything else you feel people should know about this campaign or its intentions? (If so, what?)

JF: These campaigns are not targeted towards an ethnicity; instead they target an irresponsible action of using student fees for purposes tangential to the groups' purposes. It would be on the same order as the Stanford College Republicans using student money to teach baking classes. I, myself, am a member of one of the targeted group-- the Stanford American Indian Organization--and do not believe it should have involved itself with opposing the war in an attempt to speak for all its members. They did not even have members vote on the issue.

Other students also offered their opinions on the issue. Okada RA Rachel Spitzer stated that she likes the campaign because she believes students should pay more attention to what student groups get their Special Fees, especially if those groups are making political statements, although she might not request refunds herself.
