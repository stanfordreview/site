Political Harmony? Not Yet
by Travis Menk
News Staff

On Friday, April 18, the Stanford Law School hosted regional leaders from across the political spectrum to discuss whether and how the left and right can unite.

The discussion, sponsored by the American Constitution Society, The Federalist Society, and The National Lawyers Guild concentrated on the vital issues currently facing the U.S. as advertised. The speakers were Tom Campbell, a former Congressman and current Dean for the Haas School of Business at UC-Berkeley and Matt Gonzalez, current President of the San Francisco Board of Supervisors. Both men discussed the issue of civil liberties with regards to the Patriot Act, and Tom Campbell contributed points on the domestic "war on drugs." The issue of whether and how the Left and Right was addressed only anecdotally.

Mr. Campbell argued that there is no question that the nation's "war on drugs" has failed. If the drug war was effective, in Mr. Campbell's mind, a marked decrease in the supply and increase in the price of drugs would have been observable. In reality, however, cocaine now costs a fourth and heroin a fifth of what it did only two years ago. Mr. Campbell also articulated that drugs are more readily available to high-school students, incarcerations for drug charges are on the rise, and society is not prepared with the necessary drug treatment facilities. Additionally, he also cited his beliefs in serious issues of racial discrimination in the prosecution of drug cases.

Mr. Campbell continued his remarks by focusing on the implications of the Patriot Act for civil liberties. He briefly pointed to searchable school records. He then discussed "Seek and Peak" opportunities at embassies and consulates, which were once limited to espionage but now are permitted if suspicion of foreign sponsored terrorism exists. He also looked to the government's ability to examine sender and recipient addresses not only of conventional mail but also of e-mail and to the federal government's supposed ability to incarcerate individuals on secret evidence seen only by a judge.

Mr. Gonzalez began his remarks by looking at the Patriot Act. Despite being a leftist, he feels the Democrats need to stop blaming the Patriot Act on the Republicans as Democrats, including the late Paul Wellstone, were ardent supporters of the legislation as well. However, he feels support of the Patriot Act to have been an act of self- preservation on the part of Democrats in order not to appear un-patriotic in the aftermath of September 11, 2001. Mr. Gonzalez believes that certain groups are afraid that the Patriot Act can be construed to treat civil disobedience and actions that may lead to unintentional danger as truly dangerous acts like terrorism.

In discussing these vital issues, the audience had to make an undue effort to glean the possibilities for uniting the Right and Left. Mr. Campbell mentioned that "courage is going to be required" of Democrats and Republicans not always to tread the safe route. He believes that politicians need to say and do not what is safe for them but what is best for the country as a whole. For this reason, Mr. Campbell jokingly stated, Senators should not be able to run for the Presidency and Court of Appeals judges should be ineligible for the Supreme Court so that they will stop making "safe" decisions in order to avoid ruling themselves out of future political races. Examples he cited were Senators Orrin Hatch and Edward Kennedy and every appeals court that has ever voted to allow the President of the US to wage war without a declaration of war from Congress. Other solutions mentioned included term limits, multi-member districts, and instantaneous run-offs.

Overall, the discussion avoided its basic purpose. Little rationale was offered as to how the right and left might unite. The information discussed was primarily a laundry-list of the speakers' pet issues, not a set of examples germane to the topic at hand. Another opportunity has been missed to host an honest discussion about the future efficacy of this nation's policy-making.
