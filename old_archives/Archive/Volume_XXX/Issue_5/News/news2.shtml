Sororities and Fraternities Seek Recruits in Two Week Run
by Charles Hallford
News Staff

On April 6, Stanford's fraternities and sororities began their annual drive to attract new members, known respectively as Rush and Recruitment.

The Interfraternity Council (IFC) kicked-off this year's Rush with an all-fraternity event at the Oak Room on the second floor of Tresidder Union on April 6. Then, a small number of fraternities held separate events the following four nights. Each fraternity also had the opportunity to hold an optional event on Saturday, April 12. There were no events on either Friday, April 11, or Sunday, April 13. The following week, each fraternity again held two events. Rush then culminated in off-campus weekend trips sponsored by each fraternity on Friday or Saturday. The fraternities sent out their bids on Monday, April 21.

Recruitment consisted of an orientation meeting, open houses, and invitational parties. Recruitment began on Monday April 7 with an important informational meeting, in the Oak Room of Tresidder, where female students considering sorority life learned about Recruitment procedures and meet their two recruitment counselors, each called a Rho Chi. Then, on Friday April 10, prospective members visited five open houses in Tresidder. Each day after that for three days, prospective members received invitations to invitational parties but could only attend a maximum number of four parties on Saturday, three on Sunday, and two on Monday. Then on Tuesday the sororities sent out their formal bids. According to the Intersorority Council (ISC) Recruitment Chair Katy Quarles, 211 female students came out to Recruitment, consisting of two juniors, 32 sophomores, and 177 freshmen. "Every girl that came out was awesome," she said. "Everyone that I met was very enthusiastic."

Sorority members were prohibited to speak with Prospective Members outside of Recruitment during the Formal Recruitment period (April 8-15). Such practices would be considered "Dirty Recruitment." Also, despite the stereotype of sorority events as being full of alcoholic beverages, all of Recruitment was dry.

Rush was also dry. Fraternities were allowed to take prospective members out to dinner, but only if rushing individuals were returned to campus by 7:30pm. While the Rush events were very similar to the Recruitment events, Rush had overnight retreats on April 18.

According to Miss Quarles, the pros of Recruitment consist mainly of getting to know people along while gaining the knowledge to make an informed decision as to whether or not to join a sorority. Similarly, a con is that Recruitment can feel very superficial because prospective members have short conversations with sorority members that determine whether or not the sorority will invite them in.

Miss Quarles also stated that Recruitment was very effective at putting forth a positive image of sororities that is often missing in the media. "I've heard a lot of ridiculous stereotypes on campus," she said. "[For instance] I'd really like to displace the stereotype that sororities are so expensive." Every quarter the regular active dues of a sorority are between $110 and $180. However, the average pledge quarter, before full membership, costs between $200 and $300.

Also, according to the ISC, "each sorority has a national philanthropic organization, as well as local charities to which they contribute their services." Whether spending a day at a sorority's charity or planning an exciting and innovative fundraiser, sorority members will have the chance to feel involved in the local community by helping others.

Fraternities operate similarly. According to the IFC, "The cost will vary among fraternities, especially between housed and unhoused chapters. On average, unhoused chapters have dues and expenses totaling $200 a quarter. Housed chapters, on average, cost $2400 per quarter for room, board, and social dues."

Currently, there are seven housed fraternities and eight unhoused fraternities. Phi Kappa Psi fraternity was housed at the beginning of Fall 2002. According to the IFC, "Sigma Nu fraternity will be housed beginning Fall 2003. With the housing of Phi Psi and Sigma Nu, the University's cap for Greek houses will be met. No additional Greek housing is anticipated for the next few years."

"Many chapters offer payment plans; some offer scholarships, etc. For all chapters, finances are an important commitment on the part of members," says the IFC. "For housed fraternities, new members are liable for any possible debt built up by the chapter." The Office of Student Activities (OSA) maintains "status letters" on the financial status of all housed chapters and can provide students with answers to financial questions about Greek life.

Stanford University policy is that no Rush activities may include alcohol, with stiff penalties for any fraternity in possession of alcohol at any time during the Rush period. This included more than just Rush events; it held anytime and anywhere during the two-week period, including Bid Night and Bid Day activities.

Among Stanford's Greek organizations an African-American fraternity and two African-American sororities that are part of neither the IFC nor the ISC. All seven of these Greek organizations are unhoused.
