Conservative Publication Outreach to Stanford Thwarted by Daily Delays
by Harrison Osaki
News Staff

The Stanford Daily has repeatedly wavered on printing an advertisement commenting on the anti-war protests at Stanford on March 5th. The controversial advertisement, which the Daily received from the Center for the Study of Popular Culture (CSPC) headed by David Horowitz, explained the connections between the "National Youth and Student Peace Coalition," which organized the protests, and various on-campus Communist-affiliated groups.

Entitled "An Open Letter to Student Anti-War Protestors," the advertisement explored the links between the March 5th Student Strike and various Communist and Communist-sympathizing groups. The author of the advertisement, who used the pseudonym "Brian Sayre," purported to be a former member of numerous Communist organizations including, among others, the Communist League of Canada.

Mr. Sayre explained in the advertisement various techniques used by Communist organizations in order to coerce prospective recruits into joining the protests and demonstrations that were then used to radicalize students further. One of these techniques involves what Mr. Sayre calls an "Affinity Group," which in theory is a small group of people who decide to work together by consensus on political issues but in reality is "a ruthlessly effective way of manipulating the less extreme into greater acts of extremism."

Mr. Sayre said in his advertisement that political-affinity groups on campuses are used in order to quash dissent by singling out individuals for attention, and that they are almost invariably under the control of a Ôspokescouncil.' Individual members of the parent Communist organizations usually lead these affinity groups and, using peer pressure and force, persuade their followers to commit to increasingly radical demonstrations and actions.

Mr. Sayre pointed in his advertisement to the example of the events surrounding a protest march in Toronto, Canada, which resulted in the students "huddled miserably on the floor of the lobby of a major bank", without food or water --unlike their leaders, who had apparently brought supplies for themselves. Although Mr. Sayre is not explicit, this is most likely a reference to the twenty-hour student sit-in, in January 1998.

Mr. Sayre mentioned the web site "Direct Action to Stop the War", which includes a disclaimer that the web site and its affinity groups are not linked to the Stanford Coalition for Peace and Justice (SCPJ). Mr. Sayre said in his advertisement that the placement of this disclaimer, which near the recruitment and affinity-group section of the site, is a sign of bad faith on the part of SCPJ. Since they are attempting to use students for the purpose of inciting sabotage and havoc, the "genteel professors" of SCPJ are, in Mr. Sayre's words, doing a bit of "behind-covering."

Mr. Sayre said in his article that he is a graduate student at Stanford, and that he currently attempts to avoid politics. According to Megan Knize, the Editor-in-Chief of the Stanford Daily, the Daily did not initially run the advertisement upon receiving it out of concern over the factual accuracy of the allegations within the advertisement and the usage of a pseudonym for the author.

The Daily also had concerns over certain web site links cited in the online version of the article that did not work, although an investigation revealed that all of the links seemed to function correctly except for the link pointing to the Stanford Coalition for Peace and Justice. The Daily said that no revisions of the advertisement were received from the CSPC after the initial rejection.

After the passage of a month in negotiation, according to CSPC representative Elizabeth Ruiz, the CSPC concluded that the article would no longer be current and thus chose not to resubmit the article to the Daily.

The advertisement was run successfully in other newspapers such as the Harvard Crimson.

David Horowitz and the Stanford Daily have had a controversial record of advertisements about history. In 2001, Mr. Horowitz attempted to have an advertisement entitled "Ten Reasons Why Reparations for Slavery is a Bad Idea -- and Racist Too" published in a variety of student newspapers across college campuses in order to promote one of his books; the advertisement provoked aggressive responses from student organizations, and at Stanford the article was printed as a guest editorial instead of a paid advertisement.

The Stanford Daily has a record of virtually never refusing advertisements, including one advertisement from a Holocaust-denial group that offered a reward for a person who could set up a debate between itself and the Jewish Anti-Defamation League over whether or not the Holocaust actually occurred.
