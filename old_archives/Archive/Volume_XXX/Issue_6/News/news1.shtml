News
The News in Brief
Earth Day
Stanford students celebrated the 33rd Earth Day last week. Activities included a cell-phone collection booth in which community members could rid themselves of their old and used phones. The phones will be reused or donated to South Bay Cystic Fibrosis Foundation. Activists also hosted a teach-in on the threat to the environment posed by the mercury and other chemicals that are released by dismantled computers, followed by an organized letter writing campaign to Dell Computer.

Issues on the agenda also included limiting development in the Foothills. The Stanford Open Space Alliance was asking students to sign postcards to discourage future development in the Foothills.

Recycling was set up for a wide range of items including bottles, cans, plastic bags, scrap metal, and electronic scrap. Among the iterms being recycled were athletic shoes. The material from the recycled shoes will be used to build new athletic surfaces.

Living Wage Guidelines
Last Thursday, the University administration published its latest living-wage guidelines for workers employed by contractors. The new rules will force contracted companies to pay non-unionized workers a wage of at least $11.35 an hour, or $10.10 with a health plan. The University will review the wages annually, and a new rate will be determined by the President.

The new policy has come under fire from the Stanford Labor Action Coalition, a student group, and SEIU Local 715, a union that represents 1400 workers directly employed by the University. Both groups criticized the administration for ignoring student and worker input.

Community Day
Stanford hosted its second annual Community Day on Sunday, April 6th. Community Day is an initiative designed to develop a closer relationship between the university and members of the neighboring communities. All events, which included arts, music, athletic, cultural, educational, historical, and scientific events in addition to a health fair and a carnival, were free and open to the public.

Founders' Day was held concurrently with Community Day. Founders' Day honors the lives of Leland and Jane Stanford, and their Leland Stanford, Jr. The celebration included the opening of the Mausoleum for visitation throughout the day and the display of items recently recovered from excavations of the Stanford mansion. The 100th anniversary of the dedication of Memorial Church was also celebrated.

In an apparent community protest, the Student Labor Action Committee (SLAC) organized a march through Community Day events with the two labor unions on campus. The purpose of the march was to promote universal healthcare, the institution of a Code of Conduct, and an end to the purported exploitation of temporary workers.

Holocaust Remembrance Week
The Holocaust was remembered through various events held at Stanford from April 28th to May 4th. The week began with a commemorative service that included a talk by a survivor from Auschwitz. The next event was a discussion of anti-Semitism in the present day world with Professor Deborah Lipstadt from Emory University, who discussed the libel trial she won against David Irving, who had sued her for calling him a Holocaust denier.

On April 30th, members of various student groups met in White Plaza as part of a "Vigil for Tolerance" with the purpose of remembering past intolerance in the hope of promoting greater unity in the future. Additionally, the week featured a talk with Stuart E. Eizenstat, Deputy Secretary of the Treasury under President Clinton and former Ambassador to the European Union, concerning his book Imperfect Justice: Looted Assets, Slave Labor, and the Unfinished Business of World War II. The week concluded with the showing of The Pianist at the FLiCKS Sunday Film Series.

Holocaust Remembrance Week was sponsored by the Jewish Leadership Council and co-sponsored by the Jewish Law Students Association; the Vice Provost for Student Affairs and the Dean of Students, on behalf of the President and Provost; the History Department; the Hoover Institution; the German Studies Department; the B'nai B'rith Lecture Bureau; the ASSU Speakers Bureau; the Taube Center for Jewish Studies; an Irving and Sarah Pitt Campus Creativity Grant; and Hillel Darmstaedter Holocaust Education Program Grant.
