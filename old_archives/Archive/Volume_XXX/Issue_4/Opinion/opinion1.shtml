Iraq: Quagmire or Stunning Success?
by William E. Hudson
Opinions Editor

It's an odd sort of world when 'quagmire' and 'stunning success' describe the same war, which is today just two weeks old and over two hundred miles long. Neither is really an entirely correct description for the war. Coalition forces have traversed over two hundred miles, crushed elite Republican Guard units, and liberated thousands of Iraqis, even when the liberators were met with pointed weapons. It has been a conflict that has taken over a hundred American and British heroes from their families, from their friends, and from their country.

If you look at the war in its more global terms, you'd probably agree with assessments that this war is stunning in its successes and breathtaking in its speed. If you look at the war from the gritty coverage constantly being shown on the world's television screens, you're apt to think that this conflict has been going on forever and will never end, what with the sandstorms and constant deaths.

Some liberal opponents of the war have offered something useful to clarify this muddled mess: we must, they say, remember the individual deaths and violence that are now being realized on a more massive scale than ever before in Iraq's relatively short history. Many of these people, much as people everywhere in the world, desire peace and stability more than they respect abstract concepts like political freedom and independent media. Not surprisingly, many of these people are no friends of Saddam Hussein, but they might prefer the relative stability extant under Saddam's regime to the coming tensions being awoken by coalition liberation. Liberation is, in Western eyes, a good thing for the Iraqi people. Without basic political freedoms with a life free from dictatorial tyranny, Arab countries in the Middle East will continue to be economically deficient, socially repressive, and politically ostracized. The Arab world is seen as an 'other' precisely because it possesses these three traits.

When radical Arabs rather sensibly reject this marginalization and compartmentalization of their rich culture, terrorism becomes infused with religion and a sense of cultural desperation. Basic political freedoms can help to lessen this incendiary mixture and reduce the security risks for the Western and Arab worlds while encouraging greater understanding between them. So liberation is a positive thing in the long run for the people of Iraq.

The problem is that, at least for the short term, removing Saddam Hussein unravels a regime that was such a part of daily life and reality for every Iraqi, that what comes after Saddam is a bit of a mystery. The White House has given tentative statements, and we are told there are more detailed plans being developed as I write this. The problem is that no plan is going to be able to remove entirely the painful legacy of Saddam Hussein while promoting a return to 'normal life,' because normal life is by now also bound up in Saddam's regime. This is why the Pentagon continues to speak of 'winning the hearts and minds' of Iraqis. This is also why such a project cannot ever fully succeed in the short term, which is precisely when it is needed the most.

Iraqis realize this on some level and are beginning to see that, in areas where coalition forces have already liberated local populations, there are food shortages and civil disorder. Coalition forces are dealing with issues as they are raised, but the very fact that they are having to step in as a sort of temporary government for a 'liberated' Iraq makes the state of freedom questionable in the hearts of Iraqis, who may hate Saddam but love Iraq. America in particular is governing much of Iraq now; this will change, and power will be shifted to the Iraqi people, but the shift is a long and complicated process. Add preexisting Turkish designs on Iraqi territory and the political volatility of Iran, and you have a rather volatile situation indeed.

This is beginning to sound like a sort of 'quagmire,' but we're not talking about the war here, which is an important point to keep in mind. Postbellum Iraq will be a problem that will haunt American policymakers for some time, but this 'quagmire' can be a positive thing for the future of Iraq and its people if it is addressed methodically and with an almost painful reliance on slow progression towards the end-game of a free, independent Iraq. This essential point necessitates that postbellum Iraq be administered by the United Nations. That organization's commitment to complicated, drawn-out processes has already been demonstrated in the march to war, which necessitated quick action and was therefore outside the comfort zone of the United Nations. Rebuilding countries is something with which it is more familiar and for which it is far better suited.

Coalition forces led by the United States have made impressive progress in returning the business of Iraq to the United Nations by liberating a country in dire need of sensibly methodical leadership and organization. The faster the coalition liberates Iraq and removes Saddam, the sooner the United Nations can assert itself by doing something useful for the Iraqi people.

The United Nations, either out of self-interest or remarkable alacrity, realizes this and has devoted itself recently mostly to drawing attention to the suffering of the Iraqi people as a part of coalition bombing and traditional warfare. This is very important, but at a level higher than that adopted by the United Nations.

The war has claimed the lives of hundreds of Iraqis and about a hundred coalition soldiers. Saddam's methods of torture have increased in a desperate attempt to forestall the coalition advance; these methods will fail their intended goal, but they will still kill innocent Iraqi civilians as they in turn kill coalition forces with families and loved ones. Among all this death -- even as justified as it is -- it is difficult to describe the war in terms as rosy as its ardent supporters sometimes claim. There is no question that deaths have been minimized to an incredible regard by the impressive technology of the coalition and the passionate desire by the same forces not to kill innocent Iraqis.

In the final analysis, this war is neither a 'quagmire' nor a 'stunning success.' For the operation to be truly successful, Iraq needs to be rebuilt slowly but surely. Coalition forces are doing what they do best: moving with laudable impetuousness toward a swift victory. After coalition forces spill their blood to liberate Iraq with haste, Iraq will need a sort of postbellum quagmire to lead it to a fully realized freedom. Here the United Nations -- including even the French -- can do what they do best.
