Stanford and SARS
by Ryan J. Wisnesky
News Staff

With at least nine cases of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) confirmed in Santa Clara County, officials and students are spreading information about SARS and taking steps to prevent the spread of SARS to Stanford's campus.

SARS is a new and relatively enigmatic disease originating in Southeast Asia. Over 2500 people world wide are affected, with 100 confirmed deaths.

According to the CDC, SARS usually begins with a fever. The fever is sometimes associated with chills or other symptoms, including headache, general feeling of discomfort and body aches. Some people also experience mild respiratory symptoms at the outset. After two to seven days, SARS patients may develop a dry, nonproductive cough that might be accompanied by or progress to the point where insufficient oxygen is getting to the blood. In 10% to 20% of cases, patients will require mechanical ventilation.

SARS patients are treated the same as a patient with "serious community-acquired atypical pneumonia of unknown cause." Some patients may be given antibiotics, steroids, or anti-viral medications, but to date the effectiveness of these treatments is unknown. The cause of the disease is also unknown, although it is hypothesized that it is caused by a previously unknown coronavirus.

SARS is spread primarily via droplet transmission like sneezing or coughing, although it is possible that SARS can be spread through the air or through contaminated objects. Although Stanford has been fortunate thus far to remain free from the terror of SARS, Stanford University Housing Services recently evicted several students from their housing in campus cottages to make room for potential SARS victims.

Ira Friedman, director of the clinic at Vaden Health Center, "vigorously supports" the evictions, noting that the original purpose of the cottages was short-term housing for students in special circumstances. "One group of students that we have been able to help in the past is students with communicable diseases. In the past, the most frequent concern was chickenpox. . . . With over 11,000 students and their partners/dependents in residence on campus, it is prudent for us to set aside spaces for isolation, if necessary."

Students with SARS symptoms who have traveled to Southeast Asia within ten days of the onset of their symptoms are encouraged to be evaluated at Vaden. Although there is no official test for SARS, there is a battery of related tests that can reveal with high probability a SARS case.

Should an evaluation reveal the possibility of SARS, the student will be referred to the Stanford Medical Center Emergency Department. "There, he or she will get definitive treatment in a setting which minimizes the risk of transmission to other students and patients," according to Ms. Friedman.

Currently, the only risk factor for SARS is travel to certain areas of Southeast Asia or close contact with someone with SARS. Close contact is defined as "having cared for, having lived with, or having had direct contact with respiratory secretions and / or body fluids of a patient with SARS," according to Friedman. For this reason it is important to isolate SARS patients as quickly as possible.

ASSU Senator Chris Lin, wondered if the University had more general plans for potential problems: "All we can really do is hope SARS doesn't show up here, and if it does, that Stanford has a well-thought out plan that can be quickly and properly put in place. In that sense, our response to SARS is much like our response to terrorism."
