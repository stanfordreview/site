Awareness, Not Quarantine, Key to SARS on Campus
by Kathy Hart
Opinions Staff

A handful of Stanford students have found themselves suddenly in need of new housing because of the University's preparations for a potential outbreak of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) on campus. The newly evicted residents of the Murray and Yost Cottages find themselves in the unenviable position of juggling the typical school-year mix of classes, homework and activities while moving to their new housing -- wherever that ends up being.

One has to question the manner in which the University has executed its plan to use the cottages as a quarantine area. They've been secretive, hurried, and unaccommodating to the students concerned, initially asking that some move out before they could be offered alternate housing. Any such forced transition for students must inevitably provoke discomfort, but, given the way the University has approached the situation, this transition has been particularly difficult for those involved. Independently of the methods employed in the evictions themselves, however, there exist more important questions pertaining to the efficacy of using the cottages as a quarantine zone for SARS.

Consider for a moment the symptoms of SARS. Initially, there is a fever that ranges from slight to extreme. In some cases, body aches, headaches, chills, and mild respiratory symptoms such as wheezing and coughing accompany the onset of this illness. The initial condition, which looks a lot like a bad cold or flu, can persist for two to seven days.

Yet, as much as these initial symptoms might seem like little cause for concern, the infected individual can infect others who come into contact with his bodily fluids during this time. This makes suspect many of the activities of normal college students -- kissing, drinking from the same water bottle, sharing a fork, or just living in close proximity with each other.

By the time that symptoms unique to SARS--persistent nonproductive cough and a difficulty in breathing--arise, an infected person may have spread the virus to anyone with whom they've come into close contact for as much as a week. It's a relatively easy disease to spread, and it's made a lot worse by the fact we live on a college campus -- a hotbed of fast-sweeping illnesses and colds. How many Stanford students will go to Vaden if they just think they have a common cold or mild flu? I may be underestimating the diligence of Stanford students, but somehow I doubt that the number would be significant.

So the question--if someone gets SARS, who should be quarantined? The patient herself will obviously be in the hospital, so that's not really a question. What about her roommate? Probably. What about her hallmates, those who share a bathroom with her? Maybe. Her friends who don't live in her dorm, but still spend time with her and don't mind sharing drinks? Well ... The other students in her philosophy seminar, because she still went to class when she thought it was just a cold? Where one should draw the line -- somewhere short of quarantining the entire Bay Area, certainly -- is an important question whose answer lies somewhere between practical concern for the health of students and sheer impracticability.

This is not to say that quarantine cannot be used effectively, but one has to question whether or not the Murray/Yost cottages are equal to the task, should there be any serious outbreak on campus. Furthermore, it seems that the key element in preventing the spread of SARS, should it be introduced to our campus, is the one thing that should come most naturally to people who run a university: education. Knowledge of the early symptoms and how the disease is transmitted is vital to containing the spread of SARS. Such information should be given more emphasis than the status of the school's various "preparations" when our administrators and health workers are publicly addressing concerns about SARS. While the Murray and Yost cottages could prove to be instrumental in controlling a campus SARS outbreak, should it occur, they won't be nearly as effective a quarantine measure as increased student awareness and education about the disease itself.
