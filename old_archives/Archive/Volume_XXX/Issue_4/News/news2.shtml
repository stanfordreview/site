The News in Brief
Student Global AIDS Walk
The Student Global AIDS Walk: A Call to Action was held in ten different US cities on Saturday, April 12. Students from Stanford, Cal, UCSF, and other colleges and high schools in the Bay Area participated in the event, which took place in Golden Gate Park in San Francisco.

Goals of the event included raising awareness of the existence of effective treatment for AIDS and the problem of medicine access. The organization believes that the initiative presented by President Bush to address AIDS is a step in the right direction, but that more needs to be done. The fundraising component raised over $30,000. All of the funds will go to the Elizabeth Glaser Pediatric AIDS Foundation, which provides AIDS-related medication to infected individuals in Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

The Student Global AIDS Walk was organized last year by a group of Stanford students, and the events are organized and administered by students on an entirely voluntary basis. Seven different Stanford organizations participated in the event.

Bursting the Bubble: North Korea
On Tuesday, April 8, Bursting the Bubble held its most recent panel, "War with Iraq. . . .What about North Korea." Bursting the Bubble is a series of student moderated panels consisting of faculty discussing current events with keeping students abreast of world developments.

The event was subtitled, "The Security Crisis on the Korean Peninsula: Past / Present / Future and US Relations." The faculty panel consisted of William Perry, Professor of Management Science and Engineering and in the Institute for International Studies (IIS) who is also a Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution, and Gi-Wook Shin, Associate Professor of Sociology and Acting Director of the Asia/Pacific Research Center at the IIS. Dr. Perry was Secretary of Defense during the first Clinton Administration, during which the Agreed Framework was negotiated with North Korea to halt North Korean development of fissile materials, while Dr. Shin is an expert on Korean and East Asian studies.

Dr. Perry believes North Korea to be the greatest nuclear threat in the world today. Not only is there the danger of North Korea distributing nuclear weapons to terrorist organizations and other rogue regimes, but there is also a tremendous difficulty in stopping the proliferation of nuclear arms to other Asian nations after North Korea's nuclear acquisition. Both Dr. Perry and Dr. Shin believe that diplomacy should be able to resolve the crisis. Dr. Shin cited Kim Jong-Il's desire to survive as a reason why diplomacy should succeed with North Korea where it failed with Iraq.

Community Day
Stanford hosted its second annual Community Day on Sunday, April 6. Community Day is an initiative designed to develop a closer relationship between the university and members of the neighboring communities. All events, which included arts, music, athletic, cultural, educational, historical, and scientific events in addition to a health fair and a carnival, were free and open to the public.

Founders' Day was held concurrently with Community Day. Founders' Day honors the lives of Leland and Jane Stanford, and their Leland Stanford, Jr. The celebration included the opening of the Mausoleum for visitation throughout the day and the display of items recently recovered from excavations of the Stanford mansion. The 100th anniversary of the dedication of Memorial Church was also celebrated.

In an apparent community protest, the Student Labor Action Committee (SLAC) organized a march through Community Day events with the two labor unions on campus. The purpose of the march was to promote universal healthcare, the institution of a Code of Conduct, and an end to the purported exploitation of temporary workers.

Islam Awareness Week
The Muslim Students' Awareness Network (MSAN) presented Islam Awareness Week from April 7-12. MSAN is a student group that promotes understanding among Stanford students of important issues in Muslim countries and advances awareness of Islamic culture. Its members are interested in ending stereotyping of Muslims by informing others. The events were co-sponsored by the Dean of Students, the Department of Religious Studies, and the Office for Religious Life.

Events for Islam Awareness Week included a forum on Iraq with Berkeley Professor Hatem Bazian, and a talk on Iraq from members of Physicians for Social Responsibility. Other presentations included displays of religious art, calligraphy, photo tours, and a spoken-word performance. The week concluded with a Friday prayer.
