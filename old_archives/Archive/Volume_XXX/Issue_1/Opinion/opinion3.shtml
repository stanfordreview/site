Opinion
No Diversity Without Diversity of Thought
by Laura Surma
Opinions Staff

Last week, as I was biking through White Plaza on the way back to my dorm, a small table littered with signs reading "STOP" and something about the Hoover Institution attracted my attention. Interested, of course, in any afternoon excitement or protest--there always seems to be something to protest--I glanced over at the signs, smiled to myself, and prepared to ride on by.

"Stop!" one activist called out to me. "Stop!" he repeated.

"No thanks, I support the Hoover Institution," I replied.

"What?" he asked in disbelief.

"I support the Hoover Institution," I stated more clearly, having already passed by the table.

"What?!" he called out again, just to make sure I wasn't pulling his chain.

I said it yet a third time before I finally cleared the area. "I support the Hoover Institution."

Should it be so shocking that, as a student, I support a research institution that simultaneously brings prestige, amazing resources, and top-rated faculty to my university? For the past month, those protesting the war on Iraq have also set their attack dogs on the Hoover Institution's place on campus. Groups such as the Stanford Coalition for Peace and Justice (SCPJ) protest the Hoover Institution's mission statement, which, in their view, forces the institution to maintain a hiring policy that contradicts the University's guiding principle of academic freedom and objectivity. SCPJ claims to not have any qualms with the personal political affiliations of the Hoover Fellows. Its stated problem is with the Hoover's political stance as an institution supported by the university. (This, of course, ignores the fact that only 15% of Hoover's funding comes from Stanford, and all of that money goes toward non-partisan aspects of the organization such as the archives.)

Ronald Rebholz, an emeritus professor of English who supports the SCPJ's efforts, told the Stanford Daily on January 23, "It is completely inappropriate to have a part of the University with an academic bias."

As the SCPJ asserts, academic freedom and objectivity are indeed important. However, before we oust Hoover in the name of academic bias, let's take a look at another program that would also go against this interpretation of academic freedom and objectivity: the James H. Clark Center for Biomedical Engineering and Sciences.

Back in August of 2001, Clark, the sole donor for the $150M project and the founder of Netscape, Silicon Graphics, Healtheon, and myCFO put a hold on 40% of his donation in protest against President Bush's cutback of government funding of stem-cell research. In an August 31, 2001 New York Times op-ed, Clark explained his objection as follows: "Driven by ignorance, conservative thinking and fear of the unknown, our political leaders have undertaken to make laws that suppress [stem-cell research and cloning]."

In response to the government's threat on stem-cell research, President Hennessy said, "We are hopeful that continued progress in stem-cell research, combined with compelling clinical applications, will lead to an evolution of government policy and eventually to a resumption of the funding of Mr. Clark's pledge to Stanford."

To recap, the debate over the Clark Center has led both the largest donor in Stanford history save Leland Stanford himself and the university president to express overt support for stem-cell research. Now, unless you considers the case on stem-cell research to be closed or not to be a partisan issue, which would make me wonder where you have spent the last five years, you cannot soundly conclude their statements to be politically objective.

And you can bet that the Clark Center, upon completion, will not become a bastion of academic freedom because no stem-cell research center is going to hire conservative thinkers "driven by ignorance" and "fear of the unknown" who oppose its philosophy.

I personally question the morality of stem-cell research, but not once have I considered suggesting that the university sever its ties with the center. Simply stated, if I want to reap the benefits of attending one of the country's leading research universities, I have to accept all forms of research. There's no use in trying to determine which knowledge is good and which is evil. By choosing to attend a prestigious university such as Stanford, a student states his dedication to the pursuit of knowledge--all knowledge.

We are fortunate that the Hoover Institution is not really at risk despite the efforts of SCPJ. The Stanford Daily reported President Hennessy on February 14 as saying that it's unlikely that the university will "change. . . . relations with Hoover." The fundamental issue here, however, deals with diversity of thought.

Because of liberal protests in the 1980's, Stanford sacrificed its opportunity to benefit from the research opportunities that would have accompanied its accommodation of the Ronald Reagan Presidential Library. Opponents feared that it might pollute Stanford's liberal environment.

My challenge to liberals who claim to embrace diversity of race, gender, economic background, and sexual orientation is to make a move to embrace diversity of thought. No organization can be completely impartial. Impartiality inheres only in apathy, but apathy does not make for a truly great university. The advantages must be weighed against the disadvantages, and we must realize that it's vital to allow both sides of the political spectrum to exist on campus.
