Letters to the Editor
Issue 6 of Last Volume Marginalized Minorities and Raped Women
As a regular reader of the Review, I am offended enough by two specific facets of your previous issue to write you this letter. First, I admit that, on a few issues, I lean liberal, but I believe the guide on your back page would qualify me as among the "rational but misguided." I am, however, forced to put aside my usual amusement and silence because of the portrayal of the "Insane Liberal" on the issue of Affirmative action. The poorly written rap lyrics and sad attempts at using African-American Vernacular English combine in an extremely offensive portrayal of the vocal members of the black community. The only way that piece could have been more offensive would have been if it had outright said "black affirmative-action activists are all black, uneducated imbeciles who only use their 'rap-lyrics' to influence America, rather than logical reasoning and solid statistics." There are a great number of proponents of Affirmative Action who are not black, and those who are black can back up their support with reason.

Secondly, I am also quite bothered by the ambivalent smoke signal which was listed for the "Raped Women" in White Plaza. While I appreciate that it was not a "down," I must question why someone would not give this an "up." As the justification of the rating itself said, women and their writings raised awareness about an issue "that's very serious, whether or not it actually exists as a problem on this campus." Regardless whether it is a common occurrence here, college campuses are among the most common locations for rape, especially date-rape. The "raped women" were there to remind everybody that this is a serious issue and that if you are raped it is not your fault. It is a serious issue, and odds are that it has affected or will affect someone you know in your lifetime. The "raped women" helped raise awareness of this extremely important issue, and giving them the ambivalent rating is an insult to the actual women whose stories were told, and it betrays an indifference to making sure rapes continue to be as rare as they currently are. Shame on you.

Mark E. Berghausen
Undeclared '06

Issue 6 of Last Volume Was Racist
If you walk through the halls of my dorm, you will see signs that read "No Stanford Review." Since this is my first year on campus, I didn't understand the big problem with the Stanford Review and simply attributed it to differences in political affiliations. However, the last issue published cleared things up for me. I never imagined that I would pick up a newspaper on this campus that made such racist and ignorant comments as those published in the last issue, most of which were found in Joe Lonsdale's writing. If he holds those beliefs about minority students here at Stanford University, I pity him for his ignorance. If he was trying to be controversial because he thought it would be entertaining to upset people, he needs to find something better to do with his time.

What I am most disgusted by, is the Stanford Review's claim to be the "conservative voice." Most of the last issue voiced racism, intolerance, and ignorance. I would assume that as the "conservative voice," a goal of the Stanford Review is to increase awareness and support for conservative views. Publishing things like Joe Lonsdale's letter does everything except for that.

Rocio Cárdenas
Undeclared '06

Response From Editor-in-Chief Emeritus Joe Lonsdale:
I noted in my article that because of a policy that has admittedly lower standards for demonstrated mathematical ability for people who are not white or Asian, clearly, the average person who is not white or Asian at Stanford is, on average, not as good at math. This is a logical conclusion...and Stanford would never do any public surveys of this sort based on race because they are scared of the potential impact on their admissions policies. Famous studies done in the past by the Review, however, have clearly shown this to be the case.

The signs you see in your dorms are from people who hate hearing about ugly truths that result from misguided liberal policies.

Several other things I said followed from this logic. There is a huge bell curve of people at Stanford, most of them amazing regardless of race. All I was saying was that the averages were thrown off in a way that is bad because of this racist policy of treating people differently based on the color of their skin. And I was throwing this in people's faces in an "offensive rant" section that afterwards I thought was a little tongue-in-cheek rather than racist. I abhor racism, and was showing how it created horrible inequities. The offensiveness you felt from the article, which was intended to hit some nerves that felt like "racism," was all derived from the problem of Stanford policy, not from any racism on my part.

If it were up to me, race would not be considered in the application, but other factors, like socioeconomic status, would be taken into account in extreme cases.

This may not be the case for you, but many people, when they see a minority not doing as well here, instinctively wonder about affirmative action. It's ridiculous that this sort of racial divide even has to exist, and it's hurtful to all races.

I seriously do not believe that my writing was racist.

I apologize for offending you in a manner that you believed to be racist. If you still believe as much, I would greatly appreciate it if you'd please explain to me why what I said, in this context, is racist. Sorry to overuse that word. But it's important to me to know if you still believe as much, considering this explanation. Thank you.
