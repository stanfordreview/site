"Regret to Inform" that Iraq and Vietnam Are Not the Same Conflict
by Harrison Osaki
News Staff

On Thursday, February 27, Narquis Barak, a visiting lecturer from Harvard University, and several graduate students in Stanford's Communications Department showed the film "Regret to Inform." The film is part of a series entitled the "Peace-Oriented Film" series. Sponsored by Stanford's CASA Program and Communications Department, the series is part of a concerted effort by on-campus liberal academics to encourage student opposition to potential conflict in Iraq.

"Regret to Inform is, according to its website at http://www.regretoinform.org, a documentary that covers a "journey in search of the truth about [the Vietnam war and its legacy". Ms. Barak, who has spent extensive time in Vietnam researching the Vietnam War and its effects on North Vietnamese civilians and soldiers, raised questions about the accuracy of the film and its portrayal of Vietnamese women.

The film "Regret to Inform" was made by a woman named Barbara Sonneborn and takes its title from the opening words of a telegram that she received informing her that her husband had been killed in action in Vietnam. The film's narrative charts her experience in visiting Vietnam twenty years after his death, interlacing edited pieces of interviews with other American widows and Vietnamese women about their experiences with the war. The motivation behind the film was Mrs. Sonneborn's desire to find "women who had experienced the same loss on both sides of the war, and to understand what could be learned through their stories."

The film includes numerous interviews. These usually alternate between American and Vietnamese women, both of whom talked about the effect the war had on their lives. In the case of the American women this typically involved waiting for their husbands to return from the war; most of the Vietnamese women had a more direct experience with the Vietnam War and recounted the effects of American bombing on their lives. Interspersed with the interviews and the narrative of Mrs. Sonneborn's journey through Vietnam were pieces of stock archival footage including American bombing raids and the damaged Vietnamese countryside. The movie culminated in a sequence of American and Vietnamese women recalling the moments when they had learned of their husbands' deaths.

Ms. Barak's criticism of the movie centered around what she felt were several inaccuracies and misrepresentations of the Vietnamese experience. Despite the film's attempts to draw similarities between the Vietnamese and American widows' experiences through structural construction and interviews, said Ms. Barak, the Vietnamese and American experiences of the war were not the same.

For example, the film interviews several American women and their indirect experience with Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) through their husbands after they returned from the war. Ms. Barak's own research conducted through lengthy interviews with North Vietnamese civilians indicated that psychological damage due to the war was in fact much lower than in the American experience. She further criticized the film's portrayal of Vietnamese women in that it ignored the large number of Vietnamese women who fought in the war, arguing also that the film minimized the amount of real suffering that the Vietnamese women experienced overall.

Barak also criticized emotional manipulation that she felt was present in the film, stating that despite the fact that the film begins with the idea that "One day, [Sonneborn] woke up and decided to go to Vietnam" -- twenty years and a marriage after her first husband's death -- the spontaneity was obviously hyperbolic, since she had had to bring a camera crew.

Audience questions to Ms. Barak raised several other issues, which she discussed further. One of the graduate students present commented that the movie was emblematic of a changing attitude towards documentaries, and that they ought to be about storytelling with an obvious bias instead of attempting to recount objective history.

Ms. Barak also said that many of the Vietnamese women, since they were so closely associated with the Vietcong, were trained in propaganda techniques by the government. She said that at least one young woman who told a story about being forced into prostitution was acting as a spy for the Vietcong.

"Regret to Inform" won Best Director, Best Choreographer and Feature Documentary awards in the Sundance Film Festival in 1999 and was nominated for an Academy Award in 1998.

The film signified the conclusion of the Peace Film Series, whose attempt to suffuse Stanford students with war imagery has added to on-campus discussions over the looming war in Iraq.
