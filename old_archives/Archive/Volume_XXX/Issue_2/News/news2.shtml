Stanford College Republicans Go to Statewide Convention
by Travis Menk and Bob Sensenbrenner
News Staff

A group of about ten Stanford College Republicans headed to Sacramento for the California Republican Convention the weekend of February 21-23, attending the first convention since the group's reestablishment on campus at the beginning of this year. The event provided a unique opportunity to see politics first-hand, a chance to rub elbows with state officials, local candidates, and other notables in the Republican party such as Oakland NAACP chairman Shannon Reeves, and the occasion to meet other fellow College Republicans struggling amidst the bastions of liberality constituted by California universities.

The convention was held at the Hyatt across the street from the state capitol over the course of three days. Numerous Republican and conservative groups set up booths for convention attendees to pass by, peruse the literature, and join these various organizations. The halls of the hotels were littered with campaign banners, events, and announcements of hospitality rooms each night throughout the hotel.

The main and highly anticipated event of this convention, however, was the tight race for chairman. The race pitted longtime Republican Party activist and recently retired aerospace executive Bill Back against Palo Alto attorney Duf Sundheim. The race between the two men was easily one of the most heated chair races ever in the state of California, and their debate was the hot ticket of the convention.

Mr. Sundheim, a Stanford alumnus, defeated Mr. Back by a margin of 666 to 489. Former Stanford College Republicans President Keith Proctor commented, "Duf's victory is encouraging because it means that a Party leadership that has so long operated like a private club, and that has done so much to harm Republican interests in California, may finally be opening up. Duf is an outsider, and we've needed that for a long time."

In recent years, the California Republican Party has been torn apart by bitter division between moderate and conservative Republicans. Mr. Sundheim's supporters rallied around the sentiments that, with Sundheim as the head of the California Republican Party, Republicans might finally unify in order to challenge two-term Democrat Barbara Boxer for her Senate seat in 2004 and sway California back into Republican arms. To the Stanford College Republicans, many of them new to the convention scene and some not even natives of California, the depth of division between conservative and moderate Republicans was shocking. Joe Fairbanks, co-president of the Stanford College Republicans, said, "The fragmentation and infighting here in California within the Republican party is unimaginable. I had heard stories of such divisions but did not realize the true extent of it until this weekend. This is the real reason Republicans have been unable to win California for a long time." In the same light, Nathan Mintz, another Stanford student, said, "On a whole the convention was an eye-opening experience."

In an effort to change things for the better among California College Republicans, Robert "Bobby" Elam, an active member of Stanford College Republicans decided during the convention to run for the chair of the organization. Mr. Elam is a native Californian with family roots in Modesto, just east of Stanford. He is currently a Stanford Law student and received his undergraduate degrees in history and government from Georgetown University. He worked on the Presidential Campaigns of every Republican nominee since 1988 and for every California Senate Republican Nominee since 1992. He also served as a Senate Fellow in Sacramento on the Judiciary Committee. In the past year, he wrote his thesis defending states' rights as a Fulbright Scholar in the United Kingdom.

Mr. Elam decided to run a grass-roots campaign with disgruntled Republican followers tired of the status quo state board. Typically, the California College Republican board has been dominated by only a few colleges who annually pass off the leadership positions amongst each other. Mr. Elam will be running against Michael Davidson from UC-Berkeley who is a Texas native and the incumbent secretary on the California College Republican board.

"Mr. Elam, in many ways is an outsider just like Duf," said Proctor. Likewise, his run for the CCR chairmanship might save us from a status quo leadership who haven't noticed - or worse, don't care - that the status quo for Republicans in California is pretty distressing. It's quite remarkable. Our present leadership is so afraid of change, so devoted to preserving the past, that the CCR has become more of a historical society than a political organization. We don't need museum caretakers, we need energetic, forward-thinking leaders at the top. Without a doubt in my mind, Bobby is one of the few who can turn this thing around."

The Stanford College Republicans returned to campus ready to engage the action from their home base.
