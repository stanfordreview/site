Front Page
Hoover Sponsors Exhibit Celebrating Peace
by Ryan J. Wisnesky
News Staff

Students passing by the second floor of Tresidder Union this last week have perhaps found themselves wandering through an art exhibit entitled "The Architects of Peace." This display, which features 35 photo-based informative panels about the world's most notable peacemakers was organized as a collaborative effort between the community service-oriented Haas Center and the Hoover Institution.

The 38 displays feature biographical information on a wealth of individuals ranging from Jimmy Carter to Colin Powell and Mother Teresa. The individuals constitute a cross-section of 75 peacemakers that artist Michael Collopy has been photographing for a new book.

The biographical information for each of the peacemakers is brief but enlightening. For instance, Jimmy Carter's exhibit reads, "Jimmy Carter served as the 39th president of the US from 1977 to 1981. His administration's accomplishments include the peace treaty between Egypt and Israel and the SALT II treaty with the USSR [. . . .]

"President Carter also teaches Sunday School and is a deacon at the Marantha Baptist Church. He builds homes one week each year for Habitat for Humanity and is the author of over fourteen books."

Colin Powell's inclusion among the peacemakers has created a bit of a controversy. Mr. Powell's exhibit reads in part, "Powell retired from the army in 1993. As chairman of the America's Promise -- The Alliance for Youth, he promoted volunteerism. He became Secretary of State on January 1, 2001." There is no mention of his accomplishments after that date, however.

On the student board, a large cloth-covered board where students and passersby were encouraged to share their thoughts about the exhibit; one person wrote, "I am amiss at the inclusion of General Colin Powell with this exhibit. It is an assault to the legacy of true lovers of peace to have a key member of the nefarious Bush administration along with people who dedicated their entire lives to nonviolent [. . . .] No Blood for Oil".

Several people responded to that comment, including one person who wrote, "Powell's done more for the cause of religious freedom and women's rights in the last two years than some of the others have done in their whole lives. Nice to know that not everyone who speaks of Ôpeace' is consumed with bitterness, hate, and hypocrisy. Nice Exhibit".

When asked about the inclusion of Colin Powell in the exhibit, Michael Collopy was quite verbose. "At the time I included him because of his involvement with America's Promise." America's Promise is an organization whose mission is "to mobilize people from every sector of American life to build the character and competence of our nation's youth. . . ." Colin Powell was the founding chairman of the organization. But Collopy also stated that he would have included Colin Powell even given Powell's work in the Bush Administration. "I'm trying to include all aspects of society," he said. "I want to bring in the conservatives as much as the liberals."

Collopy himself tends to be a pacifist. However, he noted that it is important for people to challenge personal dogma and that, while creating his book and the exhibit, he tried to challenge his own preconceived notions about how peace is achieved.

Collopy noted that Powell "provides balance in the administration" and that he hoped Powell's inclusion, along with the inclusion of other less well known figures, would "bring together a diversity of individuals with a diversity of opinion." He said that, in order to achieve a true dialog ue of diversity, all opinions should be brought to the table.

This exhibit is one of the things Hoover does of which most students approve. In addition to the displays, the exhibit also has a portion devoted to children's art. There are also sections from the Martin Luther King, Jr. Papers Project. Les DeWitt from the Fund for Peace Initiatives was one of the chief organizers and fundraisers for the project. When asked his thoughts on the exhibit, he noted that "it's an educational project" and that the Martin Luther King Papers, the Haas Center, and the Hoover institution have done something "remarkable."

"The exhibit is outstanding," he mused. Most students think so, too.
