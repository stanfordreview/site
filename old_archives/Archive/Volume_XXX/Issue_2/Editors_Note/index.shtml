Editor's Note
Can Anyone Explain Affirmative Action?
Piotr H. Kosicki
Editor-in-Chief
The Review wishes to thank President Hennessy for his candid, open responses. We hope that this interview bodes well for a working relationship between the Review and the administration that will allow us to begin fixing some of the issues troubling Stanford.

The nexus of many of these issues is "diversity." When Stanford's president talks about diversity, what does he mean? Is it a simple question of demographics, of forcing Stanford's student and teaching bodies to conform artificially to socioeconomic, racial, and religious divisions present in the United States? Is it perhaps more a question of forcing new perspectives into academic coursework that displace classical scholarship?

In last week's editor's note, I defined diversity as an appreciation of different backgrounds that unifies us and allows us to identify common goals. Institutional diversity of the sort perpetuated by traditional affirmative action policies isn't totally inconsistent with a move toward such common goals, but it makes common understanding much harder to achieve.

Instead of thinking about unifying those already at Stanford, we worry about superimposing artificial requirements on admissions and hiring with the goal of increasing our representativeness of society as a whole. Naturally, I'm not arguing that we totally ignore demographic representativeness--to do so would be narrow-minded. Demographics cannot, however, be at the forefront of our worries. President Hennessy conceded, "If something other than academic capability becomes the first criterion we look at then we're clearly sacrificing too much to achieve that diversity." In the status quo, however, academic capability is diluted as a criterion for selection by demographic statistics.

Even if demographics are the second or third criterion rather than the first, they change the way admissions officers define and treat "academic capability." What guarantees do we have, then, that the integrity of academic merit will be preserved?

Upcoming issues of the Review will deal more extensively with the intellectual diversity envisioned by leftist norms. Much of the discussion may sound quite familiar to readers in the Review's alumni base. After all, Review co-founder Peter Thiel and ex-EIC David Sacks devoted an entire book entitled The Diversity Myth to debunking the fallacies of Stanford curricula based entirely on idealized multiculturalism. I don't mean to pull an old skeleton out of Stanford's closet, but the pending Supreme Court decision on the University of Michigan and President Hennessy's decision to join the amicus brief in support of Michigan have themselves thrust the skeleton out into the open.

The most fundamental problem with Stanford's affirmative action policy is that we still have no idea precisely what it does. The administration throws around vague notions of diversity and representativeness, but ultimately the only standard we have for gauging whether or not Stanford's policy is a failure is the administration's word as to whether or not "we are happy with it."

This is why I chose to open my note with the above lines from President Hennessy's interview. Although our ultimate goal is to impact the way Stanford conducts its admissions policy, our first and foremost goal must be to press the administration into revealing exactly what the current policy is. Reading over President Hennessy's interview with the Review, I see that, on the one hand, the president claims that affirmative-action policies exist only to insure diversity.

Asked, however, how we will know when the desired diversity is achieved, the president responded, "I don't know that there is any simple answer to that question." President Hennessy wants affirmative action to continue--that's why he signed onto the amicus brief. At the same time, however, he says that he doesn't think Stanford's academic body needs to be more diverse than it is.

Perhaps I'm missing something, but I see some glaring contradictions in these statements. If even Hennessy admits that Stanford has achieved its desired level of diversity, why does affirmative action need to continue? More fundamentally, however, if he can't define exactly what the diversity is that affirmative action is supposed to have brought, what insight gives him the ability to judge whether or not we've achieved that diversity?

The most dangerous point--and, indeed, the most profound indictment of affirmative action overall--is the reverse discrimination it engenders in allowing admissions officers to engage their own biases while reengineering Stanford's demographic composition. President Hennessy himself admits that the problem of reverse discrimination is "what makes affirmative action challenging." Asked, however, whether such discrimination was indeed taking place, he could only answer choppily, "Probably not but in some sense in any. . . .I don't know that there's a group being heavily discriminated against."

For any listener, conservative or liberal, these responses taken together should send up a red warning flag. Before we even begin to examine the philosophical underpinnings of where Stanford is going with its leftist notions of anti-normative diversity, we need to confront seriously and honestly what the University is doing now.

If even the University President can't present us with a consistent set of responses, the work before us becomes exceedingly clear.


Piotr H. Kosicki
Editor-in-Chief
