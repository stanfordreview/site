Opinion
Viennese Ball Brings Joy of Tradition
by Hillary Connell
Opinions Staff

The princess gowns, ice sculptures, and myriad flowers donning the ballroom of the Hyatt Regency, Burlingame during Stanford's 26th annual Viennese Ball provided a stark contrast to the typical Stanford party. Nonetheless, over four hundred couples crowded the dance floor on Friday, February 21, continuing a tradition that has grown more than threefold since the first ball was held in 1978. In winter of that year, students returning from the Stanford-in-Vienna program attempted to emulate the frequent and popular balls held throughout Austria during Fasching, the festive period between New Year's Eve and Ash Wednesday. They staged the ball in the Toyon Hall lounge and attracted roughly three hundred students. In 1998, the ball was moved to an off-campus location to accommodate the burgeoning number of attendees and since then has taken place at several different Bay Area hotels. Scores of students look forward to the event as the highlight of winter quarter, if not the entire academic year.

Viennese Ball is open to Stanford alumni and faculty at $95 a couple, though most ball-goers are current students who pay $75 a couple. The tickets provide students admission not only to the ball itself, but also to events that occur throughout "Austria Week," the week leading up to the ball. This year's Austria Week commenced on Valentine's Day with a dance lesson featuring the formal Congress of Vienna waltz. The rest of the week's offerings included more dance lessons, an Austrian tea and dinner, and Heuriger Abend, featuring refreshments, dancing, and a live oompah band. While some students came to the ball well versed in the ways of social dance, many took advantage of the lessons offered beforehand to prepare themselves for the waltzes, polkas, tangos, schottisches, salsa, and swing played at Viennese Ball. A member of this year's Austria Committee estimated that over one hundred students showed up for each lesson.

Though attendees like Vice Provost for Undergraduate Education John Bravman might lead first-time attendees to think otherwise, the ball is student-run. The Viennese Ball Committee is open to all students, freshman through graduate level, and it coordinates everything from the Austria Week events to inviting honored guests to the ball.

Two members of the committee, Opening Committee chairs Qi Lin and Tiffany Lee, choreographed this year's opening waltz, danced to Strauss's "The Beautiful Blue Danube." According to Lee, "We started working on this whole project Spring quarter of last year, by picking songs, looking at videos of old choreographies, and listening to various possible song choices. The dancers were great and worked incredibly hard, with four to four-and-a-half hours of rehearsal every week, [and even] more the week of the ball." However, the hard work and extensive time commitment of the opening waltz's dancers paid off. "I was incredibly pleased with the way the opening ceremonies turned out. Everyone was on that night, and as far as I could tell while dancing, it's one of the best times we've ever danced the two pieces," said Lee.

The Hyatt opened two ballrooms to Viennese Ball attendees, one primarily for swing and the other for more characteristic Austrian dances like the waltz and polka. Two live ensembles, the Brassworks Band and the Swing Solution, provided the music for dancers who never seemed weary despite dancing from 8 p.m. to 2 a.m.

Charles Kou, sophomore and chair of the ball's Programs Committee, expressed many students' feelings about the ball when he said afterward, "I love going to Viennese Ball, from the pre-dance experience where you get to dress up and dine with your friends. . . .to the dancing. I enjoy dancing, and I love to watch other people enjoying themselves."

Though Kou is one of the students who came prepared for the ball by taking Social Dance I and attending other social dance events throughout the year at Stanford, adeptness at dancing is clearly not a prerequisite for enjoying the event. Sara Bei, a sophomore and a member of Stanford's exceptional cross-country team, attended the ball the day after undergoing knee surgery. "Since Viennese Ball was the day after my surgery, I didn't get to enjoy it to the fullest extent as far as dancing," said Bei. "However, even without dancing that much, I was kept entertained by all of the amazing performances, dance competitions, delicious food, and company of fellow classmates. Even with a bum knee, it was definitely my favorite Stanford event that I have attended thus far."

For attendees like Bei, who might want respite from the dance floor, the ball also featured performances by Stanford dance groups throughout the night. Performances this year included members of Decadence, the Academy of Danse Libre, Stanford Swing, the Stanford Ballroom Dance Team, and the Cardinal Whirlwinds. Dancers desiring refreshment needed only go so far as the hotel lobby, where a smorgasbord of delectable pastries, cookies, truffles, cheese, and meat cuts awaited them. Other ball-goers enjoyed taking a break from the night's festivities merely by people-watching; other attendees, sporting fashions from 17th century-style ball gowns to a full Scottish kilt outfit, offered quite a sight.

With this year's ball only a week past, many people are already looking forward to next year's. Senior Ben Sywulka claimed, "The waltzing, the ice sculpture, the live bands, the classiness... it was a nice escapade into the world of pseudo-European eliteness, and a reminder of the beauty of ballroom dancing. . . .I will continue to go in the future if I get a chance." Organization of next year's Viennese Ball committees and selection of the opening waltz dancers will begin during spring quarter.

Students, faculty, and alumni in the area with any interest in dancing, or simply in enjoying an experience celebrated by many colleges or in many other countries, should plan on attending next year's Viennese Ball.
