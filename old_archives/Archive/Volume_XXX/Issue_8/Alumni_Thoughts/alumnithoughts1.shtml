Alumni Thoughts
Confessions of An Academic Libertarian
by Aman Verjee
Editor-in-Chief '94

Commencement is an occasion particularly meet for pleasant reminiscences, for confident assertions that the content of higher education is improving, for assurances that the future of our university is secure. It would seem appropriate to drop lightly into history, to review the problems of the present, and to appeal then to the ideologue's ceaseless yearning for the ideal life by saying something like, "But I believe in the future."

I confess that while I was at Stanford, from 1992 to 1996, I toiled for the cause. I worked in agony and sweat, not for glory and certainly not for profit, because I believed in the inherent fixability of it all.

I felt the duty to work towards restoring academic integrity and rigorous standards of academic excellence at Stanford, towards race-blind fairness in admissions, and towards a curriculum that elevated greatness instead of politics. I thought it could be done, that Stanford could grow and adapt, and shake off the remnants of the Cultural Revolution that took place in the 1980s.

But this isn't a commencement address, so I don't have to serve you with tripe, carefully pickled. I can tell you what I really think: that Stanford University, like so many other institutions of higher learning, will be relegated to the dustbin of history regardless of what we do now.

There will be no spectacular triumph of classical liberalism, no pendular correction of its ways, no quiet cultural revolution moving underneath the surface right when the left thought they had triumphed. The humanities departments of our top universities -- in stark contrast to the physical sciences departments -- will remain bloated corpses, ideological ruins, providing little that gets taken seriously by the world outside, and subsisting on the dole of government largesse and donations.

The reason is that the humanities themselves -- psychology, sociology, modern history, literature, even economics -- are irreparably broken, because when philosophy disintegrated in the 19th century, it left in its wake a collection of new disciplines bereft of proper epistemological underpinnings. It is epistemology that guides human knowledge, sets its criteria, checks its assumptions. Yet so much of what goes on at our nation's top universities in the field of "humanities" fundamentally misunderstands the true nature of its subject: man (hence the term, "humanities").

Consider that, in psychology, one can learn about the reasons for man's behavior without reference to man as a rational, conscious agent. In sociology, one learns of political and social systems of organization without reference to man. Even in economics, reference is made to a "nation's resources," and much time is spent in determining how to dispose of them efficiently "for the common good," yet little effort is spent questioning the premise of collective ownership of resources, discussing the value of intellect and entrepreneurship or of the value of man beyond his service as a "labor input."

Nowhere at Stanford is capitalism, now the pre-eminent and newly ascendant economic system in most parts of the world, taught as a moral virtue, one respecting the essential qualities of man. Even its apologists have to use the language of the left -- "social surplus maximization," "efficient allocation of resources," and most famously "tax cuts will increase tax revenues" -- to justify their views against coercive taxation, state ownership of resources, and massive over-regulation.

Against this backdrop, the Stanford Review has continued to make a difference, to make the University better off than when we found it. We've exposed the partisan bias of the professors at Stanford to national attention, and we've demonstrated the systematic misalignment of opportunity and success that affirmative action breeds.

In 1994, my colleague Rob Corry and I worked to challenge the campus "speech code" (this is not my own Orwellian term for it, but literally the term used by the University to describe it), which prohibited any words by Stanford students that were intended to "insult or stigmatize an individual or a small group of individuals on the basis of their sex, race, color, handicap, religion, sexual orientation, or national and ethnic origin." Note that the speech code did not merely outlaw fighting words, or even epithets, but also intelligent yet provocative expositions of, say, Catholic dogma or Holocaust revisionism, both of which certainly have places in a marketplace of ideas. Moreover, the University made it clear that members of minority groups could not be found in violation of the speech code if their insults were directed at those in the superordinate majority.

Thanks to Rob's tireless work, a California court struck down the speech code. Today it is but a footnote in the political correctness wars, something to be marveled at by historians one day as a discordant note: an imposition on freedom of speech that would have violated the First Amendment had it passed a state legislature, existing in (what passes for) a marketplace of ideas, where the free and fair exchange of opinions is supposed to be encouraged.

But the basic disconnect between Stanford and the rest of the world persists, and will do so unless and until the University is subjected to some kind of market discipline. The most glaring example of this that I witnessed was in 1992, when I first came to Stanford. Five years after perestroika, professors not only still openly copped to being Marxists, but one of their leaders -- John Manley -- taught the class in political science called "The American Dream."

Witness the cruel irony of it: those least covetous of their liberties, those most hostile to the notion that the individual pre-supposes the state rather than other way around, are now in charge of preserving and communicating the American ideal of the Rights of Man to a generation of the nation's best and brightest.

How committed a commie-sympathizer do you have to be, to keep sympathizing after the commies have committed suicide?

Fortunately for us libertarians, history is on our side. While the corrupt and defeated political ideology of statism continues to find apologists from a tenured claque, we sit at the epicenter of an Information Revolution that is fast making nation-states irrelevant.

Individuals, operating at the edge of a decentralized network, will soon wrest sovereign power from the artifices of centralized control. Ironically, Stanford's political science department will have contributed little to this revolution, which was abetted by forces at the edge of its own campus.

Aman Verjee was editor in chief of the Stanford Review in 1994. He is currently a Vice President in the Strategic Planning group at PayPal, Inc.
