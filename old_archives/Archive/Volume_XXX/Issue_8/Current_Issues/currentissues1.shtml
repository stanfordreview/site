Daily Censorship Hits Popular Column
by Piotr H. Kosicki
Editor-in-Chief '03

This week, the leadership of the Stanford Daily refused to publish a column by two of their most popular columnists. The columnist-romantic duo, consisting of conservative Elizabeth Madjlessi and liberal Alexander Kendall, was selected at the beginning of the volume to write a weekly Monday column. Throughout the volume, Madjlessi and Kendall have written stinging critiques on controversial issues. The duo's past columns include indictments of the ASSU, University academic requirements, fraternity parties, and faculty ideological bias. According to the duo, however, this week's column proved too controversial.

The column, printed to the right of this article, criticizes a wide range of aspects of the Stanford Daily, including publication process, material selection, and staff attitudes. According to Madjlessi and Kendall, however, the column's methodology is "no different from columns we've done in the past."

Indeed, even incoming Daily Editor-in-Chief Ramin Setoodeh expressed support: "I didn't agree with the decision that was made not to run the column."

Upon submitting their article, Madjlessi and Kendall were told that it was riddled with "claims that have no factual support." According to Madjlessi and Kendall, "We indicated a willingness to work with them to fix our column, but they refused."

Instead, the columnists were told to interview multiple editors on the Daily's staff. Consultations recommended included the Managing Editor for the Classifieds Page, the Photography Editor, and the previous volume's Editor-in-Chief. Instructed to avoid "defaming" these editors with their "speculation," the columnists were asked to report the statements of these editors to provide a counterpoint to their arguments.

The columnists, however, maintained that, as the authors of a column intended to express their personal opinions, they should not be forced into the unprecedented position of having to interview editors. Several sources within the Daily who have asked to remain unnamed agreed with the columnists that the request was both unprecedented and unreasonable.

As a consolation, the Daily permitted the columnists to print an entirely different column in the June 4 issue. Nonetheless, feeling that the outgoing Editor-in-Chief of the Daily had impugned their journalistic integrity, the columnists approached the Review with a request that Stanford be given the opportunity to see an uncensored version of their critique.
