Front Page
Skepticism of Habit
by Joe Lonsdale
Editor-in-Chief '02-'03

Our undergraduate years at Stanford contribute greatly to our ultimate identities, as it is here that we morph from adolescent to adult. During our time at The Farm, our minds begin to accustom themselves to certain habits of thought and action. Our algorithmic responses to conflicts, politics, surprising news, stupidity, kindness, failures, and successes become more deeply encoded in the psyche, as the mind swaps its strikingly pliant heaps of SRAM for pre-coded hardware and less flexible storage devices.

My experience with conservative journalism at Stanford has been a great aid in understanding these key processes in my development. In the end, I would even say that the experience has played a huge role in saving me from poorly developed robotic responses to important areas of life.

The first thing any conservative student acquires at a modern university is a powerful sense of skepticism. When the Black Studies program conducts a study on affirmative action, a government public-policy agency analyzes information on racism and sentencing, or an economist completes research on the correlation between taxes on the rich and the lifestyles of America's poor...we can probably tell you the result ahead of time. I have seen fellow students vehemently citing studies such as these in their discussions and debates, and it amazes me that they truly take the studies seriously. This skepticism carries over well into many parts of life: by immediately responding to information by analyzing its source and biases, we gain a stronger grasp on the finer points of negotiation and reality. We shed adolescent naïveté by sadly understanding why the Muslim student group is truly against the war in Iraq.

More profitably, if we look to understand this concept and apply it further, we learn also to be skeptical of our own reactions and beliefs. By surrounding ourselves with conservatives and learning to hit "mute" when utter foolishness gets thrown at us by loud and radical groups, we become too accustomed to disparaging dismissals of outside viewpoints. Wading through excrement is a poor use of talent, and the mind can only reject defunct ideas and phrases so many times. However, habits are easily formed, and we must avoid the overconfidence and single-mindedness this instinctual response may engender. If we refuse to challenge our precepts by seeking sound reason and intelligent alternatives to our specific beliefs, we lose the most valuable benefit of this learned response -- skepticism is most useful when applied to challenge our own thoughts and reactions.

This is true regardless of who you are and what you believe. Analyzing our own beliefs has led many conservative students, including myself, to acquire a more libertarian perception of the world. In addition, I have found that it is dangerous to be absolutely sure of anything -- on a very limited basis, I now support part of a system I otherwise abhor: affirmative action. Understanding the situation of friends from difficult backgrounds has given me respect for certain specific applications of affirmative action, especially to individuals of culturally deficient communities that spurned their academic efforts and achievements. I'd rather work with a like-minded intelligent conservative, but I'd rather debate an intelligent liberal, if you'll pardon the use of that oxymoron in the Review.

Unlike the mainstream PC campus culture of "don't-be-mean, let's-dance-around-trees-and-be-nice-to-everyone-even-if-they're-stupid-and-weird," conservatives can be an unforgiving group. If you do something stupid, you should be criticized for it and correct yourself. If you don't listen and you do it again anyway, you're probably a jerk or an idiot. True, perhaps, but there comes a point where we need to recognize that both responses -- oblivious acceptance and harshly dismissive criticism -- become more trouble than they're worth.

Of all my habits, insensitive idea-bashing and anger-spurring criticism is the hardest to break. There is unquestionably a problem with the blind acceptance of too many foolish ideas, activities, and projects on this campus. Students who have never been told "that opinion is wrong" their entire lives are undeterred in whatever crazy schemes they righteously hatch and hoist on their banners. There is wisdom in both acceptance and criticism.

While it is satisfying to demolish a person or group for their tethered spin about a cosmically warped reality, it usually doesn't accomplish much. "But it's fun," I would protest. However, if I am to be concerned with the reality in which I'm working, the instinct to reply harshly and destroy any hope for dialogue needs to be repressed.

A harsh and unforgiving knowledge of what is wrong is extremely important, but the ability to communicate amicably despite this is at times even more valuable. In person-to-person contact, I've been impressed by the honor and kindness with which conservatives students conduct themselves, in contrast to the hate and violence often focused on us from students on the other side.

Conservatives here have often been raised as honorable gentlemen and ladies and do not tend themselves to cruelty in conversation with liberals, but too often we allow ourselves a dismissive tone in print that contributes an unhelpful negativity to the community. Oftentimes, people only need to be told why a portion of their philosophy may be mistaken in a manner that doesn't assault their good sense or dignity, and allows them an honorable method of fixing the problem. I'm still at a loss as to how to communicate with the makers of the big red "Poverty is not a Stanford value!" sign, but this philosophy has proven greatly useful in discussions with moderate liberals.

Applying skepticism to our habits of thought and action is one of the most difficult challenges students face at Stanford. From our midterms, many of us have learned resilience and confidence in response to failure, and hard work instead of overconfidence in response to success. The most important habit a conservative at Stanford can build is metacognition.

Instead of responding with anger or hate when you encounter a harmful point of view, analyze your intended response, and decide whether it's worth getting emotionally or conversationally involved and what impact your response and involvement will have on the participants and the issue. By questioning the habits that comprise our character, we become more mature adults, and the challenges we face as campus conservatives play a key role in our transformation.

Joe Lonsdale is a junior majoring in Computer Science.
