Front Page
Breaking the Inertia
by Brooke Brody-Waite
Editor-in-Chief '99-'00

Stanford is a surprisingly easy place to become conservative -- or at least more conservative.

My friends who liked to think of it as a great struggle might not be pleased with me for saying so, but I had a lot of fun disagreeing with the campus political consensus. It's just too tempting to slip into the happy-go-lucky Stanford culture. But then, you either have to be liberal or apathetic, and it's so much more gratifying to surprise people. And, trust me, when you're a female Californian under age 20 with a pet mouse named Margaret Thatcher, you turn a few heads.

Being a conservative at Stanford was frustrating because there was not enough political dialogue for fellow students to have any background in your stance on issues. Political correctness ran so deep that you couldn't say that you were for abolishing ethnic theme dorms and then have time to make a rational argument for your position before people generally saw red, stopped listening to you, and discounted you as a right-wing nut.

There was no room for conservative viewpoints in the prevailing campus political ethos, and the stifling political correctness made it hard to break through the Stanford bubble, in which most just want everybody in the world to have an idyllic life and nobody ever to have their feelings hurt. The problem is that such a mantra is not realistic and often comes at the expense of liberty and free speech. Then again, it's hard to be realistic when you live in as perfect a place as Stanford.

So the challenge was to break through the inertia and add to campus debate as frequently and forcefully as possible, and that's why we had the Stanford Review. It also wasn't as difficult as it's been cracked up to be because Stanford conservatives are generally pretty good at not buying into "the Movement." The Movement is this fictitious thing that conservative organizations are trying to sell students who lean right these days. This, in my mind anyway, makes them just as scary as communists.

The thought is that if you can get college students who feel persecuted for their ideology to join together and fight for conservatism, you can hook them for life. But true college conservatives and libertarians should not be touting an ideology; they should be using their energy to chip away at the liberal establishment. A college conservative should be focused on invalidating political correctness and adding to diversity of thought on campus, not pushing some Movement that doesn't exist. Stanford conservatives, and the Review in particular, have always been good about that.

There was a danger, though, of becoming reactionary. Just as conservatives point to the liberal brain-washing effect that universities often have on students, it is easy to be pushed farther to the right when you're such a small minority. I know that I went through several conservative incarnations before I settled on a more libertarian philosophy. But there were bumps along the way where I probably qualified for the epithet "scary conservative," and there were times I could see myself straying farther and farther to the right in the name of provocation.

But I think I've become more open-minded since then. If there's one thing Stanford taught me it was to think for myself, and having such a fantastic education under my belt helps me approach issues from different angles and not rush to political judgment. It's easy to fall into a dark box of ideology when you're working so hard to represent a minority viewpoint, but, luckily, Stanford also gives you the tools to find your way out and to think seriously about important things.

And finally, some advice to the non-graduating classes: stay as long as you can.
