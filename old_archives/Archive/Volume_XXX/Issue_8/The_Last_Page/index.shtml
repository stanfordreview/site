The Last Page
So What's The Job Market for Grads?
by Michael V. Petras
Business Manager '94

Given the current state of the US economy, employment prospects for this year's crop of graduates aren't what they were when I left Stanford in 1995. With that in mind, the Review's staff has asked me to present graduating seniors with some employment alternatives until the economy picks up and you can latch onto something better.

What's not on the list. . . .
Becoming a CEO and overnight Internet millionaire overnight is no longer an option. Yes, we know you all long for the days when a half-baked business models model on the back of a napkin got funded, when Stanford was the center of the universe. So forget you your senior year of high school's get-rich-quick dreams, and settle into the reality of the situation. You'll probably have a long line of lesser jobs in boring companies, hoping to claw or brown-nose your way to the top. Then again, you could always choose another path. . . .real work. . . .

Bartender
This is a recession-proof industry -- when the economy is good, people drink. When the economy is bad, people drink hard.

Chemistry or Chemical Engineering majors should excel in this field; they say, after all, that chemists make great mixologists. There's a certain satisfaction to it, as well -- you've just spent $150,000 killing your brain cells at Stanford, so why not get paid for doing the same to others?

For guys, at first the concept of interacting with attractive women will be unfamiliar and uncomfortable. I suggest treating yourself to a couple of shots to warm up nightly. By midnight both you and your female patrons will be significantly more interesting.

Military
The war on Terror needs recruits, and you might want to follow the example of Arizona Cardinal safety Pat Tillman by joining the Army of One. Although it's too late to benefit from a G.I. Bill to help with the spiraling cost of a Stanford education, at least now you can put some of those inane political science discussions to practice. Hopefully, the real world will knock you out of your ivory-tower thinking, and you'll shoot back. Yeah, we know everyone in the world hates Americans: that's what you were taught by communist hold-overs with tenure in the PoliSci and History Departments, but little speaks louder than SMART bombs dropped on a postage stamp of a target from seven miles up by nearly invisible planes. Moreover, the Don't-Ask-Don't-Tell policy will be an improvement from the coming-out fest that was Stanford housing.

Americorps
While jobs in the private sector are scarce, Congress is expanding the corps of "volunteers" who make up Americorps. In truth, you get paid to do this, so this isn't a bad gig as far as voluntarism goes.

I've been told that this is Bill Clinton's answer to JFK's call for American to serve others (outside of the intern staff). On the bright side - the $6 per day you spend on food will inevitably seem superior to any Dining Services plan you had at Stanford.

Hollywood Assistant
Several years of parroting the Newspeak of your profs probably plied you into an incipient sycophant, but at the very least you learned to spew the party line in order to survive. Congratulations, you have what it takes to get coffee and make copies for semi-important Hollywood types. May I suggest watching lots of Swimming with the Sharks and Swingers before arriving in Los Angeles. It takes a special person to backstab and develop meaningless rehashes of existing work.

While you're down there, look up former Review Editor Dave Sacks, who is producing in LA, whatever that means. The pickings are slim, so if you don't already have a job, start hitting the bricks.

For underclassmen...
if you don't want to be left behind and get on the fast track, join the Review staff, become a conservative and go work for Peter Thiel (the Review's founder) as he starts his next Paypal and makes millionaires out of more former Review staffers.

Working for the Majority . . . . any of them
Having restored the power trifecta they had surrendered when Senator Jim Jeffords of Vermont bolted the GOP and handed Senate control to the Democrats, the majority party now sees an exceedingly favorable political climate. By contrast, the Democrats are still licking their wounds after having suffered historic losses in the midterm elections.

Things should get even better in 2004, when geography and a popular president at the top of the ticket give Republicans an electoral edge. Democrats have to defend seven Senatorial seats where the incumbent won with less than 55% of the vote. Republicans only have three -- Kit Bond of Missouri; John Fitzgerald of Illinois; and Jim Bunning of Kentucky. So what the Hell? Rubbing it in to your commie-sympathizer college-mates isn't a bad use of your time if Goldman-Sachs isn't hiring.
