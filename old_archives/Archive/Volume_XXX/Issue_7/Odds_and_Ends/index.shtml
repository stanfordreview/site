Odds and Ends
"I Pledge Allegiance...to Erm?"
by Gary Raichart
Opinions Editor

The Reverend Dr. Michael Newdow visited Stanford on May 6th to espouse his views to an audience of a few dozen in an event sponsored by the newly formed Stanford Atheist/Freethinker Society.

Newdow is infamous for his June 2002 case before the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals in which the court panel ruled 2-1 that the Pledge of Allegiance is unconstitutional because it includes the phrase "one nation under God." The case sparked an immense outcry by the public and government officials alike illustrated by the Senate's 99-0 resolution in favor of "under God" in the Pledge.

Newdow also has two cases currently pending against the government. The first contends that prayers by actual reverends at presidential inaugurations are unconstitutional. More specifically, he has sued President Bush because the President allowed the Reverend Franklin Graham to pray "in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost" during Bush's 2001 inauguration. The second case challenges the constitutionality of taxpayer-funded chaplains praying in Congress and ministering to elected officials, which is a tradition that predates the Bill of Rights itself.

Newdow's crusade against religious invocations in public life began when he one day noticed and became offended by the fact that "In God We Trust" was minted on the coins he was using to buy soap. This revelation led to his first lawsuit, in which he challenged the constitutionality of having the phrase on US currency in the 11th US District Court. After he lost standing in the 11th District by moving from Florida to California, he dropped this case, using it as a learning experience, and refocused his attention on the Pledge of Allegiance.

Newdow believes his case against the pledge to be quite strong. He said, "People ask, ÔDon't you feel like David vs. Goliath?' to which I reply that I kind of feel like Goliath because the law is on my side." He cited three different tests established by the Supreme Court to determine the constitutionality of governmental actions related to religion. The first of these, the Lemon test, asks whether the action has a secular purpose, has a religious effect, or entangles government with religion. The second, the endorsement test, asks whether the action endorses a specific religion. The third and final standard, the coercion test, asks whether children are being forced by the government to listen to religious dogma. Newdow, in reasoning echoed by the Ninth Circuit ruling, argued that the Pledge fails all three tests. Moreover, Newdow claimed the argument that the inclusion of "under God" is not really religious but historical to be false because the original Pledge was written in 1892 without the phrase, which Congress added in 1954.

Newdow answered questions as to what he thought about the fact that the authors of the Bill of Rights were the same people who decided to pay the Congressional chaplains in the first place. He also fielded questions pertaining to the deeply religious people of the Founders, who invoked a "Supreme Being" often in politics in their assertions that the Constitutional Convention gave power to the people to eliminate a tyrannical sovereign.

Newdow believes that the Founders made the First Amendment "as broad as possible" in its phrasing that "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion." It is from this interpretation that he derives his belief that religion is to have no role in government whatsoever. In response to questions concerning the implications of such an interpretation for the constitutional legitimacy of the Declaration of Independence, his response is "If it came out today, maybe." However, he never quoted the next part of the First Amendment: "or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." Newdow stated that his ultimate goal is "to get any religious dogma out of government." He claimed, "Atheists are a disenfranchised minority in our society, which is why I brought the lawsuit." He cited statistics from a Gallup survey that found that 48% of Americans would be unwilling to support an atheist for President and that only about 5% of the population is atheist.

The Reverend Doctor brought along his guitar and harmonica and sang three songs during the course of his presentation. The first, "Old Religion Blues," included the lines "The Atheists are coming ... We're not about to let them bring this country into ruin. It's bad enough with Catholics and Mormons and the Jews." Each verse of the song ended with the line "They had the Pledge of Allegiance needs some old religion blues." The second song mocked Senator Robert C. Byrd, whom Newdow said he had been reluctant to ridicule because of the Senator's position on Iraq. The song was in response to Byrd's statement: "I, for one, am not going to stand for this country's being ruled by a bunch of atheists. If they do not like it, let them leave."

Newdow ended his presentation with a song entitled "Just Not History," in which he ridiculed the description of religious icons as historical. Citing the Lynch v. Donnelly case, which ruled a nativity scene constitutional, Newdow sang, "Has the Supreme Court lost its mind?" Additionally, in reference to Congress changing the national motto from "E pluribus unum" to "In God We Trust," Newdow sang, "Was Congress smoking weed? Perhaps strung out on speed? Or maybe their folks liked to interbreed."

Newdow is a practicing physician who also has a law degree. Moreover, he is an ordained minister. How can an atheist be an ordained minister? The Universal Life Church grants ordained minister status to anyone who requests it of them, advertising "Become an ordained minister in just 3 minutes. FREE" on their website. Moreover, this so-called church has also granted Newdow a "Doctor of the Universe" degree. He is also the "founding minister" of the "First Amendment Church of True Science."

Newdow also believes that "we have a deficiency in our language. We can't refer to someone without referring to their gender." To amend this situation, he proposes replacing the words "he/she" with "ree," "his/her" with "rees," and "him/her" with "erm."

Stanford's Atheist/Freethinker Society sponsored the presentation as part of an effort to organize events that will attract non-atheists. Such events are part of the society's overarching goal of promoting religious discussion on campus.
