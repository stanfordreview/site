The Rawls Report


Alec Rawls
Gary Raichart
Ryan Wisnesky
Sam Shapero
Volume XXX, Issue 7 May 22, 2002
Stanford Review - Archive - Volume XXX - Issue 7 - Letters

Letters
Probably the most disappointing thing about The Rawls Report on racial profiling was the amount of assumptions that were made by Mr. Rawls. But I'll have to get back to that, and just move through the article, because that's the quickest way. Maybe I'll just make a list:

1) "Thus a random black pulled over by the police..."

Wrong. Statistics don't work that way. While it is true to say that among criminal offenders, the race is seven times more likely to be black than white, it is not true to say that a black person is seven times more likely to be a criminal. In order to prove that you would also have to compare population sizes, which you have not. Example: If there were two white people and one white criminal, 50 percent of the whites would be criminals. If there were 70 black people and seven black criminals, 10 percent of the blacks would be criminals. While the crime rate would still be seven times as high for blacks, a white person would still be more likely to be a criminal. I'm not suggesting that these numbers are correct, but you see my point. Your logic is flawed because you do not have enough information.

2) Your statistics can also not be expanded to guess at the occurrence of "suspicious particulars." That is an opinion, not something suggested at all by the facts. It is possible that searches are being made for all races without any regard to any suspicion, and completely based on race. You don't give any data about the rates of whites versus other races who were found to be guilty of something during these traffic stops, something that would seemingly prove your point entirely if the data agrees with you. Otherwise, to exaggerate, it's entirely possible that traffic stops based on crimes caught in other ways were causing blacks to be pulled over (and in some cases harassed, which is not addressed in your article, and is probably the real problem here. When black people get stopped, they are often treated as guilty, not as likely to be guilty, and the difference is tremendous.) 100 percent of whom were completely innocent. Again, this is an exaggeration, but you see my point. I would be curious what those actual numbers are.

3) Most blacks would be from East Palo Alto - wow, your prejudice is really showing here. I wonder if you did a population comparison (or one at all) that would justify those statements, especially considering the added population of campus, which has a significantly higher than national average black population. But either way, wow, use facts, not your assumptions.

4) New York example - same issue here, you can't prove the behavior from the statistics.

5) The problem with your evidence is just that, that it is prima facie, at first look, without close inspection.

6) Not in order, but I just thought of something, it's interesting that in your article at all you seem to advocate these random stops, instead of more comprehensive investigative work. Your ideas clearly favor innocent whites over innocent blacks, who are just supposed to deal with it, I guess (Oh, and your assumption that all black people live in black neighborhoods I thought was fascinating.) What about the "few blacks" who do live in Palo Alto and now make up a great percentage of the suspicious population based solely on their skin color? If I am black, and I am the only black person in a community with seven other white people, and our crime rates are similar to those that you stated (which can be based on any number of outsider perpetrators, let's assume the innocence of the people in the neighborhood), and you make stops everyday, the white people in the neighborhood would each be stopped once a week, while I would be stopped EVERY DAY. Just because of my skin color. Don't tell me "the main beneficiaries would be law-abiding blacks," that's just ignorant. Ask a black man in a nice car what it's like to be pulled over and treated like a criminal because he's dressed a certain way, or acts in a way that makes the police assume he couldn't possible own it. The "collateral damage" is not limited to unjust laws. What about people who haven't done anything at all? I guess getting into an elevator and having old ladies grab their purses makes me a little biased, but I think that's my whole point. In a country where black people are considered guilty until proven twice as innocent (where in a suit and tie my Dad still can't get a cab in NYC), where a white person in ratty clothes just might be expected to be a grungy rich kid, but a black person dressed the same way is considered homeless, or a criminal, or both, where racist e-mails are sent to the business school mailing list at NYU and the administration can't find the time to do anything about it other than give sound bites of regret, the one place for respite would have been nice to have as law enforcement. But now I'm getting upset, so back to your article.

7) "If violent criminals are going to hide behind black skin..." wow. I guess the only thing I can say to that is that maybe we should start randomly stopping young white men who are more likely to be serial killers and white businessmen who are more likely to commit egregious fraud. Because those types of crimes are certainly hiding behind white skin. The only difference seems to be that you can actually hide there.

So that's it, feel free to ask for clarification, because I am typing fast and need to get back to my other writing, so I may not have been very clear on some points. It really is true you can make statistics say whatever you want.

Thanks for listening.

Allison Lee
German Studies '03
Communications MA '04
