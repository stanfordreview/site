Front Page
Student Strike Brings Controversy, Chaos to Stanford Academic Life
by Ryan J. Wisnesky
News Staff

On Wednesday, March 5, students nationwide skipped class as part of the Books not Bombs "student strike" to voice opposition to a possible war with Iraq.

About one-hundred Stanford students held a rally at the clock tower at 9:30 a.m. and then marched into the Main Quad, which had been peppered with signs sporting slogans such as "War is Environmentally Racist" and "Love your Enemies." The rally was just the first stage of a day filled with workshops and teach-ins by different community and faculty members. Although organizers estimate that about 1,000 different students attended the strike over the course of the entire day, only several hundred were present in the main quad at any given time.

Student speakers at the rally talked about the importance of an organized movement. "It's chants that make us a movement," said one speaker, as the crowd began to chant "Who's War? Their War." Around 10:00 a.m. the students rallying at the clock tower formed a long line and marched toward East Campus, looping around the Hoover building to the sound of drums while chanting various slogans.

The protesters entered the Main Quad without obstruction, even though the University had instructed that the protesters refrain from using the Main Quad because the noise might disrupt classes. A student in a French 3 section noted that her class had been cut short because it was difficult to conduct class over the loud chants.

Other classes were affected by the rally besides those directly in the Quad. When Philosophy 160A met that morning at 9:00 a.m., the entire blackboard had been covered in anti-war slogans, prompting a brief discussion over the merits of the protest. Class resumed without incident.

According to junior David Kale, one of the students in the class, "If people want to march around it's their business. What upsets me is that they are trying to get profs to change their plans and cancel classes. . . . It's not their right to interfere with those who want to go about their business."

Organizers boasted that 64 professors publicly endorsed the student strike, and 26 professors canceled class. Recent letters to the daily have included veiled threat of lawsuits for breach of contract by the University.

Once they reached the Main Quad, the protestors stopped chanting, "A people, divided, will never be defeated," and at 11:00 am the first round of teach-ins began, followed by another round at 11:30.

Teach-in topics ranged from Professor David Abernethy's talk about the many subtleties of a potential war and Professor Philip Zimbardo's talk on the relationship between the war, evil, religion, and fear. "If you create a sense of disaster," Prof. Zimbardo said, "people will have to cooperate." He concluded his talk by saying that "this is really just a start. . . . There are a number of things you have to do to dissent and maybe ultimately disobey." Mr. Zimbardo's talk alone attracted hundreds of people.

All kinds of people were present in the Quad. The Green Party had a table set up where students could register to vote and obtain information about the Green Party and its stance on the war. There was a table selling t-shirts with proceeds benefiting moveOn.org, the website for a community of people dedicated to preventing war by promoting tougher UN inspections. There were tables where people could call Condoleezza Rice and President Bush to voice their opinions via voicemail and where individuals could write to their congresspeople. Many of those in attendance wore green arm bands to show "solidarity for peace." One table had small cards with instructions on how to transform America's cities by not going to school and work and flooding the streets if war started.

Although the protest went off largely without a hitch, there were a number of counter-protests. One group of three students marched through the crowd with several pro-war signs, including one reading "Bomb Iraq." When asked his thoughts on the protest and the war, Ismail Simpson, one of the three students with the signs, said, "Saddam is a bad guy. They [the protestors] don't realize how bad Saddam is. To compare Bush to Saddam is ridiculous."

Once the teach-ins were finished, Lolita Roibal took to the podium to coordinate the event's closing activities. Ms. Roibal played a tape of the Zapatistas, a group of dissenters locked in low-level but continuous violence with the Mexican government. "We have the voice of the Zapatistas speaking here with us today," she offered.

Various student arts groups performed, including a group of flamenco dancers. Roibal explained their presence by saying, "There are a lot of groups uniting to end US imperialism, to get the US out of everything."

Student reaction to the protest was mixed. Many students felt that, by attending class, they would be making a political statement. Other students who legitimately missed class without having participated in the protest were also dismayed at having their actions misinterpreted because of the strike. But, on the whole, most students seemed at least thoughtful about the protest.

Doug Wilson, a sophomore at the rally, had this to say: "I think it's important that people realize the protest wasn't just a liberal knee-jerk, but actually, especially through the teach-ins, gave an intelligent anti-war argument that even if people disagree with it I hope they took the time to listen to the professors since they had some valid things to say."
