Opinion
"Silly Student Strike"
by Laura Surma
Opinions Staff

As a conservative, I place tremendous value on the right to freedom of speech. It allows me to practice my religion openly, to dress as I desire, and even to write for this newspaper. Regardless of the cause, I wholeheartedly support anyone's right to peaceable assembly. In fact, even though I am not party to the current anti-war movement, I consider myself fortunate to have witnessed the increase in political involvement that has occurred on campus since I first came here last September. My distaste for the Books not Bombs student strike that took place on March 5 does not stem from any blanket desire to suppress anti-war sentiment.

Instead, my disdain for Books not Bombs stems from the ridiculously illogical form that anti-war protesters chose to manifest their discontent with the manner in which the protest was carried out.

When I heard about plans for the strike via e-mail about 10 days before the designated date, the first thing that came to mind was my time in junior high. I remembered that we used to organize walkouts for just about any cause we could find. Some students went so far as to strike to oppose the expulsion of a seventh grader who brought a gun to school with the intention "to scare" her ex-boyfriend into taking her back. The bottom line in junior high was that we all wanted excuses to get out of class. That's not to say that the anti-war cause is on the same level as the causes embraced in junior high, but, when you consider the practical side, a strike makes much more sense in junior high or high school than it does in college.

A strike usually implies that something is being withheld in order to effect change. For example, a union of factory workers can decide to picket, thereby painfully halting the factory's production. The union's efforts are often effective because the factory cannot function without its workers, and, feeling the financial burden, the factory management ultimately compromises with the union's demands.

Striking is effective in junior high and high school because administrations are charged with the duty of both disciplining and educating the students. When students defy the authority of the administration and show that it is not doing its job of containing and instructing them, they are relatively likely to have their voices heard--as long as they ultimately agree to go back to class.

The problem with student strikes on the university level is that no one forces you to attend class in college, and, by going to class, you're not doing anyone a favor but yourself.

Essentially, you're a customer. Not attending class is akin to going to the supermarket and saying that you refuse to take the groceries that you purchased home because you oppose a potential war. The supermarket probably won't care because it gets to keep your money without even having to give up its merchandise.

Similarly, aside from feeling perhaps spurned, the University administration is not going to be terribly perturbed if you choose to pay for your classes without attending. At the university level, the responsibility for learning rests more in the students than in the institution, especially because one of the most important functions of such a university as Stanford is research, which student strikes fail to disrupt.

It would have taken a lot more courage for protesters to refuse to pay their tuition for Spring Quarter. Then, the University would have felt the pain to the tune of $12k per student. Of course, Stanford will always have the option of simply admitting other equally qualified students to replace those who, playing the role of the spoiled child, have refused to take a good thing when it came to them.

Perhaps the most fundamentally silly thing about the strike is its absolute uselessness with respect to catching the attention of the protesters' "big enemy." The federal government is not harmed in any way when students at a private school opt to ditch class for a day. It wouldn't even feel any pain if students chose to withhold their tuition--the government might in fact benefit from having to fund fewer financial-aid checks. So, if a mere protest would have had the same effect, why did organizers decide on the unnecessary strike? Why not protest on a Saturday when no one has class and all professors--even those who wish to conduct class as scheduled--could attend? Why not protest between classes?

The only motivation for a strike rather than the standard protest seems to be the increased appeal for students of having a more legitimate excuse to miss class than sleeping in. As the Stanford Daily reported on March 6, "Indeed, both the tenor and scope of the event contrasted sharply with the average campus rally." Why? More students will come when there's more hype and when they can tell their professors that they are missing class on principle rather than laziness.

The irony of the whole situation lay in the slogan Books not Bombs. In an apparently absurd attempt to learn something, I actually attended class on March 5. Right in the middle of a fascinating conversation about New Urbanism, the chanting of the strike stifled my classmates' voices, and we had to wait about ten minutes before we could resume. I'm sure that that's exactly what the protesters had in mind, but silly me, I preferred to learn about urban planning rather than the evils of "trigger-happy-Texas-cowboy-Bush."

More offensive than the disruption of other students' efforts to learn was the popular misrepresentation of the strike. "The phrase Ôbroad coalition' does not adequately capture the incredible diversity of its constituent groups, many of whose missions center on ethnic advocacy," the Stanford Daily reported on March 6. To my surprise, when I examined the list of campus organizations that supported the strike, I saw two groups of which I am a member, but interestingly, I don't recall ever being asked if I wanted those organizations to express their support. Apparently, if there are a few loud radical voices in each campus group, we can show that virtually every group on campus is anti-war.

I might have liked to check the strike out for myself, but, had I stopped by one of the teach-ins, the multitude of television cameras would have captured me as "one of the thousands of students opposed to the war." Because I'm not opposed to the war, I would be forced, in order to make sure I would be represented correctly, to wear a huge sign saying, "War is Sometimes Necessary," or, "I Trust the Bush Administration to Make the Right Decision." I can't imagine that having gone over too well with the protesters.
