Opinion
University Singers Impress
by Ming Zhu
Opinions Staff

Ever since I joined the Stanford University Singers last fall, I have become resigned to the fact that few Stanford students are actually aware of its existence. While most students recognize the Chamber Chorale, many of these same students meet the mention of a University Singers concert with puzzled expressions or blank stares.

While the crowd that gathered for the winter quarter show this past Tuesday night was hardly the largest for the group, it was definitely one of the luckiest. Those who attended the March 4 concert were treated to one of the finest musical performances of the year.

To begin, the pieces chosen were simply delightful; the melodies and counterpoint lines in the Mass were so lively and energetic that audience members could be seen nodding their heads in time with the beat. Moreover, the dramatic soft phrases -- conductor Robert Huw Morgan describes them as "piano mysteriosso" sections -- caused quite a few foreheads to turn in anticipation.

Unlike Mr. Morgan's previous concerts, this performance consisted of only two pieces. While this meant that there was less variation in terms of style and sound, the Singers easily compensated for this with an incredible amount of time spent preparing each piece. It was a truly impressive performance from the perspective of one of its performers; for a choir that meets only twice a week, the results were extremely impressive.

While the performance moved to impress, there were some flaws, particularly with balance. For various reasons, ranging from students studying abroad to musical theatre rehearsal conflicts, the alto section was cut in half over the last quarter. Consequently, the alto section was noticeably quieter in comparison to the rest of the sections. Those who stepped forward to represent the alto section this quarter deserve special praise; it isn't easy to carry a part with only three or four other voices in a choir of almost forty.

Despite this, the magical moments abounded. This was truly evident in the amazing quality of the Haydn soloists, who were professional singers with the San Francisco Conservatory. Although many of the University Singers are not music majors and have little interest in pursuing a professional music career, it was hard not to appreciate the talent and energy in the soloists' voices. The privilege of performing in a concert with such talent was quite an honor for the choir, and the audience certainly benefited as well.

To put it simply: the evening was a pleasure for everyone involved. If you weren't lucky enough to attend, you'll have one more chance during spring quarter.
