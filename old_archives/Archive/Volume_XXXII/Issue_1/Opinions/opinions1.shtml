Opinions
Howard Dean: Bad for Democrats, Bad for Democrats
by Chris Fish
Opinions Staff Writer

The current Democratic Primaries have put the magnifying glass on Democratic candidates and on the Democratic Party as a whole. However, of all the Democrats, the greatest focus has been put on the former Governor of Vermont, Howard Dean. Outwardly, Dean has been portrayed as a leftist Democrat with a hot temper. He has been shown to have a lack of political suaveness and an inability to hold the interest of the public. Dean is now currently seen as a whining and confusing character. The fact that Democrats have made Dean one of their front-running candidates will only hurt the Democratic Party and will, by default, boost the lead President Bush will have as the incumbent. In plain terms, Dean will help George Bush get re-elected.

Dean's obsession with personally attacking President Bush have worked less to expose deep flaws within the administration and more to give a clearer picture of a paranoid Howard Dean. He describes President Bush as, "not interested in being a good president", adding that, "He's interested in some complicated psychological situation that he has with his father." Dean's basic assumption that the President is motivated by abstract Father-Son psychology seems ludicrous to the average American and works specifically to alienate moderate American voters who normally would be very willing to listen to the Democratic platform. In making such statements, Dean is making the subtle political errors that will help to insure a Republican incumbent re-election.

Howard Dean also does an expert job of alienating the average American. His comments have often been seen as elitist or ignorant. For example, in a campaign speech, Dean recently claimed, "I want people with confederate flags on their trucks to put down those flags and vote Democratic". In this statement, Dean alienates the conservative and usually Republican poor Southern voting constituency that he is trying to woo into the Democratic Party. He stereotypes poor Southern voters by relating them to the racist visions associated with the Confederate past of the South. Even if Dean's statements are correct he shows his lack of political tact by saying them.

Howard Dean is prepared to carry his anti-war stance to the finish line. This is also a mistake. The decision to attack Iraq is now a political afterthought. Dean's accusations of the war as a mistake are now seen as reactionary and dated arguments. America is committed financially and militarily to the region. American voters have agreed to and support this stance. Dean's statement that, "the president made a mess of (Iraq) and "he didn't know what he was getting into", are counterproductive. Dean, by asserting what may have been an important view at one time in the past, has shown himself to be out of touch. The Democrats must support a pro-Reconstruction stance to give chase to the Republicans. Otherwise, President Bush will use the progress in Iraq against Dean in debate, which will further exaggerate Dean's incompetence.

Dean also hurts his campaign and his party when he makes blunt international relation what-if statements and then fails to provide detail or rational for his assertions; sometimes he even neglects to make assertions at all. For example, when asked how he would handle the Saudis, who have been linked to terrorist groups, Dean claimed that he "would use economic pressure". When asked what specifically he could do he replied, "I prefer to make my threats privately". This example points out Dean's inability to make strong stands. Dean's side-stepping of real international issues magnify his weakness as a leader and lack of the power needed to be a president of this country.

Howard Dean also fails to be viable competition to President Bush because he squanders opportunities to properly point out Republican pitfalls. Explicitly, he fails to portray a fiscally sound alternative to Bush's increase in spending. Instead, Dean makes wild promises to liberal groups that would support him anyway. In doing so, he fails to court the more conservative swing groups that he could possibly pick up and which he needs to win the election. Dean is simply too far to the left. He makes moderates more comfortable voting for President Bush by catering to leftist groups.

Dean's clenched-fist demeanor, hot temper, and ignorant political styles will, in the end, insure re-election for President Bush. He has neither the skill nor the tact to popularize Democratic initiatives such as repealing tax cuts and increasing social programming. His presence as a Democratic front-runner works to help Republicans court American voters. The only chance for a close election will be a mass Democratic exodus from supporting Howard Dean.

However, the race between the Democratic candidates is far from over. Kerry upset Dean in the Iowa Caucus but the reverse could still happen. Perhaps left-wing voters have learned their lesson and moved their support to the stronger candidate (Kerry) for good -- a move that will aid the Democratic party in its bid for the presidency. For now, the Republicans will just have to wait it out and hope Dean pulls back into the lead.
