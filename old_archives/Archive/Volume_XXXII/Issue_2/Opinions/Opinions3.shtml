Socially Inept Students Hurt Campus

Stanford University is full of remarkable people whose accomplishments overflowed into the margins on their applications, but as we all know, college admissions officers often overemphasize factors like race and community service while ignoring some of the most important characteristics of a student’s portfolio: social skills. When most students receive the fat envelope from Stanford, they do not anticipate a social scene that competes with state schools, but they shouldn’t have to contend with a campus full of socially incompetent geniuses.

What has happened to the well-rounded student who can juggle academic rigor and maintain some semblance of personality? While parents, teachers, and admissions officers praise academic achievements as the measure of success, students know that this means little when trying to strike up a conversation at the CoHo or sharing a laugh at a party on the Row. No one outside your Chemistry and Math TA’s is impressed that you know the atomic weight of Tungsten or can calculate an Eigenvalue, but everyone can smile when you regale the room with a story of self-deprecation or tell a joke and get to know your fellow classmates. Sadly, these nuances of personality are lost on this campus of intrepid but awkward young students, most of whom do not display the social skills needed to get a date on a Friday night.

Here at Stanford, the evidence of widespread social ineptitude is glaring. “The Stanford Bubble” pertains not only to our big-campus mentality but also to the majority of Stanford students who seem to be out of touch with general social trends. Most people spend their time in their rooms studying, and classes are filled with awkward discussions in which people participate infrequently.

Personality concerns go past benign interactions between students in the social scene. All people respond better to those who are socially amiable and can communicate their thoughts clearly. Professors and TA’s who are dynamic make lectures more interesting and enjoyable. Accordingly, teachers will respond to students who are socially apt and personable. Because shyness and awkwardness are ubiquitous traits amongst the nerdy, they are prolific at Stanford, but it is important to realize that they benefit no one.

Despite Stanford students’ obsessions with being the best, they are ignoring a key component of success. As countless professionals continue to preach, having good social skills is one of the most important qualities for success in the job market. Having social awareness and competency is as or more important than “book smarts” in pursuing a career in many fields, including business, law, and medicine. As any investment banker will attest to, knowing the price-to-earnings ratio alone will not seal the deal on Wall Street aa one must be able to impress the client with a strong presentation and high confidence.

One business-attuned Stanford sophomore agrees: “There is much more to a complete student than book smarts. I’m not surprised at all to discover that some of the most successful businessmen have had mediocre academic records in college.”

A key problem in the admissions process is that it favors those who spent more time padding their resumes than building relationships with friends. There is something to be said for recruiting students with marketable characteristics other than intelligence and the drive to succeed. Although Stanford claims that an applicant’s personality comes through in their essay, all those who wrote on such limited topics as “What’s Your Favorite Academic Interest?” will testify that their personality certainly was not encapsulated. Junior Lance Krieger laments, “When I applied to Columbia I was given a nice long interview and I think they realized there was more to me than a piece of paper.” He was surprised when he applied to Stanford that the school didn’t incorporate interviews into the application process.

Stanford needs to institute interviews into its admissions process. Interviews will allow admissions officers to identify those students who show promise in areas other than academics. These interviews would prevent the great percentage of those kids who got a 1600 on their SAT but can’t hit on a girl to save their life from becoming part of the Stanford community.

The admissions process has become more and more hair-splitting every year, with countless valedictorians, class presidents and science whiz-kids being denied admission here at Stanford and elsewhere. Certainly, a lack of talented applicants is not the problem. A perfect and necessary solution to this problem would be to make social aptitude the deciding criteria in the decision process.

Noting how concerned Stanford is with promoting diversity of any kind on campus, one would think they would support a variety of dispositions as well. Currently, we maintain a level of personality homogeneity which would disgust any high-minded admissions officer were they measuring any other distinguishable characteristic. Those students who are socially adept not only improve the academic atmosphere of our campus but also interject the sorely lacking social scene with vibrancy. These are the students Stanford needs. These are the future leaders of America.

Soon, if the administration continues to cut traditions like Full Moon on the Quad and The Bonfire, which generally attract the more social students, we will continue to see a decline in new students who actually had friends in high school. Stanford should not want to dance near this slippery slope when applicants have many other schools to choose from which in many cases offer more excitement.

Ultimately, college is about growing up and maturing, discovering yourself and letting your personality shine through. Never again will college students have the ability to goof off and enjoy the freedom of their adulthood in the same way again. Students on the Stanford campus should be serious about academics, but they shouldn’t ignore what the spirit of college is all about. Parents know that college’s most valuable reward is not your diploma, but the life experiences you gain. Don’t miss out on yours.
