Musharraf's Deception and Bush's Weak Foreign Policy
by Jeff Allen
Opinions Staff Writer

“You are either with us or against us” --President Bush, Statement after September 11th terrorist attacks.

After the tragic destruction and loss of life that occurred on September 11th 2001, President Bush defined a new US foreign policy. Regarding terrorism, he claimed that all nations of the world are either with the United States or they are an ally of terrorism and an enemy of the United States. This newly initiated foreign policy is considered by many to be aggressive and very strongly worded.

In a sense, President Bush was calling for international cooperation, which if not received, would be followed by preemptive counter-terrorism measures and possibly preventive war. The preventive war component has been witnessed in Operation Iraqi Freedom and the subsequent occupation of that country.

The President’s foreign policy, especially his decision to invade Iraq, has been an extremely controversial issue of great concern. Many questions have arisen... Was Saddam Hussein an imminent threat? Was the nuclear weapons intelligence faulty? Should Bush have gotten international approval before the invasion?

All of these questions are currently being widely discussed in the press and various political circles. However, there is another question that ought to addressed and which seems to be overshadowed by the Iraq issue. Is Pakistan cooperating with the United States and complying with President BushÕs foreign policy? And if the answer is no, why is President Bush doing nothing in response?

It was recently revealed that Pakistan is responsible for giving nuclear weapons technology to Iran, Libya, and North Korea. Dr. Abdul Qadeer Khan, a Pakistani nuclear scientist, sold these technologies. President Musharraf did not specifically authorize the transfers, and when the United States finally confronted Musharraf about Dr. Khan, he admitted to knowing about the transfers for three years. However, he did nothing to stop Khan for political reasons. The Pakistani President claimed that Dr. Khan was a national hero and made the excuse that he did not want to cause a political backlash.

Regarding the United States' stance on terrorism, there is evidence that Musharraf is allowing terrorist groups in Pakistan to continue operations and activities. In early 2002, he allied Pakistan with President Bush's campaign against Al Qaeda and other terrorist groups. Although Musharraf has declared that he is siding with the United States, there are instances in which he fails to uphold this "alliance."

One Islamic militant group which Musharraf allows to continue its activities is the Jamiat ul Ansar, whose leader is Fazlur Rehman Khalil. This group allied itself with Al Qaeda when Khalil cosigned Osama Bin Laden's 1998 edict which declared that it is a Muslim's duty to kill Americans and Jews. Jamiat ul Ansar prints a monthly magazine to raise funds, notify supporters of meetings and activities, and urge volunteers to fight U.S. forces in Afghanistan and Iraq. John Walker Lindh of California is serving a sentence for fighting on the side of the Taliban, and one of his charges was that he attended one of Khalil's training camps.

Another instance of leniency against terrorist groups involved Maulana Masood Azhar, another of Pakistan's radical Muslim clerics. Azhar has an alliance with Bin Laden that dates back to 1993. They cooperated on a project in which terrorist groups attacked U.S.-led forces in Somalia. Musharraf banned Azhar's militant group in 2002, known as the Army of Mohammed, in response to an attack on India's Parliament that left 14 dead. However, just a year later, Pakistani judges ordered the release of Azhar with no charges.

President Bush has stated a very aggressive stance on terrorism. However, in regard to Pakistan, he has not supplemented his insistent policy with assertive action. Pakistan continues to endanger the security of the United States and the world by allowing the transfer of nuclear weapons between countries and permitting terrorist groups to continue operating within its borders. Musharraf has denounced terrorism, but he has not taken full responsive action to support his statement. Similarly, Bush has made a verbal stance against terrorism, but he has not taken an active stance in terms of U.S.-Pakistani relations. Where is the dialogue between Bush and Musharraf? Where is the assertive international diplomacy? A strong foreign policy requires both a forceful statement AND the willingness to take effective action. Many would argue that our soldiers are in Iraq unnecessarily. Meanwhile, we are letting terrorist activities and the exchange of nuclear weapons to slip behind our backs within the borders of Pakistan, our supposed ally.
