Vagina Monologues Come to Stanford
by Eric Woersching
Contributing Writer

For most people, February 14th is a day saturated with hearts, flowers, cupids and chocolates. But for the fifth year at Stanford, many students have associated Valentine’s Day with something a little less idyllic; the Vagina Monologues. The two hour theatrical piece, created by activist Eve Ensler, was showcased by undergraduates on Friday and Saturday nights. Both productions of the monologues sold out or nearly sold out Dinkelspiel Auditorium.

The all-female cast of seventeen performed the approximately twenty monologues which all revolved around awareness of womanhood in both an abstract and a very physical sense. Many of the monologues openly and comically discussed topics such as masturbation, menstruation, pubic hair and vaginal discharge. Despite the callous nature of these subjects, the comedy was well received by the primarily female audience. This may have been in part because much of the humor was at the expense of their male counterparts. One narrator scoffs at the number of nerves in the penis in comparison to the clitoris by stating “Who needs a handgun when you have a semi-automatic.” Also, the sexual mishaps of men are too easily converted to jokes such as “If it could talk, what would your vagina say? ... Slow Down!! ... Where’s Brian? ... Don’t give up!”

The Vagina Monologues was littered with many vivid statements some have called “anti-man.” In one instance, the narrator recounts an episode from grade school where she is punched by a ten year old boy in the crotch, “I limp home. I can’t pee.” The audience was also thoroughly amused by a response to the question “If your vagina could wear anything, what would your vagina wear? An electrical shock device to keep unwanted visitors away!” Supporters say these statements are simply an attempt to stop the violence perpetuated against women.

Though much of the material was based on the comedy of what it means to be a woman, the serious topics of violence, rape, incest, and abuse were raised in graphic detail. A new addition to the monologues this year included the chilling account of one of the five hundred women who have been acid burned by their families in Islamabad. Another story is told from the perspective of a Kosovar refugee who describes how her feelings about her vagina drastically changed after six Serbian soldiers raped her for nearly a week. All of the Vagina Monologue’s stories of rape and abuse are so incredibly sobering that one cannot leave the auditorium without understanding why Eve Ensler, a victim of rape herself, strives to push these issues to forefront of social awareness.

Criticism for Eve Ensler’s piece is derived more from some of the less serious monologues. Many critics feel one monologue which encourages the audiences to chant the word “c-nt,” does little to empower women and only serves to dilute the more important message of believing that one’s vagina is a thing of beauty and an embodiment of oneself. In addition, the same critics contend that the topic of lesbianism, though critical to mention, should not be broached in the context of a relationship between a child and an adult, even if both parties are female. This reference to child abuse in a positive light is a major part of the monologue, The Little Coochi Sorcher That Could. Critics cite the constant emphasis on sexuality combined with a lack of discussion about abstinence or even contraception, making the Vagina Monologues especially offensive to most conservatives.

Furthermore, there has been a national outcry over the use of state funds to put on the Vagina Monologues at some public colleges. Unlike other campuses, the Vagina Monologues at Stanford were not financed by the University with the exception of a $1500 grant from the ASSU to help cover production costs. The production of the Vagina Monologues and the Stanford V-Day campaign does not receive special fees funding. However, the Vagina Monologues, as part of the nationwide V-Day campaign, are required to be able to raise $6000 for charities which serve to aid battered women or raise awareness of violence against women.

The Stanford V-Day campaign hopes their donations will exceed $7000 from ticket sales and corporate and residence sponsorship.

Stanford students were charged $7 while others were charged $12 to attend the monologues. About fifteen dorms, including the Phi Kappa Psi and Kappa Sigma fraternities and the Inter-sorority council, sponsored the event, with donations ranging from $50 to $100. Ten percent of this year’s proceeds will go to the V-Day campaign to stop the abduction and rape of women in Juarez, Mexico.

The additional bulk of the money raised will be donated to Menlo Park Rape Trauma Services.

Eric Woersching is a Junior majoring in Electrical Engineering.
