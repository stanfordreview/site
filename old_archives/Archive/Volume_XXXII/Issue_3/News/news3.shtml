Israeli Arab, Khaldi Supports His Country

by Shawn Sims
News Events Editor

A small group of students, mostly from the Jewish community at Stanford, gathered in Rains Buttery on Wed., Feb. 18 to hear the message of a concerned Israeli Bedouin. Ishmael Khaldi, diplomat in training, has spoken at over sixty universities across the U.S. and Canada since November 2002, determined to fulfill an obligation he feels to "share his story" of a mutually beneficial relationship between Bedouins and Israelis. He feels that historical Israeli help and current policy toward the Bedouins, a Muslim people, removes credibility from accusation of Israeli racism towards Muslims.

Roughly 150,000 Bedouins (2.6% of the total population) inhabit Israel. Bedouins are traditionally nomadic shepherds, living in sturdy tents in large, closely-knit family units. They hold strongly to conservative values and customs and prefer to remain somewhat culturally isolated for their own stability. Khaldi characterizes them as respectful of others' rights and differing values, though they resist modernization. "They are more secular" than other Arab factions in the Middle East, which makes them somewhat of an outcast group in the eyes of the Islamic world.

When the Zionists came in 1917 to inhabit the land that would become Israel, the Bedouin worked "side by side" with them to build the kibbutzim and to fight Britain and Arab nations. Because of friendships with early Jewish immigrants, Khaldis grandmother spoke Yiddish. A "blood covenant" developed between Israelis and Bedouins, and Khaldi feels that now, "Israel's fate is our fate." He repeated many times that he truly sees himself as an Israeli and takes pride in that identity, though his ties to his own culture are strong.

Living now in the boundaries of one of the most modernized Middle Eastern countries, Bedouins have had the benefit of help from Israel to "settle down," establish sanitation facilities, medical care, and schools. The change is occurring gradually: Khaldi's family lived in a tent with 11 children, and he was a shepherd until age 8. He said, "it was hard," but from the work he gained strong familial bonds and a great work ethic. His village got electricity only two years ago, and the slow pace of technology's entrance into their society is welcome to Khaldi.

Not being accustomed to amenities, he says that the Bedouin are better without the luxuries he sees as detrimental to American society, which retains "nothing about family values." When his brother donned a stylish belt, he was outraged, feeling that "next would come the earring, the tattoo, the hippie haircut." He takes pride in the Bedouins' simplicity and sees American society as indulgent.

Khaldi retains his ties to home and his conservative views while immersing himself in global society and its problems. After graduating high school, he went to New York, a rare opportunity for a Bedouin, and became determined to use the lessons he learned while traveling for the good of his country and people. He studied political science at Tel Aviv University and served in the Israeli Defense Force and as a political analyst for the Israel Defense Ministry. Bedouins are not required to serve in the Israeli military, which would force them to leave behind their families and livestock. Yet many men, including Khaldi, serve out of respect for Israel and its troubles with the knowledge that they will be protected should the need arise.

Khaldi grew up in northern Israel, between Haifa and Nazareth. Bedouins here have fairly stable settlements and increasing access to resources. The majority of Israeli Bedouins (120,000), live in the southern Negev desert in settlements over which debate of legality has raged. Khaldi pointed out that many of the conflicts over Bedouin settlements are exacerbated by the fact that they do not traditionally value owning land. Though he supports the Israeli people and nation, he feels that the bureaucracy is flawed and that this is one of many situations that could have been handled better.

The government is currently implementing a plan to resettle southern Bedouins and improve their living conditions. An attendee of Khaldis talk questioned him about Israeli treatment of Bedouins that contrasted with the helpful "blood covenant" he portrayed. The student cited the bombing of a mosque, property qualifications for government services, and the spraying of Bedouin crops in an unlicensed area.

Khaldi admitted that the Israeli government is not perfect, stressing that they are dealing with many problems right now. He praised the efforts of Israel to actively bring in terrorized groups, such as the Ethiopian Jews and Russian Jews from the USSR, when they were no longer safe in their homelands. In Israel, these minorities are living in even worse conditions than Bedouins, but Khaldi considers them all to be better off than the alternative. Khaldi criticizes those who expect Israel to solve all the complexities of cultural clashes and value differences among its citizens, especially since it is much freer country than those that surround it.

Khaldi emphasizes that difficulties in aiding Israel's citizens, minorities and Jews alike, stem from the critical defense issues that Israel currently faces. With random violence being perpetuated in public places, the Israeli government has to concentrate on saving the lives of its citizens. Khaldi says that despite any problems the Bedouins face as a people, "destiny or fate" has linked them to the Jews by the very "human" desire to stop senseless killing.

Khaldi proposes these solutions that he sees coming soon: a two-state society with a responsible leader, since "Arafat is not doing anything." Israel and Sharon want conciliation but do not want all the responsibility; Palestinian leadership should also be blocking terrorism. As a Muslim, Khaldi has been hated by others who feel he is a hypocrite. To those who say that security fence money could be better spent on humanitarian causes, such as the improvement of Bedouin living conditions, he says that the death must end first. With an air of disgust for those who target civilians, he said, "If my brother who grew up next to me became a suicide bomber, I would kill him, even tomorrow morning."

Ishmael's reception at Stanford was mild; he was banned from speaking at Langara College in Vancouver, though a conference entitled "Why Israel is an Apartheid State" was held days earlier. At Rutgers University in New Jersey students shouted questions and interrupted Khaldi, forcing him to step away from the podium. Over dinner after the talk, he expressed his surprise at the lack of factual knowledge about Israeli history among students in the U.S., particularly Jewish young adults who challenge Israeli policy.

Ishmael, whose name means "God will hear" in Hebrew, was brought by the Stanford Israel Alliance. Advoacy V.P. Dan Kaganovich extends the hope that Khaldi's unique message of tolerance be heard by as many people as possible.
