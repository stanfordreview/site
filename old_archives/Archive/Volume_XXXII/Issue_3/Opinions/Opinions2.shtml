Homosexuals Will Weaken Marriage
by Chris Fish

The Defense of Marriage Act states that the word 'marriage' means only a legal union between one man and one woman as husband and wife. Marriage as it exists today is an integral part of our society. It is the seal that binds a couple and allows for the formation of that all-important unit of society: the family. Accordingly, we as a society place great importance on the institution of marriage and see its value clearly. However, gay rights activists are now pushing for the legalization of gay marriage, something that would do much to harm the institution of marriage as a whole as well as the many heterosexual marriages formed by it.

Furthermore, allowing for gay marriage opens the door to further and more extreme degradations of marriage. Legalizing gay marriage provides a precedent for polygamists and polyamorists to demand the right for their marriages to be legalized as well. Once such groups gain the right to marriage, the term will come to represent a bond or bonds, weak or strong, between any number of individuals of any sex or sexual orientation. What then will be special about a heterosexual couple getting married? Their marriage will mean just as much as that between three men and two women. Some argue that allowing gay marriage would not lead to the legitimization of group marriages, saying that each scenario would be considered individually. However, altering the basic structure of the institution of marriage does provide a precedent for further modifications.

After all, the argument used by homosexuals is that they ought to have the right to marry whomever they want, male or female. If we legalize gay marriage, we will in effect be making the statement that everyone ought to have the right to marry whomever they want, regardless of their sexual preferences. We could obviously not then deny polygamists and polyamorists from having the right to enter into group marriages, for they are simply people of different sexual preferences. If we allow the institution of marriage to be so broadened and corrupted, not only will it have no longer have any meaning for heterosexual couples, it will lose its meaning for gay couples and everyone else that is married, an end result that benefits no one.

In addition, polyamory and polygamy will remove monogamy from the meaning of marriage. If people in a group marriage can be married and yet sleep with multiple people, it will make infidelity in traditional, heterosexual marriage seem more acceptable. With the already alarming level of divorces in this country, marriage means less today than it ever has, and we cannot afford to permit any further modifications of the institution of marriage that would further weaken the strength of its meaning. If we do, we could very well witness the effective end of meaningful marriage as we once knew it.

According to many nationwide polls, those opposing gay marriage outnumber those supporting it by a factor of two. Hence, there is no popular mandate for the legalization of gay marriage. The United States laws (as well as its constituent states laws) ought to reflect the opinions of the majority, especially in cases such as these where the outcomes affects such a great number of people. The legalization of gay marriage, in either an individual state or in the United States as a whole, would force those who are opposed to it to support it by association with their state or country. We ought to respect the majority of the United States citizens, who wish to preserve the current meaning of the term marriage because it defines and gives meaning to their relationships with their husbands and wives.

By asking for the right to gay marriage, homosexuals are forcing heterosexuals to equate their own relationships to those between two gay people, something that for those opposed to gay marriage would change how they view their own marriages. Many heterosexual couples feel that gay marriage degrades the quality and strength of meaning behind their own marriage, for it broadens the term to include a union looked down upon by many.

Gay marriage is obviously a widely-discussed, heated, and controversial topic of debate. However, the duty of a country is to protect the rights of its citizens. In the case of gay marriage, where the rights of two different groups are mutually exclusive, we must defer to the larger, majority group. If we continue to grant rights to minority groups that impinge upon those of majority groups, our country will be no longer be governed by anything even resembling democracy.
