Gay Marriage, an Issue of Equal Rights

by Christine Boehm
News Editor

More than three thousand gay and lesbian marriages have taken place in San Francisco this month. Most lives in the Bay Area havent changed. Families are going about their days as usual, college students are as moral as they were before, and society as a whole is no more broken than it was last February. No god has let loose his wrath on us.
Those against same-sex marriage argue that it will wreak horrible moral and practical damages on society. Empirically and logically, they are wrong.

Marriage, as it is regulated across the country, isn't as pristine as those who say gay marriage will hurt the sanctity of marriage envision it. Heterosexual couples, if they even choose to become husbands and wives, mistreat each other, get divorced, and don't give sufficient attention to their children.

Heterosexual couples have long since destroyed pristine marriage. While the millions of dysfunctional and failed marriages are sad, there are still plenty of healthy, happy, and intact marriages.

If neglect, abuse, and divorce in some marriages haven't hurt other marriages, then allowing committed life partners of the same sex the legal protection of marriage certainly won't harm anyone's heterosexual marriage.

Since same-sex marriage won't harm society any more than heterosexual marriage has, that leaves the moral argument against same-sex marriage.

It is admirable to live a moral life as one's religion or spirituality defines morality. However, it is widely recognized that religion ought not to dictate laws because laws based on the religions of some unfairly impose on the consciences of others.

It would be un-American and unconstitutional to have laws that proscribed belief in any gods other than the Christian God, even though many Christians think that those who don't believe in their God will burn in hell. When creating law, it doesnt matter what any one religion believes the consequence of a given action will be.

Laws that reflect religious values are allowed only so long as they have universal application regardless of religion. Murder is prohibited because it takes away an individual's dearest property (his life), not because God forbade it in the Ten Commandments.

Same-sex relationships may be abhorred by a number of religions, but religion does not and ought not to decide the rights granted or taken away by the government.

If one looks at civil marriage from a practical standpoint, it is a contract between two people to love and be faithful to each other for life. The government, in recognition of the special nature of this contract, codifies rights that are in accord with the intertwined lives that the contract makes.

No one is hurt when homosexual couples have the same rights as heterosexual couples to visit each other in the hospital, have their life commitment recognized, file taxes jointly, get death benefits, or any of the other myriad privileges of the married.

Legalizing same-sex marriage does make a difference. The lives of 6,000 homosexual Americans have already been enriched by marriage. The futures of countless more have been brightened by the prospect that they can have the same legal recognition as their heterosexual friends and families when they decide to devote the rest of their lives to someone.

Finally, while the majority of Americans arent homosexual, the legalization of same-sex marriage is good for all Americans because it affirms dedication to equal protection under the law of this country.
