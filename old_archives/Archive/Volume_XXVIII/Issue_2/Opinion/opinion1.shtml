Opinion
Our Democracy has been Usurped
by Alec Rawls
Senior Staff Writer

Imagine if the only people allowed to run for leadership of some arm of government were current and recent members of that arm of government. Would government still be accountable to the people? Would we still have a democracy?

This is not a hypothetical question. The California Sheriff's Association, one of the biggest spending lobbies in the state (they run the states biggest business: the jails) managed over a decade ago to buy a law banning their electoral opposition. No one who is not a current or recent member of the law enforcement establishment is allowed to run for Sheriff. Not that this law is all corruption. It is also ideology.

For the last fifteen years, electoral warfare has raged across the over gun rights, with civilians and law enforcement on opposite sides. Florida citizens were the first to rebel against the anti-gun views of law enforcement leaders, passing a "shall issue" law that stripped Florida sheriffs and police chiefs of the power to deny Concealed Carry Weapons permits. Since then, over thirty states have gone "shall issue." Americans are picking up law enforcement by the feet and shaking loose their right to bear arms.

One way to do that is to elect a "shall issue" sheriff. But California's anti-gun legislature protects its anti-gun law enforcement establishment from the challenge of civilian viewpoints simply by denying them access to the ballot. You have to put it all together to see what a mountain of violation this is.

Our entire system of limited government power and checks and balances is designed from the ground up to counter the inherent tendency of government to strive for tyrannical power.

The law enforcement establishment has exemplified this tendency, pursuing an unconstitutional monopoly on arms by denying the citizens their right to bear arms, and now they are also being exempted from civilian challenge at the ballot box. Somebody ought to sue!

Four years ago, that somebody was me. Precedent, I discovered, is perfectly clear. Ballot restrictions are constitutional only if the serve the purpose of facilitating accurate expression of the will of the electorate, as by keeping the ballot simple and uncluttered. The purpose cannot be to limit what The People are allowed to choose. Government, as the servant, can never be the master. Yet California voters are not allowed to choose the civilian opposition that has been sweeping the nation.

Imagine being a judge and getting the chance to investigate such a seemingly blatant attempt on the Constitution? Yet this Judge refuses to hear arguments, ruling summarily for the state. It is only logical after all. He can't strike down a law on constitutional grounds without studying the case thoroughly, and the two deputies holding the chained up man in the orange jumpsuit look like they both need to go to the bathroom. He isn't going to give me an hour so why give me a minute?

Now, four years later, I'm suing over the other half of the law. Not only are people from outside of government banned from the ballot, they are also banned from running as write-in candidates. I overlooked that provision the first time around, but now that I realize how incompetent the courts are, I realize it might be just the hook I need.

The ban on write-in candidates is ludicrously unconstitutional. There is no issue of complicating the ballot. Write-in candidates are not on the ballot. To elect a write in, a majority of the voters have to remember the name and write it in. That is a much clearer indication of the will of the voter than checking a name on the ballot, which could well be random. The only possible purpose of the restriction is to limit what the voters are allowed to choose. The people's representatives are caught red handed, crossing the line from servant to master.

Did they actually write a law that is so blatantly and egregiously unconstitutional that one of our harried judges might even bother to pay attention? Have the judges actually given the legislators enough rope to hang themselves? If I sue again, and win the slam-dunk on the write-in restriction, might the judge notice that the next clause in the same sentence--the ballot restriction--violates the same constitutional principles?

So here I am, writing briefs again, filling out summonses, working to gain a place on the ballot for what is far and away the most effective crime-reducing tool in a sheriff's repertoire. The dramatic crime reducing effect of gun rights is by now one of the best-documented facts in social science. Not knowing who is armed, the criminals are deterred from attacking anyone. Thus the anti-gun position is revealed to be what one might call a pure illiberalism.

To believe in liberty is to be willing to pay a price for it. In contrast, the anti-gunners are willing to pay a high price in order to get rid of this liberty, gladly forgoing the very substantial crime reducing benefits of the right to bear arms. It is the liberty itself that they hate. They don't care if enforcing gun rights would make us safer, they still don't think people should be allowed to have guns (never mind carry them). The gall of such people, calling themselves liberal.

From there it is no jump at all to call oneself a Democrat while banning one's electoral opposition. Luckily, most people actually do believe in liberty and democracy and the Constitution, and even the enemies of liberty can come around. It just hasn't penetrated their skulls yet. It's like these two-minute judges. A snap judgment seems to make more sense than working through the logic.

Believers in liberty just need to keep pressing the case, appealing, reaching for light switches, storming the tower, so cross your fingers, and on the first Tuesday of March, vote for your gun rights: write in Rawls for Sheriff of Santa Clara County.
