Opinion
Condi, Afghanistan, and North Korea
by Alex Robbins
Senior Editor

Foreign policy is not usually an apposite topic of conversation for a Review column, but the aftermath of September 11th has certainly made the rest of the world seem closer to ours as we bike across Quad at midday. This same Quad will in a few months see another intersection of Stanford and the issues of the rest of the world: Condoleeza Rice will give the 2002 Commencement Speech, and if I can somehow swing it with housing, I'll be there watching.

If some folks have their way, I'll have plenty of good, open seats to choose from as well: there's talk of a walk-out protesting... something. The talk I've heard hasn't yet decided whether Condi is repugnant because of who she is or because of what she's done--as if the would-be protesters could even come up with a believable list of sins. In any event, though, let's just speculate that her primary crime is being so closely associated to the Bush administration and with the war in Afghanistan. Using that as a starting off point, let's try and see why Condi deserves not just our respectful attention, but, if Stanford students are worthy of their status, a veritable hero's welcome.

Since Sept. 11 we have finally been pushed into acting as we ought always to act: with strength and resilience. Thomas Friedman, never known as a right-wing lunatic or hawk, recently summed up this sentiment in an op-ed piece: he wrote that now, in the wake of our war in Afghanistan, America's enemies are afraid of her again. He lauded the Bush administration's rhetorical flourishes, particularly the "axis of evil" reference which so galls Stanford leftists. The short and simple truth is that the regimes of North Korea, Iraq, and Iran really are some of the most repulsive and oppressive regimes in the world. They--North Korea and Kim Jung Il in particular--really are evil, if that word has any meaning at all.

Does using accurate language mean we're warmongers? I suppose it could. It could also mean that we've simply stopped apologizing for the most murderous regimes still out there, and admitted that we see them for what they are. Yes, any government whose "Dear Leader" has ordered the bombings of airlines and diplomatic summits in Rangoon, the kidnapping of various and sundry South Koreans (such as one couple whom he wanted for his nascent North Korean film industry), and has caused a famine whose death tally soars into the millions--any such government can be reasonably described as "evil."

If the lives of North Koreans and Kurds were half as important to the United States government as those lost in the World Trade Center, perhaps Kim Jong Il would have even more reason to fear. But a government's first duty must be to its own people, and the North Koreans still have so much artillery pointed at Seoul that we would spark a military and civilian bloodbath if we tried to remove Kim Jong Il by force.

Still, America's successful humanitarian missions have only begun in the past with a more immediate and pressing threat. Thousands of Soviet nuclear missiles pointed at the continental United States made communist advances in China and Korea seem far less remote. In the end, although we were powerless to stop a communist revolution in China which eventually claimed upwards of 30 million lives, we did halt Kim Jong Il's father at the 38th parallel, and today South Korea is a thriving, prosperous, and free nation, while starving peasants to the North try to eat grass to survive, avoid concentration camps, and due to malnutrition tend on average to be about a fourth of a foot shorter than their erstwhile kin to the South.

Perhaps our war against terrorism will have the same galvanizing effect. Perhaps, in protecting the skies over New York, the United States will once again see past its occasional parochialism and back up its physical might with the resolve to effect change. Maybe we won't see any more instances in which the country turns and flees "like a dog with its tail between its legs" (to quote Mr. Bin Laden), after Somali warlords ravage a Ranger Battalion.

Perhaps. I'd like to be so optimistic, since it saddens me to see my country's tendency to turn overly inwards and away from the fact that most humans in the world, while generally better off that twenty years or thirty years ago, are suffering under brutal and tyrannical regimes whose rulers can't seem to keep their collective jackboot off the necks of the ruled.

So I applaud Ms. Rice, not only for her success, not only for her leadership within the Bush administration, and not only for her hand in winning the war, but for her role in waking this country up from its slumber, much to the chagrin of isolationists everywhere, but especially those on the left. Some governments are evil. Some governments, if we had our way, would be destroyed, just as I have a moral duty to kill a rapist if necessary to defend one of my fellow human beings.

The United States is not powerful enough to solve all problems everywhere. We cannot yet eradicate the rape, torture, and murder of a people by their own government--that state which sometimes seems the norm in the world. But we can accomplish some good, and the first step is to clearly identify the nature of our enemies. The truth is the truth, as Camus wrote, and the more America speaks it the closer she is to fulfilling her duty to both the world and herself. By identifying the "axis of evil", Bush has shown that his advisors are clearly leading him in the right direction.

I'm proud of the effort with which Condoleeza Rice is associated, and I hope that Americans, including Stanford students, will be proud to support their valiant endeavor to rid the world of some its worst forms of evil and corruption.

Alex Robbins is a sophomore who isn't sure if he'd prefer that Condi run the country or the University.
