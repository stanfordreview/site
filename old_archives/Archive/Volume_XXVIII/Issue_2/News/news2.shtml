Group Proposes Cultural Awareness Associates
by Ryan Wisnesky
Staff Writer

In addition to the typical HPAC and PHE's, next years' dorms may have another staff position: a cultural awareness associate. This position, set forth in a proposal by LEAD (Leadership, Education, Activism, Diversity), would be a staff position that helps students discover the diversity related resources on campus. The proposal is aimed specifically at freshmen dorms, where students are "not typically aware of the cultural and ethnic resources on campus."

Nadiya Figueroa is one of the principal architects of the plan: "Stanford has a high level of diversity compared to other colleges of its caliber - but are we really benefiting from this diversity? Is there enough meaningful exchange?" asked Nadia.

The cultural awareness associate would foster cultural exchange and help students discover or forge an identity for him or herself--"Everyone has an identity. How do you develop it?" she questions.

The presence of a cultural awareness associate would give students the opportunity to discover the resources available for different ethnicities and cultures across campus. But rather than trying to bring more resources into the dorm, the cultural awareness associate would direct students to resources around the campus. In essence, the position would be very similar to the current cultural positions in ethnic theme houses. "One of the important things is that we aren't trying to reinvent the wheel," said Donielle Newell, another architect of the plan. "This person would be a resource, a confidant."

In addition to serving as a nexus for cultural events and opportunities, the cultural awareness associate would help during freshman programs like Crossing the Line. Crossing the Line is a dorm program for freshman in which students step across a line in the room to respond yes to questions about who they are, e.g., "are you the first in your family to go to college". This program helps each student understand the ethnic, cultural, and socio-economic status of each member of the dorm in a non-judgmental environment. As a member of the staff, the cultural awareness associate would "help make the staff more sensitive" during potentially controversial or uncomfortable moments. But the position would also be more than a helper position; the ultimate goal would be supplementing programs like Faces, where speakers of different ethnicities, cultures, and socio-economic backgrounds speak at Memorial Auditorium during orientation about their unique viewpoints about campus life.

The problem, according to Nadiya, is that after programs like Faces, freshman receive no further dialogue. "A freshman does Crossing the Line, and then often times there is no sustained follow-up," she said.

Although Stanford offers many panels and events about diversity, the impact of these programs is limited because they cannot come into the dorm. Its creators hope the position of cultural awareness associate will help create a beneficial environment. Stanford may be a statistically diverse school, but the environment isn't always reflective of such. "Diversity isn't just about figures," comments Nadiya.

If implemented, the plan would require the support of Residential Education and the ASSU. The ASSU itself is firmly behind the program [Editor's note: Just like the ASSU is firmly behind every liberal-sounding program this side of the Atlantic...].

"The ASSU Community Team (ACT) Diversity has stated interest in helping LEAD get the program implemented," said the ASSU President. "[We will] help support the LEAD group in institutionalizing a Cultural Awareness Associate in every dorm on campus," he continued, in the ASSU State of the Association speech.

The program may be supported by the ASSU and LEAD, but reactions among current freshman are mixed. Some students wondered about the value of the office.

"Why are they so interested in cultural awareness?" asked freshman Chris Bravman.

Other students expressed concern that something may have triggered the need for this new office. "We don't have any problems here. Have issues concerning culture been raised?" asked freshman Peter Lubell-Doughtie .

Others feel that the office might encourage cultural isolation. "I happen to be a foreign student from Mombasa, an island town in Kenya which is a melting pot of diverse cultures. Even though we have our share of social difficulties, I think that the excessive emphasis placed on singling out separate cultural elements in a society is what eventually leads to inter-cultural friction, which strikes me as being a problem here in the US, " said Babar Rana.

Still, there is some support among freshmen. "It sounds cool, but would we really want another staff member?" asked freshman Whalen Rozelle.

In addition to the RA's, most freshman dorms have and HPAC, a PHE, and a RT. In a small dorm, another staff position may push the staff / student ratio above 10%. The ramifications of another staff position have yet to be worked out, but the proposal is moving along.

Regardless of the success or failure of the position, students agree that the proposal is another concrete instance of Stanford's unremitting pursuit of a nirvana-like state of diversity.
