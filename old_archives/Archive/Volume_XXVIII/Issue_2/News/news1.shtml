Wilbur Dining Disorganized and Overpriced
by Harrison Osaki
Staff Writer

Wilbur Dining's new A La Carte point system was modeled after the plan in Manzanita Dining. All other dining halls are on the all-you-can-eat system.
Since Winter Quarter at Stanford began, Wilbur Dining Hall has changed. The wake of this change has brought alternating praise and disgruntlement; although Dining Services says that the new "A la Carte" meal plan allows students to ". . . .exercise 'choice'", and to ". . . .choose what food items and the amount of food they wish to purchase", in reality most students find themselves hampered by a system which they feel overcharges them for basic essentials.

According to Dining Services, the A la Carte plan was designed "in response to student requests", for greater eating flexibility. The Wilbur dining plan, like the Manzanita Hall plan, offers students choice of "Lite", "Regular", or "Premium" dining plans which respectively offer approximately 12 to 14 points per day for students to purchase food, with one point roughly corresponding to one dollar. The A la Carte plan, however, does not strictly observe this exchange rate; twenty-three percent of the cost of the dining plan pays for general "overhead cost", and is not returned directly to the student. This general category includes expenses for general maintenance and repair, debt service, and labor and insurance for all dining halls and the central office, and other costs which relate to all dining halls. Students on point plans are thus implicitly encouraged to spend as little on possible on the meal plan itself and to use Cardinal Dollars or standard money, both of which are not penalized by the percentage deduction, as needed. Dining Services did not directly respond when questioned about this system, and did not explain why the A la Carte plans are penalized while the traditional plans are not.

A hamburger and fries at Wilbur Dining cost $3.95; a 32-ounce cup of juice, $1.95. These two purchases could run from as much as half of a student's daily point allowance. Many students particularly worry about the drink pricing, such as Stanford student Cheryl Padua, who asked "When are they going to fix the Snapple prices? Two dollars for a drink?" In response to Review questions, Stanford Dining Services directly responded only that they are "continuously monitoring our prices, and adjusting them accordingly".

One manager at Wilbur listed several factors affecting pricing. Dining halls such as Wilbur often find difficulty in purchasing in bulk, despite a daily diner turnover rate of over two thousand students; the manager found that he could "go to an alternate distributor and find lower prices than if [he] bought directly from the source". This is apparently due to Dining Service's rigid commitment to satisfy every single student possible and the unpredictability of student desires; however, Dining Service also says that they offer "high quality 'branded' products", which are significantly more expensive even in bulk than their generic counterparts. Despite the supposed higher degree of choice given by A la Carte meal plans, cheaper sodas and juices are not offered in dining halls.

According to one manager, dining hall employees are also not permitted to significantly pursue students suspected of theft. One staff member gave the example of a student who took a pizza and ate it while in the purchasing hall; the student claimed that he had paid for the food at the register on the far end of the hall, and despite the fact that the staff member had watched the student perform the theft he was unable to pursue the matter. These self-maintained restrictions on who can and cannot reduce dining hall crime increase the overall cost of dining. Dining Hall cashiers also sometimes mischarge students; Gina Schiel, a Stanford freshman, noted that while paying for several yogurts she noticed that the cashier had "charged her for soup, when [she] obviously didn't have any food on her tray." Many other students have reported that workers are surly and inflexible in customizing orders, particularly for entree combinations.

The cost of food on the A la Carte plans is supposed to correlate with the amount of money students have available, so that students are not unduly burdened; however, for the purposes of pricing students are assumed to have twenty-one dollars, or points, to spend per day. One manager, when asked by a student at a general forum, "How am I supposed to buy a healthy meal on fourteen points a day?" was shocked that students had from seven to nine fewer points than he had been informed they had. Both A la Carte residence dining halls use similar pricing structures. This disparity between assumed and actual point values, along with the percentage deduction, both contribute to the general sentiment that students on the A la Carte plan receive much less food.

Stanford Dining Services details out three major culinary advantages of the points plan over traditional plans: a greater range of available foods (including sushi and bottled drinks), superior food quality, and lower amounts of wasted meals. According to Susi Nelson of Stanford Dining Services, students on the A la Carte plan have ". . . .a level of flexibility that allows students the freedom to eat in any of the dining halls"; realistically, however, this is not usually an option. Dinner at a traditional rate, for example, would cost nine points the vast majority of the point reserves for any given day, of any student on a point plan. Students who simply wish a greater quantity of food, and who do not feel that the quality of their A la Carte food is significantly superior to other halls, are barred from alternatives.
