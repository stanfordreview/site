Condoleezza Rice to Speak at Graduation
by Matt Barrett
Staff Writer

A substantial minority of Stanford students have begun to denounce the selection of Condoleeza Rice as commencement speaker. Their negative feelings are not unprecedented; upon hearing the news, last winter, that HP boss Carly Fiorina had been picked as the speaker for the class of 2001, disgruntled seniors initiated a wave of protests to decry the administration's choice. Not to be outdone, many of this year's seniors, unhappy with the selection of the National Security Adviser, have continued the tradition of antipathy.

After The Stanford Daily announced on February 11 that Rice had been designated to speak at graduation this June, it was struck with a barrage of letters from angry students. The first letter was from senior Josh Zide, who called for students to protest the choice of Rice and even for fellow seniors to protest, albeit quietly and peacefully, during the commencement ceremony itself. Zide opined that Universities ought to serve as "government watchdogs" and that inviting a government official to speak undermines that role, but his letter was largely a declaration of his own political views. "I am personally opposed to the actions in Afghanistan," he wrote, and "Racial profiling should not be tolerated."

Zide's letter catalyzed a stream of commentary in the letters section of the Daily, both pro and anti -- Rice. One group of students objects to the idea of politics of any kind behind the podium at graduation. "The bottom line is that we question the appropriateness of selecting a highly political figure." These students suggested that Condoleeza Rice could be divisive, should she choose to incorporate her political agenda into her commencement address.

Other students feel it is unfair to choose a figure whose specific politics many graduating seniors disdain, since they don't have much of a choice in attending commencement. "We are faced with two unpleasant alternatives: being hurt and offended at our own commencement, or missing one of the biggest milestones in our lives."

Of course, not everyone shares Zide's sentiments. Many students feel not only that Condoleeza Rice is an excellent choice, but that the nature of the enmity directed towards her has been inappropriate. One group of students wrote, "Let us welcome the return of one of the Stanford community's most distinguished speakers," and, in reference to the complaint that political figures should never be invited to commencement, "she was invited for her prominence, not her politics. . . .politicizing the event by protesting is unfair." Some students added that it was rude to protest Rice in her academic homeland.

A Daily editorial welcomed the dialogue that Zide and others have established, but suggested that some complaints have gone too far. "The mixed responses to Rice's selection have been positive insofar as they have facilitated thoughtful debate about her career as national security adviser. But to suggest that her selection was inappropriate merely because her views do not represent those of every individual in the senior class is closed-minded and unrealistic."

Other students have explicitly praised the selection of Rice. Serge Kassardjian, one of the senior class presidents responsible for nominating Rice, was quoted in the Daily as saying, "The events of [Sept. 11] will forever frame our senior year. We wanted a speaker who would be able to talk about that day with authority and perspective."

Junior Patrick Dempsey echoed Kassardjian's thoughts and applauded the senior class presidents for their nomination. "Considering the events of the last several months, I can think of no more relevant nor more powerful speaker to address this year's graduating class."

A separate category of students find the protests unreasonable, but nonetheless would prefer a different speaker. Junior Michael Kives noted, "The protests have been misguided," but he added that he thinks Stanford could have a more prominent graduation speaker. Among the contingency agreeing with Kives are the many students who were unsatisfied with Fiorina last year.

Throughout the rest of the campus, opinion is varied. Very few student organizations have declared official positions on the matter. Representatives of the BSU and the Women's Center did not respond to an inquiry concerning their members' feelings on Rice.
