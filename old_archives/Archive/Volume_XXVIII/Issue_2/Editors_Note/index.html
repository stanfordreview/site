Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Henry Towsner, Editor-in-chief
We have a theme this issue. It wasn't intentional, but I noticed it as the articles started coming in. The underlying question to keep in mind while you are reading is: what personal accommodations does Stanford owe each individual student?

Consider the dilemma of Dining Services (no, not "how much can we over charge the students before they notice," a different one). Many students have various dietary needs, some more pressing than others. Coping with them is an expensive process; does everyone have to cover the cost for a few people? Some people have allergies. Others are Muslims or Jews with specific dietary codes. Others are vegetarians or vegans. Some people dislike food with melted cheese. Is Stanford obligated to provide for some of them? All of them? Does the fact that students are obligated to buy meal plans change anything?

These are a few points to think about as you read Harrison Osaki's article on the perpetually overpriced Dining Services. Speaking of pernicious, over-funded Stanford bureaucracies, one group of students has proposed that the department of Residential Education create yet another new staff position, the "Cultural Awareness Associate." The position would promote dialog within dorms about ethnic and cultural issues, and be able to direct students to cultural resources on campus. Obviously the position is unnecessary, since the Review's distributors already do this.

Unfortunately, the group appears to mean "dialogue" in the traditional Stanford sense, which is to say, liberal monologue. Nonetheless, if you can stomach it, read Ryan Wisnesky's article for more on this particular proposal.

Perhaps the biggest news story this issue, as reported by Matthew Barrett, is that former Provost and current National Security Advisor Condol-eezza Rice will return to Stanford this Spring as the commencement speaker. Needless to say, students are celebrating at the thought of having someone prominent, successful, scholarly, and apropos speaking. Well, actually they're protesting her political positions. While a few people have limited their suggestions to calling for a symbolic protest at commencement, many more appear to be leaning towards more vocal protests, including a number of demands that she be withdrawn as commencement speaker. The reasons given? Well, some students disagree with her politically. And since every student obviously has the right to a commencement speaker who agrees with them, Condi's got to go.

Incidentally, if you have doubts about Ms. Rice's stature, read Alex Robbins article for a reminder about just how important (and justified) fighting the "axis of evil" is.

Alec Rawls has also written about accommodating differing political views. He's running for Santa Clara Sheriff again, and, once again, the county is trying to keep dissenting voices off the ballot. California law states that only professional law enforcement officers can run, something comparable to prohibiting non-lawyers from running for Congress. Never mind the popular will, never mind the basic principles of a free election. California says only people who are already law enforcement officers can lead law enforcement officers, a policy guaranteed to encourage corruption.

Back on campus, Joe Lonsdale offers a counter to the latest batch of loud campus activists: the Stanford Labor Action Coalition (or Committee, or Council, or something like that). Is it Stanford's obligation to pay workers a so-called "living wage," even if that is more than the market compels? Does Stanford have to pay workers enough to support children, or are the workers responsible for not having children until they can support them?

Finally, when you're done with all that, return once more to Stanford's commencement speaker. Last year the CEO of Hewlett-Packard wasn't good enough for Stanford students, this year the National Security Advisory. On the back page, you can find out that, truly, no one can satisfy the purile demands of Stanford's students.
