Stanford Fails Cultural Literacy Quiz
Five years ago The Stanford Review undertook an investigation of Stanford students' "cultural literacy," with an aim towards evaluating the Area One program at the time, CIV. CIV--"Cultures, Ideas, and Values"--had recently replaced the old "Western Civilization" requirement, and so we were interested in seeing how well Stanford students performed on a quiz designed to test a basic Western cultural literacy. We defined such cultural literacy as the sort of knowledge about history and current events that might come up in any given discussion: the sorts of things that educated Americans are generally expected to know by their peers and employers. ....Full story in Front Page.....by Alex Robbins

Anti-Condoleezza Rice Students Plan Protest
About twenty Stanford students gathered in Kimball lounge on Tuesday, May 21st. These students were upset at Stanford's choice of Condoleezza Rice as this year's commencement speaker. They gathered in order to plan a protest during her speech to the graduating class. The assembly was originally convoked by the Redwood Action Team at Stanford (RATS), an anti-globalization activist group, as a general forum for Stanford students. Most of the twenty attendees, however, were from RATS. ....Full story in Front Page.....by Sam Shapero

The American Flag is 'a Symbol of this Nation's Creation and Composition'
We've all seen the American flag streaming in the wind outside the Bing Wing, or remember it dancing to middle-school marching bands at football games. I dare say that as close as we live to Berkeley, we've seen our fair share of flag burning, too. It's a heated topic that has many implications -- is flag burning an acceptable way in which to utilize free speech? Or are the stars and stripes of this nation such an important symbol that they need stand above petty politics; is the flag of the United States of America so vitally connected to our identity as a great nation that desecrating it makes one a traitor the country and its basic values? ....Full story in Opinion.....by Will Hudson

Grading at Stanford (Is All Messed Up)
For many Stanford students, high school was a difficult four-year trek of working to get good grades. In my dorm room here at Toyon, two out of three residents were Valedictorian of their school; this is relatively common here. As we put in late nights and invested tireless effort to reap future rewards, the light at the end of the tunnel for many of us was the myth of college's grade-less academic bliss. We believed that once we got here, it would be all about learning, about exploring our minds and the world around us; grades, we thought, would be behind us. While many professors appear to believe in this doctrine, most Stanford students soon come to realize that grades are still extremely important. They're needed for higher education, intra-Stanford programs, and you're even supposed to put your GPA on your resume. The trouble is, here at The Farm, everyone seems to have a different opinion on just how important grades are, and how to give them out. ....Full story in Opinion.....by Joe Lonsdale
