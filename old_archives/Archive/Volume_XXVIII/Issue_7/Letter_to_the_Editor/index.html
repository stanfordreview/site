Letter to the Editor
I would like to respond to Joe Londsdale's [sic] Back Page article in the last issue of the Review, in which he advocates oil drilling in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge. Simply put, Londsdale's [sic] portrayal of Arctic drilling is misleading, incomplete, and at times flat-out wrong. When all the real facts are considered, it becomes obvious that ANWR drilling is a terrible policy choice.

To begin with, let me refute some of the points Londsdale[sic] makes in his article. First off, he claims that only 2,000 acres would be developed, out of a total acreage of 18 million. However, this figure is highly misleading. Nothing in the drilling proposal says the 2,000 acres must be concentrated in one place. Indeed, given the nature of oil deposits in ANWR, drilling would have to be spread out over a vast territory. The roads and pipelines connecting far-flung oil derricks would severely fragment wildlife habitat and mar the ecology of a huge area, even if they actually "cover" only a small amount of land. Just to put the numbers in perspective: currently, the Alaskan North Slope oil fields technically cover only 12,000 acres, but their web of roads and pipelines sprawls over a 640,000 acre area.

Furthermore, Londsdale [sic] waxes eloquent on the rights of Northern Alaskans, saying "the local people are for the drilling. . . .Why are you not letting them do this on their own land?" One problem, Joe-- ANWR is not "their" land at all. It is a national wildlife refuge, and as such it belongs equally to every citizen of the United States. Nationwide public opinion polls consistently show that most Americans oppose drilling.

So much for Londsdale's [sic] points. I won't list all the arguments against ANWR drilling, for they're too numerous for a brief letter. But just to touch on two:

1) Over 95% of Alaska's North Slope is already open to drilling. It it too much to ask that we stringently protect that remaining five percent?

2) The amount of oil projected to come from ANWR in a proverbial drop in the bucket when it comes to total U.SD. energy needs. Instituting common sense fuel-efficiency measures, such as closing the absurd loophole allowing SUV's to burn so much more gas than regular cars, would save far more oil than could ever be extracted from ANWR.

In short, Londsdale [sic] gives a highly distorted and incomplete view of ANWR drilling. He claims to value rational thought over emotional appeals, but rational thought demands that we keep ANWR off-limits to oil drilling.

-- Josh Sohn
Junior, Political Science
