Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
by Henry Towsner
Editor-in-chief

Henry Towsner, Editor-in-Chief
Just about every volume, people at the Review discuss changing the term of an Editor-in-Chief from one and a half quarters to two quarters. And in some ways, it would make sense to run longer: I feel like I've only just gotten the hang of making sure we have enough articles to fill an issue without having extras we have to cut. The production staff has gotten layout down to a science, and I've gotten the knack of finding that last story to fill an issue when I'm out of ideas.

In other ways, half a year is more than enough. The writers can attest to my vicious, two a.m. e-mails demanding articles which haven't been turned in. At the beginning of my term, I had a friendly dinner with the President of the Stanford Progressive, yet since then I've written a letter to the Stanford Daily calling him a liar at least three times, while he's publicly hinted at bringing a case against me through Judicial Affairs. And, most importantly, every story on the list I made at the beginning of my term has been written. So perhaps its just as well that I'm passing the paper on to the capable hands of our current Opinions Editor, Joe Lonsdale.

Looking over the list of stories I thought would be interesting, I've realized that many of them haven't been as important as I'd expected. Some turned out to be less interesting than I'd thought, while others were pushed aside by breaking stories. In the first issue, we investigated a Stanford supported event which barred participation from students of the "wrong" race. Then Stanford turned down a potential football coach because he had the "wrong" religion. (Of course, the people involved have different twists on these stories, but the details are in our previous issues).

This issue does, however, tie up some of the loose ends. First, foremost, and at long last, we have a new Western Culture Quiz. You can read Alex Robbins' story for the details, but it suffices to say Stanford's new curriculum is only a small improvement over its old one. Condoleeza Rice's upcoming commencement speech continues to rankle with Stanford's activists, who have decided that she is, personally, to blame for each and every thing the Bush administration has done which they disagree with (that is, each and every thing the Bush administration has done). Sam Shapero boldly infiltrated one of their meetings, and reports on their plans.

Meanwhile, our writers offer opinions on a variety of issues. Nels Hansen challenges one of the latest multicultural trends, the criticism of inter-racial dating for its oppressive power dynamics (it's hard to believe, but there really are people on the left getting close to advocating the return of anti-miscengenation laws...). Of course, as Nels points out, if we follow their logic through, dating in general will just have to go.

Joe Lonsdale reveals a recent, but secretive, Stanford study revealing just how bad grade inflation has gotten, Will Hudson defends the flag, Kathy Hart attacks Stanford's bizzare refusal to allow men and women select housing jointly, and Alec Rawls accuses the pro-choice movement of really being against choice but for abortion.

If you're already convinced of the eminently sensible claims being made by our writers, you can skip to the entertainment section, where Hilary Connell and Ming Zhu report on the Stanford dance scence.

The paper is finished, the articles edited, the new Editor picked, and now, my last Editor's Note is written. Wish our graduating staff luck as the enter the real world, and congratulate those entering graduate school for continuing to avoid it. And have a nice summer.
