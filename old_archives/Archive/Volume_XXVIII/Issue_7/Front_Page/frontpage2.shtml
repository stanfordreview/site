Front Page
Anti-Condoleezza Rice Students Plan Protest
by Sam Shapero
Staff Writer

National Security Advisor Condoleezza Rice will speak to Seniors at Graduation on June 16th
About twenty Stanford students gathered in Kimball lounge on Tuesday, May 21st. These students were upset at Stanford's choice of Condoleezza Rice as this year's commencement speaker. They gathered in order to plan a protest during her speech to the graduating class. The assembly was originally convoked by the Redwood Action Team at Stanford (RATS), an anti-globalization activist group, as a general forum for Stanford students. Most of the twenty attendees, however, were from RATS.

The criticisms of Ms. Rice largely stemmed from her position as former provost of Stanford from 1993 to 1999. Many students also criticized her former position on Chevron's board of director and her current position as National Security Advisor to President Bush. In fact there has been a small but consistent outcry of the decision to let Ms. Rice speak ever since it was first announced in February.

The senior class presidents nominated Ms. Rice along with twenty-four other candidates, and she was eventually chosen by Stanford's Board of Trustees as the best speaker. Stanford's President Hennesy, who is on the Board of Trustees, praised the choice of Ms. Rice. "This is an inspired recommendation and it gives me great pleasure to accept it," Mr. Hennessy said. "Professor Rice is not only someone who has a central place on the world stage, she has a long-standing relationship with the University and a deep loyalty to its students, faculty and staff."

The students in RATS, however, did not think that this decision represented "Stanford as a whole." One student at the meeting, giving only Forrest as his name, said he was "concerned about who the university is presenting as someone they consider inspiring."

As the decision of a commencement speaker is not open for appeal, Joshua Zide, a member of RATS, decided to have a meeting to organize a protest during Ms. Rice's speech. Although he asked students to email him items for the meeting's agenda, he received none. Therefore, when the meeting began at 10:00 last Tuesday, there was no agenda.

At the beginning of the meeting, several students listed reasons they thought Ms. Rice was unqualified as commencement speaker. One Stanford Senior, who only gave his first name (as did all the students in attendance) Matt, blamed Ms. Rice for "failure to act on information regarding September 11th." Mr. Zide gave four reasons he disliked Ms. Rice, including her "foreign policy in Afghanistan, denial of civil liberties, environmental policy, and actions as provost." Several minutes into the meeting, a pair of African-American students spoke up, voicing concerns that the planned protest might disrupt the graduation ceremony. Melanie and Tiffany, while expressing that they were "by no means fans of Condoleezza Rice" whom Melanie thought had "sold out her community," they were afraid that the protests might ruin a very special occasion for them.

Melanie questioned "what the group was trying to achieve. I don't want this to disrupt my graduation ceremony," she said. "If this is about education [of the student body] we should start now. A lot of us are the first in our family to graduate. . . . . to some of us, this is as good as it gets: it's not just [for] me, it's not just [for] my family; I have a whole community behind me."

The other students agreed that they should not have a disruptive protest during Ms. Rice's speech. They set about deciding other ways to protest the speech. They settled upon several propositions to demonstrate their discontent to the university. First they planned to have an open-microphone rally on June 6th, where students would be invited to express their opinions about Ms. Rice. They also formulated several plans to occur during Ms. Rice's speech, most notably a plan to turn their backs while Ms. Rice was speaking. Someone almost immediately brought up that they would need to stand up for this protest to have any visible effect, and this might upset some audience members.

"Turning around might really piss some people off," said a student named Amber. "I don't want to have to stare at some stupid person yelling at me." The students thereafter decided to move to a different section and then turn their backs around. Mr. Zide hoped that they might get one hundred students to join him, and perhaps even some professors. Several students mentioned contacts in the biology department, and mentioned the Professor John Berger might be willing to turn his back during Ms. Rice's speech.

The students also hoped to distribute armbands and pamphlets during the graduation ceremony. Initial plans to have white armbands symbolizing peace were dropped after one student suggested that the color might be interpreted as a criticism of Ms. Rice's race.

The pamphlet provoked further discussion: how would it be distributed, and what information would it contain? In fact, as the meeting was getting ready to end, it became clear that most of the students there did not know what Ms. Rice had done as provost that merited protesting, including several of the seniors who attended Stanford while she was still in office. The official grievances against Ms. Rice could only be found on a petition at RATS' web-site. They included "signing an agreement with the Reserve Officers Training Corps authorizing the use of Stanford facilities and obtaining credit for classes on campus . . . . supporting the proposal of a Comparative Studies on Race and Ethnicity department to counter demands for Chicano studies and Asian American studies," and firing "Cecilia Burciaga, the highest ranking Chicana at Stanford."

The petition condemned Ms. Rice of presiding over murder while a member of Chevron's board of director. The RATS petition also criticized her for working with the Bush in administration in designating "Iran, Iraq, and North Korea the 'Axis of Evil,'" expanding the war on terrorism, and supporting "oil drilling in the Artic National Wildlife Refuge." It claimed on behalf of the entire Student body that these actions represented "values that are deeply offensive to a large portion of the University community." The petition never elaborated on what these values were or how they might be offensive.
