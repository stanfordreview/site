Front Page
Stanford Fails Cultural Literacy Quiz
by Alex Robbins
Senior Editor

View larger image
Five years ago The Stanford Review undertook an investigation of Stanford students' "cultural literacy," with an aim towards evaluating the Area One program at the time, CIV. CIV--"Cultures, Ideas, and Values"--had recently replaced the old "Western Civilization" requirement, and so we were interested in seeing how well Stanford students performed on a quiz designed to test a basic Western cultural literacy. We defined such cultural literacy as the sort of knowledge about history and current events that might come up in any given discussion: the sorts of things that educated Americans are generally expected to know by their peers and employers.

Q1) Who is the current Prime Minister of England? (Tony Blair)
Q2) Who wrote "The Wealth of Nations" (Adam Smith)
Q3) Which came first, the Renaissance or the Enlightenment? (Renaissance)
Q4) Which 16th century scholar first proposed that the earth revolves around the sun? (Copernicus)
Q5) Name ONE inventor of the printing press? (Gutenberg)
Q6) What was the exact date of the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor? (December 7, 1941)
Q7) Which came first, the American or the French Revolution? (American)
Q8) Who was the first leader of the Soviet Union? (Lenin)
The results were disappointing. The only question that had a correct response rate of over 50% was the one asking for one of two answers, which would mean that anyone guessing would already have a 50% chance of being right.

Last month the Review gave Stanford students the quiz again, though adding two new questions. The respondents were selected randomly and contacted by telephone. The results, while still dismal by some standards, do indicate that the new Area One Requirement, IHUM, might be doing a better job than its predecessor at helping make students culturally literate.

The results can be seen in the charts corresponding to this article. As you can see, there were two noticeable trends. First, freshmen had the lowest scores, while other undergraduate classes were approximately equal. Second, the students quizzed last month, on average, scored higher than the students quizzed five years ago in all questions but one. Moreover, one particular wrong answer to this question--Galileo--might arguably indicate a superior response to any other incorrect response, since Galileo is, after all, famous for his outspoken advocacy of Copernicus' principle that the earth revolves around the sun. 64% of the students quizzed did in fact give this answer (compared to the almost 80% who got the question wrong), though unfortunately we do not have information on the percentage of students who gave this answer in 1997.

The fact that average scores increased in the last five years, coupled with the fact that average scores increase after freshman year, indicate that perhaps IHUM is teaching some amount of cultural literacy, and that perhaps it is doing so more effectively than its predecessor CIV. Nonetheless, there are other potential causes for these two trends, besides mere statistical anomalies caused by the sampling size or non-random data collection. Any improvement in the general educational atmosphere at Stanford--that is, the sort of various and sundry knowledge students pick up from their history classes, their seminars, or even their dorm-mates--would also account for the above-mentioned trends.

Another question is the extent to which "cultural literacy" and "western cultural literacy" are the same in this country. The Soviet Union, for example, is not generally considered part of the western world, yet the Review staff still felt that well-educated people--especially Americans--ought to know who Lenin was. Some might also try to excuse poor performance on any such test by arguing that "cultural literacy" in the United States increasingly includes non-western elements: in that case, it would also be interesting to see how well students answer questions about Deng Xiaoping, the kingdom of Mali, or the Mayans.
