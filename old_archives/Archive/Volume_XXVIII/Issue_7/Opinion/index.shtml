The American Flag is 'a Symbol of this Nation's Creation and Composition'
We've all seen the American flag streaming in the wind outside the Bing Wing, or remember it dancing to middle-school marching bands at football games. I dare say that as close as we live to Berkeley, we've seen our fair share of flag burning, too. It's a heated topic that has many implications -- is flag burning an acceptable way in which to utilize free speech? Or are the stars and stripes of this nation such an important symbol that they need stand above petty politics; is the flag of the United States of America so vitally connected to our identity as a great nation that desecrating it makes one a traitor the country and its basic values? ....Full story.....by Will Hudson

Trusting in Truth: Religion and Moral Logic
For those who already regard religion with a great deal of skepticism and distrust, the perverted religiosity of the Islamist terrorists only strengthens their anti-religious convictions. Witness the almost panicky hatred that the Democrats feel for Attorney General John Ashcroft, just because he is a devout Christian. On judgeships the Democrats have simply drawn a line in the sand. No devout Christians will be confirmed. Even Stanford seems to have gotten into the act, with athletic department officials at one point admitting that they dropped interest in a top coaching prospect when they found out he was an evangelical Christian, without any suggestion he would impose his religion inappropriately on the team. Just the fact of his religion seems to have been Stanford's grounds for not considering him further. (See "Religion a Factor in Hiring Decision?" 5/16/02). ....Full story.....by Alec Rawls

Grading at Stanford (Is All Messed Up)
For many Stanford students, high school was a difficult four-year trek of working to get good grades. In my dorm room here at Toyon, two out of three residents were Valedictorian of their school; this is relatively common here. As we put in late nights and invested tireless effort to reap future rewards, the light at the end of the tunnel for many of us was the myth of college's grade-less academic bliss. We believed that once we got here, it would be all about learning, about exploring our minds and the world around us; grades, we thought, would be behind us. While many professors appear to believe in this doctrine, most Stanford students soon come to realize that grades are still extremely important. They're needed for higher education, intra-Stanford programs, and you're even supposed to put your GPA on your resume. The trouble is, here at The Farm, everyone seems to have a different opinion on just how important grades are, and how to give them out. ....Full story.....by Joe Lonsdale
