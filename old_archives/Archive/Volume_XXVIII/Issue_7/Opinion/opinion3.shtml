Opinion
Grading at Stanford (Is All Messed Up)
by Joe Lonsdale
Opinions Editor

For many Stanford students, high school was a difficult four-year trek of working to get good grades. In my dorm room here at Toyon, two out of three residents were Valedictorian of their school; this is relatively common here. As we put in late nights and invested tireless effort to reap future rewards, the light at the end of the tunnel for many of us was the myth of college's grade-less academic bliss. We believed that once we got here, it would be all about learning, about exploring our minds and the world around us; grades, we thought, would be behind us. While many professors appear to believe in this doctrine, most Stanford students soon come to realize that grades are still extremely important. They're needed for higher education, intra-Stanford programs, and you're even supposed to put your GPA on your resume. The trouble is, here at The Farm, everyone seems to have a different opinion on just how important grades are, and how to give them out.

Three weeks ago, Stanford University conducted an internal survey of its 75 departments. Several professors have declined interviews about the results, but the information we have has been confirmed by numerous reliable sources. Note that we have not gotten an official confirmation on these figures, but speaking to people on campus familiar with the departments, we have found that these results are completely within reason, including the shocking information contained in #1.

Note that these figures were obtained secondhand--if this has aroused anyone's interest, feel free to pressure the University to release more information. These figures bring up several important points, including but not limited to, "What the heck is wrong with the International Relations department?" One associate of the Review staff commented, "I have lots of friends in IR who have GPA's over 4.0. They're bright people, but they didn't seem that bright. I'm not surprised." Personally, I think that maybe they raised the mean so that Chelsea could get into Oxford.

Firstly, it's clear that average grades here are not a C, and in none of the departments is the mean even below a B. To students accustomed to getting A's in high school, this probably feels reasonable. Of course, several professors have their own methods, residing at both extremes. Last quarter, I had a mathematics teacher who clearly cared more about getting the concept across than figuring out grades--he gave everyone I spoke to in the class some sort of an A. An old mathematics teacher who has been grading for thirty years, however, may feel that in making the class average a B, he is being generous in his old age. Students in his class who perform better than I may have performed could still be left with a "below-average" B+ on their record.

A large percentage of Stanford students go on to some form of graduate school, and grades are an important part of these admissions. Clearly, one student should not have an advantage over another simply because he took the class the "lucky" quarter, when the nice teacher who gives all A's was teaching it. Departments need to communicate about their grading distribution, decide on the average grade for a class, and fix the grades as such. There shouldn't be "easy" and "hard" teachers, when it comes to grades.

Inter-departmental communication is necessary, too. Some fields of study may be generally less stressful than others, but when the average grade is above an A-, sufficient effort in evaluating student performance is clearly not present. Medical school admissions are often extremely conscious of students' GPA's. A medical student interested in Computer Science with a 3.4 GPA, however, looks like a slacker compared to one studying International Relations and Music with a 3.90. This is ridiculously unfair, as both students have done about equally well, when taking the information above into account.

While some professors may not care about grades, they are a relevant part of our experience here, and are needed to accurately measure performance. They provide an extra incentive for students to focus on their classes and work hard, and by ignoring them or giving them out disproportionately, departments are screwing up our education and our future. It's clear that we need someone to come in and streamline this entire system into something logical and fair. Unfortunately, Stanford doesn't appear to care enough to do so. Perhaps some alumni with influence could take action to make the University fix the system. . . .the administration has shown that the only students they'll listen to are those who have time to organize protests and pickets. Ironically, those who have time to organize are probably the people with easy classes that give all A's. Unless somebody steps up to take action, we're stuck with the system. I'd do something about it, but I have to go study. . . .I need good grades this quarter.

Joe will not be seen in public again until after finals, when he will emerge in his new manifestation as Editor-in-Chief of The Stanford Review.
