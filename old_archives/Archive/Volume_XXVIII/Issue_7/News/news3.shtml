Spotlight on Social Dance Groups
by Hilary Connell
Staff Writer

Who said that there aren't common interests that unite students on campus these days? There is an interest so compelling and enjoyable that hundreds of students are willing to participate in all-night demonstrations year after year. It is so integral to the Stanford experience that it formed the entire platform of one of the 2002-2003 ASSU Senate hopefuls. It brings together engineers and artists, professors and students. What is it? Social dance. Stanford's campus is filled with social dance performance groups and everyday dancers. Approximately one week ago, these individuals were given a night to shine. Big Dance showcased some of the best groups Stanford has to offer. Here is an insider's glimpse at two of the groups that performed. . . .

Vintage Dance
Vintage traditionally focuses on a single dance style/era per year. In the past, they've covered 19th century, Ragtime, and the Roaring Twenties. For the 2001-2002 season, it was time to bop. Performing various routines throughout the year, they manage to make every practice as interesting as the first and every performance as fresh as the last. Anne Hamilton, a senior involved since her sophomore year, described her most rewarding experience with Vintage. "Norma Miller, a living legend of swing dancing, came to teach us several dances that she choreographed. She's about 82 years old and still swingin'!

She invited our group to be the grand finale of a documentary she's making on swing, which was definitely an amazing experience." Personally, I would be happy to be walking without a cane at 82, but swing dancing? That's pretty impressive. Besides Big Dance, Vintage performed at Jammix sessions, the Viennesse Ball, an area nursing home, and much more. Vintage holds auditions each year. It is directed by Richard Powers and choreographed by Ryan and Monica Knotts.

Salsa!
Unlike Vintage, which changes its particular focus from year to year, Salsa! is committed to "sharing Salsa with the Stanford Community." In an interview with member Tiffany Silas, I was given a brief look into the time and commitment that Salsa! demands from its members. Besides two-hour practices twice a week, there are extra rehearsals in preparation for upcoming performances. This year, Salsa! also began conducting informal salsa workshops around campus that have been greeted quite favorably by those of us who are still trying to count the beats. As with all performing groups, there is a strong bond of friendship that builds among the members. Ms. Silas' favorite memory is from their first performance of the year, "the best feeling was walking off stage forming a group huddle and savoring the moment." Though the school year is almost at an end, Salsa still has several performances scheduled including the Salsa for El Salvador party, a fundraiser for disaster relief as well as Latin Graduation Night.
