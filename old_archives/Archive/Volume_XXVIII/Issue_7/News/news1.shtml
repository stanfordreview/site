Congressman Levin Speaks at Stanford
by Joe Fairbanks
Staff Writer

Congressman Sander Levin calls for Core Labor Standards
Bearing words of warning across the country, Congressman Sander Levin (D-Mich) told the Stanford International Labor Standards Conference Sunday "we've got to use all means at our disposal to push core labor standards worldwide" and urged the conference attendees to act by speaking out and discussing the issue.

"By bringing together individuals from the public and private sectors, academics, students, and experts working in the fields of labor, human rights, and business, we want to give you a new opportunity to your ideas, learn from the experiences of scholars and practitioners, and facilitate discussion and action among students and hopefully spread the movement to the other side of the desert," Mr. Levin told the room of mostly Stanford Law students.

"Trade has grown enormously over the past 30 years. It should no long be classified as an international policy; it is a domestic policy and we should treat it with the utmost importance and now we need solid core labor standards in trade agreements," the Representative said at the very first event of the conference intended to give students a look into research, teaching and policy development initiatives on the impact of globalization and the condition of labor internationally.

Mr. Levin is in his 10th term in the House of Representatives. Currently serving on the Trade Subcommittee of the House Committee on Ways and Means, which has broad jurisdiction over international trade matters, Mr. Levin has worked with Representative Charles Rangel (D-NY), co-authoring the Rangel-Levin bill (HR 3019), the Democratic alternative to the fast-track legislation that now awaits Senate consideration. His proposal, the so-called Levin amendment to the bill extending normal trade relations treatment to China, created the Executive-Congressional Commission to monitor China's performance in the areas of human rights, labor rights and the rule of law. In a week that took him from the East Coast to the West Coast, a defiant Mr. Levin answered critics of his trade initiative plans. He denounced anyone who would ignore the importance of trade labor standards and not embrace the challenge of facing large scale globalization.

"The reality is that the dispute over the role of core labor standards in trade agreements is not new and will only become increasingly salient over time. This is because the trade and labor market standards issue is part of a larger disagreement, it is part of a larger challenge of how we respond to the phenomenon of economic globalization—do we seek to move back the clock, embrace it without question, or shape its rules?"

Later, Mr. Levin took questions from audience members on a variety of labor issues. One woman seemed unsatisfied by Mr. Levin's proposals, insinuating that they fail to address Child Labor standards. Mr. Levin disagreed, claiming that his work on Permanent Normal Trade Relations with China consisted of much debate in the area of restricting child labor in mines and sweatshops. Though progress was made, Mr. Levin said, China remains on Bush's list of the world's biggest human rights violators for their failure to enact effective Child Labor Standards.

While most people at the conference applauded Mr. Levin's proposals and goals, one man in attendance happened to be a French member of the United Nations and took issue with some of the "falsehoods" Mr. Levin was purporting.

"I'm sorry to interrupt, but I just have to correct some of the misconceptions there are about these so-called labor standards. The numbers are greatly exaggerated and do not do justice to this problem. These proposals on trade agreements are far from your beloved free-trade philosophy in the states and, I believe, they do harm to the developing nations of the world instead of aid them like you think you are doing," the man said of Mr. Levin's descriptions of the increase in trade volume and the need to place restrictions on trade concerning the labor involved for manufacturing and agricultural products.

After the man interjected for the third time Mr. Levin did not respond and moved on to the next questions which mainly dealt with broad based trade issues.

Mr. Levin's speech was only the first of several that were to take place over the weekend. The conference focuses upon the questions raised by proposals to link international labor standards to international trade agreements. Each panel addresses these issues from a different perspective. The papers presented at the conference will be published by Stanford University Press for anyone interested in obtaining the speakers' remarks.
