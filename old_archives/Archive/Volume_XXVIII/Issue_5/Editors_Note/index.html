Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Henry Towsner, Editor-in-Chief
With the ASSU elections just over, it's a good time to think about accountability. This is a perennial favorite promise ("I won't do anything, but I'll keep you informed the whole time!"), although, as each successive group of elected officials discovers, not especially useful compared to, say, doing something useful.

Of course, it's not the Review which will be accountable. Rather, we plan to hold other people accountable, preferably ones we don't need favors from. Samuel Shapero starts with the ASSU administered Special Fees, the money charged to every student and distributed to student groups. The ASSU Constitution requires that the groups receiving the money make their events open to everyone, but a number of groups charge, often prohibitively, for their events. Which means that first students pay to support the group, but then have to pay again before they can actually get anything.

You may have heard about the recent disaster at UC Berkeley's student initiated course (called a DE-CAL). If not, unfortunately, I'm afraid the details would be inappropriate for this column, but suffice it to say the course on male sexuality included (you'll have to fill in the details) pictures, stripping, and, well, certain other activities. This raises an interesting question: are the ASSU run Student Initiated Courses as much fun? The answer, according to Harrison Osaki, is no, but the topics are just as inane.

We can then examine another branch of the ASSU: the Speaker's Bureau. Since our last issue, they invited two speakers, Jesse Jackson and Michael Moore. No more really needs to be said, although we have articles about each, in case you want more.

Given all this student government coverage, it's only fair to disclaim that I was, until about a month ago, a paid employee of the ASSU. We did, in fact, part on amiable terms, although I occasionally deny it.

Moving on, Alec Rawls questions studies of the Middle East, and their fixation on names rather than substance.

We also try something new this issue: an entertainment section. As an experiment, we plan to include one or two articles on campus events, local dining, and similar topics. We'll see how it works, but launching the section is an article on an upcoming a cappella concert.

All in all, it should be an interesting issue. As for the Review itself, feel free to hold us accountable however you like, although we prefer large checks.
