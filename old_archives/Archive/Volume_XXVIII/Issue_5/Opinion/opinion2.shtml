Opinion
The Connection Between Olympic Skate Judging and Middle East Studies
by Alec Rawls
Senior Staff Writer

When our lovely skate princess Michelle Kwan sat on the ice, her misfortune avoided a travesty and kept an important lesson from being exposed. Justice was done. Sarah Hughes, the younger star who had just gone supernova, won the gold she deserved, in spite of rules that committed a cardinal error for any system of judgment: they gave the less important quantity absolute priority over the more important quantity.

The long program, on the final night of the Olympics, is obviously a more important performance than the short program, with its emphasis on required elements and its lesser virtuosity. Yet according to the judging rules, the fact that Hughes trailed the top three (and the top one) by a razor thin margin coming out of the short program meant she had no chance to win the gold, no matter by how wide a margin she excelled in the long program, unless the top three from the short program not only all fell down, but did so in the right order, a sheer lucky fluke.

In effect, the medals had been turned into a party for the short program winners. The secondary virtue, a tiny victory in the short program, was given absolute priority over the primary virtue, a victory of up to any size in the long program. If Michelle hadn't fallen down, it would have been another skate judging scandal, exposing more corruption, this time in the rules themselves. Why were the rules written backwards like this? So that the judges could do their cheating in the less scrutinized short program, insuring that their favorites got the medals?

Giving secondary virtues absolute priority over primary virtues is a staple of corrupt power. Academia, for instance, is built on it from top to bottom. The necessary condition for publishing in an academic journal is a standard of scholarship that excludes all but those who have made scholarship in the particular area their life's work. Merit is considered secondarily, and only for those who pass the "short program" requirement. But scholarship is a secondary virtue. Merit is the primary virtue.

Why are the rules written backwards like this—Why is scholarship given absolute priority over merit? Because power corrupts. The people who run the journals are the scholar/specialists. On the one hand, they want to reserve this professional resource for people like themselves. On the other, they may be positively hostile to merit, and use scholarship as an alternative standard by which to promote the power of illiberal ideas that cannot stand up to standards of merit.

There are numerous departments in the humanities that are packed to the gills with deconstructionist and other neo-Marxist trash, neatly operating to exclude every Sarah Hughes who applies. Sorry, no medal for you. No publication. No job. She isn't reading and citing who the ensconced monopolists say she needs to read and cite. Is she right and they are wrong? Bah. Merit is a figment of the racist, sexist, homophobic, capitalist mind. Get with the multi-culturalist ethic man. There is no such thing as merit, only difference (and we won't tolerate any).

To the present point, when there is no such thing as merit, there is only scholarship. The priority of the secondary virtue over the primary becomes complete. Monopoly power is unopposed. Each little bastion of totalitarian oligopoly is secure. Thus we end up with such perversions as Middle-East studies, lorded over by smooth demagogues like Stanford's Joel Beinin, an Israel hating dissembler who pretends to be objective while spewing the most bigoted analyses imaginable. Promoted first by illiberal cohorts at Stanford, Beinin has now been promoted to leadership of the Middle East Studies Association. It is a national cancer and disgrace.

Incredibly, Beinin blames the Palestinian-Israeli conflict on the inability of Israel "to accept the principle that two peoples have equal rights to the land they jointly inhabit." Israel is full of Palestinians who live in safety and with minor exceptions having to do with military service, have equal rights with Jews, more rights by far than any other Arabs anywhere else in the Arab world. In contrast, any Jews discovered in Palestinian controlled areas are summarily put to death. Even Palestinian law is murderous. It is an official death penalty crime, under the Palestinian Authority, to sell land to a Jew. It could not be plainer who rejects equal rights and who the intolerant racists are. But here comes the score from the Stanford judge: Tonya Harding six, Nancy Kerrigan zero. (No contest: Tonya CLOBBERED her.)

While the United States fights terrorism, academia is infected by this fifth column of Islamist sympathizers, trying to use their status as anointed experts to convince Americans that it is Israel that is evil (Israel, who with the Palestinians at its mercy is constantly forbearing and open to any legitimate claim) and that it is the Palestinians (who constantly answer mercy with renewed attempts at murder) who are the victims. Just let the Jews fall to the Arab knife the "experts" insinuate, just let the Nazis have Poland, and all these storm clouds will disappear. The fundamental institutions of academia are inherently prone to this kind of subversion. It doesn't have to be political. For fifty years music theory was dominated by atonal "music," busy work instead of talent. Why? Because academia is a pig-pile. Sometimes Sarah Hughes emerges gloriously on top. Sometimes the battle is won by the corrupt rules and the French judges and the Tonya Hardings.

When a travesty is revealed, as with the judging scandal in the Olympic pairs skating, or with Middle-East studies, overseers need to step in and do their duty. They need to hose out the dirt and the injustice and the illiberal power and try to set the institution back onto its feet: that is, onto standards of truth and merit. The Olympic committee stepped up to its duty. Will Stanford?
