Stanford Board of Trustees and Alumni Committee to Choose New Board Members
by Nels Hansen
News Editor

Board Chairman Isaac Stein
This year, 4 new members of Stanford's Board of Trustees will be selected. The eligibility requirements do not appear to be especially stringent. Anyone who has received a degree from Stanford University, who has attended as an undergraduate for 3 or more academic quarters (not including summer quarter) with the constraint that their academic class must have graduated and that the person is not enrolled at the University, or attended as a graduate student for 3 or more academic quarters (not including summer quarter) who is no longer enrolled at the University. Also, anybody who is on the Alumni Association Board or staff, or who is a current student or faculty member, is ineligeable.

The Alumni Nominating Committee, which nominates these candiates for trusteeship, is itself nominated by the Alumni Association Board. And these individuals have certain strictures on them as well: none may currently be trustees of the University, and at most 3 may be members of the Board which nominates them. The Alumni Nominating Committee consists of 9 people. At least 3 of these alumni must be of less than 35 years of age on September 1st in the year for which they were appointed. One currently enrolled student may be a member of the committee. The chairman is selected by the Alumni Association Board from within the committee.
What The Board of Trustees Does (From Stanford Alumni Association)
"The Stanford Board of Trustees meets for five two-day (Monday morning through Tuesday noon) sessions through each year. Additionally, the Board may be called into special session and Board committees or subcommittees occasionally meet at other times. Trustees may be asked to serve on school or departmental visiting committees or other panels that need institution-wide representation. Additionally, Trustees may be asked to represent Stanford at official occasions on an ad hoc basis, and are encouraged to participate in alumni and development activities when needed. Preparation for each Trustee meeting involves two to six hours of reading of materials that are mailed twelve days before the meeting. Trustees must have a real commitment to and interest in the work of the University.

"By law, Trustees are responsible for all University decisions that are carried out in their name. Generally, matters coming before the Board are considered by one or more of the standing committees (Academic Policy, Planning and Management; Medical Center; Finance; Land and Buildings; Audit; Development; and Alumni and External Affairs); or by additional special committees (Investment Responsibility, Nominations, etc.) or another cognizant Board panel. Trustees constitute the majority of each committee, and may be joined by student and faculty representatives who are also full voting members of the panels.

"Trustees appoint the president of the University and interact with the University's faculty, staff, students, alumni, and the public.

"In a typical year, trustee attention may be given to issues such as: strategic allocation of resources, land use, housing and other facilities' planning, the academic program, regulatory responsibilities, federal and public support of education, community relations, minority affairs, audit and financial controls, and fund-raising planning."

The Alumni Board is required to pay attention to the ethnicity of those it appoints to the Nominating Committee. It is also required to use age and geographic location.

This is also the case with regards to the stated policy of the Nominating committee, which stipulates that "geographic representation, age, gender, ethnicity, fields of expertise and other factor that may be helpful to the University" are to be used in selecting trustees. In particular, the final selection of trustees must include one trustee who is 30 or younger, one 31-40, one 41 or older, and one of any age at all. It must include at minimum twice as many nominees as there exist positions.

The Board of Trustees also create a nominating committee that meets with the Alumni Nominating Committee to submit the slate of Alumni Nominated Trustees to be submitted to the Board of Trustees for election or appointment. Both committees must give a majority endorsement for any candidate who is to be included in this final slate.

In summary, the Board of Trustees' members are selected from a slate of candidates created by the joint action of two nominating committees. The members of the Nominating Committee and the nominees for positions on the Board of Trustees are selected using, among other factors, ethnicity and age. These members have control over the long-term financial planning of the institution, and by way of their selection of the University president, have some control over the day-to-day operations of the University.

Isaac Stein, the Chairman of the Board of Trustees, generalized that the Board deals with the long-range planning rather than the day-to-day operations, saying "We're more involved in the long term issues surrounding the university; [for example], whether the endowment is adequate for the long term future of the university."

At the end of the selection process, the Board usually consists of around thirty to thirty five members. There are no student or faculty members serving on the Board itself. "[Students and faculty] are too caught up in the current activities within the institution and our purpose is long term governance. No member [of the board] is directly involved in activities of the university other than the president," explains Mr. Stein. The Board of Trustees does ensure a student and community voice by having student and faculty members on most Board Committees, despite the fact that such individuals may not serve on the Board itself.
