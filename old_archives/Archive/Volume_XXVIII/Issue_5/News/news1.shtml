News
The Week in News
Bill Gates and President Hennessy
Microsoft Corporation chairman Bill Gates joined University President John Hennessy for a question and answer session on April 25. At the session, Mr. Gates discussed how he sees technology developing in the near future. Mr. Gates anticipates that personal computers (the field dominated by his company, which produces software run on almost every personal computer) will continue to spread, becoming more powerful and even more ubiquitous. He also criticized current internet based products for merely repeating what has already been done, rather than using the format to improve service. During his discussion with President Hennessy, Mr. Gates stated that he opposed laws requiring computers to detect illegal music, preferring to leave PC manufacturers unfettered. He concluded by suggesting that the next step in computer development will be the replacement of keyboards by other methods, such as speech or handwriting recognition.

Faculty Senate Debates Interdisciplinary Program
The Faculty Senate debated the renewal of the Interdisciplinary Studies in the Humanities interdisciplinary program before approving a standard five year renewal. Although the program was considered quite strong, and some professors suggested that it should receive an eight year renewal instead, other professors expressed concern that the program was overly focused on Western culture.

ASSU Elections
Under the ASSU's Instant Runoff system for its Presidential Election, M—nica Henestroza and Nick Rodriguez were elected to the ASSU Presidency, beating out Joshua Benson and Kendra Arimoto by two hundred out of some four thousand votes cast. Senior Patrick Crosetto has challenged the results, claiming that a traditional runoff is necessary. Graduate student Dave Robinson, who wrote the proposal, has responded that Mr. Crosetto's challenge is based on a faulty understanding of the ASSU's By-Laws, and the election is expected to be certified.

The Undergraduate Senate race took a surprising twist as the fifteenth (and final) place was a tie. The outgoing Senate will vote between freshmen Ellie Martin and Sarah Schulman.

With fifty-two student groups applying for Special Fees, the student body approved all but seven, meaning that the student group fee charged to all students will climb again this year, to approximately $85 per quarter. Most of the groups which lost the referenda had never applied before: SALSA!, a performance group specializing in salsa dancing; the Stanford Asia Technology Initiative, which provides internships in Asia for Stanford students; Stanford Biodesign, which seeks to involve students in Stanford's biotechnology research; and WAVES, which "is dedicated to addressing social issues facing China's youth through education and community service." Other groups, however, had received Special Fee funds in previous years, including the Stanford Mock Trial team; Masque, which "aspires to provide Stanford's queer community with a needed forum for artistic expression;" and SCBN, Stanford's student run television station.

Sophomore College Adds Four Hundred Dollar Fee
Stanford's Sophomore College program, which brings rising sophomores back to campus two weeks early to take one of a number of small seminars, will now charge. In the past the program has been funded through a combination of general funds and specific grants, but, in order to make the program more self-sufficient, students will now be charged $400 for the program, although financial aid will be available.
