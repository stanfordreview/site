Stanford Offers Student Initiated Courses
by Harisson Osaki
Staff Writer

Every quarter students receive e-mail about the "Student-Initiated Courses" offered that quarter; posted around bathrooms and walls across campus. But what are these courses, and how does one join them? Who provides them with funding?

Reading one of their advertisements one is immediately struck by the ambitious nature of the program: "Student Initiated Courses (SIC) is a program created to enhance the academic climate on Stanford's campus by building a structure for students to design and take courses in areas that are currently unexplored at this University." Students can design their own classes, which are supported by ASSU special fees funding. Since these classes are meant to cover perceived gaps in Stanford's curriculum or other educational niches, the classes are generally only worth from one to two units. What may be surprising about the student-initiated courses is that, according information in its own e-mail advertisements sent out to dorms to encourage students to create their own classes, student-initiated courses are intended to ". . . .* integrate academic pursuits and public service * [be] socially relevant to communities within or beyond Stanford"

It is unclear why the advertisement places such emphasis on the fact that their courses are "socially relevant to communities" when there is no such requirement for student-initiated classes according to the online Stanford Research Project Handbook. However, although a few of the student-initiated courses being offered are not necessarily socially relevant (such as Fundamentals of Indian Art Music), coincidentally the majority of SICs offered for students are politically charged. SIC did not reply when asked whether or not classes were selected based on political content or affiliation. The group also did not respond to inquiries about funding. SIC helps select courses which students wish to initiate along with the administration.

Several of the classes, such as "Alternative Building" and "Sustainable Agriculture", focus on developing Ôalternative' methods of approaching conventional problems (in this case construction and food production, respectively). These classes attempt to implement their alternative theories in research; for example, "Sustainable Agriculture" conducts farm-level research on alternative agricultural processes on the Stanford Farm, for example. There are also a number of classes that confine themselves to talking about things; the class "Housing and Homelessness in the Bay Area", for example, says that it uses "readings and dialogue" in order to "find out what others think". These classes do not do any research outside of the classroom, and seem to act merely as discussion forums where students can vent and meet others who think like they do. Many of the courses also possess an extraordinarily broad range of scope. "Prison Issues", for example, asks its students to "work together to develop policy alternatives that would alleviate the social costs of our nation's rapid prison expansion", in a two-unit class, and does not address what alternative deterrent against crime would be used instead of prisons. The course "Casino Capitalism", begins with the assumption that globalization has been responsible for destabilizing international markets, and then asks its students to "formulate policy recommendations for socially responsible global financial regulation". The overly broad scopes of these classes, along with their severe political bent imply that there is an unspoken agenda behind SIC.

Many of the course instructors for SICs were inspired by their taking of their respective class, and wish to see the course offered again. This creates continuity for the course, where the offered course's curriculum becomes standardized and more recognized by the administration. Several courses, such as "Sustainable Agriculture", involve a community service component and as such are more easily accepted by the administration for renewal.
