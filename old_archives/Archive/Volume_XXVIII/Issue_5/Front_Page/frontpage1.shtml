Front Page
Special Fee Recipients Double-Charge Students
by Sam Shapero
Staff Writer

On April 17th, Stanford students voted for to allocate special fees to over to forty-five Voluntary Student Organizations. Among the groups receiving funds were several organizations that have a history of violating the constitutional limitations for the special fee they will receive. Several student groups 'double charge' for their services, asking students to pay once with their student fees then again with actual admission to events.

The special fee money is one of the central methods of funding for campus organizations, and every year students vote to decide which groups will receive the special fees at the same time as they elect their student government. In order to be eligible for special fees, an organization must first get ten percent of the relevant student population, graduate or undergraduate, to sign a petition to put them on the ballot. The request for special fees is then reviewed by the senate, which usually approves the request. Groups that receive signatures of fifteen percent of the population can bypass the senate approval, while groups that have received special fees in previous years can skip the petition and only need senate approval.

Once eligible for the ballot, the organizations are free to begin campaigning for votes. This year fifty-two student organizations were on the ballot. If these groups receive a majority of the vote, then the following year, the university adds to students' tuition the amount of money requested by the organization.

Students, however, can choose to get this money refunded every quarter. During the first several weeks of every academic quarter, the ASSU offers students the ability to request refunds from the individual fees paid to each student group. Students can therefore choose to support all, some, or none of the student groups that are elected to receive special fee money.

Many students do not request refunds, and are charged special fee money for events which they cannot attend or afford. The Stanford Viennese Ball is one of the worst offenders, charging seventy-five dollars per couple for admission to their main campus activity, the annual ball they host. This cost is being subsidized by the large portion of the student body which is dissuaded from going by the high price tag.

The Stanford Concert Network also "double charges" students, asking them to subsidize the costs with special fee money, then asking them to pay up to twenty dollars in admission to individual concerts.

Stanford graduate student Harris Jan Shapiro, former ASSU Parliamentarian and current constitutional council member, commented on whether double charging is in accordance with the ASSU constitution.

"Groups receiving Special Fee funds may not charge any member of the funding population for their services, unless that member has received a fee refund for that group," Mr. Shapiro said, quoting the ASSU Constitution. "A student organization may not charge a total amount greater than its per capita Special Fee for services provided to members of the Association outside of that organization's funding population." According to Mr. Shapiro then, if a group only asked undergraduates to pay special fees, it cannot charge graduate students more than the amount of special fees they received from each student. They cannot charge undergraduates at all, unless an undergraduate received a refund for his special fees.

Mr. Shapiro mentioned that student organizations might separate the events for which they charge admission and those for which they receive special fees. "The specific items for which such groups may assess charges depends on how the term 'services' applies to that group, which will vary from case to case." The constitution itself delineates the areas for which special fee money should be used, saying "Special Fees shall be sought by student organizations to cover their organizational and basic programming expenses."

The constitution and bylaws also expect the student organizations to offset the amount of money they ask for in special fees by their external revenues. A Voluntary student organization may therefore ask for donations or try to raise money with a fundraiser, but it cannot charge for the actual service for which it receiving special fees.

The double charging by certain organizations and the substantial increase in fees were a source of concern to many students, including the current President of the Associated Students of Stanford University, Matthew Brewer.

Mr. Brewer was worried that students might simply let these special fees issues pass without examining them, also saying that continued increases in student fees would fuel a dangerous trend. "Four years ago the special fee was forty dollars [per quarter], this is year it might be close to one-hundred twenty. I think it's bad when students just approve and don't criticize," he said. "This year's election will really set the tone for future years."

Mr. Brewer wanted to make sure students knew about how the groups that use special fees use their money. He said, "I hope [the increase in fees] makes students more critical of how accountable the groups are to their constituencies."

Despite Mr. Brewer's concerns Stanford students did not appear considerably more critical than in years past. Of the 52 groups that applied for special fees, students elected all but seven to receive money, meaning over three hundred dollars will be added to student's tuition bill during the 2002-2003 academic year, the highest charge in Stanford history.
