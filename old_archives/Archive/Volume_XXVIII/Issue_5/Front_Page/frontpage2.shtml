Front Page
Jesse Jackson Speaks for César Chavez Day
by Ming Zhu and Hilary Connell
Staff Writers

"Si, se puede." "Yes, it can be done," was the motto of farm worker and Latino civil rights advocate César Chavez (1927-1993). Mr. Chavez is best known for his efforts to better the lives of migrant farm workers in the American southwest, particularly through the founding of the first successful farm worker's union in American history, the United Farm Workers of America. Five states, including California, now recognize Mr. Chavez's birthday as a state holiday. In recognition of César Chavez Day (April 1), Stanford students and the surrounding community have been organizing events that commemorate the life of Mr. Chavez throughout the month of April. This commemoration culminated on April 3rd at the Viva César Chavez! celebration, hosted by El Centro Chicano, MEChA, the ASSU Speaker's Bureau, and the César Chavez Foundation. The evening included an exhibition of artwork submitted by children from local schools, various speakers from Stanford, and a keynote address by Rev. Jesse Jackson, Sr.

Mariano Florentino-Cuellar, an assistant professor at the Stanford Law School, and Andrew Irlando, executive director of the César Chavez Foundation, opened the event by reflecting on Chavez as a man "who gives us hope at a time when we desperately need hope." Vice Provost for Campus Relations La Doris Cordell followed the opening speakers by extolling both Mr. Chavez and Mr. Jackson, describing the two men as "dear friends" and noting that Mr. Jackson actually served as a pall-bearer at Mr. Chavez's funeral. Ms. Cordell highlighted Mr. Chavez's and Mr. Jackson's partnership in representing grape and lettuce-pickers in Calexico, Ca., and in investigating the high cancer rates among farm workers in MacFarland, Ca. She then introduced Mr. Jackson to his audience, crying, "Jesse Jackson, we need you now."

Mr. Jackson opened his address by encouraging the audience to honor Mr. Chavez by continuing Mr. Chavez's struggle for the advancement of civil rights. "If you long for a day that's past," warned Mr. Jackson, "you couldn't be a part of it. You couldn't be a part of it, but the day will continue. . . .the struggle will continue." According to Mr. Jackson, this struggle persists everywhere, from national foreign policy to daily life on the Stanford campus. Mr. Jackson fiercely criticized the Bush administration's stance toward the Middle East. He identified two possible courses of action--demanding that Israeli troops withdraw from Palestinian territory or "allowing Sharon to finish what he started"--and admonished the U.S. that "our interest is not in one particular state; it's in the region." America reached "a fork in the road," accused Mr. Jackson, "and Bush chose the fork."

Most of Mr. Jackson's speech, however, struck much closer to home for his audience. Mr. Jackson attested that the civil rights struggle progressed from the fight to end slavery, the fight to end legal segregation, and the fight to win suffrage, to its current stage--the fight for equal access to capital, industry, and technology. Mr. Jackson exhorted Stanford students and members of the surrounding community to contribute to this cause by expanding online resources available in East Palo Alto, lobbying for living wages and health insurance for university workers, and helping at-risk children. Mr. Jackson advised students, in particular, to "look at every janitor on this campus as your own parent." "When you fight," he concluded, "it makes all of us more secure and safer. . . .and you strengthen your own character."

In response to audience questions, Mr. Jackson commented briefly at the end of his address on the current situation in Cuba, supporting open trade, communication, and student exchange between Cuba and the U.S. In addition, he reprimanded Condoleeza Rice for "looking over the President's State of the Union address and ignoring the Congo and AIDS [. . . .] how can you be from Birmingham and Stanford and not put in something about Congo and AIDS?" He also candidly denied that he had any intentions of running for governor or president in future elections.

Mr. Jackson's address was well-received by the majority of his audience. Says freshman Matthew Kaufmann, "He was a very definite presence. He definitely takes up a lot of your attention. . . .In a way I think the treatment he gave to the [situation in the Middle East] was oversimplified, but Jackson was right on with his analysis." Others, however, did not express such a favorable response. According to freshman Aaron Port, "Jackson assumes official authority he does not possess. If he were in Bush's shoes, he would find his own ideas too narrow-minded." Regardless of one's opinions about Mr. Jackson's address, his person and ideas maintain a strong influence in campus dialogues surrounding current issues, including those that Mr. Chavez addressed during his lifetime.
