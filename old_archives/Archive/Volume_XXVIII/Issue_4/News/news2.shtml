Feminist Gloria Steinem Speaks at Stanford
by Ryan Wisnesky
News Editor

Last Thursday, Gloria Steinem, a leading feminist and political activist delivered a speech entitled "Everything you've ever wanted to know about feminism but were afraid to ask" to an overflowing audience at Kresge Auditorium. Ms. Steinem, now 66 and recently married, opened her speech with a solemn promise to the audience: "If all goes well each of us will leave here with [among other things] a new subversive organizing tactic."

After making a joke about Condoleezza Rice, Steinem launched into her speech. She began by talking about the great need to for the feminist movement in today's world. Even after thirty years of activism, she contended, there was still much work to be done. "The first wave of activism, of woman suffrage and abolition of slavery, took 100 to 150 years. We have 70 years to go yet," she noted. "In the last 12 years Time magazine has declared the woman's movement over 27 times."

Ms. Steinem went into great detail about why she thought the feminist movement is still necessary. She noted that "both men and women may fear female authority" and that "a man who last saw a powerful woman in childhood may regress to childlike behavior" when confronted with women in positions of power. She did not, however, explain how the feminist movement would help society fix these problems; instead she just reiterated that feminism was necessary because of the "depth of the rage [of housewives]". Ms. Steinem closed her remarks about the necessity of feminism by noting that women seem to talk a lot because "women are measured against the expectation of female silence."

Once Ms. Steinem had finished her spiel about the necessity of feminism, she talked about the stereotype of feminists as lesbians. Quoting her old lecture partner, she contended that "lesbians get along with men fine" and that "it is contradictory for the same forces that oppose contraception to oppose lesbianism". Ms. Steinem dismissed the claim of lesbianism by noting that "about a third of women consider themselves feminists."

In a typical post 9/11 maneuver, Ms. Steinem noted that she could not give the same speech now as she could before the attacks. She then began to focus on what she saw as the cause of terrorism. "In my opinion violent means can never meet a peaceful end" she said, speaking about the US response to the attacks and US policy in the past. She asserted that because all of the hijackers and most terrorists are middle class men that feminist attempts to blur the distinctions between male and female would help reduce terrorism. Moreover, it would help reduce murder rates and crime in general, she said. "All of the hijackers were male, middle class, and heterosexual. Who are the people that go into a school or a post office to kill indiscriminately? All white, heterosexual, middle class males. So are serial killers." She qualified her remarks, however, by saying "I'm not saying women are never terrorists. Women are just as capable of violence as men but we don't have our masculinity to prove". She then quoted a former Swedish Prime Minister who believed that gender roles make violence inevitable.

Ms. Steinem then concluded her speech, and took questions from the audience. When asked what she thought about the political role of feminism, she noted that "You can't have democracy without feminism" but never explained why. Following several questions about feminism and trans-sexuality, Ms. Steinem asked the audience several questions of her own: "How are we the only industrialized country in the world without a national healthcare system? Why is the work pattern in this country adapted to a certain type of man? Let them adapt to us!"

Several audience members asked questions about how feminism relates to the role of government. A student asked, "Does feminism need an anti-capitalist movement? Can we have a truly egalitarian society in a capitalist system?", to which Ms. Steinem responded, "No. But up to now socialist societies have been patriarchal." When asked if we should "settle for capitalism," Ms. Steinem answered flatly "no". Responding to a question about increasing the number of female faculty members, Ms. Steinem said, "We should take kids as a positive thing for tenure. If men don't have kids, no tenure." Ms. Steinem did not address whether this would type of tenure system would lead to a more skilled faculty, but did comment on a related question that "Affirmative Action has raised standards. I don't think there are instances of unqualified women or minorities because of affirmative action."

In addition to the in depth questions poised by the audience, Ms. Steinem delivered a series of one line answers. Addressing a question about the damage done to children who do not have stay at home moms, Ms. Steinem remarked that "having more baby-sitters when we're young helps us adapt to other people." When asked about how Title 9 (the law requiring equal numbers of athletic teams for males and females) was resulting in a quota system that led to sub-par female teams, she responded, "Athletic teams aren't about winning." Asked about recycling, she told the audience, "I'd make each manufacturer of refrigerators responsible for recycling them."

Ms. Steinem also hopes that "we would pay reparations to countries where we have land mines" and that although "equality doesn't mean sameness", "men should have the same perks [like maternity leave]." Ms. Steinem also proposed that government should punish "biased shows" on television, but gave no definition of what a biased show is. Ms. Steinem finished the question and answer period by telling the audience when the feminist movement would be over. "When we look at Congress and see it looks like the country. When I see as many well paid white men wheeling babies of color in central park as colored men wheeling white babies. When 5% of the people don't control 75% of the wealth. When our social position isn't dedicated the amount of melanin in our skin."
