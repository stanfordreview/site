Student Group Demands Intelligent University Response to Labor Issues
by Joe Lonsdale
Opinions Editor

There have been many labor disputes this year on the Farm, and more than one group has been formed regarding different parts of the labor issues. Pat Bomhack has recently created the campaign for Stanford to join the Workers Rights Consortium (WRC). "We are going to meet with [President] Hennessey next Wednesday [April 18th], present our concerns, and present proposals that will include but are not limited to joining the WRC," he said in a phone interview.

The main goal of Mr. Bomhack's group is to fight for the University decision-makers to be more aware of what he claims they are supporting through their athletic apparel purchasing decisions, and more active in their response to "unethical" business practices. The University does have a board already that oversees the ethical use of investment money: the Panel on Investment Responsibilities (PIR). However, according to Mr. Bomhack, no organized policy currently exists for evaluating the ethics of a situation before the University makes a business contract: "Stanford is not currently an informed institutional consumer... no access to adequate information concerning origins of athletic apparel exists." The University defends its contract with Nike, a company often accused of mistreating third-world workers, by explaining that Nike is a member of the Fair Labor Association (FLA). The response to this, according to Mr. Bomhack, is that while the FLA contends to be an independent monitor, it is organized and funded primarily by major apparel corporations themselves, who also have members on its Board of Directors.

"Ultimately, we are concerned as constituents [of Stanford] as to what we are representing [with our athletic apparel purchasing decisions]," said Mr. Bomhack. His group recently put up hundreds of large yellow fliers in dorms entitled, "A Call For Fair Play: Athletic Apparel at Stanford." When asked what hard evidence existed that would cause others to share his group's concerns, he pointed to WRC investigations, and a rare admission of guilt by the Nike corporation, published last year in Forbes magazine:

"In a report issued today by Nike, the athletic apparel giant confessed to facilitating worker exploitation. The study uncovered the exchange of sexual favors for jobs, as well as other physical and verbal abuses, at factories in Indonesia that manufacture Nike products."
"DISASTER OF THE DAY: NIKE", Forbes, Thursday, Februrary 22nd, 2001

"Clearly," says Mr. Bomhack, "violations of workers rights [in underdeveloped countries] is an ongoing concern... it's not something of the past that's already been taken care of." He was quick to point out that, though Nike is the example chosen, it is not the sole concern. Mr. Bomhack's group would like Stanford to join the WRC, an organization created to address these concerns. From the WRC website:

"The Worker Rights Consortium (WRC) is a non-profit organization created by college and university administrations, students and labor rights experts. The WRC's purpose is to assist in the enforcement of manufacturing Codes of Conduct adopted by colleges and universities; these Codes are designed to ensure that factories producing clothing and other goods bearing college and university names respect the basic rights of workers. There are more than 90 colleges and universities affiliated with the WRC." In a move new to this campus's labor groups, Mr. Bomhack's group made an intellectual effort to reach out to those still unimpressed with his goals; his recent flier addressed the question, "Don't Corporations Like Nike Help These Countries?"

The group's response is that "It is not a goal of this campaign to initiate a Nike boycott or an anti-Nike movement -- exactly because we recognize that the costs of that action could fall upon the workers themselves... Factory workers want consumers to continue purchasing Nike goods and/or apparel from other corporations, so they can keep their jobs; however, these workers also wish to be treated as human beings."

Mr. Bomhack elaborated on this position. "We're not attempting to refute the economic argument that Nike provides jobs and that quite possibly by not buying Nike apparel...the cost of that will fall on the workers themselves. So at every step that we take, it is very important to us that we keep our ultimate goal, and that is the improvement of the lives of the workers, in the forefront of our mind, and not to get swept up with good intentions, but have those good intentions bear a negative result."

Mr. Bomhack acknowledges the difficulty of the task that he faces, and is not sure how much they will really be able to change. One reason for Stanford to increase its attentiveness to these concerns might be to make sure that "...corporations do not disobey the labor laws in the local areas that they subcontract out to. In these areas, not only do many of these countries have minimum if nonexistent labor laws, but they rarely enforce them--or it can happen that they rarely enforce them. They don't want Nike to go somewhere else, because they need the economic benefit."

Aside from these goals, the labor organization is dedicated to making the University "fill the void in its infrastructure, with regard to the process of dealing with labor issues." Fighting what observers say is an uphill battle with the ambiguous goal of "making things better" with regard to the labor issues at the University, Mr. Bomhack ends the interview with this remark: "In a large way it's a symbolic act--we may not be able to change what Nike or other apparel corporations will do, but we can change what we bring to this campus...at the very least it's a formal statement saying that we are no longer willing to stand by the wayside and be blind institutional consumers."
