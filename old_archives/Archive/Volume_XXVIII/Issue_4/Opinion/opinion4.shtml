Opinion
WRC Campaign Hits the Mark
by Alex Robbins
Opinions Editor

Last week I found myself lamenting to a friend of mine that the spectrum of people in this country who actually care about the lot of their brethren overseas seems about as slim as Callista Flockhart while fasting. It seems--I said in a rather irrational and emotional moment--that Americans tend to either xenophobic isolationism or patronizing, elitist, and anti-capitalist counterculturalism. Both groups seem to focus on what they dislike in the world: the former on those people and institutions outside their culture, and the latter on those within it. Neither does much to ameliorate the plight of the impoverished and oppressed who compose the majority of our species.

I was wrong, of course. A great many people from across the political spectrum do genuinely strive to improve the world around them, from George W. Bush to (admittedly) Bill Clinton to Bono to President President Abdoulaye Wade of Senegal, and it was more than just a bit self-righteous of me to assume that I was somehow the only one (or one of the only ones, at least) who could be idealistic without being some sort of revolutionary nutcase.

From what I've seen of the WRC campaign so far, Pat Bomhack and his coterie belie this assumption (for more information on the WRC, refer to the news article in this issue). Unless we discover some sort of bizarre political agenda over at WRC headquarters, I fail to see how we should not all support Stanford's joining the organization. Pertinent information regarding how one's business contacts conduct themselves is hardly ever a bad thing, and most often crucial from both an economic and a moral perspective.

Those of you who believe that free trade is inherently exploitative can stop reading here--and this includes anyone on the Right who might somehow believe this and still support it solely for reasons of self-interest. Free trade is justifiable empirically because it leads to greater economic growth and prosperity, to be sure, but it is imperative morally because it allows individuals to control the destinies and fortunes of their own lives. Put another way, free trade is the minimization of external coercion on the interaction of individual human beings with one another, while both protectionism and "fair trade" policies involve governmental institutions limiting such interactions to either defend the interests of a privileged class (steel workers, most recently) at the expense of everyone else, or to arrogantly manage the lives of people considered to have inferior judgment, such as the impoverished farmer who would leap at the chance to work in a factory to support his family (the anti-globalization crowd eschews the term "inferior judgment," though, preferring instead to refer to the hypothetical farmer as "desperate" or "oppressed" and thus in need of external deliverance from a job he would chose to take).

In essence, the moral argument for free trade rests on the right of individual human beings to make voluntary decisions in pursuit of their own priorities and dreams. This would suggest the absurdity, for example, of forcing all U.S. companies to pay a minimum wage to their overseas labor, on a moral level as well as because of the fact that it would merely result in U.S. business relocating to the States. If the worker knows ahead of time that he or she will receive a certain amount of money for a certain amount of time worked, then he or she ought to be free to chose whether or not to take this offer.

On the other hand, if U.S. or U.S./multinational corporations really are abusing their workers (and, as the news article suggests, there is ample evidence of instances in which they may be), the moral argument for free trade flies out the window. Does anyone really believe that a corporation would inform its prospective employees that they might be beaten or sexually abused? Does anyone believe that a corporation would explicitly and publicly allow this sort conduct?

Of course not. True capitalism and economic liberty require the rule of law, something that is far from established in most Third World nations--otherwise we wouldn't be able to label them "Third World nations." Contrary to both nineteenth century and contemporary racists, the reason that they're Third World nations is not due to any sort of immutable characteristic of the population, but rather the corruption, arbitrariness, and lawlessness of their governments. One can hardly expect these governments to uphold labor laws or even to enforce contracts, but without the rule of law capitalism and economic liberty cannot exist.

If a corporation signs a contract with a worker for six months, but then beats her and fires her in the first week, who does the worker appeal to? A corrupt local government official who could receive more money under the table from the corporation than he receives in six months from his job? Even disregarding the inability of the local government to conduct effective, impartial antitrust regulation (since I know that some of my more libertarian friends disagree with the concept of antitrust regulation altogether), we cannot ignore the basic inability of many foreign governments to establish the rule of law or create a truly capitalist, economically liberal environment.

That is where we must come in, both as a nation, as institutions, and as individuals. As a nation we ought to require that corporations doing business in the U.S. publicly disclose their employment practices, and then make sure that the businesses are actually following them. After all, it is already a federal crime for the officers of a publicly held corporation to lie to shareholders--accurate information is a necessary prerequisite to economic liberalism. As individuals, we can chose not to do business with companies whose practices we find objectionable--this too is a free and individual choice, and one which financially hurts us rather than other people (as when elites in this country rail against globalization).

Finally, as members of institutions, especially a culturally prominent one like Stanford, we can all do our part to help pressure--rather than force--multinational corporations to respect human dignity, the rule of law, and the sanctity of contract. Without these, workers become slaves and capitalism really does become exploitation. With them, globalization and the spread of economic liberty promises a world in which all of us--not just those of us fortunate enough to be born in a particular country--can have the protection and freedom necessary to pursue our dreams and live out our own lives, for better or for worse.

Alex Robbins is a Sophomore who would like to apologize to a friend of his for initially dismissing the WRC campaign as useless left-wing inanity without knowing anything about it.
