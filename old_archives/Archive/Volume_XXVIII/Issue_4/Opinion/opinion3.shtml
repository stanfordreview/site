Opinion
Financial Aid Office Should Present Pro-Diversity Policy with Pride
by Nels Hansen
News Editor

The financial aid office has a policy of reducing the self-help expectation for students who "bring diversity to campus", meaning that they are of some particular ethnic group (see the news article for more information). The self-help portion of the total cost of education here is minuscule to begin with in comparison to the tuition, room and board (though it's certainly not negligible in comparison to something cheap, like a new car). So let's assume that for a black or Hispanic student, the self-help portion is reduced by $1200--this is based on the 1995 figure with a bit tacked on for inflation and leeway, because the Financial Aid Office declined to give any specifics. Even though this does not amount to large-scale redistribution of wealth, we are obliged to question the motives of anyone implementing a racist policy such as this one. Why does the Financial Aid Office do this? According to Cynthia Hartley, the Director of Financial Aid Planning and Policy Analysis at Stanford, it is "to make the Undergraduate experience richer for all."

It seems, though, that this policy is aimed not at making the Undergraduate experience richer, but some people richer. However, let us assume that this is not their true aim, and that this policy does make the Undergraduate experience better for everyone; concede for the sake of the lack of established knowledge one way or the other that increased racial diversity enriches our experience. Although the Financial Aid Office has refused to provide details of this policy in terms of actual difference in self-help expectation this year, in 1995 such details were provided to the Review. Approximately $2000 to $4800 is provided to students on the plan.

As this extra money goes to individuals who will almost certainly make that number seem insignificant next to their salaries at the end of their college career, it will probably not affect the distribution of wealth significantly. This policy is a part of the continuing concerted effort to increase the number of academically talented underrepresented minorities at Stanford, but differs from affirmative action in that it does not bring people in who are less qualified to be here, but instead provides incentive to those students already admitted to choose Stanford over competing schools. In fact, this ethnic scholarship is more modest by far than those offered at many other schools. The real tragedy is that it is kept under the table, as though the Financial Aid Office feels ashamed of its policy. If the people of the Financial Aid Office believed in the benefits of a diverse campus as they claim, they would make the policy public to maximize the benefit accrued. Let's make it public: Stanford offers a modest scholarship in the range of $2000-$4800 to students of particular ethnicities, other students who are especially academically talented, and a group who come from unusually difficult backgrounds--this would be in addition to the enormous athletic scholarships offered. In fact, let's increase these scholarships; if they work at low dollar amounts, then if they were increased to some meaningful sum they might be a reasonable substitute for affirmative action, since Stanford would be the school of choice for underrepresented minorities.

Some might resent this policy, since it means that their families may have to make greater sacrifices to send them here. In particular, the poor but not-underrepresented families who are given only need-based scholarships might resent that their underrepresented counterparts would be able to maintain a higher quality of life. Some financially troubled members of economically successful racial groups (Caucasians, Asians) would have to forego certain luxuries their minority counterparts would retain in sending their children here. However, this resentment would no doubt be tempered out of recognition of the fact that at least their child got in, which might not have occurred in the presence of affirmative action. Another benefit would be that nobody could claim that any minorities got in for anything other than merit, which would help do away with what racism exists here. This increased money for minorities policy at least does not mean that who gets to come here will be evaluated less on the merits of their transcript and more on the color of their skin. What it does do is increase diversity without diminishing academic excellence. All the benefits of affirmative action are preserved, and the alleged downsides are slimmed down. While it is somewhat unfair to students like me who do not get this financial aid, it is not that significant in quantity and, if it were increased in magnitude, we would merely be paying the price of a higher quality education, with more educated peers and a more diverse student body.

Affirmative action should be supplanted by an expanded ethnic scholarship program. Despite the fact that this money is in addition to calculated need, it can be justified more soundly than affirmative action and has equal benefits at a smaller cost. Our underrepresented minority population would probably increase or at least remain about the same, and our strength as an institution would multiply. Racism would be somewhat squelched, and those opposed to affirmative action would be somewhat appeased. While the policy is questionable in that it, like affirmative action, is racist, it is better than the alternative, since it doesn't control directly who may come to this school; it merely influences whether they wish to or not.

Nels Hansen has too much fun making arguments sometimes.
