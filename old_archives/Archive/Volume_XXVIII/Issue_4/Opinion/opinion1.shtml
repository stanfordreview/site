Opinion
Giving "Diverse" Students Extra Financial Aid Is Worse than Nothing
by Chris Fish
Opinions Editor

The racial and socioeconomic realities of our society result in minority students needing financial aid more often than their Caucasian and Asian counterparts. This is not Stanford's doing, and the disparity here is nothing that Stanford can fix. What is wrong, however, is when two people of similar economic but different racial backgrounds are given different amounts of monetary aid. This policy is racist, divisive, and harmful to all parties involved.

Stanford has a policy of giving more financial aid to equally needy students if one of the students brings more "diversity" to the school; and racial background counts as diversity. Why do we put up with this? Because it's politically incorrect in the flamingly liberal environment of our politically correct University to say that a policy that gives aid to minorities is a bad idea. Upon closer inspection, however, reasonable liberals and conservatives both agree that the financial aid office has made a misguided decision.

By giving more money to someone simply because of their race, you are sending them all of the wrong messages. You are suggesting that they are oppressed, and deserve payment from society in return for their state of oppression. Any student capable of getting into Stanford is capable of fending for themselves and succeeding in today's world--this is no longer a country saturated with hateful bigots. By sending them the message that they need more help, you are saying that they cannot make it on their own, that they simply cannot accomplish as much as equally positioned white students. Perhaps the financial aid office secretly thinks this is the case -perhaps it thinks that, regardless of the diversity they are required to encourage, minorities are too stupid to make ends meet, whereas their white counterparts with equal economic backgrounds will be fine, simply because of the color of their skin.

Regardless of what they think, that is simply not true, and suggesting as much is the wrong way to lead people to success. This free money is the "let's help our little brown brothers" policy of the early twentieth-century clad in neoliberal attire, only it's even more harmful in a today's world. Treating individuals of different races differently only promotes mentally categorizing individuals into different classes of society - creating this form of segregation should be the antithesis of the goals of a successfully diverse community.

Unfortunately, the University is a confused with regards to what diversity is all about. I do not presume to understand the vast multitude of reasons that diversity is as wonderful as everyone says it is. But what I do know is that one of the most precious things about interacting with people, regardless of their backgrounds, is recognizing that we are all human beings. Successfully living in a diverse environment means thinking of people as individuals, and after awhile, not even noticing that they are white, black, Indian, Asian, Arab, or anything else. My roommate last year was from Malaysia, but I was surprised when a month into the year, I realized he didn't seem any more distinct from me as a person than my white friend from Pennsylvania, or the African-American pianist/football-star down the hall. Rather, I though of them as individuals. Each had unique experiences and points of view to offer, and unique personality traits that were derived from the fact that they were different people - not merely of different races. By thinking of them only as classmates, and not categorizing them, I was able to fully appreciate what they had to offer. Racism and hate are created by thinking of people as belonging to a "different" group, instead of seeing them as unique individuals.

Financial aid is necessary to provide for students from disadvantaged economic backgrounds. Giving extra money to "diverse" students creates boundaries between races that unnecessarily divide the community, while simultaneously sending the wrong messages to these students. As a Caucasian student from a disadvantaged background working 20 hours a week to make ends meet, I would probably strongly resent a Hispanic student from a similar background who was being given free money, for no reason other than their race. This is human nature, and the jealousy would work the other way around too, if the situation were reversed. Treating students differently based on race is harmful to the community, and harmful to the students involved by the message it sends. If the financial aid office were at all serious about making the University a better place, it would end this policy immediately. Racism and stupidity in the name of diversity is still racism and stupidity.

Joe is sometimes bothered by the fact that, had his parents been black, his accomplishments would have attracted hoards of attractive scholarships from liberal groups.
