Stanford Uses Race in Determining Financial Aid
by Nels Hansen
Staff Writer

On page 6 of the financial aid handbook there is a sentence which reads, "The self-help expectation will be lower for certain categories of students including those from low-income families, those who are academically in the top 5-10% of an entering class, and those who bring diversity to the student body." Pursuant to this policy, I sent an email request for elaboration on this policy to Stanford Dean of Admissions Robin Mamlet, who forwarded the request to Cynthia Hartley, the Director of Financial Aid Planning and Policy Analysis. Ms. Hartley responded with a general overview of financial aid policy, including the following statements:

"The University's aim is to not overburden any students with excessive debt or work while enrolled in school. Therefore it's been interesting to note that these expectations for all categories of students have continued to drop from a high of $6,600 which represented 21% of a student's costs, down to a low of $5,250 for the current academic year which represents approximately 14% of a student's costs."

As I had wished to know what exactly the Financial Aid Handbook had meant by the term "diversity", I queried Ms. Hartley about this again via e-mail on February 15th. The following was sent in response on February 27th:

"Dear Nels,
I'm sorry for not getting this response to you earlier. I know you understand this is a very busy time of year for admission and financial aid.

The term 'socially' was misleading. What we recognize are those who bring aspects of their lives or experiences which, in addition to their academic achievements, add interesting perspectives to the campus community. Some examples might include those who have grown up in non traditional families, who are the first in their family to attend college or university, are immigrants or an exiles, have faced adversity in their still very short lives.

When we look at those from low-income families we are looking not at those who have had a bad year but rather those that come from a history of financial adversity.

I hope this gives you the idea: the possibilities are unlimited. But the aim, of course, is to make that undergraduate experience richer for all.
Regards, Cynthia"

On March 1st I specifically asked whether by "diversity" the financial aid office means that people of particular ethnicities would have a lowered self-help contribution. After some time with no response, I resubmitted my request. On April 9th, Ms. Hartley sent the following via e-mail:

"Nels,
When we say 'diversity' we are considering and including factors such as culture, socio-economic background, race and ethnicity, work and life experiences, and exceptional skills and talents. Our goal is to put together a class that cuts across these many dimensions so as to provide a rich and varied undergraduate educational experience.
Cynthia"

When Ms. Mamlet was asked for specifics on the modification in addition to need to the self-help contribution due to ethnicity, she responded via e-mail,

"You should know, though, that we do not release either financial aid or admission data by subgroup -- but Ms. Hartley can get you information on the program as a whole, and you can take it from there."

All students interviewed were surprised that this policy existed, so none of them offered their opinion on whether or not it was a good policy or whether they supported it.
