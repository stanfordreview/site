Front Page
Students Hold Labor Protest on Community Day
by Justin Diener
Staff Writer

On a day when Stanford was reaching out to the surrounding community it was forced to confront internal labor issues. On Sunday, April 7th, 2002 Community Day celebrations took place under a heightened level of security as protesters with the Stanford Labor Action Coalition (SLAC) and SEIU 715 protested the administration's failure to accept their demands to allow workers to have the right to organize and function under the code of conduct which would extend the right to unionize to workers in subcontracted positions.

Prior to the procession of university officials from the Oval in front of the Quad along Palm Drive to the Mausoleum, protesters walked down Palm Drive and attempted to proceed toward the Mausoleum. When police prevented the protesters from doing so, they proceeded to a location near Frost Amphitheater to hold their own rally where they opened a mock time capsule with historical items from the sponsoring student groups on campus while the Community Day celebrants opened a time capsule from the early days of the University. During the protest, one of several phrases chanted by the demonstrators was, "uno, dos, tres, quatro, nos pagan muy barato," meaning "one, two, three, four, they pay us very little."

This demonstration is a continuation of the campaign by SLAC and SEIU 715 to win concessions from the administration through quasi-peaceful protest. The campaign began last fall with protests of the proposed hiring of subcontracted employees to fill janitorial positions in the Stanford Hospitals. Several concerns which the students and Stanford employees involved with SLAC feel that Stanford President John Hennessey has failed to address revolve around the institution of a "Code of Conduct" extending a "living-wage" to all workers subcontracted or not and extending the right to unionize to all Stanford workers. SLAC and the local Stanford unions are concerned over trend to replace employees with subcontractors who they say are paid less, have fewer benefits, and perform lower quality work.

Protesters stated that they felt shunned due to what they claim is the administration's refusal to meet with both employees and students. The University has repeatedly offered to meet with students alone and has steadfastly declined to meet with workers. During the protest, the protesters called upon the administration, indirectly, to acknowledge the entire community of demonstrators, including the employees.

Chuck Cashion, a food-service worker at Ricker and Lakeside Dining halls who attended the demonstration to show his support for his fellow workers, said the following, "I am here today to support my fellow co-workers and to send a message to President Hennessey to listen to the concerns of the workers." He thought that the actions of the administration in refusing to meet with employee representatives closed the door on a large part of the community. Mr. Cashion said that a large number of jobs are disappearing on campus to be replaced by subcontracted positions; he stated that more and more housekeeping is being contracted out, and he is worried about it going further and would like to have his representatives meet with the administration.

Mitch Bousson, the Chief Steward for the United Stanford Workers, believes that the University needs to recognize the needs of all workers rather than just a limited few. He stated his belief that this begins with the acceptance of a Code-of-Conduct guaranteeing the right to organize and a basic compensation package to all workers.

The reaction to the protests by other community members was mixed. James Cook, a student at Community Day, made the following observations about the demonstration: "The demonstration was well organized and rather decorative. I don't generally agree with some of their causes but I thought this demonstration was pretty peaceful."
