Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
by Henry Towsner
Editor-in-chief

Henry Towsner, Editor-in-chief
Are activists the enemy? Most of the time, obviously, yes (unless you're one of the people reading this because you're keeping tabs on your enemies). But all the time? Is there room for an occasional alliance on issues of joint interest? After all, there's a certain political cache to being able to say "The Review and the activists both support position X." And if we could actually find issues we agree on, it might even be worth doing it on occassion.

What I’m leading up to is that I very nearly ran for the ASSU Presidency with Monica Henestroza, a former co-chair of the Student Labor Action Coalition, and probably the most prominant activist on campus. While the political merits are substantial (that is, we had a good chance of winning), the surprising part is that we actually found a common political platform to run on.

The point is that we have, at least some of the time, a common enemy: the Stanford beaurocracy. And while there are times we disagree, there are issues we can potentially work together on. Of course, most of the time we're too afraid of the other side gaining influence to do so. Also, they're all vegetarians, which causes problems at dinner meetings.

No, that wasn't the point. The point is that this issue talks about some topics we could work together on. Let's start with perhaps the most anti-student organization at Stanford (and that's up against some stiff competition): Residential Education. From the factory, deep in the bowels of the underworld, which produces most of the horrendous ideas we've come to expect from ResEd comes yet another. In keeping with their mission to promote intellectual discussion in the dorms, ResEd has announced their plans to promote intelligent ASSU voting by...banning all campaigning within dorms. While they eventually agreed to allow limited flyering, the resulting policy is still extremely limiting (and has been publicly criticized by almost all of the ASSU officers, myself included, who have been involved with election policy). Anyway, Ryan Wisnesky goes into more details. This isn't a liberal vs. conservative issue. This is a good vs. evil issue, or at least a reason vs. ass-covering beaurocrats issue.

Religion is traditionally a risky topic, but Nels Hansen proposes something we can all agree with: that people should think about their religious beliefs, and try to understand them, rather than just regurgitate them. Another vaguely neutral topic is the Special Fee system, by which students pay money on their tuition bill to an assortment to student groups. The annual cost has doubled over the last five or six years, and with the number of groups applying skyrocketing from thirty last year to more than fifty this year, the time has come to start cutting back. Last year every group that applied received the majority vote needed to get the fee. Read Dave Myszewski's article about some of this year's new and returning groups, and consider carefully who really deserves your vote and your money.

Unless you were going to vote against one of the groups I like. In that case, I recommend you give it the rubber stamp it undoubtedly deserves. And if you like groups I don’t like, try making your choices by throwing darts. (Conservatives, or at least the sort of conservative I am, believe that people can make better decisions than the government could make for them. It's just that in some cases, the better decision is flipping a coin.)

Nels Hansen has given Professor Harry Elam a chance to respond to our article about the workshops for whites only and people of color only. He's the director of the institute which brought Aya de Leon, who ran the workshops, to campus, and he denies that they were limited to people of any particular race: they were just advertised to people of different races. I guess that's okay; it's the difference between putting up a "No coloreds allowed" sign and an "Open to whites" sign outside a restaurant. A world of difference.
