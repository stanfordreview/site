Kevin "Silent Bob" Smith Raises the Roof
by Andrew Wright
Staff Writer

Kevin Smith talks for the ASSU Speaker's Bureau.
Over six hundred people packed into Kresge Auditorium on March 7, to see film writer/director/producer Kevin Smith for a night of laughs in what became one of the most entertaining events of the quarter.

People began lining up more than thirty minutes before the doors were scheduled to open. Excitement ran high in the Speaker's Bureau as tickets went quickly on Monday afternoon, and anticipation mounted as the week went on. In the days preceding the event, dorm circles gathered to hold Smith-movie-marathons.

Some feared that the hype surrounding the appearance would only end in a disappointment. One look at the students exiting the auditorium, however, would show that Silent Bob did not let them down.

Kevin Smith began the night by announcing that his intention was not to present a lecture, but to entertain the audience's questions. And what a variety of questions they were. Students asked everything from, "Boxers or briefs?" to "What advice do you have for an aspiring director?" The New Jersey director met the challenge with an equally wide array of answers, ranging from the funny to the serious to the lewd. He shared everything from tales of the movie set to intimate accounts about his wife.

Smith also told the story of his modest beginnings as a film producer. At the age of 21, he applied for several credit cards to raise the capital necessary for a movie. Working at night he shot "Clerks" at a Quick Stop where he worked during the day. He was surprised by its success when "Clerks" won first place at the Sundance Film Festival in 1994. "For whatever reason, we've found a small pocket of the population that likes our work," he commented.

More than one audience member was curious about how Smith felt about being a mainstream celebrity. He shook his head at the mention of fame. "I wouldn't call it mainstream. I still go out food shopping and $#!* like that." Later, he joked, "I don't have any stalkers, but I think that's because I'm not a woman."

To the delight of the crowd, Jason Mewes and Ben Affleck were frequent subjects of the night's conversation. Affleck, said Smith, was definitely on his list of top ten best friends, and would be used in every one of his movies if it were possible. Speaking of Mewes, Smith said, "I think he's funny and charming." The character of Jay, Silent Bob's sexually enthused sidekick, was modeled to fit Jason's actual personality.

The question and answer session took on a more serious tone when Smith began to speak about the importance of integrity in the film industry. He criticized formula writers, who emphasize pyrotechnics and visual effects rather than content. "I could never tell a story just to pay my mortgage."

When writing a script, he only incorporates elements that are of personal interest to him. "I would like to watch a movie where people talk about [stuff] that I would talk about. I look at these other movies and wonder why we aren't making more money."

Of all of his films, perhaps "Dogma" was the most personal. Exploring the facts and implications of institutionalized religion, particularly the Catholic Church, Smith wrote the script at a time when he was personally having a crisis of faith. The film is an expression of his belief that religion can be used as a tool for both good and evil. Religious hysteria initially delayed the release of "Dogma," but when the movie finally hit the theaters, protests ended. After his exploration was complete, Smith concluded that he ultimately believed in Christ, but not the institution of the Church.

Afterwards, Freshman Sam Maglio offered words of admiration for Kevin Smith and the progression of his films. "I think it just speaks to his genius that he can go from fart jokes in 'Clerks' and 'Mall Rats' and then do so well in a movie like 'Chasing Amy.'"

Jamil Snead, a student artist, agreed. "I appreciate the fact that he doesn't produce just one type of movie, and that he's willing to actually make a film to help himself through his own issues, like with 'Dogma.'"

Reflecting on Smith's remarks about a "small pocket of the population," Freshman Anne Friedman observed, "I don't think he would normally consider Stanford students as his target audience, but I didn't feel like there was any hostility.

Judging by the cheers and applause, one would assume there was nothing but praise.
