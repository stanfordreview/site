News
Professor Elam Defends "Beyond White" Event
by Nels Hansen
News Editor

Last issue, due to various scheduling issues, the news article on the "Beyond 'White'" workshop was not able to include the comments of either Aya De Leon, the leader of the workshop, or Professor Harry Elam, director of the Stanford Irvine Institute for Diversity in the Arts. As such, it mainly consisted of a summary of the events surrounding the expulsion of Review reporters Alex Robbins and Ming Zhu from the workshop.

Mr. Robbins and Ms. Zhu approached Ms. De Leon prior to the beginning of the evening's workshop and told her that they would like to attend the workshop. After expressing concerns regarding the ethnicity of Ms. Zhu (who is not of "European-American heritage") as the group was to explore the issues at hand apart from all such people apart from herself, Ms. De Leon said she would ask the participants how they felt about the changes in the groups dynamics and let that determine whether the reporters could remain. At this point Ms. Zhu and Mr. Robbins identified themselves as Review staff members, and Ms. De Leon then expressed further concerns about their attendence, even though Ms. Zhu and Mr. Robbins offered to omit all personal details regarding individuals present. Once 7 other individuals arrived, the workshop began, and Ms. De Leon asked the group about how they felt about the presence of persons of non-European descent (Ms. Zhu among them) in the group, and the group felt that this was acceptable. Ms. De Leon then stated that there were journalists present, but did not specify from which publication they were from. Group members felt that, as long as they followed and respected the posted rules regarding confidentiality (i.e., only published purely auto-biographical accounts of the event), they would be comfortable sharing as usual. Ms. De Leon then returned to the issue of the remaining (2 had left by this point) non-whites present, but the group remained comfortable with their presence. Finally Ms. De Leon, who already knew that Ms. Zhu and Mr. Robbins were from the Review, asked the journalists which publication they worked for. When they stated that they were from the Review, one group member stated that she was uncomfortable with their presence due to the fact they were from the Review in particular, and the reporters were asked to leave.

Questions Ms. De Leon has Declined to Answer
1) What is the affiliation of the "Beyond 'White'" workshop with the university and the drama department.
1a) Is it sponsored by either?
1b) What is the workshop's purpose?

2) Do your workshops on Tuesday and Thursday night ("Beyond 'White'" and "Raise the Roof") have an arrangement with the Harmony House for use of the space during those times?

3) Why is it your policy to expel a non-white (or non European-American, though I'm about as European as my car... :) ) member of the group if anyone feels uncomfortable with his/her presence?

4) Why is it your policy to expel journalists from group if anyone feels uncomfortable with his/her presence, even if they agree to follow the rules posted on the wall, particularly regarding confidentiality?

5) Why did you allow Ming and me to stay once the group accepted the presence of journalists (with confidentiality restrictions) but ask us to leave once a group member expressed discomfort with our being from The Stanford Review rather than our being journalists, per se.

6) Why do you base the decisions to expel someone on the complaint/objection of any member of the group rather than a consensus or majority vote?

Though the Review was able to set up an interview with Mr. Elam, Ms. De Leon did not respond to email requests for comments or have time for an interview. She wrote Mr. Robbins in an email that "Most of all, Alex, I wish you'd returned my calls & email prior to the first article, but I appreciate your willingness to try and make it happen for a follow-up. Too bad our schedules conflicted." The calls she refers to here were one call on Thursday, February 21st telling Mr. Robbins that she would be leaving for Berkeley that afternoon, and another call directly afterwards adding a detail. The email she refers to was never received by Mr. Robbins. Ms. De Leon, according to an email sent by her assistant, Christine Cordero, was too sick for the usual Thursday evening workshop on March 7th, and Ms. De Leon again declined to comment for this issue due to other commitments.

INTERVIEW WITH PROFESSOR HARRY ELAM

What is the affiliation of the "Beyond 'White'" workshop with regards to Stanford?

"The workshop is sponsored by the Stanford Irvine Institute for Diversity in Arts, which is funded by the President, the Provost, and the Irvine Foundation. Aya De Leon is one of 4 guest artists from California of international and national renown hosted for winter quarter. Ms De Leon is a spoken word artist, which is the most popular art form at this time, so she suggested the 2 evening workshops to help meet the high level of interest students have in this art form. The workshops were a sort of extra-curricular event on her part."

What is the policy of the workshop and affiliates with regards to race and ideology of participants?

"Anyone could sign up for the classes, and no one was to be prohibited from signing up, which she knew and understood from the beginning. Pedagogically, the focus of the two workshops is different in that they talk about individual's experiences in different ways - approaching whiteness as an ethnicity. Often people associate race first with Blacks and then other non-whites. So it approached the issue in a new and interesting way."

What is your understanding of the incident in which two Review reporters were asked to leave the "Beyond 'White'" workshop, in particular as it relates to Title 6 and University Bylaws and policy?

"Having spoken with the legal department, the students involved, and Aya herself, this is what I understand regarding this incident: the reporters identified themselves as members of the press, though they did not state which publication they were from. The concern was that the class, which had been going on for several weeks before this time, would not be able to maintain the safe space it had created, and the dynamics would be changed by the scrutiny of the press. While the freedom of the press is an important one, in some situations the ability of the press to be present at some meetings is overridden, and this is certainly such an instance."

On being told of the perspective of the Review reporters on the event (that Aya knew they were from the Review, tried to get them kicked out based on race, then based on journalistic affiliation, then because they were from the Review in particular):

"That differs from my understanding of the incident, and as such it is difficult for me to speculate on the legality of what the reporters say happened; however, it seems to me that the earnestness of their desire to participate in the project of taking the class was reasonably in question at that point."
