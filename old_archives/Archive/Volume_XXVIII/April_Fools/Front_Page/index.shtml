Front Page
Review Staff Burns Own Issues in Protest of Self
by Christopher Fish
Comrade

Several members of the Stanford Review's staff were arrested yesterday during a protest outside of the Office of Student Activities. The formerly-conservative paper was objecting to the OSA's policy of tolerating the organization, allowing it to print and even recruit freshmen; staff members picketed the office, carrying signs with slogans such as "Stop us before we print again," "Protect students: shut us up," and "We're sorry."

According to reports, the protest began shortly after six in the evening, as an assortment of writers and other staff of the Stanford Review gathered outside the office. Around 6:30, former Editor-in-Chief, now Comrade Henry Towsner, delivered a brief speech in which he denounced OSA for failing to shut down the paper. Although the OSA's office is closed after five, the protestors demanded to be let in to read a list of demands. When no one arrived to open the empty office, Comrade Alex Robbins proceeded to read the demands to the assembled students. Following this, a group of staff members hauled over a bundle of previous issues of the Review, doused them in gasoline, and lit them on fire. Comrade Brooke Brody-Waite reportedly removed her bra, and added it to the pyre.

Demands Presented to the OSA by Review Staff
1) That all copies of the Review on campus be confiscated and destroyed
2) That all people at Stanford currently affiliated with the Review be forced to apologize to those they have wronged
3) That all peopled wronged by the Review apologize to the paper's staff for reading it
4) That the Review's office be preserved in perpetuity as a monument to human folly
5) That the Stanford Law Review be renamed to end any possible affiliation with the Review's crimes
Apparently drawn by reports of the fire, police arrived shortly before seven, and ordered to the crowd (which by now included a large number of bystanders) to disperse. When they refused, Comrades Nels Hansen and Joe Lonsdale were arrested.

From jail, Lonsdale criticized the University's response to the protest. "We think that this arrest is just further evidence of Stanford's conservative bias. For years we have printed and distributed our so-called newspaper with only minimal harassment, yet now that we have recognized the error of our ways, the University arrests us."

Police apparently released both Hansen and Lonsdale after the University declined to press charges.

Speaking after the protest was ended, Towsner noted that, "For years, the Review has been allowed to spew its hateful drivel all over campus. We believe that it was OSA's obligation to protect students from the despicable ideas we were advocating." Other staff members agreed. Said Robbins, "Every Stanford student has a right to be shielded from ideas they disagree with. Yet we were allowed, for years, to print a newspaper filled with nothing but conservative ideas. Imagine all the psyches we've scarred with our opinions."

According to staff members, the paper changed its political views when an e-mail was forwarded to the Review's mailing list which called the paper "mean-spirited" and "conservative." Said Comrade Robbins, "Once we saw that, we all realized that we had been wrong all along. We've been so busy worrying about freedom and morality that we forget to get in touch with our feelings." Asked about the reasons for the protest, Robbins responded that, "As soon as we became liberals, we realized that someone had to be to blame for our mistakes. It wasn't too hard to pick out OSA as being a big part of the problem."

Several of the protestors emphasized that while the Office of Student Activities was a part of the problem, others were responsible as well. They named the department of Residential Education, which failed to block the paper from being distributed, but added that much of the blame falls on their fellow students, some of whom read the paper and a few of whom, according to some of the paper's staffers, even sought it out.

Towsner warned that students must "be forever vigilant" against those who seek to challenge liberalism, and added that "they must do so without letting [their] ideas be threatened" by such challenges.
