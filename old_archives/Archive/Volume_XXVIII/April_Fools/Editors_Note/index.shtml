Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
At long last, my goal has been accomplished. For years I have hidden in the Review, a traitor in their mists, while I worked to accomplish my ultimate goal: the conversion of the Review into Stanford's leftist publication, dedicated to forwarding the cause of equality at all costs.

It has not been easy. I am not a naturally evil person, and pretending to be one was difficult. So many times I have sat in a cushy room in Hoover Tower, smoking a cigar with a rifle on my lap, and been asked questions like, "Would you still support a tax cut if the poor got some of the money?" While my true ideological allies have been protesting in White Plaza, I have been forced to have lunch with the fools who mocked them as ineffective and misguided. I even wrote article after article, hiding my true feelings and instead writing article after article about such vague absurdities as property rights and western culture.

But it has been worth it. As Editor-in-Chief, I was able to change the Review's editorial mission. No longer will we espouse the pallid greed of conservatism, and instead we will promote the ideals we know to be right, because they are left:

1) An end to the study of western "civilization" at Stanford, in all its forms.
2) An explicit affirmative action policy for students, faculty, and staff, requiring that each department, and the school as a whole, have a racial and gender balance comparable to the national average.
3) The enlargement of the Area 4 GER, both in the number of courses offered and the number students are required to take.
4) The abolition of the ethnic studies programs, as well as the departments of History, Political Science, Comparative Literature, Economics, and Linguistics. In their place, new departments of African and African American Studies, Latin American Studies, Native American Studies, Feminist Studies, and Asian American Studies. History and other abolished departments may be offered as interdisciplinary programs of these new departments.

With our new goals in mind, we hope you enjoy the first issue of the new Stanford Review. Christopher Fish has written about our protest outside OSA, where we demanded that the University ban us from printing. Alex Robbins has documented the horrible damage inflicted by the recent government raids on various campus buildings. Finally, at a recent meeting, Joe Lonsdale and Alec Rawls got into a vicious argument over the nature of moral reasoning, and they have presented their respective positions in a Point/Counterpoint pair of articles. We hope that their well-reasoned pieces help you make up your mind on this tricky issue.
