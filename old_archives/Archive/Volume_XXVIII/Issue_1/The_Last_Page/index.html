The Last Page
Panelists, ACLU Executive Discuss USA Patriot Act, Privacy after 9/11
by Sam Shapero
Staff Writer

John Elwood, left, and Kent Walker, listen to Mark Rotenburg at a debate hosted by the American Constitution Society
The American Constitution Society hosted a debate concerning national security and personal freedom on January 31st. The debate, entitled "Searching for Balance: National Security's Threat to Civil Liberties" took place at Stanford's Law School.

Kathleen Sullivan, the Dean of Stanford's Law school, moderated the debate between the five panelists that the American Constitution Society had invited.

Four of the panelists were Stanford alumni, including Anthony Romero, the executive director of the American Civil Liberties Union. The other alumni were Kent Walker, General Counsel of Liberate technologies; Chris Painter, a Department of Justice official; and Marc Rotenburg, Director of the Electronic Privacy Information Center. The fifth panelist was John Elwood a counsel for the Department of Justice.

Ms. Sullivan opened the debate by introducing the theme of the evening to the debaters and the audience. "There's a date we remember," she began, recounting a brief history of the events since September 11th. "As of September 14th, a national emergency exists in the United States. On September 19th Ashcroft proposed an immediate anti-terrorist bill, and urged congress to pass it as quickly as possible."

This anti-terrorist bill, the USA Patriot Act, was to be the main focus of the debate. Ms. Sullivan asked a pivotal question to the debaters, "How do we balance and understand the need for national security and personal safety with a right of privacy and protest?" To answer the question, she said the American Constitution Society had invited "libertarian members of the government and reasonable members of the libertarians [sic]" to debate the issue that evening.

Ms. Sullivan then introduced a hypothetical situation to serve as a framework for the debate. "My name is Muhammed, and I roomed with a man who is connected with the bombing. [...] Funny things start happening on my phone and email. I read there's this thing called the USA Patriot Act. I'm beginning to think I'm being tracked."

Mr. Romero responded first. "There's a good chance," he said. He pointed out several problems with current surveillance laws and mentioned the FISA court, which makes warrants in secret for suspected agents of foreign governments. Mr. Romero asked, "How do you ensure the line between foreign intelligence and domestic criminal investigation?"

Mr. Painter countered that the USA Patriot Act had not really given the government broad new powers. "Surveillance positions have very limited changes in the law. Wiretap laws already allowed us to go through emails." He added that the government is not permitted to look at the subject matter of an email without probable cause, as described under Title III.

Mr. Painter mentioned Carnivore, a computer program the government has that can sift through millions of email looking for key words and names. Mr. Rotenburg recounted his own investigation into the computer system:

"When Carnivore slipped out, we filed a FIA [Freedom of Information Act] Brief for technical specifications. The answer we received was 'we can get it all now, and can sort it out later.' Carnivore lacks traditional Title III safeguards." He said that the government's access to our email is too broad.

Kathleen Sullivan, Dean of the Stanford Law School, moderates the debate on Civil Liberties on January 31. The event was sponsored by American Constitution Society
The panelists also discussed the detainment of Arab and South Asian immigrants. Ms. Sullivan again brought forth a hypothetical situation with an Arab alien being held in a detainment camp.

Mr. Elwood explained the deportation process. "When you come to the country you have to follow immigration laws," he said. "We have to remember we're talking about deportation. If you come to the country and are out of status and are deported, it's not considered punishment."

Ms. Sullivan asked Mr. Walker, as an attorney, to advise an immigrant who thought he had been racially profiled. Mr. Walker said he could only help "If Muhammed is a victim of racial profiling; if the filter being used by the government is Arab men between the ages of 18 and 33."

Mr. Romero also discussed the detainment of Arab and Muslim immigrants. He said the ACLU had to call foreign embassies to find the names of immigrants being detained because the federal government wouldn't tell them. He also described the treatment of one man who had called him. "He was in a room with 20 other men. All the men were called Mohammed by the guards. It took three months for him to be charged and he was without access to counsel."

"In a few years, we'll see," said Mr. Elwood in response. "If we've been bad, we'll pay monetary damages."

Another important issue for the evening was national ID cards. Mr. Rotenburg opposed the idea. "We have constructed a system of government in the United States to limit the sharing of information because we don't want big brother surveillance laws," said Mr. Rotenburg. "There's a coercive influence in always being observed and I don't think I should be responsible to [the government] for accounting for my private life."

Mr. Romero mentioned LEAN, a identification database in Detroit that was used by police to harass their ex-girlfriends and harass their neighbors."

Mr. Painter responded that the federal government would prosecute such offenses. "I think this will be a private sector issue," he said.

Towards the end of the debate Ms. Sullivan left the floor open for questions from the audience. One woman addressed a question specifically to Mr. Elwood. She asked how law enforcement could engage in racial profiling against Arab-Americans while gathering information from their communities. "Aren't these counterproductive?"

"You're right," replied Mr. Elwood. "We have spent an enormous amount of hours finding a solution. A lot of people who have relevant information are in the Arab-American community."

The final question was also directed towards Mr. Elwood, asking if he thought the government had upset the balance between liberty and safety.

"I don't think we've altered the balance at all," responded Mr. Elwood. "We're not infringing on rights anywhere."
