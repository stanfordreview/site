News Briefs
by Harrison Osaki
Staff Writer

Trustees raise Stanford undergraduate tuition, room and board by 4.9 percent
The Stanford Board of Trustees has approved a 4.9 percent increase in undergraduate tuition and room and board rates for the 2002-2003 school year, purportedly for the purpose of helping the need-blind financial aid assistance program. Although this is the fifth consecutive year in which the Board has raised tuition costs, this most recent enlargement represents a slightly smaller percentage increase in tuition than the previous year. In addition, the Board raised each graduate programs' tuition by 5 or 7.4 percent.

Study: Hormone replacement therapy of mixed usefulness
A study from the Stanford School of Medicine involving almost 2,800 women found that while hormone replacement therapy, in which the hormones estrogen and progrestin are given to women, may reduce post-menopausal symptoms, in pre-menopausal women therapy causes significant physical problems. In addition, replacement therapy can have side effects including abnormal bleeding and increased skin tenderness. The study also found, contrary to what is conventionally believed, that the therapy did not reduce the chances of heart disease. About 38 percent of United States women between the ages of 50 and 74 are on hormone replacement therapy.

Scientists successfully test technique to "switch off" bacteria genes
A group of scientists, led by a Stanford professor, have successfully targeted and deactivate specific bacterial genes. The scientists synthesized a circular molecule of DNA known as a nanocircle, which can be inserted into bacteria. The nanocircle in this experiment was capable of synthesizing ribozymes, molecules capable of neutralizing specific genes by destroying the RNA of the genes. In this experiment a specific drug-resistant gene in the bacterium E. coli stopped functioning over 90 percent of the time after the nanocircle was added to the bacterium. This technique could possibly used to inhibit disease-causing and mutant genes in humans.

Stanford alumnus kidnapped, held hostage
Daniel Pearl, a Wall Street Journal reporter, has been reported missing in Pakistan and is allegedly being held by a group that calls itself "The National Movement for the Restoration of Pakistani Sovereignty". Pearl is a 1985 Stanford alumnus who used to write for the Daily. An e-mail containing several pictures of a shackled man, apparently Pearl, was sent out to several major newspapers and publications around the world soon after his disappearance, although the Wall Street Journal did not receive the e-mail. The e-mail accused Pearl of working with the Central Intelligence Agency and made several demands for his return, including the release of Pakistani prisoners captured in Afghanistan. The CIA denies any connection with Pearl.

Prison experiment to be mimicked on BBC
A show called "The Experiment", which will be run on the British Broadcasting Corporation, is modeled after the 1971 Stanford Prison Experiment. Professors from Exeter University and St. Andrews University split a group of fifteen people into prisoners and guards and examined the phenomenon of domination between the groups; the results of the experiment will be shown in five 50-minute installments on television. Philip Zimbardo, the psychologist who ran the original 1971 prison experiment, was asked to advise the professors on how the experiment ought to be run; Zimbardo declined, commenting that because the prisoners knew they were going to be televised no useful information about their behavior could be collected.
