Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Henry Towsner, Editor-in-Chief
I guess the basic question I should answer in my first Editor's Note is, what is the Stanford Review, and why am I working for it? The answer to the first question is that the Stanford Review is a conservative, college newspaper. Conservative is a vague term if there ever was one. Read conservative newspapers and you'll find them filled with references to neo-conservatives, paleo-conservatives, capital-O Objectivists, little-l libertarians and other assorted breeds. The Stanford Review includes all of them. Here at Stanford we need to stick together. Let the left quibble about whether the Stanford Progressive is "really" progressive; we have no time for internecine feuds. That means that, common parlance aside, there is no "Review position" on anything. We occasionally write editorials, but those reflect the opinion of most of the senior staff, not everyone who works for us. Many of our writers (not to mention senior staff) have individual positions which might be described as "moderate," or even with the l-word. I don't dictate opinions to writers; we don't expect anyone to write something they don't agree with. That doesn't mean we print liberal articles, however. That's not our purpose. If you want that, read one of the other campus publications. Any of them.

News is a little different; we try to keep it unbiased. I make no claim that we entirely succeed, but we try to keep the opinions to the opinion section. There's some slant to our news section simply because we only try to cover those things conservatives would want covered, but the articles themselves are supposed to be neutral. You should, however, feel free to read any article about activists in the sneering tone in which it was probably written.

The Review is also a college paper. That doesn't just mean it's written by college students, it means it's about college students. We don't usually cover national politics. If you want to hear about the latest in the Enron scandal or the news from the White House, there are other papers which cover that. We're the Stanford Review. We're about Stanford.

The second question, why am I working for the Review, is more complicated. After all, not only did I grow up in one of the most liberal states in the country, I grew up in the People's Republic of Cambridge itself. Cambridge, probably a candidate for the leftist capital of the United States, is the sort of place where Ralph Nader could defeat George Bush in the 2000 election. So how did I end up as a conservative?

The answer is that, if you keep your eyes open, there is no better place to become a conservative than Cambridge, Massachusetts. What makes up the radical fringe in the rest of the country, the sort of thing Democrats try to keep quiet so it doesn't frighten away the moderates, is policy in Cambridge. One example: while I was in high school, the Cambridge School Board realized that there were disproportionately few blacks in the advanced placement program. Ever vigilant for equality above all else, the School Board seriously considered abolishing the program, preferring that none benefit from it if the cost is racial imbalance. The thing I find most surprising is not that I became a conservative, but that anyone could live in Cambridge and not become a conservative.

But if Cambridge is the Washington, D.C. of liberalism then Stanford is the Los Angeles. In this issue we report that "feel-good" racism is alive and well: as Alex Robbins and Ming Zhu report, at a Stanford funded workshop, Ming Zhu was warned that she would be asked to leave if any student was uncomfortable with her race. On another front, as Andrew Wright reports, Stanford has retreated slightly in returning houses to two fraterneties, but almost simultaneously putting two others on probation.

On the national level, Sam Shapero reports on a Stanford Law School debate over the balance between civil liberties and security, while Matt Barrett reviews a new book by Carl Djerassi, the inventor of the birth control pill. Finally, in our annual Man of the Year article, we name Glenn Campbell, former director of the Hoover Institution, as the 2001-2002 Man of the Year.
