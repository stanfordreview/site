Opinion
This Man's Pill Could Have Been Aborted
by Matthew Barrett
Editor Emeritus

Carl Djerassi's sometimes tedious account of the history the "Pill" is part personal narrative, part scientific chronicle, and part social commentary, and worth reading only if you have finished your Winter reading list very early.

The birth control Pill, according to Carl Djerassi, celebrated its 50th birthday in October of last year. Though the idea of the pill was conceived in the 20s and not approved by the FDA until 1960, Djerassi argues that October 15, 1951 is the birthday of the Pill because on that day his laboratory "completed the first synthesis of a steroid eventually to be used for oral contraception." Of course, in choosing this date, he is thereby able to lay claims to fatherhood of the Pill.

Djerassi argues that his title is much more important than we already think it is. In the opening pages, Djerassi addresses the question of why his book was ever written. After all, he authored a set of memoirs (The Pill, Pygmy Chimps, and Degas' Horse) just ten years ago. To justify the current update, he professes that he is "ten years older and. . . .ten years wiser", and that we can learn from his life experiences. This seems a bit of a stretch. A history of oral contraceptives might be interesting enough, but This Man's Pill begs the question of why we must know how it affected Djerassi personally. He is guilty of the autobiographical mistake of assuming his life is intrinsically interesting. To his credit, he is not entirely unapologetic. In the book's conclusion, he notes, "As I showed in possibly too-excruciating detail, I would have been a very different human being (without the Pill)." But this just leaves us wishing he had made the realization about 200 pages earlier and spared the minutiae.

Fortunately, when Djerassi deviates from the description and praise of his own career, he contributes some interesting social insight. For instance, he addresses some myths about the Pill, including the one that its invention was an act of chauvinism. Margaret Mead claimed that men invented the pill because they are unwilling to experiment with their own bodies but more than willing to experiment with women's bodies. Djerassi points out the falseness of this claim, noting that the Pill was designed for women because researchers had clues about why women do and do not get pregnant.

". . . .women do not get pregnant during pregnancy because of the continuous secretion of progesterone -- whereas no such clue exists in male reproductive biology." Thus, based on existing scientific evidence, the pill was much more feasible for women, whether or not men were afraid of trifling with their own bodies.

Furthermore, feminists complained that men conducted most of the early research on the Pill. While this was true, it was not unique to the Pill. Men, of course, dominated scientific research during the early phases of the Pill. Though feminists were understandably annoyed by this fact, they were not correct in arguing that male scientific researchers only wanted women as human guinea pigs in birth control research and desired no female input. Djerassi does concede that the Pill gave men an excuse to relinquish their own responsibility, but this was not the primary intent.

He also dismisses the notion that the Pill is responsible for the decline of birth rates in industrial countries. Djerassi argues that the birth rates are lower because of families' deliberative decisions not to have children, not because of the increased availability and ease of the Pill. He also notes that birth rates in industrialized countries had been on the decline for decades prior to the appearance of the Pill. Djerassi also goes further, addressing various religious attitudes on birth control and the Pill, and discusses the relationship between the Pill and abortion. He also provides a helpful layperson's description of the actual chemistry behind the Pill. His musings are interesting enough to justify having been printed, but not terribly original. For a personal fan of Djerassi himself, or the most scrutinizing historian of the birth control movement, this book is worth reading. Understanding the career of one of the pioneers of the Pill, admittedly, may potentially shape the way you think about the Pill and its social implications. But most likely, you will be unimpressed by the book's pedagogical value. This Man's Pill is eloquently written and addresses a relevant social issue, but in the final analysis, is probably not worth your time.

Matthew Barrett might be telling us something about the amount of excitement he has in his daily life.
