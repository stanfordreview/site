Opinion
Ideological and Racial Discrimination at Stanford Disappointing
by Ming Zhu
Staff Writer

Discrimination. I knew the word. I knew that it existed. . . .but I had never experienced it before. I grew up in a little town on the west side of Michigan. It was a neighborhood of basketball hoops, Dodge minivans and White Anglo-Saxon Protestants. And yet, not once did I feel uncomfortable because of who I was. Unbelievably, I had to come to Stanford to discover what discrimination meant.

Stanford - paragon of diversity. That was the first impression I had of Stanford when I visited last April during Admit Weekend. Stanford was a place where diversity flourished. I could walk down the hall hearing multiple Chinese conversations and stroll into Tresidder catching snippets of heated arguments in French. On a single day in sunny October, I could bike past a table at White Plaza petitioning in support of war alongside a rally shouting, "make peace! Not war!" In my mind, Stanford was going to be a place where all religious, sexual, political, and ethnic lines would be erased and thrown out the window.

To be fair, Stanford did deliver. Within the first week of orientation, I was ushered into programs, performances, and discussion sessions celebrating diversity and acceptance. During the activity fair at White Plaza, there were groups that catered to every niche of the student body. I don't deny it -- I was impressed.

Then, last Thursday night, I was thrown into a situation that I had never experienced before. Assigned by The Review to cover a workshop called "Beyond White: exploring European-American identity", I expected the experience to be thoroughly enjoyable and enlightening. Though I knew it's focus was to explore "what it means to be European-American" by examining "ethnic and class diversity within the European-American community and how European-Americans fit into the wider diverse community," I speculated that my presence would provide a welcomed extra angle to the group. I went, armed with a pen, an open mind, and a ready voice. Less than an hour later, I left the group -- still with a pen and an open mind. . . .but with my mouth shut and my eyes wide open. Upon arrival, I had been told: "If anyone feels slightly uncomfortable with this being a mixed group, then unfortunately, you will have to go."

To begin with, there's something not quite right about the idea that someone would be uncomfortable with a mixed group. This implies that certain things might become taboo if a person of color (the workshop's term for people of non-European American descent) were present. Someone might feel uncomfortable voicing their opinions. I am of the opinion that if you can't say something in front of everyone, then perhaps it shouldn't be said. One might argue that Asians are given safe havens to revel in their Asian roots and Blacks are allowed to celebrate their Black ethnicity -- why aren't European-Americans allowed to focus on being European-American? A wonderful point -- none of these groups should be exclusive. There is nothing wrong with a group that explores what it means to be white, but there is something wrong when such a group is scared or embarrassed to share their feelings with the rest of the population. If the workshop's focus truly was to explore the European-American identity in the hopes of going beyond to understand its interaction with the wider diverse community, then there should not be two separate workshops for whites and non-whites. If the workshop's true focus was to explore identities in order to celebrate the differences between these groups, then we might as well call it by its real name: exploring segregation.

Technically, I was not kicked out because I wasn't of European American descent. I was eventually asked to leave because I was a reporter from the Stanford Review (emphasis on Stanford Review). But just the fact that it was brought up, just the fact that the idea was mentioned is enough. If anyone had been uncomfortable with the fact that I was not white, I would have been gone in a second.

That was the first time I've ever been told that my participation could be dependent on my skin color. My parents had often told stories of how they felt mistreated or offended due to racial prejudices. I had always thought of myself as immune to such experiences. I have lived in America since I was three. I speak English without any trace of accent. In dress, in manner, in thought -- I am no different from any of my European-American peers. Apparently, that's just not enough. There was something about me that separated me from all the other participants in the workshop -- I was not the right color.

I was not white.
