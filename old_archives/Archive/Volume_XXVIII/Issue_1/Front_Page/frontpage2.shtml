Front Page
Glenn Campbell: 2001-02 Man of the Year
by Henry Towsner
Editor in Chief

Glenn Campbell served for 30 years as director of the Hoover Institution, during which time he built it into a nationally renowned think tank
Every year the Stanford Review honors a Man of the Year, a tradition dating back to shortly after our founding. However, in all those years, we have overlooked an individual who has earned the title many times over. Perhaps too late, given his recent death, we have decided to rectify the situation by naming Glenn Campbell, former director of the Hoover Institution, as the 2001-2002 Man of the Year.

Mr. Campbell's contributions to the nation as a whole are plentiful. He served two terms on the University of California's Board of Regents, one of them as its chairman. He was a member of both the National Science Board and the President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory Board, as well as chair of the President's Intelligence Oversight Board. None of these, however, would be enough to make Mr. Campbell the Man of the Year. It is for his work at Stanford that we are thanking him.

In 1960, Mr. Campbell accepted a position as director of a small library on the Stanford campus. Thirty years later he retired from a world class think tank. While a few particularly prominent people (Milton Friedman, George Shultz, Edward Teller, and a few others) are usually mentioned to demonstrate the caliber of people Mr. Campbell brought to Hoover, it is the complete list of Hoover scholars which is so impressive. They have influenced national policy for decades, advising one president after another. Indeed, in the last twenty years, Hoover's scholars have served in a host of positions in the federal government, including Secretary of State and National Security Advisor. It is worth noting the researchers from Hoover have worked for every president, Republican or Democrat, since Nixon.

Most important, at least from Stanford's point of view, is the resource the Hoover Institution has been to the campus. Besides bringing many outstanding researchers to the school, it has collected one of the world's largest libraries of documents, a treasure trove for anyone in the social sciences. Hoover's scholars have been part an integral part of Stanford's rise to be in the top handful of Universities in the country, and it was Mr. Campbell's work that put Hoover in the top handful of think tanks.

It has often been acknowledged, if not by Stanford itself, that neither Mr. Campbell nor the Hoover Institution have ever been given the respect they deserve by most of the campus. While I was interviewing students for a position in Stanford's student government, one, who stated on his resume that he was a research assistant at Hoover, felt the need to disclaim that the job did not reflect his political views. And while the campus has been quieter lately, throughout the sixties and seventies some professors joined students in literally assaulting the Hoover Institution, breaking windows and attempting to occupy the buildings.

A continuous battle between Stanford and Mr. Campbell began early in Mr. Campbell's tenure as director, only a few months after he began. Over the next thirty years, the Academic Council and its successor, the Faculty Senate, along with several University Presidents and, of course, hordes of students, cursed Mr. Campbell, demanded his removal, and even burned him in effigy. They profited little; Mr. Campbell managed to fight off attack after attack, maintaining the Institution's ideological and financial independence as well as his own position as director.

Indeed, Mr. Campbell has often turned the attacks of his opponents into victory. He turned broken windows into a fundraising bonanza, and in 1989, the year he turned 65, he managed to extract a substantial retirement package and a reaffirmation of the Hoover Institution's independence in exchange for his retirement.

However the Stanford Review has a more direct reason for honoring Mr. Campbell. From its founding, the Review has had a friend and supporter in Mr. Campbell. His advice was essential to the paper's early years, and we would probably not have survived long without the aid he provided.

Glenn Campbell's years at Stanford have had an enormous impact on the school; without his tenacity Stanford might have faded into obscurity. Mr. Campbell richly deserves to be recognized as the Man of the Year.
