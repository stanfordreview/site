Opinion
Three Cheers for the Stanford Management Company
by Will Hudson
Staff Writer

We've all been working on the letters for The Stanford Fund, either for ourselves or for beleaguered dormmates. Writing neatly with consistent margins and a capitalized "The" obfuscates the fact that there is an incredible amount of money at play here, enriching student academic and social life in more ways than are easily visible. While The Stanford Fund is only one aspect of the massive $8.2 billion endowment that Stanford invests and utilizes in a broad range of missions, it is a central and crucial part of student life.

You probably have never heard of it, but the Stanford Management Company is the group responsible for much of the Stanford community, and the fifty-seven member staff directs the gargantuan endowment that impacts academic programs, student initiatives, and even the gardens at the Stanford Shopping Center.

Never heard of them? To be quite honest, neither had I, though it soon became apparent that this division of the University, established in 1991, touches far more aspects of our daily lives than we would be led to believe. Founded to manage and oversee Stanford's financial and real estate investment assets, the Stanford Management Company (SMC) consists of at least three trustees, many investment and real estate experts, and some university representatives. It is overseen by a Board of Directors appointed by the University Board of Trustees, making the process doubly political and contested, but despite this, the SMC does manage to do quite a bit of work with its roughly $10 billion of endowment and trust assets, temporarily invested expendable funds, and commercial real estate investments, including the Stanford Shopping Center, Stanford West, and the Stanford Research Park.

All of these investments are strategically located with the obvious aim of higher returns. To that end, during the fiscal year 2000-2001, the SMC shifted fifteen percent of its assets from domestic and international public stocks to private equity, absolute return, and real estate. This was largely in response to the global economic recession that made public stock indices fall and become unstable, resulting in negative returns for sixteen of the largest twenty university endowments in the country for the period ending June 30, 2001. Stanford was among this group, ranking eighth with a --2.1% return. Further difficult market conditions compounded the problem, and Stanford ended the fiscal year on August 31, 2001 with a --7.2% return.

The SMC thinks their problems are over, and points to a ten-year return of 13.3% as an indicator of their long-term strength. To test this, I took a look at the University tax returns from September 1, 1999 and August 30, 2000. In 1999, the University invested roughly $2.6 billion in the public stocks of domestic corporations; in 2000, this number increased slightly to about $3 billion. Meanwhile, investment in the stocks of foreign corporations jumped from about $709 million to $917 million. These numbers indicate a growing dependence on foreign corporations; while the domestic portfolio increased by roughly 18%, the foreign investments increased nearly double that amount, by 30%. The relative strength of the American economy and the SMC's seemingly increased reliance on foreign corporations, which were much harder hit by the recession, struck me as curious. I wanted to resolve this issue, but repeated attempts to contact the Stanford Management Company were met with silence, and so I can only speculate why the SMC would be unwilling to answer questions regarding the rationality of their investment choices.

Other investment areas pronounce the SMC's increasing reliance on foreign investments: an increase of 16%, or $135 million, in U.S. corporate and government bonds to $935 million, compared to a 207% increase of $28 million in foreign government and corporate bonds. Again, the reasoning for this investment strategy is unclear since no one at the SMC would comment on any aspect of their program.

Investing in foreign stocks is luckily not the only purpose of the endowment, and much of it does trickle down to daily student life. The Stanford Campaign for Undergraduate Education (CUE) funds programs in conjunction with The Stanford Fund, and is described as "an enduring part of the Stanford undergraduate experience."

Enduring, yes, and so are the chairs of the campaign, including such notables as former Stanford president Gerhard Casper, investing genius Charles Schwab, and Robert Bass. These Stanford alums seek to support and strengthen undergraduate education by developing an endowment specifically for undergraduate programs and by increasing annual giving to The Stanford Fund for Undergraduate Education. The goal of this campaign is $1 billion, earmarked completely for undergraduate efforts. Hallmarks of the program include finances to support individual research and smaller seminar classes with acclaimed Stanford faculty, things we can all be happy about.

This is hard work, and for all of us writing letters this time of the year, the scars of the work come in the form of cramping hands and lost sleep, but in the end it's well worth the investment. By focusing on student initiatives and responsibility to undergraduate education, the Stanford Campaign for Undergraduate Education is helping to make a difference for students who need scholarships and by letting all of us enjoy the benefits of a strong undergraduate education.
