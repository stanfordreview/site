Front Page
Former President Carter Opens Lecture Series
by Joe Fairbanks
Staff Writer

Former President Carter speaks on Oskenberg
On May 6th, as part of the Oskenberg Lecture Series, former President Jimmy Carter returned to Stanford to speak to a large group of students, faculty, alumni, and visitors about his time as President and the relationship between the United States and China. Though Carter was to speak primarily about relations with China during his administration, he was also here to honor one of his closest friends and advisors.

The Oskenberg Lecture was formed in the honor of Professor Michael Oskenberg, a foremost authority on China who died last year. Oskenberg, an esteemed government official, began his academic career at Stanford and was soon acquired by President Carter to assist in the shaping of American policy towards Communist China; Carter credits Oskenberg for bringing Asia and the United States together and as a man who "changed (his) life and, indeed the life of the country and China."

Carter, in beginning his tribute to his former adviser, told a story about a trip to Japan in which he was to speak to a crowd of Japanese students about American foreign policy. He started his speech with an old joke he had used many times before and, in this instance, he got the best response he had ever had, as the crowd burst into an uproar of laughter. After the speech he asked the translator how he had told the joke so that he could replicate it later. Apparently, however, the translator thought the joke was not funny and, instead, told the students that "President Carter has just told a joke, everyone must laugh."

When Carter became president he was immediately interested in changing the relationship between China and the US and, thus, made it one of his top priorities. In order to ensure the feasibility of negotiations with the Communist state, Carter sent Oskenberg to speak to with their government officials. According to Carter, the first trip was unsuccessful, but did not say the reason. One year later, Carter sent Oskenberg back on what was supposed to be a secret trip, but "nothing could be kept a secret, there seemed to be a pipeline between the State Department and the Washington Post building." This time the trip was successful and negotiations began immediately.

Carter's first trip to China, however, was not as President, but as a Naval officer aboard a submarine. He had pulled into the Chinese port the week the Communist were attacking the nation and spreading quickly. They had not yet reached the port he was stationed. Then, on the last day of their stay, "I could see the Communist campfires just a few hundred yards away readying to take the port the next day." That day was his birthday--the birth of the People's Republic of China.

After long, hard negotiations with Communist leader Deng Xiaoping, Carter was able to establish Normal Diplomatic Relations with China. Xiaoping asked Carter if there was anything he could do for the United States or Carter personally. Carter replied that there were several things Xiaoping could do that would help relations stay normalized. Among Carter's requests was a change in China's Constitution allowing freedom of religion. At the time, no one in China had free exercise of worship and Bibles were banned from the country. Carter also requested democratic elections--a request that partially fulfilled as China allowed some elections in small towns, but not on a large scale. According to Carter, this led to the "most successful economic revolution in China."

But not all was well between the United States and China even after Normal Diplomatic Relations was established. There was a strain in the relationship due to the United States' support for Taiwan, a province China considers to be a rogue, break-away of the mainland. In negotiations, Carter agreed to sell only defensive weapons to Taiwan instead of resuming offensive weapon sales. But Carter wanted Deng Xiaoping to reciprocate and agree that any conflict between China and Taiwan would be settled peacefully. Xiaoping did not agree. Carter also mentioned his concern for the "enormous human rights violations China perpetrated and their oppression of Tibet and the Dali Lama."

Our future relations with China will be the most important, Carter said. Issues such as missile defense and the United States' support for Taiwan will be difficult issues. China feels threatened by the US missile defense system because they fear they will not be able to attack the US mainland in case of military conflict, according to Carter.

Finishing his discussion on China, Carter said he did not know for certain how things will work out with China, "but (he) did know one thing: the relationship between the US and China would be better today and Chinese life would be better today if Michael Oskenberg was still alive."

In the final minutes of his visit, Carter took a few questions ranging from the 1980 Olympic protest because of Soviet aggression to the Cuba embargo. He said he regretted advocating the protest of the Olympics, but it was the necessary thing to do, and everyone concurred. On the topic of the Cuban embargos, Carter felt they were responsible for the plight of the Cuban people and that they are responsible for strengthening Fidel Castro and making him look like a hero--an image he does not deserve. Carter visits Cuba this week to push Castro for economic and political freedom.
