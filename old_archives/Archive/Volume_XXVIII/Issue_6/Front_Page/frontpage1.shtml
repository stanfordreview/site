Front Page
Religion a Factor in Hiring Decision?
Potential coach told by Stanford that he was not hired because of his Christianity

by Sam Shapero and Mark Zavislak
Staff Writers

In January of this year, Stanford's athletic department was in search of a new football coach. The Assistant Coach for Nebraska's football team, Ron Brown, decided to fly out to interview for the position. Unfortunately for Mr. Brown, he was not invited back for another interview.

This situation is perfectly normal when there are several highly qualified people interviewing for the same position, as only one applicant can become the new football coach. But Mr. Brown was fortunate enough to have received some reasons why he was turned down so quickly. According to an article published in the Daily Nebraskan, he was told that his religion played a role in the interview process.

This kind of discrimination is unusual in modern America, and more so at a university like Stanford. That is why, when the Daily Nebraskan heard what was going on, its editors knew they had an important story on their hands. In a follow-up editorial to the original article, the editorial board noted Mr. Brown's reluctance to name Stanford directly, and emphasized that they immediately corroborated his story with a staff member of the Stanford Athletic Department. "Stanford Assistant Athletic Director of Human Resources Alan Glenn was one of the major sources in the story, and he told our reporter that Brown's religious beliefs certainly were a key factor in Brown's not being granted a second interview. He also said there were other factors." The next day, Mr. Glenn wrote back to the Daily Nebraskan, stating that his comments "were of a general nature and not specific to Ron Brown or his candidacy for the head football coaching position at Stanford University." As the editors of the Nebraskan pointed out, the paper was investigating the reasons behind why Mr. Brown was not granted a second interview, not on Stanford's general hiring practices. Mr. Glenn's statement implies that Stanford in general watches out for characteristics of people, not limited to their religion, that causes them to stand out. The Nebraskan reported Mr. Glenn as stating "Anything that would stand out that much is something that has to be looked at."

Petition: Stanford should relieve Ted Leland from his duties as Athletic Director if, as evidence suggests, Leland rejected Ron Brown's application for head football coach because of Brown's religious convictions. But Mr. Glenn still emphasized at the close of his letter to the Daily Nebraskan that "The comments attributed to me in the article were taken out of context. Ron's religious views were not a consideration in the decision-making process." Ted Leland, Director of Athletics at Stanford, echoed that "Religion played no role in our decision-making process, and to assert that Ron's religious views were a consideration is inaccurate." Mr. Brown was a radio talk show host who was proud of his openly Christian values, which sometimes drew controversy from different public sources. Stanford Athletic Department representatives have been unclear on whether his public religious stances made him an undesirable candidate.

Stanford Provost John Etchemendy spoke to the Review about his role in overseeing the athletic department. When asked whether he had investigated the decision not to hire Mr. Brown, he explained that "It wasn't a decision not to hire Ron Brown; it was a decision to hire Buddy Teevens. There was no need to investigate." "Ted Leland kept me appraised of each of the decisions, from the initial interviews to the final interviews to the final selection of a candidate, as well as the reasons for choosing them. Religion was not one of them." The issue of religion's involvement with Mr. Brown's interview process remains controversial possibly because of some disagreements on the facts. The Daily Nebraskan has stood by its initial article, and disagrees with Mr. Glenn that his words were taken out of context. The paper felt particularly defensive because the letters sent by Mr. Leland and Mr. Glenn were essentially critical of the paper's journalistic worthiness. In the followup editorial, the editorial board wrote, "We realize it is easy to interpret Brown's silence as somehow implicating the Daily Nebraskan for doing something wrong. After all, we're simply a college newspaper full of inexperienced students just cutting their teeth on the craft, right?" Instead of opening a dialog with the Daily Nebraskan, the editors felt that Stanford simply adopted a policy of denial, including denying the veracity of the quotes in the original article. Mr. Glenn, the day after the initial article was published, stated his words were taken out of context, while Mr. Etchemendy stated that, even if Mr. Glenn did tell the reporter that religion was a criteria in hiring, "He was wrong."
