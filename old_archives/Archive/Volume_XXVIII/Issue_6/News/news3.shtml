Stanford Professor Russell Hardin Talks About Book, Madison, and Racial Profiling
by Mark Zavislak
Staff Writer

Stanford Political Science professor Russel Hardin discusses his new book.
Russel Hardin, professor of political science at Stanford, gave a talk on Friday, April 27 entitled "Terrorism, generalized distrust, and civil liberties." Mr. Hardin wrote the book Trust and Trustworthiness, which he described as an attempt to explain "What does it mean to trust? What makes us feel secure enough to place our confidence - even at times our welfare - in the hands of other people?"

Mr. Hardin opened the talk with the topic of his book, but quickly moved onto focusing the central relationship between civil liberties and terrorism. He said that civil liberties have the central purpose of protecting individuals from the state. Madison, for instance, based much of his governmental thought on how one ought to distrust the state. Mr. Hardin pointed out the liberalism has a tradition in a default state of distrusting government. He noted that civil rights in this century has been focused upon two main areas: the civil rights of blacks, and the civil rights of individuals accused of crimes.

Common political thought often places the concept of stereotyping in a negative light no matter what the circumstances. Mr. Hardin here argued for differentiating between the actions of the state and those of the individual. Placing certain characteristics into different boxes in our minds is perfectly natural and rational, he said. Since it is impossible to get to know everyone well enough before knowing how to trust them, it is natural for a person to make some quick decision on limited knowledge.

People naturally have several reasons for trusting, but much of the trust has to come from valuing relationships enough to keep them going to retain that trust, Mr. Hardin argued. Time is a limited quantity, so people reflexively make an assessment of reliability, which especially factors in risk. Trusting someone to hold your place in line is not the same as trusting them to babysit your child for an evening.

Mr. Hardin agreed, however, that stereotyping becomes a serious problem when it is institutionalized in governmental structures. Whereas it might be acceptable for one police officer to stop people according to his preferences, it becomes a much greater problem when an entire police force is acting in the same manner.

Now Hardin introduced a strong issue: should the state have a forthright policy of stereotyping when it comes to terrorists. He related the incident where Ahmed Ressam was caught with explosives at the border in late 1999, based upon the custom agent's watchfulness for qualities of Ressam that would make him more suspect. An alternative to state stereotyping would be hassling everyone equally. But Hardin asked if that is really reasonable. After all, the Mexican-American border has a large number of Mexican nationals regularly traveling back and forth. It would be pointless to check them on equal terms with everyone else, said Hardin, because no Mexican has been implicated in any terrorist plots.

Mr. Hardin saw the inability of criminal law and the criminal justice system to deal with terrorism as the newest manifestation of an old problem. He introduced the story of David Hume's account of when the Danes continually attacked North England. Mr. Hume saw two choices: kill the resident Danes (retaliation) or sink their ships before they could arrive (interdiction).

Traditional criminal law procedures usually follow around deterrence (in the form of possible future punishment) and punishment itself. Mr. Hardin pointed out that this cannot be applied to terrorists, because they are not concerned about the punishment. The only available method is interdiction of some kind--arresting for intent. Such laws do exist, and are especially applicable when dealing with mafia structures in society.

Mr. Hardin believed that if the U.S. became infused with terrorist attacks, it would be easy to convince the populace to favor social order over civil liberties. After all, argued Mr. Hardin, what are freedoms good for if you cannot deal with any normal, peaceful semblance of life. Efforts to prevent terrorism, says Mr. Hardin, seem to necessarily go against civil liberties. Mr. Hardin said that much of social order depends upon trust relationships; without them, most people would agree that life would be much worse.

One of the most interesting questions after the talk concerned the use of limitation clauses in state constitutions. Mr. Hardin pointed out that the use of these clauses, in the U.S. at least, has been to invade civil liberties by finding holes and construing ways around restrictions. This may not have been the case if Mr. Madison had his wish, and that the constitution would be lacking any specific grants of rights to the people. Hardin said that their has been an interesting shift in the world post World War II. Constitutions now will first list a set of civil liberties to be protected, and then immediately following there is a list of "positive liberties" to be satisfied. While the former are duties to "refrain," the latter is a duty to come up with resources that is almost inevitably never met. The result is that the citizenry of those countries take the constitution less seriously since the government is perpetually in violation. Mr. Hardin closed by saying that countries would be better off with constitutions that they could meet consistently.
