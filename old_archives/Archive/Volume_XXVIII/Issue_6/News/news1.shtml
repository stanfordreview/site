ASSU Election Scandal
The controversies surrounding the recent ASSU elections continue to build. The Review's Editor-In-Chief, Henry Towsner, exposed a serious hole in the security of the online balloting process. A week before the election began, Mr. Towsner notified Elections Commissioner Gedioen Aloula that anyone with access to the ASSU computing system could improperly access and alter restricted information. Subsequently, Mr. Towsner, the former Chair of the ASSU Nominations Commission went on-line and accessed the total number of voting students, although log records indicate that he did not tamper with the results nor view the number of students voting for any particular candidate.

This breach of security was not the only problem with the election. Three Special Fee groups were left off of the ballot for several hours. Although one, the ASSU Online Course Guide, was passed anyway, the other two, SCBN, Stanford's student run television station, and Stanford Mock Trial, both failed. Both groups were eventually funded out of a special fund reserved for that purpose.

The other challenge to the election, Senior Patrick Crosetto's claim that the "instant runoff" voting used during the Presidential election has been dropped. Mr. Crosetto has claimed that the Elections Commission took too long to respond, making his threatened court case moot, while the Elections Commission states that it responded as quickly as was reasonable.

International Student Concerns
The September 11 attacks resulted in a change in US policy concerning student visas that leaves some international students concerned over their ability to study in America.

While in the past acceptance into a major university such as Stanford would almost guarantee the acquisition a student visa, many are less enthusiastic about their chances given current Bush Administration policy. International students comprise around five percent of the undergraduate student body.

The Bush administration has stated that the new policies are an attempt to balance the vitality and quality international students bring to universities with the danger posed by students (or people posing as students) who might abuse their access.

Specifically, the bill passed by Congress places much more stringent requirements on students from North Korea, Iraq, Iran, Libya, Sudan, Syria, and Cuba, and requires much more in depth investigation of students before a visa is granted and a monitoring system once they are in the States.

Rogue States
Thomas Henriksen, associate director for program development and a senior fellow of the Hoover Institute delivered a lecture concerning "American Foreign Policy and Rogue States." The lecture was part of a student initiated course, IR 192: Global Hot Spots, and was open to the public. Dr. Henriksen began by claiming that Nazi Germany and Soviet Russia were the two most rogue states of the 20th Century. Rogue states were defined as nations that repress their own people, promote international terrorism, seek weapons of mass destruction, and stand outside the global community. Most of the rogue states in existence today, such as North Korea, Cuba, Libya, Syria, Egypt, Iraq, and Iran came to be rogue states because of the Cold War. The vacuum of power left after the collapse of the USSR left many nations searching for identity and many leaders grasping for power. Nations like North Korea and Cuba were left without the support of the powerhouse they had thrown their lot in with. The world of international politics changed so dramatically that some rogue states committed atrocities merely to get the attention of the US so they could receive aid or agreements.

Ultimately, though, Dr. Henriksen argued that rogues states cannot exist without the support of some power. He supported this with the evidence of Chinese support of North Korea in retaliation for US support of Taiwan and with the supply of weapons to Iran and Iraq by Russia. Additionally, he argued that rogue states band together even if they fundamentally disagree ideologically. Consequently, the US must adopt a new ideology in combating rogue states. First, while the US must invade and destroy terrorist groups, the battle will not be won through military means alone. Therefore, the US must be willing to act as a patient police force, though help from the British would be desirable because they are better at it. Next, the US must engage in state building to prevent a new rogue government from forming. Finally, the US must use public diplomacy to get the American message out to the uninformed citizens of countries with censored media. Ultimately, it will be a long-term and difficult process to resolve the problem of rogue states.

Stanford Holds Annual Powwow
Stanford held its thirty-first annual powwow, which brought more than five hundred dancers and tens of thousands of spectators to campus. This year's theme is "We Dance With Peace in Our Hearts, Fire in Our Eyes, and Rage in Our Feet!"

The event, which includes dancing, drumming, and more than one hundred arts, crafts, and food booths, would also commemorate the thirtieth anniversary of the replacement of the "Indian" as Stanford's mascot.
