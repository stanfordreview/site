ACLU President Speaks at Stanford
by Ryan Wisnesky
Staff Writer

Nadine Strossen, current director of the American Civil Liberties Union, was invited by the ASSU Speakers Bureau to give a lecture on May 9
Nadine Strossen, President of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) spoke last Thursday to a packed audience at Kresge Auditorium. Ms. Strossen spoke primarily about the new security measures implemented by President Bush following the September 11th attacks.

Ms. Strossen began her speech by asking the audience the question, "Would you be willing to give up liberty to gain safety?" A few people softly responded with the negative, but otherwise the auditorium was silent. Having established that new security measures are needed in the post September 11th world, Ms. Strossen went on to define specifically which reforms which had taken place were justifiable.

The first target of Ms. Strossens' attack was racial profiling. Ms. Strossen noted, "Whenever I debate a conservative Republican I like to cite other conservative Republicans." That "Profiling tactics violate civil rights," seemed to be the gist of her argument. Ms. Strossens did not specify how it is that such rights are violated by this policy. She also advanced an argument from pragmatism, saying that looking for someone who looks like a profile is less effective that looking for someone suspicious, implicitly claiming that such profiling replaces the screening of individuals who looks suspicious.

Also, Ms. Strossens predicted that future attacks may include Asian or African Al-Queda members. Ms. Strossen spent some time on a general ideological analysis of post September 11th laws. She said, "We cannot let the terrorists pressure us into abandoning the ideals that they attacked." Ms. Strossen also told the audience that the ACLU wants the government to base any action on a reasoned cost-benefit analysis. She raised the possibility of disturbing civil rights violations by law enforcement, as several of the recently passed laws allow the government to intercept electronic communications from people who aren't suspected of any criminal act. Because of the possibility for abuse by law enforcement, many people say that the ACLU's work in repealing these laws is of paramount importance.

Another instance of increased governmental power stemming from post-September 11th laws that Ms. Strossen referred to was that of the government's new ability to try people in secret; she noted that 2,000 people have been imprisoned since September 11th but that the government refuses to release any information about them. The ACLU is working with one prisoner whose hearings are so secret that he himself is not allowed to attend them. In speaking about such new governmental powers, Ms. Strossen told the audience we should not adopt a measure just because its name has "anti-terror" in it. Ms. Strossen spoke briefly on a number of issues that campus groups have protested against. Speaking of a new law that prevents non-citizens from being airport screeners, Ms. Strossen said, "We think it is bad for security." She elaborated, saying "It doesn't make sense that I can work in the army but not in an airport." When the audience laughed at this remark, she dryly noted "I think we can cite your laughter to show that the law is irrational."

Ms. Strossen wrapped up her speech by telling the audience how successful the ACLU has been in fighting these new laws. "The one case we brought where the government did not appeal was a victory," she said, and noted that the government has opposed the legal actions the ACLU has brought against it. "When we were fighting for prisoners in New Jersey, they tried to move them to another state before the judge issued an order preventing it," said Ms. Strossen. She said that the ACLU even tried to use international laws in a few cases, and that each judge has ruled in the ACLU's favor. She concluded by directly challenging President Bush by saying "We are not at war." Following her speech, Ms. Strossen answered questions from the audience. When asked about the methods of the ACLE, Ms. Strossen said that the ACLU used litigation, legislation, and education, with the lawsuit as a last resort. She gave a variety of short positions statements, saying that "We support personal privacy and the ACLU is neither pro nor anti gun control." Strossen took a fair amount of time to answer a series of questions about race and ethnicity. When asked specifically about Affirmative Action, she said that the ACLU enthusiastically supports the program.

Another question asked about her feelings on proposition 209, Californias attempt to eliminate all types of racial identification on government forms, and she said, "We opposed proposition 209. We went to court, won in the lower court, but unfortunately it was overturned." She then remarked "We support personal privacy."

Other topics that Ms. Strossen covered included the bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms: "We've asked for civilian control over the ATF because they attack people just for exercising their constitutional rights," said Ms. Strossen. On Campaign Finance Reform, Ms. Strossen remarked, "We're not big fans of finance reform. Complete public financing of candidates would be a more constructive approach." On the subject of "Prisoner's Rights," Ms. Strosen told the audience, "We believe that you do not forfeit rights other than physical freedom when you are convicted."
