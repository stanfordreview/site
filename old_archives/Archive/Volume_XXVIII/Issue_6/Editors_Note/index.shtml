Editor's Note
A Word From The Editor
Henry Towsner, Editor-in-Chief
The last few weeks have seen a small handful of tales of liberal excess pop up repeatedly throughout the news. One of them comes from Stanford: in our lead story, Sam Shapero and Mark Zavislak report that Stanford may have turned down a candidate for Football Couch because he was (are you ready?) Christian. Once you get over your relief that Stanford made sure that a (sorry to repeat the word) Christian wasn't selected for such an important position, you'll notice that the Athletics department can't even give a straight denial (at one point they even suggest that they consider the religion of all applicants when making decisions).

Our editorial staff calls for Stanford to put this issue to rest: the Provost needs to investigate the Athletics Department and either give a convincing denial, or fire anyone and everyone involved with this shameful decision.
Editorial Staff Calls for Investigation
Two things are disturbing about the recent allegations regarding Stanford's Athletic Director, Ted Leland. The first is the possibility that he rejected a candidate for head football coach (Nebraska assistant coach Ron Brown) in part because he is a devout Christian who has called homosexuality a sin. The second is that the Stanford administration has failed to respond.

Stanford must reject any sort of religious litmus test for employees; Assistant Athletics Director Alan Glenn's statement that Mr. Brown's religion might be objectionable to some alumni bears a frightening resemblance to arguments once used against Jews, and which could be used today against Muslims or atheists. As a University committed to the exchange of ideas, Stanford needs to prevent even the appearance that one group of students and alumni can blacklist anyone critical of them.

The University needs to take decisive action to end the speculation that Mr. Brown's personal religious beliefs were a factor in the selection process. Mr. Glenn's half hearted denial, claiming that his comments were "of a general nature and not specific to Ron Brown," only compounds the problem. Are we to conclude that the Athletics Department considered the religion of every applicant a relevant topic?

If Mr. Brown's religion was not a factor, so much the better, but Stanford needs to be able to say so convincingly, and only a thorough investigation, conducted from outside the Athletics Department, will be able to satisfy this need. And if Mr. Brown's religion was considered, Stanford needs to remove everyone involved, up to and including Mr. Leland, if necessary.

It’s worth addressing the details of the story briefly. The specific problem with Ron Brown, the candidate in question, is that he has publicly denounced homosexuality as a sin. Obviously hiring him could precipitate a serious battle within Stanford. It’s worth noting, however, that there is no evidence that he has descriminated against homosexuals, nor intends to. Indeed, his statements suggest that he would not do so. Unfortunately, with Mr. Brown no longer willing to comment publicly, and Stanford issuing denials (albeit somewhat confused ones), it becomes difficult to find out what really happened.

Another story shows Berkeley at its best (well, worst, actually). One course description warns that conservatives are unwelcome, while another, more general one, warns away anyone who might disagree with the TA’s opinion. Although we leave the detailed coverage for our compatriots at Berkeley, we do make a few jokes at their expense (and Stanford’s) in Smoke Signals.

On the theme of security and liberty, and tradeoffs between them, we cover two recent speakers: Political Science professor Russel Hardin is covered by Mark Zavislak, and ACLU director Nadinne Strossen by Joe Fairbanks. Both spoke on the legal changes which have taken place since September 11, as well as the changing ladscape of the racial profiling issue. Perhaps unsurprisingly, Ms. Strossen is firmly opposed to any form of racial profiling, while Mr. Hardin suggests that it is not only legitimate, but may be neccessary in some areas.

Meanwhile, Stanford ushers in a new series of annual lectures, the Oksenberg lectures, named after recently deceased Political Science professor and Hoover fellow Michael Oksenberg. This year’s speech was given by former President Jimmy Carter, the President he once advised.

We also continue to shine a spotlight on Stanford’s administration. Will Hudson reports on the status of Stanford’s endowment, investigating what Stanford does with its billions to keep them turning in a significant divident. Meanwhile Harrison Osaki looks up the members of the Board of Trustees, revealing just who is in the mysterious body which runs Stanford.

Joe Lonsdale takes campus moderates to task for allowing the far left to dominate campus e-mails lists and political discourse without defending their positions. He challenges a number of traditional leftist positions which most Stanford students seem to accept unthinkingly.

Finally, having criticized student apathy, we encourage it with restaurant and movie reviews by Ming Zhu and Hilary Connell.
